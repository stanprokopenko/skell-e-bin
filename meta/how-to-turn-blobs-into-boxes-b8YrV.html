Understanding Blobbing and Blobs to Boxes

Andrus: First Attempt at Blobbing

Andrus, great job on your first attempt at blobbing. You're working with three sets of lines:

  • Left to right lines
  • Up and down lines
  • Front to back lines

When you're unsure where lines go, remember they follow the front to back lines. Every line will stop where it hits the up and down lines, tuck under, and go in the appropriate direction. Blocking simplifies forms to these three sets of lines, making it easier to manage.

Advanced Hints on Foreshortening

When you look directly at a form, it can be challenging to perceive its 3D nature. As you change your viewpoint, moving above, below, or to the side, planes will foreshorten and curve differently. For example, as you move above an object, the curves and ellipses change due to foreshortening. Don't worry about perfecting this now, but be aware of how rapidly things foreshorten in extreme positions.

Overlaps and Squash and Stretch

When fleshy or blobular forms bend, one side stretches while the other squashes. This concept adds a sense of realism to simple forms. In perspective, this means the closer portion of a form overlaps the further part, enhancing the illusion of depth. Exploiting overlaps can help convey how a form moves back in space.
