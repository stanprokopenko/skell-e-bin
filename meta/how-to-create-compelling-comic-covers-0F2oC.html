My Path to Comic Book Covers

I'm Eric Gist, often credited as EMGIST. From a young age, I wanted to draw comic books. At around eight, I became a comic fan, trying to get my hands on everything. Initially, I aimed to do interiors, submitting samples monthly to companies and collecting rejection letters. It wasn't until I trained with Jeff Watts that I realized I had a knack for single-image storytelling.

I shifted my focus to creating covers, a process that took about ten years to get good and another five to catch on. I started showing my work at Comic-Con, and in 2007, I did my first cover. By 2016, I began working with Marvel, starting with covers for the Haunted Mansion series. My first major Marvel character cover was for the anniversary issue of Crazy in 2019.

Developing My Style

My work is often dictated by how I feel about the characters. I'm known for deep shadows, high contrast, and a moody, noir, or horror feel. However, in the last couple of years, I've branched into brighter characters like the Fantastic Four, drawing inspiration from one of my favorite artists, Norman Rockwell.

The Importance of Specialization

For those starting out, I believe it's crucial to specialize. Get really good at one thing before branching out. Comics are moving towards artists who can do it all, but it's better to be exceptional at one thing than mediocre at many. Be a jack of all trades, master of one.

Enhancing Your Skills

To get better at making covers:

  • Study the masters, not just contemporary artists but those from the entire history of illustration.
  • Work on your fundamentals: figure drawing, anatomy, perspective, composition, and color theory.
  • Make covers. Practice by creating covers, even if they're bad at first.

When it comes to fundamentals, figure drawing and composition are paramount. If you can draw a cool, dynamic figure and place it in an interesting composition, you can get away with a lot.

Embracing Limitations for Creativity

Fitting a story into a single image can be limiting, but in a good way. Limitations force you to be creative and think through problems, leading to unique solutions. This is why thumbnails are crucial. The first few ideas might be unoriginal or bad, but pushing past them leads to something unique.

Advice for Aspiring Artists

  • Avoid forcing your style. Let it emerge naturally. Be yourself.
  • Don't shy away from any influences. There's no such thing as bad knowledge.
  • Embrace limitations as they can breed creativity.
  • Focus on being as good as you can be before seeking broad exposure.

Looking back, I wish I knew how good you have to be. Each level brings tougher competition. Even now, I sometimes wish my work was better when I see it next to artists like Alex Ross. Remember, your job is to sell comics, and you need to be good enough to do that.
