How to Paint a Full-Color Portrait

Copy Code

Choosing the Reference Image

I selected a reference image with strong, easy-to-read lighting and an appealing shape. The clear separation of lights and darks, along with a simple background, sets us up for success.

Creating the Wash

I begin by creating a wash:

  • Using the square selection tool, I define my composition.
  • I select a saturated dark cadmium orange as my base color.
  • With the paint bucket tool (shortcut G), I fill the canvas.

This wash provides a warm undertone for the painting.

Initial Sketch

Using the Loomis method combined with straights and angles, I sketch the head:

  • I start with a simple sphere and carve out the side plane.
  • I pay close attention to the tilt of the eye line, which is slightly down towards the right.
  • I establish the T of the face to maintain symmetry.
  • I block in features like the jaw, neck, and hair, keeping shapes graphic and flat.
  • I use construction lines to ensure accurate proportions and alignments.

Refining the Drawing

I take time to refine the drawing:

  • I place facial features using landmarks like the keystone (glabella), eye line, and lip line.
  • I check alignments by drawing straight lines between features.
  • Using the lasso tool (shortcut L) and transform tool (shortcut Ctrl+T), I adjust parts as needed.
  • I clean up unnecessary lines to keep the drawing soft and clean, which is especially important for female portraits.

Adding Flat Colors

I organize my layers for painting:

  • Wash layer at the bottom.
  • Flat color layer in the middle.
  • Line layer on top.

I add flat colors to the drawing:

  • For the skin, I choose a mid-tone color, adjusting hue and saturation.
  • I fill in the skin areas like a coloring book.
  • I select local colors for the hair, eyebrows, eyes, and lips.
  • For the background, I use a color similar to the straps on her shoulders, leaving some texture for a traditional feel.

Separating Lights and Darks

On a new lights/darks layer, I separate the lights and darks:

  • I select a darker, slightly desaturated version of the skin tone for shadows.
  • Squinting my eyes helps simplify values and see shadow shapes better.
  • I block in shadow areas on the face, neck, and features, keeping them graphic and flat.
  • For the hair, I think in terms of big, medium, and small shapes to add interest.

Adding Highlights and Dark Accents

I create separate layers for highlights and dark accents:

  • For highlights, I choose a lighter, slightly cooler skin tone.
  • I add highlights to areas catching the light: forehead, nose, cheeks, lips, and eyes.
  • For dark accents, I select a dark, warm color (avoiding pure black) for areas of occlusion: corners of the mouth, nostrils, under the chin, etc.
  • I emphasize features by adding depth and contrast.

Blending and Refining

I flatten the layers to start blending:

  • Using a blender brush, I soften transitions between lights and shadows.
  • I add subsurface scattering in areas like the cheeks and nose, where light penetrates the skin.
  • I refine facial features, adjusting shapes and values for accuracy.
  • I introduce a secondary light source with a cool tone on the side of the face and neck.

Final Touches

I add finishing details:

  • I refine the hair, adding texture and defining individual strands.
  • I enhance the eyes and lips with subtle details and highlights.
  • I check overall values and contrasts, making adjustments as needed.
  • I save my work, appreciating the progress made.

Reflection

Throughout the process, I focus on:

  • Organizing values and designing shapes for clarity and appeal.
  • Building a strong foundation with careful drawing and accurate proportions.
  • Ensuring lights and darks are clearly separated.
  • Being patient during the initial stages to avoid issues later on.

By taking the time to study and refine each step, I find that the painting comes together naturally. The key is to enjoy the process and learn from each session. I hope this walkthrough inspires you to tackle your own full-color portraits.
