I like to think of art as an idea. Any idea can be art, whether it’s about beauty, politics, spirituality, or anything else. An artist is simply someone who has a particular idea about the world. And an art movement is a group of artists sharing similar ideas, like cubists or impressionists.

Creativity as Connection

For me, creativity is about connecting two or more separate ideas in a way that most people may not realize. You can connect:

  • Warm and cool colors
  • Foreground and background
  • Light and shadow
  • Positive and negative shapes
  • Architectural and organic forms

Artistic strategies often revolve around finding new ways to join things that seem separate.

Structure and Gesture

I see structure as the separate parts of a subject, like the nose on a face, or the arm on a body. That structure can be broken down into simple shapes (triangles, eggs, boxes, tubes) or it can be turned into three-dimensional forms (spheres, cubes, cylinders).

Gesture, on the other hand, is the life or energy in that structure. When I draw organic subjects that are mostly water, like animals or people, I want to show a fluid, watery quality in the lines.

Structure

• Structure is mechanical and architectural.
• It can be as simple as a single shape or as detailed as a carved, nuanced form.
• Structural thinking gives us clarity about where each form sits in space.

Gesture

• Gesture is the flow or movement within the subject.
• It represents momentum, fluidity, and life.
• A flowing, curving line can capture how watery and dynamic the body can be.

The Figure as Architecture

A living form can be seen as architecture when broken into basic solids. For example, a bird can be simplified into balls, boxes, and tubes. These basic shapes can then be arranged in any position we want on our page, two-dimensionally or three-dimensionally.

There’s also the option to stylize the figure. Renaissance art, like Michelangelo’s work, used egg-like shapes for heads. Some sculptures flatten and simplify the figure in relief, giving it a more mechanical or geometric feel. The underlying principle is the same: see the figure as architecture before adding any further detail or style.

The Figure as Fluid

At the same time, I can layer on the idea of water or fluid motion. Imagine the flow and currents of a river. Each contour of the body and each eddy of movement can be fluid. This allows for stylization that can be playful, expressive, or dramatic.

When I combine structure and gesture, the result can have both architectural solidity and a flowing, lively rhythm.

Combining Ideas for Personal Expression

I like to keep these ideas of structure and gesture with me at all levels:

  • Small details, like a single eyelash, become a simple shape with a bit of life.
  • Larger components, like entire limbs, also have a clear structure and a fluid flow.
  • Multiple figures can share a grand overall composition that still feels alive and connected.

By stacking powerful ideas, like solid structure and fluid gesture, we open up vast possibilities for personal style. We can explore how shape, contour, and flow can be combined for unique expressions in any medium.

Art can be as simple and childlike or as complex and nuanced as we choose. If we keep our focus on strong core ideas, everything else tends to fall into place.
