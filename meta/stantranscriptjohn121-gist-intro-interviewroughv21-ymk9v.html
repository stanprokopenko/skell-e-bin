My Journey in Comics

My name is Eric Gist, often credited as EMGIST. I've worked on various comic covers, including Fantastic Four, Iron Fist, Wolverine, and Hulk. Some of my favorite works are the Fantastic Four and Hulk covers.

I've wanted to draw comics since I was eight. Initially, I focused on interiors, sending submissions and getting many rejection letters. Training with Jeff Watts, I discovered a knack for single image storytelling and pursued cover art. I started doing covers in 2007 and began working for Marvel around 2016 with the Haunted Mansion series. My first Marvel character cover was for the anniversary issue of Crazy in 2019.

Developing My Style

I'm known for deep shadows, high contrast, and a moodier, noir or horror feel. Lately, I've been branching out into brighter pieces with characters like Fantastic Four and Iron Fist.

I aim to create images that balance between an iconic image and storytelling. I study artists who create memorable images that stand the test of time.

Advice for Aspiring Artists

When starting out, I recommend specializing. Get really good at one thing before branching out. In comics, being well-rounded is valuable, but it's better to be excellent at one thing than mediocre at several.

To improve at making covers, study the mastersâ€”both contemporary and historical artists. Look at cover artists throughout history and narrative painters like Bouguereau, Caravaggio, and Rembrandt. Work on your fundamentals: figure drawing, anatomy, lighting, perspective, composition, and color theory.

Practice by making covers. Create many thumbnails; your first ideas might be unoriginal. Pushing to the tenth or fifteenth sketch can lead to unique ideas. Don't force your style; let it emerge naturally. Be yourself.

Embracing Limitations and Creativity

Limitations can breed creativity. Constraints force you to think through problems and find interesting solutions, leading to more original work.

What I Wish I Knew

I wish I knew how good you have to be. When I was younger, I submitted work that wasn't ready, but perhaps ignorance kept me motivated. Even now, I sometimes wish my work was better. Remember, your job is to create compelling images that help sell comics.
