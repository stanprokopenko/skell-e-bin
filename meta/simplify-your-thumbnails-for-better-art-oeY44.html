Rachel did a really nice tutorial for you guys. I just wanted to point it out here because it communicates what I've been saying. She gives you options:

A lot of you have been choosing the lighting and sacrificing the objects. In her version, with two values, I can see how you can go either direction, and they are both fine. When you add that third value, you can have both, you can communicate the lighting by having a separation between the shadows and the fruits, and you can communicate the separation between the outline of the fruits and the background behind them.

When you only have one value separation to choose from, you have to pick either lighting or items on the plate. Every time you add a value, you can communicate the next most important thing. So you have to choose what is the most important thing to communicate, and then what is the next important thing if you get another value.

You have to be very clear in your decisions, communicating with simple shapes, no detail, just very big decisions of what it is.

Questions

You have some questions:

How Much Time Should I Spend on These?

I like to start with quicker thumbnails, really small, and simplify them a lot. For the first ones, they could be one minute to five minutes. It really depends. Anything longer than that, I think you're either drawing too big, or you're putting too much detail into it.

Then, once you go a little bit bigger, you can spend up to ten minutes. That allows you to think through your decisions a little bit more, get that shape just right, and make sure your proportions are correct. You don't want to quickly scribble it in and it's not clear.

Your thumbnails show that you're putting a lot of time into making sure your shapes are designed well, which is good. This is exactly what you should be spending that time on.

How Do I Approach Dark Colors Within Light and Light Colors?

Not sure what you mean by that. Maybe you're talking about a floating dark shape within a lot of light? If that's what you mean, then it depends on how important that floating shape is. If it's really small and not important, I just take it out, it's a detail, so you can ignore it. But if it's a floating dark shape and it's really important, like a bird flying through the sky and it's small but an important detail, I would put it in.

It depends on the context and what you're communicating. If you put that dark shape in, does it add to the composition or does it distract?

How Much Detail Should Go Into the Shape Design?

Not much, just enough to be able to figure out your value compositions. When I look at yours, it's great. You're at a great level of simplicity while still maintaining the context of what it is you're drawing. You've removed all the unnecessary details, and I can still see what it is.
