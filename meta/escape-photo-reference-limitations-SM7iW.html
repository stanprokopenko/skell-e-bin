Photographic Distortion

Artists need to be cautious when drawing from photos due to photographic distortion. I wrote a book on this topic called "The Lion Eye." A camera lens has one eye, while humans have two, giving us binocular vision and depth perception. Cameras see in 2D, which can distort the perception of distance and size. For example, in a photo, an extended hand may appear much smaller than a foot, which can be misleading. When drawing from photos, avoid letting distortions affect your artwork, as viewers will notice inaccuracies.

Drawing Techniques and Tips

Focus on gestural structure when drawing, finding shapes and rhythms in the body. Here are some tips to improve your figure drawing:

  • Use pentimento (reworking lines) to refine shapes and proportions.
  • Adjust head size to fit into the rib cage proportionally.
  • Legs should be thicker at the top and taper towards the bottom.
  • Err on the side of making legs longer rather than shorter for better visual appeal.
  • Simplify hands in quick drawings, focusing on the overall shape before detailing fingers.
  • The scapula changes the appearance of the back, affecting how the arm and ribcage are drawn.
  • Push drawing tools to their limits before switching to more permanent mediums like pencils.
  • The sight-size method is useful but can be pushed further for artistic style.
  • Consistency in stylistic choices helps create a recognizable style.
  • Use pentimento to adjust and refine proportions, especially in areas like the ankle and foot.

Mnemonics for Figure Drawing

Mnemonics can help remember complex concepts. For example, "Rhythm helps your two hips move." Use mnemonics to remember anatomical landmarks, like the high-waisted swimsuit shape for the sacrum.

Personal Style and Artistic Growth

Emphasize favorite anatomical features for personal style. For instance, the deltoid on female figures is less pronounced but still has a distinct shape. Emphasize what you love in your drawings to convey personal style and preferences. Sometimes draw what you know rather than what you see to correct distortions. Simplify anatomy for aesthetic purposes, especially in female figures. Consistency in stylistic choices helps create a recognizable style.

Practical Advice for Drawing Practice

  • Use short drawings to build skills and confidence, focusing on mileage over detailed rendering.
  • Embrace mistakes as learning opportunities to improve future drawings.
  • Practice finding and emphasizing beautiful anatomical moments, like the rectus femoris.
  • Short drawings help develop skills faster than detailed renders.
  • In life drawing classes, focus on your work rather than comparing with others to avoid pressure.
  • Kill the ego to improve focus and artistic growth.
