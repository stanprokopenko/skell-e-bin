How to Tackle Challenging Figure Poses

Embracing Foreshortening Through Shortened Lines

When tackling foreshortened poses, I focus on shortening lines to convey forms that are moving away from us. By making lines progressively shorter, we create a sense of depth and perspective. It's essential to emphasize wraps, curved lines that follow the form, to suggest volume and the object's orientation in space.

For example, when drawing a figure where the rib cage shifts away and the pelvis pushes back, I shorten the lines of the torso accordingly. This approach helps depict the in-and-out motion of the body, giving it a dynamic presence on the page.

Using Asymmetry to Add Dynamism

Even in poses that seem symmetrical or straight-on, I introduce asymmetry to add interest and movement. This could be through asymmetrical tilts in the shoulders or hips, or by showing the forward and backward projection of the rib cage and pelvis. Asymmetry in space ensures the figure doesn't look stiff and helps convey the natural balance of the body.

Handling Foreshortened Limbs

When a limb is coming directly at us, I shorten its length dramatically. For instance, an arm foreshortened towards the viewer might only be represented by a brief line, and a series of wraps indicating its volume and direction. Practicing ellipses is invaluable here, they help depict cylindrical forms in perspective. Incorporating exercises with ellipses, like gradually opening them or drawing them within angled planes, strengthens this skill.

Incorporating Line Weight and Design Elements

Line weight is a powerful tool to direct the viewer's eye and emphasize certain aspects of the pose. By varying the thickness of lines, I highlight areas of importance, such as weight-bearing limbs or focal points of action. This not only adds depth but also enhances the overall composition and rhythm of the drawing.

Considering the design of the pose, I look for ways to create appealing rhythms and flow between body parts. For example, aligning the curves of the arms and legs can guide the viewer's gaze through the figure, creating a cohesive and dynamic image.

Dealing with Challenging Poses

In poses with overlapping forms or extreme foreshortening, I remind myself of the underlying gesture and structure. It's crucial not to get overwhelmed by the complexities, but to focus on the essence of the pose. If a pose feels too static or symmetrical, I don't hesitate to change it, adding a slight twist or tilt can make a significant difference.

I also use relational measurements to keep proportions accurate, such as aligning the knees or comparing limb lengths. This helps maintain the figure's believability, even when parts are dramatically foreshortened.

The Importance of Gesture as a Foundation

Throughout the process, I ensure the rhythm and movement of the figure remain central. The gesture drawing is a notational tool, a starting point, not the final piece. It's there to capture the action and flow. This will guide the subsequent development of the drawing.

I keep in mind that the gesture doesn't have to be perfect. It's about laying down enough information to understand the pose, and proceed with confidence. There's no pressure for it to be impeccable, it's a step in a larger process.

* * *

By systematically applying these techniques, I've found it easier to approach even the most daunting foreshortened poses. Remember, it's about understanding and conveying the movement and depth of the figure. With practice and patience, handling foreshortening becomes a natural part of the drawing process.

I hope these insights help you feel more comfortable tackling challenging poses. Keep experimenting, stay mindful of the fundamentals, and most importantly, enjoy the journey of drawing dynamic figures.
