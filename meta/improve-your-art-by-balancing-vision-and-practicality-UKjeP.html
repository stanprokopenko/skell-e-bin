Improve Your Art by Balancing Vision and Practicality

Copy Code

I recently chose a thumbnail featuring a biker gang and a bunny boy. I went with this concept because it offered the opportunity to draw edgier, cooler elements that would appeal to a different audience.

Art Brain vs. Business Brain

Something I often grapple with is my art brain fighting my business brain. My art brain wants to create purely for fun, exploring concepts that excite me. It's an essential part of being an artist. On the other hand, my business brain considers factors like the audience, whether someone would want this on their wall, and if it would appeal to studios I aim to work for.

Balancing these two sides is crucial. If either one takes over completely, I wouldn't be happy with the result.

Adjusting the Concept

With this in mind, I started making changes to the thumbnail. I thought about transforming the rough, eyepatch-wearing biker into a more appealing female character. Perhaps she could be doing something more wholesome than smoking, maybe eating a bowl of noodles.

I began by blocking in the main character without worrying about final lines, just to establish the space. I played around with poses, considering how the arms and legs affected the silhouette and overall composition.

I also pondered adding a cat to the scene to introduce a playful element. Using my own body as reference, I worked on hand positions, imagining her holding chopsticks and pointing.

Struggling with Multiple Ideas

As I continued, I realized I was juggling too many concepts: tough bikers, a wholesome meal, post-apocalyptic settings with plants growing everywhere, and playful cats. The piece was losing its focus. I remembered that when you make a piece about more than one thing, it often ends up being about neither.

Refocusing on the Original Vision

Recognizing this, I decided to return to my original vision. I committed to the initial composition and focused on making it a cool piece that relied on the appeal of the bikers. Sometimes, it's important to stay true to the artistic goals of your art brain rather than overthinking with your business brain.

I re-sketched the main character, ensuring her silhouette was strong and the pose conveyed the emotion I wanted. I kept the bunny boy character as initially planned, preserving the contrast between the two.

Finalizing the Composition

With the thumbnail remapped to the desired aspect ratio, I began working towards the final line art. I included elements from my research to create cohesion, like the unique design of the motorcycle and the central back tattoo on the biker.

Sometimes, the most important part is nailing the tone you want to achieve. By refocusing and not trying to force too many ideas into one piece, I was able to move forward confidently.

In the end, balancing my art brain and business brain allowed me to create an illustration that stayed true to my vision while still considering its appeal to others.
