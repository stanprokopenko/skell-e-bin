Materials I’m Using

  • Strathmore 500 paper
  • Conte 2B stick, assorted charcoal pencils/sticks
  • Kneaded eraser (dirty is fine) and a firm plastic eraser
  • Blending stump, sanding pad, soft brush
  • Fingers or a paper towel for scrubbing

Use whatever brand you like, the ideas stay the same.

Two Pillars: Gesture + Structure

I think of every figure as a duet of energy (gesture) and mass (structure).

  • Gesture = the long-axis curve that runs through a part and links it to the next part. It’s watery, alive, always moving.
  • Structure = the form’s volume and planar turns. It shows how light and shadow travel over the surface.

Curves flow, corners break. Everything I do, in line or tone, is just a dance between those two shapes.

Construction: Lines First

  1. Start flat. Block a clear 2-D silhouette for head, rib cage, waist, and hips.
  2. **Add volume. ** Wrap simple tubes, boxes, or eggs around those shapes. Draw one side, the other side, then the end, that end tells the viewer it’s 3-D.
  3. Ride the long axis. Let a single S-curve run from rib cage into waist, then into hips. Stack each form end-to-end so every structure begins the next gesture.
  4. **Use curves and corners. ** A curve means forms flow, a corner means they stop and a new gesture begins. Straight edges are OK, but too many make the pose wooden.

Shading: Tones on Top

I shade with the same logic I draw.

  1. Find the core shadow. Draw it right on the long-axis curve. That line is both gesture and the corner where light turns to shadow.
  2. Fill the shadow. Scrub pigment from the core into the body of the shadow. Squint: shadow must stay darker than every halftone.
  3. Gradate over the form. Zig-zag strokes soften the edge and roll the form. Blend side-to-side, then top-to-bottom if the light drops off.
  4. Cast shadows = hard corners. Where one form abruptly interrupts another, lay a firm edge and drop the value.
  5. Highlights and reflected light. Pull with a hard eraser at the hottest spot, then let it soften naturally into the halftone.

Shadow shows the corner, gradation shows the curve. Same story, different tool.

Working Order

  1. Big gesture and big structure.
  2. Broad shadow shapes and main gradations.
  3. Secondary forms (ribs, shoulder blade, small muscles). Change their spacing, size, and rhythm so nothing repeats mechanically.
  4. Final accents, sharp edges where you need snap, lost edges where you need calm.

At any point I can erase, push darker or even move a line. Wrong lines often add motion, so I leave some in, multiple spines feel like time-lapse action.

Simplify, Then Refine

When in doubt, I drop detail and keep the big egg. I only add smaller structures after the main gesture reads. Enough structure to find the next gesture, that's the rule.

Mindset

I’m not chasing engineering diagrams, I’m composing connections. Line, tone, anatomy, even “mistakes” are just ways to bind parts into a greater whole. Keep squinting, keep curves alive and let corners signal new ideas. Everything else is style.
