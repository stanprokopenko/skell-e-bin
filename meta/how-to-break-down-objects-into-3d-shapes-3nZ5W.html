How to Break Down Objects into 3D Shapes

Copy Code

In this lesson, I'll show you how to draw 3D objects from memory by adding and subtracting basic shapes, or primitives. This technique is incredibly useful for constructing complex objects and improving your visual memory. Let's start with the basics and then move on to a practical exercise.

Adding and Subtracting 3D Primitives

Many objects around us are built from basic volumes combined in various ways. This is especially true for man-made objects, but even organic forms can be broken down into simpler shapes.

For example, a camera is essentially a box with a cylinder for the lens. A pen might be a cylinder with a boxy part that has half-cylinders at the top and bottom. By combining these simple shapes, we can create complex objects.

Adding Shapes

Let's start by adding a cylinder to a box:

  1. Draw a simple box.
  2. Sketch a light ellipse on top of the box.
  3. Draw a similar ellipse on the other side of the box.
  4. Connect the ellipses to form a flattened cylinder.
  5. Darken the lines that represent the outer form of the combined shape.
  6. Erase or lighten construction lines that should appear "ghosted" or hidden.

Remember, it's okay to do this intuitively. You don't always need to be perfectly precise - a good guess often works well.

Subtracting Shapes

Now, let's try subtracting a shape:

  1. Draw another box.
  2. Sketch an ellipse that intersects with the box at an angle.
  3. Draw the corresponding ellipse on the other side of the box.
  4. Instead of adding this shape, we'll subtract it. Darken the lines that show where the shape is removed from the box.
  5. This creates a curved "valley" in the box.

When subtracting, it's helpful to think about the entire volume you're removing. This makes it easier to visualize how the subtraction affects both sides of the object.

The Memory Drawing Exercise

Now that we've covered the basics, let's try a memory drawing exercise. I'll use a whistle as our subject.

Analyzing the Subject

Before drawing, I spend some time observing the whistle:

  • The main body is a cylinder.
  • It has a boxy part (the mouthpiece) that tapers outward.
  • There's a small cylindrical subtraction at the top.
  • The mouthpiece connects about halfway down the cylinder.
  • The mouthpiece proportions are similar to a deck of cards.

Drawing from Memory

Now, I'll hide the whistle and draw it from memory:

  1. Start with the top angle of the box (mouthpiece).
  2. Draw the box without tapering, then add the taper afterward.
  3. Add the cylinder (main body) to the box.
  4. Draw an ellipse inside a square to create the circular end of the whistle.
  5. Connect the box and cylinder, adding a slight curve at the connection point.
  6. Add the subtracted portion at the top using another small ellipse.
  7. Include details like the thickness of the whistle and the small rectangle on the mouthpiece.

Comparing the Result

After finishing, I compare my drawing to the actual whistle:

  • The overall shape is similar, but I drew it from a more top-down view than the original.
  • The proportions are a bit off - I made it wider than it should be.
  • Despite these differences, it's recognizably the same whistle.

Importance of Visual Memory in Drawing

This exercise isn't just about creating a perfect replica. It's about training your visual memory. As you practice, you'll improve your ability to remember and recall visual information. This skill is crucial when drawing from imagination, as it allows you to pull from a larger "visual library" in your mind.

Tips for Practicing Memory Drawing

  • Start with simple objects and gradually increase complexity.
  • Time yourself - try looking at the object for 30 seconds to 2 minutes, then draw for 3-5 minutes.
  • Practice regularly to see improvement over time.
  • Don't worry if your drawings aren't perfect - the act of trying to remember is what trains your brain.

Remember, most of us aren't born with an exceptional visual memory. It's a skill that needs to be developed through practice. By consistently exercising your visual memory, you're telling your brain that this information is important and worth retaining.

So keep practicing, and you'll be amazed at how your ability to draw from memory and imagination improves over time!
