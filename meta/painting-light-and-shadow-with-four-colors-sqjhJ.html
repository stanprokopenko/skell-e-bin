Painting Light and Shadow with Four Colors

Copy Code

Limiting the Palette for Better Lighting

There are a lot of colors on the color wheel, which can really complicate things when it comes to painting good lighting. In this painting, I aimed to push the sense of light and color by limiting my palette. Even though the painting looks colorful, I achieved this effect by deciding on the overall temperature of my light source and carefully selecting my colors.

Choosing Warm Light and Cool Shadows

I wanted my light source to be warm and inviting, so I chose an orange hue. For the shadows, I selected the opposite colorâ€”a blue hue. But I didn't want to use just two colors, so here's the trick: I split these colors off to use two colors in the light and two in the shadows.

Creating a Quad Palette

By splitting the warm and cool hues, I created a quad palette:

  • Light Colors: Orange and Green
  • Shadow Colors: Blue and Purple

This gave me an entire area of the color wheel to work with, allowing me to reach the colors I needed without feeling overwhelmed.

Setting Up the Line Drawing

To start, I generated a line drawing using Photoshop's tools:

  1. Duplicate the Layer: I duplicated the original layer.
  2. Apply Filters: Went to Filter > Stylize > Find Edges.
  3. Adjust Levels: Pressed Ctrl+L to adjust levels and simplify the lines.
  4. Desaturate: Used Ctrl+U to reduce saturation and remove unwanted colors.
  5. Clean Up: Manually cleaned up the line drawing with the brush tool.

I changed the line drawing layer to Multiply mode, renamed it for organization, and locked it to prevent accidental painting on it.

Blocking in the Background

I started by flooding in the background with cool colors to make the warm light pop forward. Using a textured brush, I applied cool blues in the background. For the water, I used a pure, saturated blue.

Applying Local Colors

Local colors are the inherent colors of objects. I laid in the local colors for the main elements:

  • Monster: A saturated green.
  • Girl's Skin: A peachy reddish hue, slightly grayed.
  • Dress: A lighter, grayed-off version of the skin tone.
  • Hair: Dark color with enough contrast against the background.
  • Eyes: Used blues to give them more pop.

Introducing Light and Shadow

Determining Light and Shadow Areas

With the local colors in place, I determined where the light and shadows would fall. Since my light is warm, I used the warm colors for the light areas and the cool colors for the shadows.

Painting Shadows with Multiply Mode

I created a new layer set to Multiply mode for shadows:

  • Started with the blue from my shadow palette.
  • Desaturated and lightened it, since Multiply mode darkens colors.
  • Applied shadows loosely, not worrying about exact shapes.
  • Switched between blue and purple to introduce variety in the shadows.

Adding Light with Overlay Mode

For the light areas, I:

  • Selected the orange from my light palette.
  • Used a soft brush set to Overlay mode.
  • Applied light where the warm light would naturally hit.
  • Introduced green to ensure the monster still looked green.

Building Color Harmony

To achieve color harmony, I:

  • Carried colors through the painting: Introduced colors from my palette into different areas.
  • Desaturated colors to bridge hues: Bringing colors closer to gray brings them closer to each other.
  • Varied saturation: Added neutrals into saturated areas and vice versa.

Enhancing Shadows with Ambient Light

I added ambient light to the shadows to reflect the environment:

  • Used desaturated blues for ambient light on the monster's face.
  • Lightened shadows where ambient light from the sky would naturally hit.
  • Maintained the limited palette while adding richness to the colors.

Refining the Painting

Adjusting Values and Edges

  • Value Contrast: Ensured the monster's shadows were lighter than the dark background.
  • Edges: Used medium edges to have room to make them harder or softer later.

Developing the Characters

I focused on the characters to bring them to life:

  • Monster's Features: Brought out his eyes and smile, adding subtle details.
  • Girl's Features: Added eyes, nose, and hands holding the book.
  • Hair and Clothing: Introduced colors from the palette to add depth and cohesion.

Using the Smudge Tool for Texture

I employed the smudge tool to:

  • Create diffuse edges on the monster's fur.
  • Blend colors seamlessly for soft transitions.
  • Add texture to areas like the monster's belly and the girl's hair.

Adding Final Details

Ambient Occlusion and Highlights

  • Added ambient occlusion in areas with limited light, like where the monster meets the water.
  • Applied highlights to the monster's eyes and other key features.
  • Adjusted occlusion shadows to enhance depth.

Enhancing Color Temperature

  • Introduced warmer or cooler hues to various areas to add interest.
  • Used soft edges to blend colors naturally.
  • Carefully increased saturation in select areas for emphasis.

Knowing When to Stop

It's important to recognize when a painting is finished:

  • Shape Refinement: When tweaking smaller shapes and details.
  • Color Dispersion: When the palette's colors are well-distributed throughout.
  • Feedback: Showing the work to someone else can offer fresh perspectives.
  • Avoid Overworking: Remember, adding more isn't always better.

Final Touches

I added reflections in the water:

  • Used an Overlay brush with the monster's colors.
  • Suggested reflections with loose, horizontal strokes.
  • Incorporated colors from the palette to ensure cohesion.

Lastly, I added subtle God rays using a soft airbrush set to Linear Dodge mode:

  • Used warm orange to enhance the magical lighting.
  • Carefully applied to avoid overwhelming the existing light and shadow interplay.

* * *

By limiting myself to four colors, I was able to create a painting that feels rich and colorful. This approach simplifies the process and enhances the light and color in the artwork. Would you guess that this painting only uses four colors?
