Using Four Colors

I like to use a four-color ballpoint pen (green, red, black, and blue) because each color has its own value and reacts differently when I smudge it:

  • Green has the lightest value. I use it for subtle details or backgrounds.
  • Red is warm. I often outline with red first, then pass over it with black to create contrasts and cooler tones elsewhere.
  • Black is versatile for outlines and darker shading.
  • Blue smudges more easily than black. It’s helpful when I want to go really soft and blurry with shadows.

I sometimes lick my finger or pre-wet the paper to help with blending. When we wet the surface, the pen’s ink seeps in more, creating thicker lines or softer edges. I don’t mind if the paper tears a bit. Thicker paper adds texture and supports heavier smudging.

Smudging and Shading

When I shade, I often feather in some lines and then smudge to form a core shadow. Smudging does the work of blending values for me. If I want a strong highlight, I darken the light side around it, so the highlight stands out. I also use a tissue to wipe off the pen’s tip when it clots or to brush ink onto the paper in a subtle wash.

Drawing Basic Shapes

Sphere

I start by outlining the sphere lightly with a chosen color, then place a cast shadow beneath it. I like to follow the sphere’s contour lines to suggest its form. After I draw a few soft lines, I smudge them to create the core shadow, letting the gradient appear naturally. If I want a highlight, I keep one area clean and make the surrounding values darker so the highlight pops.

Cylinder

I outline the ellipse on top and the base, then add a cast shadow. If the light source is on one side, the shadow will stretch in the opposite direction. I apply broad strokes along the curve, then smudge to get a smooth transition. By building darker tones on the side away from the light, I create a distinct light side, core shadow, and reflective light.

Box

I draw the box with simple lines, then figure out where the light hits. Each plane has its own value. I make one plane darker, smudge a bit, and leave the top or front plane lighter. An occlusion shadow tucked beneath the box makes it feel heavy and grounded. If I want beveled edges, I simply add narrower planes and keep the same light and shadow rules consistent.

Cone

For a cone, I draw the circular base and outline the sides tapering to the tip. The cast shadow fans roughly in the direction of the light source. I outline the side, smudge the area that should be darker, and let the core shadow form. A line can help define that core shadow before I blend it. Sometimes the paper tears, but I go over it again to smooth out the edges.

Additional Techniques

I like to vary my line weight. Thicker, bolder lines come forward, while thinner lines recede. A ballpoint pen can easily switch between thin and thick lines depending on my pressure. If I want a softer wash, I might wet a tissue with ink and brush it across the drawing to deepen or unify values.

Overall, my drawing process is part drawing, part painting, since I rely heavily on smudging, blending, and adjusting edges. I don’t shy away from rough textures or minor paper tears, I think they add character.

I hope these demonstrations help you understand how I render basic shapes. Feel free to experiment with different pressures, pens, and papers to find the look and texture you like best.
