How to Design a Cottage in 3 Views

Copy Code

I'm going to draw an orthographic projection of little Blake's cottage. This involves creating the front view, top view, and side view of the cottage. By the end of this exercise, we'll have a detailed representation of the cottage from multiple angles.

Drawing the Front View

I begin by sketching a general square shape for the main part of the house. Next to it, I add a little side shed, aligning it with the main structure.

Drawing the Roof

I estimate the space for the roof, making sure it's equal on either side. I darken the lines to show that the roof is indeed wider than the house. This will serve as the front view of the cottage.

Placing the Door

To place a door right in the center, I find the center by drawing an X from corner to corner, an old trick to quickly locate the middle point. I mark the height of the door with tick marks, and estimate it to look proportionate.

Adding Pots for Plants

I decide to add a couple of pots for plants out front. I line them up, making sure they're pretty much centered. I'm eyeballing it, but it looks accurate. These pots are miniature versions of the door, perhaps not quite as tall since they'll have plants on them.

Adjusting on the Fly

As I design, I make choices on the fly. This involves estimating proportions and making adjustments as needed.

Adjusting the Shed Roof

I notice that the shed roof is as tall as the main house roof. I decide to lower it to create a more pleasing design.

I quickly adjust the height of the shed roof, bringing it down slightly. Working in pencil makes these changes easy.

Drawing the Top View

To show the depth of the house, I need to create a top view.

Flipping the Drawing

I imagine flipping the house to look down on it. All the width lines remain the same, so I draw vertical lines from the important features of the front view.

Establishing the Front Wall

I decide to place the front wall in the top view close to the edge to save space. The width of the roof remains consistent.

Determining the Roof's Center

To find the center of the roof in the top view, I measure the width and find the halfway point. An easy way to do this is by marking the ends on a piece of paper, folding it to match the marks, and finding the center crease.

Drawing the House and Pots

I outline the house in the top view, making it clear where the walls and roof are. For the pots, I place them in front of the house, ensuring they're properly aligned.

Drawing the Side View

Now, I'll create the side view to show the height and depth.

Transferring Heights

I transfer the heights from the front view by drawing horizontal lines across. This ensures that all the heights remain consistent.

Aligning Features

I align the peaks of the roofs from the front view to the side view. This means the peaks will line up vertically.

Determining Depths

I use the top view to transfer the depth measurements to the side view. By projecting lines from the top view, I can accurately place the features in the side view.

Adjusting the Shed Depth

I make a decision about how far back the shed extends. I want the roof peaks of the shed and the main house to align vertically, so I adjust the depth accordingly.

Connecting the Views

With all three views, the front, top, and side, I can ensure that all elements of the cottage are accurately represented.

Using Projections

I use projection lines to transfer measurements between views. This includes heights, widths, and depths.

Adjusting Angles and Dimensions

As I connect the dots, I adjust angles and dimensions as needed to ensure everything aligns properly. If something doesn't look right, I make the necessary changes.

Finalizing the Drawing

Adding Details

I add details to the drawing, such as texture to the roofs, perhaps giving them a thatched appearance. I might even add a little hand-drawn wiggle to suggest texture.

Ensuring Consistency

In orthographic projections, it's important that dimensions remain consistent across views. I resist the temptation to add perspective distortions.

* * *

By creating these three views, the front, top, and side, we can deduce most of the structure of Blake's cottage. Orthographic projections provide a clear and accurate representation of the object from multiple angles.
