How to Combine Bridgeman with Photo Reference

Copy Code

Overview

I’m taking a torso pose from a photo of my model, Yoni, and I am combining what I’ve studied from Bridgman and other references to create a strong anatomical drawing. I like to think of this approach as “bridging” my knowledge from various sources, especially Bridgman, to interpret what I see and clarify forms in my drawing.

I begin with a simple block-in, focusing on big shapes and key landmarks: the rib cage, the pelvic region, and essential muscle groups. My goal is to map out how those muscles and bones overlap before I commit to refining edges and shading.

Basic Block-In

I start by lightly sketching:

• An oval or simple shape to represent the rib cage.
• A “V” shape where the arms lift and connect to the torso.
• The sternal notch area at the center of the chest.
• A rough outline down to the pelvis, keeping track of how the torso twists or bends.

As I place these shapes, I think of overlaps, who’s on top, who’s behind. For instance, when arms are raised, the deltoid and the top of the pectoralis often blend into each other. I also sketch through the form, lightly indicating where the navel might sit, while keeping track of centerlines.

Major Anatomical Landmarks

I pay close attention to where muscles insert and overlap. Some of the big areas I keep in mind are:

Pectoralis Muscles
• There are upper, middle, and lower heads of the pec.
• When the arms are raised, the lower portion can stand out more.

Serratus and External Oblique
• Serratus muscles run between the ribs and appear as “fingers” along the torso’s side.
• External obliques intertwine with the serratus.
• This area gets visually complex with raised arms, so I map one shape at a time.

Abdominals
• The “six-pack” or rectus abdominis sometimes shows distinct separations.
• The linea alba is the separation down the midline.
• Below the navel, you might see a small “apple top” separation in the lower abs.

Inguinal Ligament
• This cord-like structure runs from the pelvis toward the lower abs.
• It creates a defined groove in lean figures.

Latissimus Dorsi (Lat)
• Inserts up high on the humerus.
• Shows more distinctly on one side depending on the pose and lighting.

Hard and Soft Edges

I keep repeating a mantra of “hard under, soft on top.” With overhead lighting, upward-facing planes are often lighter and softer, while downward-facing planes get sharper shadows. This approach helps me clarify form:

• Hard edges where the form turns abruptly into shadow (like beneath a pronounced rib or muscle).
• Soft or lost edges where the plane transitions more gradually (often on top-facing surfaces).

By adjusting edge sharpness, I communicate the structure of muscles without flattening them.

Establishing and Refining Values

Once my shapes are mapped out, I lay in shadow shapes at about half their final darkness. I call this “placeholder shadow.” Later, I can deepen shadows to create reflected light effects:

  1. Lightly shade the larger shadow areas with a mid-tone.
  2. Identify the darkest core shadows and push them incrementally darker.
  3. Use the original placeholder tone as reflected light, rather than erasing too much.
  4. Feather edges where surfaces transition softly, and keep edges firm where the form is crisp.
  5. Blend carefully (with a finger or a stump) to avoid losing the structure I built.

Personalizing the Finish

After I’ve located everything accurately, I decide how far I want to render. Sometimes, I carry it all the way with multiple passes of darkening core shadows, refining transitions, and clarifying smaller shapes like subtle muscle striations. Other times, I do a simpler block-in focusing on main forms and call it done.

The finishing stage is partly about taste. Some artists like crisp, well-defined edges, while others prefer softer, more painterly transitions. As I gain confidence, I explore different styles, always keeping in mind the core principle: the drawing must communicate the structure clearly.

Practice Tips

• If a pose is intimidating, like arms raised overhead, begin with big shapes and gesture lines. • Overhead lighting is ideal for learning form because it shows clear top planes and cast shadows.
• Re-draw or “chisel” shapes if something seems off. A small correction early saves big headaches later.
• Focus on each muscle’s geometry: how it wraps around the rib cage or overlaps the next group.
• Revisit drawings like this occasionally. Each pass helps you memorize anatomy and deepen your skill.

* * *

This exercise is about bridging your knowledge from references (like Bridgman) to a live or photo reference, and then interpreting that information in your own drawing. I like to stay flexible, using my knowledge of anatomy as a guide rather than a set of rigid rules. Stay curious, take your time, and let each pass refine your understanding of the figure.
