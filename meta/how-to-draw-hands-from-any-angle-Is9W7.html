How to Draw Hands from Any Angle

Observing Asymmetry and Gesture

I like to look at hands from different angles. I often notice that each gesture has an asymmetrical flow. Sometimes it’s a curve, a curve, or an S-curve. I prefer an S-curve because those subtle transitions show how the wrist meets the articulating hand. The thumb typically sits at an oblique angle, which adds a nice contrast to fingers that may be aligned vertically.

When drawing, I watch how the knuckles might widen and how the wrist stays slender. I try not to exaggerate that disproportion. If the wrist is too thin compared to the knuckles, it can distort the overall structure. I like to keep it more parallel before building out the hand shape.

Establishing the Hand’s Core Shapes

I often start with a simple form for the back of the hand. If the hand is going away from me, I imagine it as a layered construction with a half-and-half split: part that is closer, part that recedes. Then I can add the fingers one by one. I minimize how far the side of the hand juts out at first and only add more if needed.

When the little finger is pressed against a surface, such as a shoulder, it might flatten. Sometimes the thumb also expands. I’ll mark out these overlaps and expansions later in my drawing. If I see a slight curve across the fingers, I emphasize it to keep the shape attractive and graceful.

Finger Placement and Foreshortening

When the fingers are foreshortened, I remember that they should look shorter than expected. I divide them into halves or quarters if needed. It helps me keep them from becoming too long. Once I place the joints, I can always make small adjustments by bending or flattening them to correct their length.

With perspective, some fingers might be at a flatter angle (appearing longer), while others recede more dramatically (appearing shorter). I sometimes prefer to start with a rigid, tube-like shape for each finger and then refine the gesture after. This avoids “spaghetti fingers.”

Boxy vs. Curved Structures

When I see a hand that looks like a two-by-four or two-by-six, I might draw it as a box with one corner chiseled away. If the knuckles are aligned in a curve, I’ll round the top edge. Sometimes I’ll leave it boxy on the underside and more curved on top. Renaissance artists often portrayed a cupping action on hands, which gives a graceful feel.

If I need to be more precise, I observe how each finger angles back in perspective. The middle finger is often the longest, but it might look shorter if it’s foreshortened. I measure each subsequent finger by comparing it to the one I just drew. I also curve or loop any webbing between fingers to anchor their spacing.

Palm Side and Wrist Connection

On the palm side, I often imagine another box-like shape, but I bulge it slightly to resemble the natural curve of the palm. Where the wrist attaches, I might see a clear corner in the form, or it could be flatter. I check the foreshortening at the wrist, visibility can make it appear shorter or longer than I first expect.

When adding the thumb, I look at how it lines up with the rest of the palm. Sometimes I cut off the top corner of the thumb or finger shape with a flatter line to indicate the nail plane. This helps orient the finger and nail correctly.

Incorporating the Ulna

When examining the forearm to hand connection, I remember the ulna is on the little-finger side. I like the mnemonic: little finger has an L, ulna has an L. The ulna has a prominent head near the wrist, and the hand often articulates on that side. It provides a good landmark but doesn’t usually drive my gesture since it’s often a straighter line. Still, including that influence in my construction can add realism to the wrist and forearm area.

I usually start with the sweeping gesture of the forearm, then look for that straight ulna to lock in structure. Even if it’s subtle in the final drawing, visualizing it helps me place the rest of the forms more confidently.

That’s how I approach these hand poses when focusing on gesture, proportion, perspective, and structure. By paying attention to curves and angles, I keep the hands looking coordinated and expressive.
