Working with Spheres

Sphere studies are a great way to practice fundamental concepts. When painting spheres, it's important to explore the full range of grays and saturation levels. Don't keep your saturation locked within a narrow range. Introducing more grays can add depth and relieve the eye from overly saturated areas.

Be mindful of shadow placement. The sphere only touches the table at a single point, so the shadow shouldn't extend too far back or be too wide. This helps the sphere feel like it's sitting on the surface correctly.

Also, soften the terminator line, the point where light turns into shadow. A softer edge creates a natural transition, and makes the sphere appear rounder. Remember to include reflected light in the shadow areas, especially where the sphere is further from the surface. This adds depth and realism to your study.

Managing Color Saturation

When working with colors, try not to keep your saturation within a tight range. Vary your saturation levels to create interest and depth. Think of saturation like sweet candy and grays like veggies, you need a balance to keep your painting healthy.

If you're using a complementary palette, consider making one color dominant by increasing its saturation, while limiting the saturation of its complement. This helps to organize your colors effectively.

Carry your colors throughout the painting. If you're using a specific color in one area, find ways to incorporate it subtly in other areas. This unifies the painting and creates a cohesive color scheme.

Understanding Light and Shadow

Light and shadow are crucial in creating believable forms. The light source, especially if it's strong, will influence everything it hits. Be consistent with your light temperature across the painting. If you have a warm light, it should affect all areas in light with that warmth.

Be careful with reflected light. Rather than showing it everywhere, choose specific areas where it makes sense, like where ambient light might bounce onto an object. This helps to organize your values and colors more effectively.

When depicting a glow or bloom effect, like light wrapping around an object, apply it where the light is brightest. Use a soft brush to add a subtle glow without overdoing it. This enhances the brightness without making it look too digital or obvious.

Using Edges Effectively

Edges play a significant role in painting. Soft edges help blend colors naturally and add to the realism of your forms. When transitioning from light to shadow, especially on round objects like spheres or human figures, soften the edges to create a smoother gradient.

Avoid using the same edge and width consistently. This repetition can make your painting look unnatural or stiff. Vary your edges to create a more organic and dynamic feel.

When rendering textures like grass or foliage, avoid repetitive patterns with hard edges. Instead, use softer strokes and vary the shapes to mimic the randomness found in nature.

Composition and Value

A strong composition relies on effective use of contrast and silhouette. Ensure there's enough contrast between your subject and the background to make it stand out. Manage your values carefully to prevent visual fatigue and to guide the viewer's eye through the painting.

Use gradients to create depth and atmosphere. For instance, the sky often gets darker the higher it goes. Incorporating this gradient can add a sense of realism. Similarly, soften edges where values are closer together to enhance the atmospheric effect.

General Painting Advice

  • Ambient Occlusion: Use it to ground objects and create a sense of contact with surfaces. It's a subtle darkening where objects meet or where light has difficulty reaching.
  • Soft vs. Hard Edges: Default to softer edges for a more natural look, then introduce hard edges where necessary. This can prevent your painting from appearing too harsh or digital.
  • Light as a Bully: Remember that strong light sources overpower local colors. Be consistent with how light influences objects in your scene.
  • Reflected Light and Bounce Light: Thoughtfully incorporate these elements to add depth and realism to your shadows.
  • Avoid Repetitive Patterns: Especially in natural elements, repetition can look unnatural. Train your hand to make varied strokes and avoid creating uniform shapes.

By keeping these tips in mind, you can enhance your sphere studies and paintings, creating works with greater depth, realism, and visual appeal.
