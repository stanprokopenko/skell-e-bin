Overlapping Forms for Dynamic Hands

Copy Code

I like to start every hand study by sketching simple shapes that represent the palm, thumb, and fingers. That keeps things clear when I begin adding details. In a pronated hand (palm facing down), for example, I focus on the structure behind the thumb, making sure I see the fullness of the hand before worrying about each finger.

Pronation vs. Supination

I remember that pronation is when the palm faces down and supination is when the palm faces up. If you think “holding a bowl of soup,” that’s a supinated position. The difference in wrist rotation changes the angles I see in the palm and knuckles.

Basic Hand Architecture

I often imagine the wrist and back of the hand as a blocky shape, like a 2x4. This helps me remember it’s wider at the palm side and narrower at the wrist. It also helps me track perspective when the hand angles in space.

The “Bulging Box” Idea

From many angles, I’ll draw the back of the hand as a bulging box, where the edges can curve gently instead of staying stiff and straight. That box can widen or narrow to fit my gesture and perspective.

Adding the Thumb First

Sometimes the thumb will be in front, and other times it’s on the side, but I usually sketch it in early. It overlaps the back of the hand and attaches at that padded thenar eminence (the fleshy base of the thumb). I often let the thumb overlap a construction line for the palm to remind myself which shape is on top.

Placing the Fingers

I add fingers once the thumb and palm feel solid. I look for overlaps and corners: - Knuckles and Tendons: Before sketching each finger, I note a small corner or “crest” on the back of the hand where knuckles and tendons begin.
- Length and Spacing: I’ll sometimes exaggerate finger length instead of making them too short. It’s an easier mistake to correct later, and it gives the hand a bit more grace.

Handling Complex Angles

If a view seems complicated, I break it into smaller parts and compare each new addition against everything else. For example:
- Sketch the palm block.
- Attach the thumb.
- Map out each finger’s knuckle line.
- Add curves for tendons and bulges.

I adjust each area as I go, looking for overlaps that keep the drawing coherent.

Profile or Near-Profile Positions

In a pure profile, the hand might look almost like a teardrop or drumstick shape when I consider the forearm. The back of the hand may appear thinner because we see it from the side. I decide whether to group the ring and little fingers together or separate them, depending on how they overlap.

Adjusting Proportions

I often tweak proportions until the hand looks right in the context of the overall figure: - If the thumb feels too thick, I trim it down.
- If fingers appear too short, I lengthen them slightly.

Nothing is locked in until I finalize the drawing. I keep it flexible so I can modify lengths and widths as needed.

* * *

Once I set the main forms, I can go back and refine details, like tiny tendons or the shape of each fingernail. The main lesson is to draw through the form and look past the immediate contours. By simplifying the hand into blocks, bulges, and teardrops, I can control the structure before layering in correct anatomy and gesture.
