How to Simplify Complex Subjects

Copy Code

Starting the Skull Study

I chose to study a Guinea fowl skull from my Skull and Bones reference pack. Skulls are perfect for simplifying complex forms, they combine simple shapes with intricate details. This skull's cool crest gave it a fantasy or dinosaur-like vibe. It was a fun subject.

Setting Up the Canvas

In Photoshop, I started with a big canvas and shrank my reference image, cropping unnecessary space. Opting for a casual mindset, I skipped creating a frame and began with brushy strokes, letting the edges taper out naturally.

Working from Big Shapes to Small

My strategy was to work from big shapes to small shapes. Rather than starting with a sketch, I pretended to have a small canvas and broad brush. I kept my strokes broad, zoomed out, and squinted to focus on big shapes of value and color.

I laid in dark brown and light yellow values, the dominant impressions. Ignoring intricate details at first, I concentrated on big forms. I aimed for general accuracy, to keep the study fast and expressive.

Observing Light and Color

To make the skull feel three-dimensional, I observed how light interacted with it. A direct white light from above, warm yellow light reflecting off the surface, and cool ambient light influenced the colors. The beak's tip reflected cool tones, while downward planes were warmer.

Including the cast shadow was crucial for conveying volume. Anything that blocks light casts a shadow, enhancing the illusion of form.

Adding Details Carefully

I refined existing shapes before adding complexity, practicing restraint. When I added smaller shapes, it was satisfying. I focused on the impression of light rather than copying every detail.

Using Photoshop Tools Effectively

Using Photoshop's mixer brushes from my master pack, I achieved a traditional texture. In digital art, creating surface texture prevents a mushy look. Humans appreciate the tactile feel in images.

I let brush texture imply details instead of overworking them, keeping the painting loose.

Maintaining a Loose Painterly Look

Aiming for a loose look, I imposed limitations. Zooming in to add tiny details would ruin the vibe, so I stayed zoomed out. Flipping the canvas gave me fresh perspective.

As I neared completion, I added final touches while avoiding overworking.

* * *

I hope this inspires you to strategize your paintings and embrace limitations, especially for a painterly style. With this reference pack, you can choose any approach, detailed studies, line drawings, or expressive works.

I'm content with this study and might refine it later with fresh eyes. Taking a break can greatly benefit your work.
