Conquer Complex Scenes with Basic Forms

Copy Code

How do you get good at drawing something as complex as Jack Frost's Palace? You learn to think of the picture in several separate ways.

Breaking Down Complexity

Consider the image as a composition, focusing on how elements fit together. Think about color, line, and shape and how they interact. View it as a graphic story device, maybe turning one shot into three panels. Reflect on the subject matter itself: kids walking up a stairway led by a guy with strange skin.

But there's another side to this. The artist is thinking of the room as round, and we're inside it. He's considering the stairway's big structure and simplifying it down before adding details like the steps.

Simplify Big Structures

This is a basic lesson for artists who want to control complex environments: boil things down to the simplest form possible. Think in big cylinders or big simple shapes before adding complexity.

This setting is particularly challenging because we're inside the structure. We'll deal with that problem when we study the difference between exteriors and interiors.

Recognize Three-Dimensional Structures

Even if the artist didn't draw an explicit analysis, he was aware of the three-dimensional structures, macro, mid-level, and micro. Look at how the pillars in the back of the pavilion get thinner farther away and crowd together, the spaces between them get thinner.

And in case you want to know, this isn't technically accurate. Those far corner pillars would compress much more. But so what? It looks good!

As you study, you'll notice things like that. As you work, you'll choose between technical truth and aesthetics.

See Like a Cinematographer

As you study lenses and proximity, you'll recognize that this is a wide-angle lens. You'll start to see like a cinematographer. You'll notice big three-dimensional structures.

If we were outside the structure, this big cylinder would look different. But the camera is inside. How do you draw that? Think in big cylinders.

Work in Stages

The artist worked in stages. He roughed the whole image in loosely with pencil, placing positions before detailing when working on something this complex.

Sometimes he didn't. He just went straight for it, fearless, spontaneous, planned, or meticulous. We'll deal with that in our next set of lessons.

Focus on Big Simple Forms

The big simple lesson here is to look for big simple things. This was to set sight on big simple forms.
