Common Mistakes in Proportions

Sizing the Width of the Head to the Rib Cage and Pelvis

A common mistake is misaligning the width of the head with the rib cage and pelvis. Ensure that the width of the head matches the width of the rib cage and pelvis. Sometimes, the head may appear too wide or narrow compared to the body. Double-check your distances to maintain consistent proportions.

  • Tip: Use the head as a unit of measurement. The width of the head should align with key points on the body.

Understanding the Eight-Head Division

Most of you have successfully applied the eight-head division in your drawings. Remember, this division helps maintain proportions throughout the figure:

  1. Head 1: Top of the head to the chin.
  2. Heads 2-4: Down to the bottom of the rib cage.
  3. Heads 4-6: Down to the pelvis.
  4. Heads 6-8: Legs down to the feet.

Ensure you're consistently applying these divisions to keep your figures proportionate.

Adding Anatomical Details

While gesture drawing focuses on capturing the essence of a pose, integrating some anatomical details can enhance your understanding and depiction of the human form.

The Sternum as a Necktie Analogy

Think of the sternum as a big necktie:

  • The top of the sternum (manubrium) is like the knot of the tie.
  • It extends down to the second head, resembling the length of the tie.
  • There's a small piece of cartilage at the bottom called the xiphoid process, which makes it look like a sword.

Differences Between Male and Female Rib Cages

Understanding the nuances between male and female rib cages can add realism:

  • Male Rib Cage: Typically has a 90-degree thoracic arch. Represent this by bowing the curve upward.
  • Female Rib Cage: Tends towards a 60-degree thoracic arch. Draw two curves that drop down.

These distinctions help in customizing your figures based on gender.

Building the Pelvis with Precision

The Checkerboard Method

To construct the pelvis with more precision:

  1. Divide the pelvis box into thirds both horizontally and vertically, creating a checkerboard pattern.
  2. Draw a diagonal from the top corner to the first division horizontally.
  3. This diagonal gives you the angle of the ilium bones.

The "Mickey Mouse" Analogy

Visualize the pelvis as resembling Mickey Mouse:

  • The ears represent the iliac crests.
  • The face is the pubic symphysis.
  • This analogy helps in remembering the key landmarks and their placements.

By incorporating these methods, you can achieve a more accurate representation of the pelvis in your drawings.

Placing Limbs Correctly

Adjusting Leg Lengths

Ensure your legs are proportionate:

  • In an eight-head figure, the knees should be at the sixth head.
  • If your legs appear too long or short, adjust the placement of the knees accordingly.

Understanding Knee Placement

When drawing knees:

  • Use the halfway point between the pelvis and feet for knee placement.
  • Represent the knee joint with a cylinder or hinge, showing the connection between the femur and lower leg.

Adding Scapula and Clavicle

Incorporate the scapula (shoulder blades) and clavicle (collarbone) for more detailed anatomy:

  • From the back view, the scapula can be drawn as triangles positioned a head width apart.
  • The clavicle connects to the acromion process on the scapula, allowing for arm movement.
  • Male Clavicle: Has a higher lift.
  • Female Clavicle: Tends to be more horizontal.

These details contribute to the realism of your figures.

Drawing the Arms and Hands

When sketching arms:

  • The elbow aligns with the bottom of the rib cage.
  • The wrist falls at the top of the pelvis.
  • The hand extends halfway down the femur.

Remember to consider the carrying angle:

  • Women often have a greater carrying angle, causing the arms to angle outward slightly.
  • Men typically have a straighter alignment.

Considerations in Gesture Drawing

Don't Worry Too Much About Male/Female Adjustments Yet

At this stage, focus on capturing the basic framework of the pose:

  • Concentrate on overall proportions rather than specific male or female characteristics.
  • Detailed adjustments can be made later when adding muscles and fat pads.

Focus on Basic Proportions First

Establishing accurate proportions is crucial:

  • Use the head as your primary unit of measurement.
  • Ensure the rib cage, pelvis, and limbs are proportionate to each other.
  • Regularly double-check your measurements throughout the drawing process.
