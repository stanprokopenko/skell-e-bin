Understanding Rhythm

Let's start by understanding the term rhythm. These are graceful flows, contours, or gesture lines that connect parts of the hand. Whether it's the gentle curve of a relaxed hand or the dynamic tension in a clenched fist, understanding the gesture adds depth and mastery to your art. If we're not conscious of these rhythms and gestures, it can be very difficult to represent the hand in a natural-looking way.

Five Key Rhythms of the Hand

There are five rhythms that you should be aware of and look for when you're representing the hand:

  1. Rhythm of the knuckles
  2. Rhythm of the first set of knuckles (palm)
  3. Rhythm of the fingers
  4. Rhythm of the fingers and thumb from the side view
  5. Middle's pinky thumb rhythm

Let's dive deeper into each of these rhythms.

Rhythm of the Knuckles

When observing the hand from the front or back, notice the curving rhythms that connect the knuckles. When the fingers are extended, the arcs formed by the knuckles add a sense of fluidity and grace to your artistic representations. These rhythms are informed by the proportions of the hand.

I personally enjoy the halves method as a quick and easy way to remember where the knuckles are located:

  • Take the distance from the wrist to the tip of the middle finger and divide it in two. This is where the first knuckle is.
  • Take the remaining distance of the middle finger, divide it in two, and that's where the next knuckle is.
  • Continue this process to find the final knuckle and where the fingernail begins.

Once you've used the halves method to find the knuckles on the middle finger, you can then use those gestures to locate the knuckles on the remaining fingers.

Rhythm of the First Set of Knuckles (Palm)

From above, there's a curved rhythm that causes the back of the hand to be convex (rounded) and the inside of the palm to be concave (curved inward). When we make a fist, this rhythm is clearly seen on the first set of knuckles. When the fist is tightened, this rhythm becomes even more prominent as the pinky is pulled down. These subtle curves define the palm.

Rhythm of the Fingers

This rhythm is how the fingers curve inward slightly as they move towards the fingertips. This curve becomes more difficult to see as the fingers are spread out, but being aware of this curve can help you avoid the mistake of making the hand feel stiff, rigid, or robotic.

Rhythm of the Fingers and Thumb from the Side View

In this rhythm, we're paying attention more to the pose itself rather than the generalized gestures present in all poses. For example, in some poses, you might have a sweeping gesture, or it might curve inward depending on if the fingers are extended, hyperextended, relaxed, or flexed. Each finger and the thumb might have their own gesture as they flex and extend.

Adding more gesture than what you're observing from the reference can help you breathe life and energy into the pose. If you're gonna err, always err on the side of too much gesture rather than too stiff. Just remember to maintain the proportions and the placement of the knuckles. Adding gesture while removing the structure of the bones and joints will detract from the work of art.

As the fingers and especially the thumb curl, they also turn inward slightly. You can see this as the thumb and pinky touch. Try to touch the outside of the palm of your hand with your pinky. It wants to move inward toward the center of the palm because of the previously mentioned rhythms of the knuckles.

Middle's Pinky Thumb Rhythm

This set of rhythms simplifies understanding the pose of the hand by breaking it into separate parts: the middle three fingers, the thumb, and the pinky. Depending on the pose, it might be helpful to break up the gesture differently. For some poses, it might make sense to break the gesture into the last three fingers going down to the wrist and then have the pointer finger and the thumb as their own separate entities.
