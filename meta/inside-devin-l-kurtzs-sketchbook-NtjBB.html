Inside Devin L. Kurtz's Sketchbook

Copy Code

I often get asked how I blend fantasy and reality so effortlessly in my work. The truth is, I fill sketchbooks with studies of the elements I incorporate into my paintings. Here's a look into my process. Maybe it can help you work through your ideas.

My Practice Sketchbook

This sketchbook is my practice zone. It's different from an art sketchbook where I'm trying to do finished pieces. This one is me studying. Most of the sketches are from reference, and I'm essentially trying to memorize the material. I'll draw right on top of other sketches. For example, I might do one page in pencil and then draw plants right over top.

Studying from Life and Reference

I have about 40 house plants, so I'll draw them from life, but I also love to pull up Pinterest and draw them. There's a little bit of everything in here, motorcycles, bike helmets, keys, gloves, anything I could use for set dressing. I like to come up with shapes and draw many different versions of the same thing. I find that really appealing.

Giving Objects Personality

What kills a scene for me is when everything feels like a stock image version of an object. I never just want to draw a generic chair. I want the chair to have an attitude, a life it's lived beforehand, a history, and tell you something about the character. But to do that, I need to have enough chairs in my visual library to pull from different shapes.

Using Online Resources

I'll go on websites of furniture stores, especially when I'm drawing furniture. I'll visit a lamp store and draw 50 different types of lamps. I love finding an Instagram account that's really into something, like someone who collects vintage tea sets. Their whole Instagram is just different vintage teacups, and stuff. **Gold mine! ** This is exactly what I want.

Studying Animals

I've got some cats here, and I noted the Instagram account I referenced them from. Then I've got frogs, I love frogs, they're my favorite animals. My favorites are poison dart frogs, they're so cool.

At the end of my sketchbook, I have some Shiba Inus. I have a Shiba Inu, so I'm very partial to them. One is saying, "I have never in my entire life done anything wrong," and another is saying, "I have," which I believe.

Drawing Everyday Objects

I love little objects in real life. My rooms are covered in little trinkets. So I've done sketches of glass bottles, more plants, and chairs. I love chairs. Chairs tell you so much about the characters who use them, especially in animated shows. If I'm designing a bedroom for a character, I like to give them a chair that means something.

Even with something as simple as a pillow, just throwing in one accent pillow into a couch gives so much personality. A bohemian pillow gives such a different impact for what character would have it versus a Victorian-looking pillow. You can do so much storytelling with what you put in an environment, so I like to have lots to pull from.

Hands and Figure Studies

I've got lots of studies from other artists too. These are all Milt Kahl hands. Anyone in the animation industry probably knows that Milt Kahl did so much emoting with hands in his work. I have a million studies of his hands.

Then I have some Tarzan hands, probably Glen Keane, and some other people too. There's so much weight in the hands. They did such a good job giving ape-like hand motions and the feet too, they are so good.

I also did some Studio Ghibli studies. I love their body language and the way they draw kids. I did these Studio Ghibli studies, then looked at some photos of actual toddlers and tried to pull in some of the face shapes and stuff into these studies.

Working in Pen

I do most of my studies in pen. I have some in pencil later because that's all I had with me at an event. But I love to do it in pen because if you mess up, great, whatever, just do the next one. It's very permanent, it makes me think about each line more. I don't like to be finicky about it. I like to just make a line and then live with it.

Sometimes I learn more about what mistakes I usually make if I leave the mistake on the page. If I look back over my mistakes, I can be like, "Oh, I'm making the wrists too lumpy," or whatever. I like that aspect of it, so that's why I always work in pen pretty much.

Thumbnails and Concept Work

Here's a thumbnail for a painting that I did, it's Katara with Momo. I love to do a pencil one first and then use pen over top to fix it, or sometimes I'll get wax paper and put that over top.

These are thumbnails from my book "The Bakery Dragon". They're so rough, but you can see how basic my sketches are.

Practicing Composition

I made some groupings of objects from imagination. Once I feel confident in drawing the object, I like to create compositions with it. I take a group of them and try to compose different shapes, big and small, narrow and wide, square and round.

I want the page to look nice to me while I'm looking at it and working on it too, which I think is fun. I'll use the pen to create focal points, like a girl in the middle with a darker shade for her hair and a funny expression.

One thing I love about arranging objects is that you get practice knowing how much space a sketch is going to take up and being able to predict it and envision it on the page. You can see I don't always nail that, but you're practicing, getting mileage arranging objects next to each other, which obviously if you're working digitally doesn't matter, but if you're sketching traditionally, it's always nice to have that muscle.

Compositional Tricks

I think it's really about finding a balance between big, medium, and small shapes. Layering them in a way so there's not a ton of tension but also enough overlap that they feel like they sit next to each other naturally. Varying height and width, sharp versus round.

Reptiles and Dragons

This sketchbook is full of lizards. I always loved drawing lizards, but I was also practicing a lot because I have a dragon book series, so I'm drawing a lot of dragons. Lizards are very helpful for that.

I love doing these where you draw a head looking forward and then shift the angle down in all different directions. I feel like I learn so much about the face shapes and the head shapes and how to turn forms in space from doing that.

These are crested geckos that I've turned into dragons by lengthening the spikes on them, essentially just to make them look like dragons.

These are Komodo dragons and monitor lizards. They have such bulky, fun shapes. They're so weird looking, I love them so much. Their legs are so narrow for how wide their bodies are, and how big their heads are. You wouldn't think that, but that informs the way I draw dragons a lot. I love the look of that.

Drawing Through Shapes

Here I'm drawing through the shapes, trying to do form wireframing where I use lines to give myself an indication of what the forms are. I like drawing flat across a lot of planes and creating more of an angular, blocky look to it. It's easier to turn simpler forms in space, so to draw something from a bunch of angles from imagination, it's easier if you have simple shapes that you can map that onto.

From Reference to Imagination

These are from imagination where I was taking a lot of those shapes that I've been learning and posing them, putting them in different positions, adding expression, making them stand up and walk around and stuff like that.

Studying Different Species

I have bearded dragons - they are so floppy and wide, they're such funny little guys. Their legs are so narrow for how wide their bodies are, and how big their heads are.

These are leopard geckos. I had a leopard gecko as a pet when I was seven, and I love them. They have really strong eye lines that I think are so cool, and they feel so expressive to me. Look how sparkly their little eyes are. I get why the Geico gecko is so iconic, geckos make such a great little icon.

I drew sailfin lizards, they are gorgeous. They have so many different colors, and they have these soulful eyes. They feel so intelligent.

Tuatara are like their own branch of the animal kingdom, they're so unique looking too. I love their little knobby elbows, their legs have such fun shapes.

Leveraging Online Resources

If you want to draw geckos, there's this website called Morph Market, where people sell their geckos and snakes and stuff like that. There are tons of pictures of geckos and all kinds of stuff you can use for reference.

Practicing with Variety

These are families based on family photos, lots of stuff for children's books. I really had to get used to drawing children because children's shapes are so different. Young children are so different from adults, and there's a real danger of drawing a little adult, which looks so weird.

Form Wireframing and Shape Understanding

I'm trying to do form wireframing here, using line to give myself an indication of what the forms are. I'm practicing drawing through the shapes, and that helps me understand how to turn them in space.

Imagination and Repetition

I'm pulling in all the things that I've learned over the course of doing all these studies. I start to pull it together and do some designs and fun posing, just feeling very fluid with my sketching. I think that's the best part of doing this, then I feel like I understand the shapes and the forms, and I can pose them however I want and turn them into whatever.

Internalizing the Studies

I rely on the muscle memory that I've worked on. I don't really look through my sketchbooks very often. I think it's more like I store it here, more than actually the drawings themselves.

Experimenting with Color

I've experimented with colored mediums like gouache and watercolor. I used to do watercolor sketchbooks. I haven't done that for a few years, but I would definitely go back to it. I think it's really fun to do color as well.

This sketchbook is really form-based, and as soon as I add color, I feel like I switch more into work mode. I need to practice staying out of work mode and being more experimental with color. When I'm doing this kind of thing, I really don't care whether it looks good, but once I start adding color, the caring switches on, and suddenly I have a harder time being okay with a sketch turning out bad. I know it's just another hurdle to get over because I don't want to care about that. I think color is such an integral part of my identity as an artist, but I think the only way to go through the emotional turmoil is to keep going, you know what I mean?

* * *

I guess I'll show off this book because this is very tied to everything that I was studying in the sketchbook. This is a book all about a little dragon guy, and he needs to be able to walk around, be poseable. I wanted him to be able to emote, to do all kinds of fun motions, to feel like a lizard but also be highly mobile and able to emote through him.

I really needed that looseness and understanding of the shapes in order to be able to pose him with enough fluidity, and I felt like I could act through him. With storytelling, you want the characters to feel like characters. I didn't want him to feel like a lifeless lizard, I really wanted him to embody a human child but through a dragon. That's why I really wanted to become super familiar with lizard shapes, and feel like I had that fluidity. I pulled in all different lizard shapes for all these different dragons.

Thanks for having me!
