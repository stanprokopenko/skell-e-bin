Level Up Your Art with Melted Pancakes

Copy Code

In this lesson, we explore Melted Pancakes, a drawing approach that helps us understand perspective and organic shapes. Originally taught by Mr. Graber to seventh graders, this method allows us to create shapes that convey depth and realism in our drawings.

The Two Methods of Drawing Melted Pancakes

Method 1: Using Center Lines and Wrapping

We begin by drawing an organic shape, such as an irregular blob. Then, we:

  • Draw a center line that goes across the shape.
  • Raise a line upward from the center line.
  • Wrap over the top of the shape by connecting the ends with a curved line.
  • Continue wrapping lines back over the top, curving them as needed.

This creates a simple lump that we can draw through, adding dotted lines to show what's on the other side.

Method 2: Drawing Walls and Mimicking Curves

Alternatively, we can:

  • Start with the same organic bottom shape.
  • Draw walls on the sides by copying the curves of the bottom shape.
  • Round the ends and connect the lines to form a three-dimensional shape.

We again draw through the shape using dotted lines, allowing us to see its full form.

Understanding the Horizon Line

We introduce a horizon line to establish perspective. The horizon line represents our eye level. As shapes move relative to the horizon line, their elliptical angles change:

  • Shapes below the horizon line show more of their top surfaces.
  • Shapes at the horizon line are flat, with no visible top or bottom.
  • Shapes above the horizon line show more of their bottom surfaces.

By understanding this, we can accurately depict how shapes appear in space.

Stacking Shapes and Creating Depth

We can build upon the basic shapes by stacking them:

  • Draw another organic shape above or below the first one.
  • Ensure that the curves correspond to their position relative to the horizon line.
  • Draw through the shapes using dotted lines to understand their connections.

Stacking shapes helps us create scenes with depth, resembling natural formations like Bryce Canyon or the Grand Canyon.

Drawing Through the Shape

An essential part of this method is to draw through the shape:

  • Use dotted lines to show hidden edges and surfaces.
  • Visualize how the shapes connect on the other side.
  • Never leave the shape incomplete, always consider its full form.

Drawing through shapes enhances our understanding of three-dimensional form and improves our spatial awareness.

Using Overlap to Convey Depth

Overlap is a powerful tool for creating depth:

  • Place shapes in front of each other, partially obscuring the ones behind.
  • Use overlap intentionally to guide the viewer's eye through the composition.
  • Overlap helps distinguish between the foreground, midground, and background.

By incorporating overlap, our drawings become more dynamic and engaging.

Slicing Shapes at Angles

We can add complexity by slicing shapes at angles:

  • Draw a large organic shape.
  • Slice it with a line at an angle, like cutting through a piece of clay.
  • Erase the unnecessary lines to reveal the new surface.
  • Apply contour lines to emphasize the angled cuts.

Slicing shapes creates variety and introduces different planes, making our compositions more interesting.

Composition and Scaling

Understanding scale and composition enhances our drawings:

  • Use the transfer of scale to depict how objects appear smaller as they recede into the distance.
  • Consider the positioning of shapes to create a balanced composition.
  • Apply foreground, midground, and background elements to establish depth.

By thinking about composition and scale, we make our drawings more realistic and visually appealing.

Connecting Shapes and Guiding the Eye

We can connect shapes to move the viewer's eye through the picture:

  • Extend lines or curves from one shape to another.
  • Create pathways that lead the eye across the composition.
  • Use large shapes in the foreground to frame the scene.

Connecting shapes unifies the composition and enhances the overall flow of the image.

Practice and Mileage

Improving our drawing skills requires practice and mileage:

  • Regularly sketch and experiment with these techniques.
  • Don't be afraid of making mistakes, use them as learning opportunities.
  • Remember that each drawing helps us get better and more comfortable with the subject matter.

By dedicating time to practice, we build confidence and improve our abilities.

Exercises

To apply these concepts, we can follow these steps:

  1. Draw the Two Types of Melted Pancakes

    • Start with a horizon line across the middle of the page.
    • Practice Method 1 by drawing organic shapes, adding center lines, and wrapping over the top.
    • Practice Method 2 by drawing walls and mimicking the bottom curves.
    • Draw shapes both above and below the horizon line to understand how elliptical angles change.

  2. Stack and Overlap Shapes

    • Stack shapes on top of each other, drawing through each one.
    • Create overlap to establish depth.
    • Experiment with placing shapes in the foreground, midground, and background.

  3. Slice Shapes at Angles

    • Draw large shapes and slice them at different angles.
    • Erase unnecessary lines to reveal new surfaces.
    • Apply contour lines to emphasize the shapes.

  4. Connect Shapes

    • Link shapes by extending curves or lines between them.
    • Create pathways that guide the eye through the composition.

  5. Create a Composition

    • Combine all the techniques to create a full composition.
    • Consider scale and placement of shapes.
    • Add details to enhance the drawing.

* * *

By exploring Melted Pancakes, we develop a better understanding of perspective and organic shapes. This method helps us create realistic and dynamic drawings. Through practice and experimentation, we improve our skills and unlock new creative possibilities.
