How to Use Figure Drawing for Growth

Watercolor Warm-ups

In the past month, I filled a sketchbook with various studies and exercises. One of my favorite spreads features three-minute warm-up poses done with a Kuretake Gansai Tambi watercolor palette. I arranged these quick sketches to create a sense of action and motion. This composition gave me ideas for future illustrations, showing that even short practice sessions can spark inspiration.

Quick Value with Watercolor

To capture five-minute poses efficiently, I prefer using watercolors over pencil. Loading my brush heavily with water and ink allows me to quickly lay down values and create gestural marks. I start with the darkest areas when the brush is most loaded, then use the softer values as the ink fades. This technique helps me cover more ground in less time.

Enhancing Sketches with Backgrounds

Sometimes, I revisit sketches by adding backgrounds. Applying color blocking behind a character allows me to reshape and fix mistakes using the outer contour. For instance, adding an edge on a hat can create a highlight that looks intentional. This approach not only polishes the drawing but also teaches me how form and highlights can enhance a piece.

Bridging Loose Sketches and Finished Illustrations

There's often a disconnect between loose drawings and finished illustrations. By going back and polishing sketches, I've begun to understand their core essence. Taking a drawing I initially disliked and transforming it into something I appreciate means that no effort is wasted. Every piece becomes an opportunity to learn and grow.

Experimenting with Mediums and Colors

To keep my practice interesting, I switch mediums, like using a Kuretake Bimoji pen. I also enjoy mixing colors in my palette, intentionally loading my brush with multiple hues. This results in natural gradients and unexpected color combinations, teaching me which pairings I like and encouraging me to try new ones.

Figure Drawing as a Playground

Figure drawing is my safe space to play and develop fundamentals. I focus on capturing volumes and leaving white space intentionally. By doing the bare minimum in some areas, I can imply forms and create interesting visual effects. Composing multiple poses on a page to form a scene also helps me explore storytelling within sketches.

Emphasizing Volumes and Shapes

When drawing, I break down shapes into understandable volumes. Using cross-contours reveals cylinders and other basic forms, making complex anatomy less intimidating. Focusing on these underlying shapes allows me to convey volume with every line, enhancing the three-dimensionality of my figures.

Pushing Style and Experimentation

I use figure drawing sessions to push my style, varying between capturing likeness and exploring anime or comic influences. Tackling challenging poses or subjects, like integrating animals with human figures, helps expand my visual library. Even when a subject feels frustrating, I know that persistence will eventually lead to mastery.

Overcoming Challenges

Sometimes, I encounter poses that seem uninteresting or difficult. Instead of avoiding them, I give them a fair chance and often surprise myself with the results. Tackling these challenges not only improves my skills but also keeps the practice engaging.

Tools and Techniques

Throughout this sketchbook, I used a travel set with a water brush that squeezes water onto the bristles. This allows for quick and efficient use of hard watercolors. I also experimented with loading different colors onto the brush to create gradients and dynamic strokes. Delicacy with pressure and being mindful of brush tip wear are important for achieving desired effects.

Final Reflections

Filling this sketchbook and making each page about something new was super informative. My approach focuses on understanding volumes to have the freedom to draw from imagination. By practicing efficiency and continually challenging myself, I feel that every drawing contributes to my growth as an artist.
