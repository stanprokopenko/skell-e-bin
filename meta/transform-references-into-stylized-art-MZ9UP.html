Transform References into Stylized Art

Copy Code

Hey, I’m Ben, and I love to draw, paint, and sculpt more stylized art. In this lesson, I’m walking through my process of using a photo reference without sticking too closely to it. My goal is to capture only the important parts of the photo, then push and pull the design to create a unique stylized character.

Sketching and Basic Shapes

I begin with a simple shape or silhouette that grabs my attention. I’m not tied to the reference, so I let my imagination lead. Once I have a loose shape for the head, I block in the neck, ear, and nose as more or less 2D shapes. I find it freeing to bounce around the face, sketching the eyes, lips, and hair, while staying loose and not committing to details too early.

I like to flip the canvas horizontally from time to time. It helps me spot any big mistakes and keeps my shapes balanced.

Thinking in 2D and 3D

I often slide between 2D and 3D thinking. For example, a nose can be imagined as a simple triangular or cylindrical shape. By treating forms as basic volumes, I get a better sense of how light will wrap around them. I also like to break the head into planes so I know where I’m going to place light and shadow.

Line Refinement

After my rough blocking, I reduce the opacity of my sketch layer and trace over it more confidently. In this stage, I make sure the face looks cohesive and that I understand the planes enough to shade. I keep my strokes more decisive, still allowing for edits like warping, liquifying, and adjusting shapes.

Establishing Values

Next, I lay down base values in black and white. I’ll use the lasso tool and fill areas of the hair and face with different local values. To keep each region separate, I put them on different layers (sometimes using clipping masks). Once I establish local values, I add a multiply layer on top to figure out my areas of light and shadow.

I like to simulate a top-down lighting scenario so the ear, upper lip, and beneath the nose will catch shadows. As I block shadows, I stop relying on the photo reference. I trust my constructed forms and let them guide my shading.

Using the Filthy Plugin (Optional Speed Boost)

I sometimes use a Photoshop plugin my friend and I developed called the Filthy Plugin. It speeds up tasks like lasso-filling, erasing, and adding shapes. But you can do the same things with the regular lasso, paint bucket, or brush tools. It just helps me work faster.

Building Up Gradients and Adjusting Shapes

I make selections with the lasso tool and use gradients or an airbrush to suggest form. I keep my edges rather hard at first, since everything is still quite rough. I check my shapes, merge layers if I want to make big changes, and then push or pull features for more attitude, expression, or appeal.

Adding Color

Once I’m happy with my values in grayscale, I add color layers. I set the layer mode to Color and pick a base skin color. Then I adjust saturation or hue until I like the look. I do the same for the hair, maybe experimenting with contrasting colors (warm skin, cool hair, or vice versa).

Color Variation

I create extra layers set to Multiply or Overlay to introduce color variation in the skin and hair. I push reds in the nose, ears, and cheeks for a more lifelike look, and sometimes cool down the shadows for a bit of contrast. A bit of freckles or subtle color shifts go a long way in making it interesting.

Highlights and Shadows

To introduce highlights, I’ll use Screen or Color Dodge layers. Light is often strongest on areas like the nose, forehead, and inner corners of the eyes. I’ll also hint at subsurface scattering by painting saturated reds in places where light might pass through the skin (like the nostrils or ears).

For the hair, I might add an extra top-facing highlight. I often mask out shapes to create bolder outlines or stray hair strands. The lasso and a soft brush let me define edges quickly.

Final Refinements

Once the main colors and lighting are in place, I merge my layers again and do normal painting on a single layer. This is where I add subtle transitions, refine lines, and tweak the mood. Sometimes I’ll:

• Darken the background for contrast.
• Paint in stray hairs for a more natural look.
• Adjust the silhouette to keep it appealing.

At this point, my stylized portrait is set, and I can go as far as I want, either a finished painting or something looser.

I hope this breakdown helps you understand how to stylize a reference without relying too rigidly on it. The trick is balancing basic shapes, clear values, and experimental color to bring your character to life.
