Avoiding the Vanishing Point Trap

Starting with vanishing points and drawing straight lines with rulers can be misleading when you're unsure where the vanishing points should go. It's simple in a book where the artist has placed them for you to copy, but on your own, there are countless wrong places to put them. Let's not start there.

Beginning with Blobs

It's better to rough things in first. Start with a simple, rough shape, a blob. Draw a line around something, a line across it, or maybe slice off an end. You can begin by slicing off an end, and then echo that line across the visible side. The blob exists.

Making It a Box

To transform the blob into a box:

  • Snap lines tight.
  • Add corners and square it up.
  • Remember that there are three axes: the long axis (aspect line), and the two other axes at the corners.

But start roughly and lightly. Don't worry about vanishing points yet. Just aim in a general direction and fling the line away.

Understanding Convex and Concave Curves

Get into the habit of recognizing that the convex side of a curve indicates depth. The curve is convex in the direction it goes away. Think of it like a rubber band stretched around your blob, the convex outside of the curve says away. Conversely, the concave side appears closer.

Training Your Perception

Practice this concept:

  • Draw a blob with a convex curve pointing away.
  • A concave curve can make a side look closer.
  • Remember, it's an illusion created by lines on flat paper.

You're training your brain as much as your hand. Get that habit first and fast.

Advancing Your Skills

If you're ready for a challenge and feel that blobs are too simple, try reversing the curves:

  • Scoop out a section of your blob.
  • Reverse the rubber band concept - the concave curve now goes away from you.

Inspiration from the Masters

Want to see this in action? Look at the work of great masters:

  • Their freehand sketches show breathtaking skill.
  • Notice how they reverse lines where necessary.
  • Gaze at greatness to increase your chances of steering toward it.

Squaring Up the Blob

To further challenge yourself:

  • Square up your blob.
  • Add precise lines and corners.
  • Try this multiple times, inventing new positions each time.

If your lines go wild, ask yourself:

  • Do you understand the relationship between the scoop and the squarely chiseled groove?
  • Are you advancing in making your lines recede and controlling the world in your picture?

Practice and Progress

Blobs may not be sophisticated or precise, but they are 3D. Starting with a blob gives you a three-dimensional something to make precise. This is the simplest way to get started with drawing objects.

By continually practicing and understanding these concepts, you're getting closer to reality in your drawings.
