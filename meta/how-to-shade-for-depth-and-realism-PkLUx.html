The Importance of Proper Shading

We know that perspective adds depth to our drawings, but proper shading is crucial for realism. I've seen many students draw beautiful outlines, only to flatten their forms with incorrect shading. Let's change that by learning how to shade effectively.

Understanding Light on Form

Planes and Light Interaction

When light hits an object, it reveals its planes. I imagine them as small flat tiles across the surface. Planes facing the light are lighter than those facing away. Even on smooth forms, thinking of them as composed of many smaller planes helps me imagine how the form turns.

Light and Shadow Families

When shading, I need to clearly communicate which parts are in light and which are in shadow. This simple organization does the most in communicating form.

Elements of Light on Form

The Terminator

The terminator is the edge where light transitions into shadow, dividing the light and shadow families.

Form Shadow and Cast Shadow

  • Form shadow: The area turned away from the light source.
  • Cast shadow: Occurs when an object blocks light from hitting another surface.

Center Light and Halftones

  • Center light: The point facing directly toward the light source.
  • Halftones: Areas where the form turns away from the center light, becoming darker until they reach the terminator.

Highlights and Reflected Light

  • Highlights: Reflections of the light source itself.
  • Reflected light: Light bouncing into the shadow areas, gently lightening them.

Core Shadow and Occlusion Shadow

  • Core shadow: The darkest part of the form shadow along the terminator.
  • Occlusion shadow: The darkest values found in tight crevices where objects touch.

Building the Value Structure

Grouping Values

I think in terms of light, medium, and dark values. I separate light and shadow by blocking in the entire shadow area with a darker value for the reflected light, setting the lightest the shadow can get.

Values in the Light Family

  • Without a highlight, I use three values for halftones: lightest, medium, and dark halftone.
  • With a highlight, I simplify to light halftone and dark halftone, using the brightest value for the highlight.

Values in the Shadow Family

Within the shadow, I use two values:

  • The initial shadow value for the reflected light.
  • A darker value for the core shadow.
  • The occlusion shadow is usually even darker.

Transitioning Between Values

Shading isn't a simple gradation from light to shadow. Transitions within lighter halftones are subtle, while the jump from halftone to core shadow is quicker. Understanding and maintaining the correct relationships between these values is key.

Mastering Light and Shadow

Light and shadow create the illusion of three dimensions on a flat surface. As I practice, I develop intuition for how light describes form, becoming comfortable with the subtle shifts in value that create realistic depth. Mastering these tools ensures my drawings won't look flat.
