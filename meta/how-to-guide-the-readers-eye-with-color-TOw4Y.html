How to Guide the Reader's Eye with Color

Copy Code

In this project, I'm working on a panel featuring a celestial being covered in ice. My goal is to enhance the lighting on the celestial, especially his arm and the ice, to define the form and frame his face. I want to direct the viewer's attention to these important areas while letting other elements recede.

Using Gradients for Highlights

I'm starting by applying gradients to lay in values, colors, and contrast. Using gradients is a quick way to show how I'm thinking about the lighting. I select a desaturated teal, almost a gray, to add highlights on the ice. This color is cooler but not fully blue, and it's less saturated to avoid drawing attention to less important areas.

Around the focal points where I want to draw the eye, I use a more saturated teal. This adds saturation where needed without overwhelming the scene. I carefully select the areas to apply these effects, sometimes using the wand tool to isolate specific parts.

Managing Saturation and Contrast

By adjusting saturation and contrast, I can guide the viewer's eye. I introduce contrast by making the dark sides of the ice even darker, placing the darkest parts next to the lightest parts. This high contrast draws attention to the important features.

To add warmth, I might edge the color closer to purple, moving towards the warmer side of the color palette. This helps in creating depth and interest.

Using Color Holds to Push Elements Back

To push background elements like the mountains further back, I use color holds or knockouts. This involves changing the color of the inked lines from black to a color. I create a clipping mask to affect only the desired lines without altering the foreground elements.

By lightening the line art of the mountains and adjusting their colors, I reduce their contrast against the background, making them recede. This helps focus attention on the celestial and other foreground elements.

Painting Highlights and Adding Texture

I use brushes to add highlights and texture. For the mountain tops, I choose a warm color like yellow to suggest sunlight streaming in from the left. I prefer to build up layers of color gradually to avoid harsh, inorganic looks.

When the foreground and background elements lack separation, I adjust colors or add highlights to enhance the silhouette. By defining these edges, I ensure the important elements stand out.

Working with Selections and the Lasso Tool

Using the lasso tool, I make selections on the arm to apply highlights. This method, sometimes called cut and grad, involves selecting areas and filling them with color. I can repeat this process to build up highlights and shadows, working within the selections to control where the colors are applied.

This technique allows me to render specific areas efficiently, creating depth and form without overcomplicating the image.

Defining Edges and Accentuating Silhouettes

To accentuate the character's silhouette, I work on defining edges. For instance, I'll add light behind the hand to create contrast between the hand and the background. This makes the hand stand out.

By adjusting colors and contrasts, I ensure that the foreground elements have sharp edges against the background. This helps in directing the viewer's eye to where I want it.

Balancing Warm and Cool Colors

I balance warm and cool colors to create depth and atmosphere. For shadows on the arm, I might use cooler blues. For areas catching sunlight, I use warmer magentas. I consider the overall environment to decide on the color temperature.

For instance, if the scene has a lot of cool elements, I'll introduce warmer shadows to add contrast. This interplay between warm and cool tones enhances the visual interest.

Maintaining Focus and Avoiding Over-Detailing

I avoid over-rendering areas that aren't the focus. If the artist hasn't drawn detailed features, I don't add unnecessary detail in the coloring. For example, small background characters don't need highly detailed coloring.

I pay attention to the artist's lines and work with them. Adding too much detail can work against the art and distract from the main focus.

Adjusting Background Elements

In panels with less importance, I let elements recede. I might darken or desaturate areas to prevent them from drawing attention. For instance, I push background mountains further back by lightening their lines and reducing contrast.

When working on interiors, like a conference table scene, I use warm shadows to contrast with cooler floors. I brighten up areas where light hits directly and let the edges fade into darkness.

Final Touches and Special Effects

I add special effects like glows towards the end. To suggest sunlight streaming in, I use gradients set to hard light blending mode. I use selections to control where the effects are applied, preventing them from affecting unintended areas.

I might also enhance highlights on the characters to make them pop. These final touches help in directing the viewer's eye and enhancing the storytelling.

* * *

Throughout this process, I'm constantly assessing and adjusting. By managing lighting, contrast, saturation, and color temperature, I guide the viewer's attention and enhance the artwork. The goal is to support the narrative and the artist's intent, creating a cohesive and engaging image.
