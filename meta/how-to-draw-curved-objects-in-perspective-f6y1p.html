How to Draw Curved Objects in Perspective

I wanted to share some insights from analyzing an arrow drawing by Blondie the Good. The arrow presents several technical challenges worth exploring, particularly regarding structure, perspective, and curves.

Understanding the Arrow's Structure

At first glance, the arrow appears to taper, but upon closer inspection, it maintains a constant width within its box. The illusion of tapering arises from the curve interacting with perspective.

Dealing with Curves in Perspective

When dealing with curves in perspective, it's crucial to use X lines, lines parallel to the X-axis, to keep track of an object's width. This helps avoid confusion, which is caused by the illusion of narrowing due to curvature.

Orthographic Projection

To better understand the arrow's structure, I projected it onto the ground plane, essentially creating an orthographic view. This allows us to see the arrow as a flat shape, eliminating perspective distortion.

Projecting the Arrow onto the Ground Plane

By projecting points from the arrow down to the ground plane using vertical lines, we can trace its outline on the ground. Connecting these points reveals the true shape of the arrow, confirming it doesn't taper.

Introducing Tapering

Adding tapering to the arrow, changing its width along the X-axis, introduces additional complexity. Now the arrow not only curves on the Y-axis (pitch) but it also narrows along the X-axis.

Solving for Tapering and Curves

To accurately depict the tapered arrow, I used a method involving projecting points from the top view down and across, using right angles to find exact positions. This process involves:

  • Determining the new, tapered widths at various points.
  • Projecting these widths down to the ground plane.
  • Combining these with the heights from the curved path to find intersection points.
  • Connecting these points smoothly to form the compound curve of the tapered arrow.

This method ensures precise construction but can be quite complex.

Compound Curves and Artistic Choice

Drawing compound curves, where an object curves and tapers simultaneously, is challenging. While technical accuracy is achievable through meticulous construction, it might not always serve the artistic purpose.

Technical Methods vs. Artistic Expression

As artists, we may choose to simplify or exaggerate certain aspects for visual impact. Overemphasis on technical correctness can hinder creativity. Recognizing when to deviate from strict accuracy allows for more expressive and effective artwork.
