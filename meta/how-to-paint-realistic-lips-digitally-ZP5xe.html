How to Paint Realistic Lips Digitally

Initial Sketch and Lay-In

I began my final painting for the lips chapter with a light gesture and lay-in. Starting with an ovoid shape for the denture sphere, I lightly roughed in the lip line, focusing on the tilt to ensure accuracy. Establishing the center line and the cupid's bow was crucial at this stage.

Next, I defined the lower lip shape and indicated the receding plane under the lower lip that feeds into the top of the chin. I blocked out the philtrum above the upper lip, noting how it connects to the septum of the nose. Ensuring all facial features lined up symmetrically was important, so I paid attention to parallel alignment.

I lightly indicated the nasal labial folds and outlined the chin. After punching in the dark accents in the corners of the mouth, I went over the initial sketch with darker lines for added confidence.

Building Up Layers in Photoshop

I flattened the drawing layers and labeled the line layer. To eliminate the intimidating white background, I created a quick color wash layer underneath, using cad reds and oranges. This wash serves as a base that matches flesh tones and allows for subsurface scattering.

I then colorized the line layer using Ctrl + U (hue/saturation), checked Colorize, and adjusted the saturation and lightness to achieve a burnt sienna hue. This gives the lines a warm tone that blends well with the skin colors.

Adding Local Colors

On a new layer between the wash and the line, I began filling in the local colors with flat shapes. Using a squared textured brush, I chose midtone colors for the skin and lips. My wife's lips are naturally darker and redder, so I selected a suitable hue for them.

I filled in the skin, lips, and nostrils, keeping it graphic and not worrying about gradients or details at this point. I also added a neutral background color for contrast.

Separating Lights and Darks

Next, I created a layer to separate lights and darks using graphic flat shapes. I chose a color slightly warmer and more saturated than the skin tone for the shadow shapes. I carefully designed and simplified the lights and darks, generalizing shadowed areas even if they appeared lighter in the reference.

This exercise helps in designing the shapes and simplifying the values, which is essential for creating a strong foundation.

Adding Dark Accents and Highlights

On a new layer, I added dark accents using warmer dark reds to enhance areas of occlusion without resorting to black, which can flatten the image. I then created a layer for highlights, using cooler colors in the pinks, and kept them graphic. Simplifying numerous small highlights into larger, more manageable shapes helps in maintaining clarity.

Between the highlights and dark accents, I added mid-tone areas, increasing the value and saturation slightly to bridge the gap between lights and shadows.

Blending and Refining

I flattened all the layers and began blending using the blender brush. This step softens the edges and unifies the painting. I focused on separating the shapes in the lips, adjusting values, and refining edges. Using the polygonal lasso tool, I added smaller highlights, aiming for variation in shape and size.

I balanced pushing and pulling the warms and cools to enhance depth and volume. Experimentation at this stage is key to achieving a pleasing result.

Final Touches and Review

I continued to refine the painting, adjusting the nose shape, chin, and secondary light sources in the shadow areas. After final adjustments, I reviewed the anatomy of the lips:

  • Cupid's bow
  • Corners of the mouth with dark accents
  • Middle and flanking tubercles
  • Two tubercles on the bottom lip
  • Hard edge at the base of the lip and the recessed plane under the lower lip
  • Philtrum connecting to the septum of the nose

Remember, the mouth is rounded, and the lip line is like a flattened version of the cupid's bow.

By focusing on these elements and following a structured approach, I've achieved a harmonious balance of warms and cools, enhancing the depth and realism of the lips. This method ensures that the painting conveys the volume and subtlety of the mouth's anatomy.
