Gesture drawing is a fundamental skill for artists working with the human figure. It serves as the foundation for more complex figure drawings and helps capture the essence of a pose. This article explores the key principles and techniques of effective gesture drawing, based on Brent Eviston's approach.

Understanding Gesture Drawing

Gesture drawing is a simplified form of figure drawing that captures the action of a pose. It focuses on two key elements: simplicity and action. The primary purpose of a gesture drawing is to create an armature that can be developed into a more detailed figure drawing later.

Benefits of gesture drawing include:

  • Allowing for easy adjustments and corrections
  • Preventing artists from getting stuck on details too early
  • Serving as a foundation for more complex drawings

Approach to Gesture Drawing

When creating a gesture drawing, focus on capturing action lines that represent the overall flow and movement of the pose. Simplify body parts into single lines, exaggerating curves slightly (up to 10%) to enhance the sense of motion. Draw with some speed, but avoid rushing.

Mindset for practice:

  • Treat each drawing as practice for the next
  • Don't be precious about individual drawings
  • Experiment and explore different approaches

Remember that gesture drawings are not meant to be finished pieces. They often look unfinished, which is entirely appropriate for this stage of the drawing process.

Techniques for Effective Gesture Drawing

To create dynamic and expressive gesture drawings:

  • Use curved lines instead of straight ones
  • Draw the convex side of forms
  • Allow lines to collide if necessary
  • Pay attention to the overall flow of the pose

Common challenges in gesture drawing include avoiding stiff or rigid poses, maintaining believability while exaggerating, and balancing simplicity with necessary detail.

Applying Gesture Drawing Skills

Gesture drawing skills are relevant to various forms of art, including animation and comic art. They help create a sense of potential motion in static poses and are important in all forms of figurative work.

When creating life-like figures:

  • Consider the functional aspects of the human body
  • Develop empathy for the figure's physical structure
  • Ensure poses are anatomically plausible

Critiquing Gesture Drawings

When evaluating gesture drawings, look for:

  • Simplification and capture of action
  • Overall flow and dynamism
  • Potential for further development

Areas for improvement often include adjusting proportions and placement of body parts, refining the use of action lines, and incorporating more three-dimensionality in simple forms.

Practicing Gesture Drawing

To improve your gesture drawing skills:

  • Draw regularly, focusing on the process rather than the outcome
  • Experiment with different approaches and techniques
  • Draw from life when possible, as it provides a different experience than drawing from photographs
