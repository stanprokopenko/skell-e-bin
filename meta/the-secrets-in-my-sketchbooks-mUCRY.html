The Strathmore Watercolor Sketchbook and the Dynamic Bible

I want to start with something a bit more historical, and go into something a bit more modern day. This book is an old one, the Strathmore watercolor sketchbook. I used this sketchbook a lot during that time period because of its watercolor paper, which I really liked. The book is falling apart now, it's been in archives on my shelf for many years. But this is the first book I used to create the Dynamic Bible back in 2016.

The Dynamic Bible is essentially a collection of lecture notes, visuals, and teaching methods that have been curated into a single book from all the classes I taught at Art Center and many other schools. For about six months, I started collecting all that data. So, certain sketchbooks for me can have a purpose beyond just drawing, they can be used to produce something with them.

These are all the originals, drawn straight in with pen and watercolor. I scanned these pages and printed them directly into the book. The only thing I edited was some of the text, which I wrote down as I went along. This was the original Dynamic Bible that I made back then.

To be frank, this book wasn't intended to be sold as a printed book. It was only suggested I do so from people on Instagram and friends who said, "You should really print this and sell it." I did this purely for myself at first. I'd been teaching for about three or four years at this time, and I asked myself the question, Do people understand what I'm talking about? So, I wanted to jot everything in terms of notes and processes onto the page for myself.

As I was doing it, people saw it as a nice collection of all the notes I was doing in the classroom, which wasn't available outside of Art Center. That's why people kept pushing me, **"Make a book, make a book." ** Initially, I told them, "No, I'm not printing a book." This is for me and my own experience. But eventually, as people talked to me more, I realized there seemed to be quite an audience for it. That's when I created a Kickstarter, made two books of these originals, put them together myself, edited them, and did all the work, distribution, marketing, Kickstarter, and successfully campaigned it.

So, starting from a simple question of whether people understood my note-taking, I completed two of these books in less than six months. This is the original Dynamic Bible, filled with the typical sketches and drawings that I do, processes of shape, form, observational sketching. A lot of them were done on-site, I would go to the museum, a caf√©, sit down in some location, and draw these things by looking at them.

The Challenge of Drawing Arm Wrestling

If you really want a challenge, draw arm wrestling figures. It is one of the hardest things to figure out. Most people draw arm wrestling figures with equal competition, meaning the arm is straight up, and they both have equal force being put into it. But in arm wrestling, it's tug and war. There's going to be one of dominance and one of submission.

Where do I put the torque, the power pushed through where the other guy's being strained? This build of the composition, the perspective, the figure, and the pose is one of the hardest things you can do, especially when it's out of your mind. And this is out of my head.
