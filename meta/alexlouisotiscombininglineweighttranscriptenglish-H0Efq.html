Combining different line weight methods allows you to develop your own unique drawing style. These methods are tools to help you make decisions in your artwork. You don't have to stick to just one, you can mix them in any way you like.

The Three Line Weight Methods

Importance

Emphasize the most important parts of your drawing by making their lines thicker. Usually, the outer shapes are more important than the inner details.

Light and Shadow

Use line value (lightness or darkness) to show light and shadow. Lines in shadow areas are darker, while lines in light areas are lighter.

Depth

Create a sense of depth by adjusting line thickness. Closer objects have thicker lines, and lines taper as they recede into the distance.

Balancing Conflicting Methods

Sometimes, these methods can conflict. For example, a line that should be lighter to show depth might also be in shadow, which suggests a darker line. To balance this, use different aspects of line weight:

  • Thickness: Adjust how thick or thin the line is.
  • Value: Change the darkness or lightness of the line.
  • Tapering: Make the line gradually thinner or thicker.

Demonstration: Drawing a Box

Let's apply these methods by drawing a box with a lid.

Establish the Light Source

Choose a light source, for example, from the top right. This determines which planes of the box are in light or shadow.

Assign Line Values

  • Shadowed sides: Use darker lines.
  • Lit sides: Use lighter lines.
  • Mid-tone sides: Use lines of medium value.

Show Depth with Tapers

  • Start lines thicker where they are closer to you.
  • Taper lines as they move away to show depth.
  • Use an eraser to adjust line thickness and value as needed.

Highlight Importance

  • Make outer lines thicker to emphasize importance.
  • Keep inner lines thinner.
  • Balance the line weight to maintain both depth and importance.

Combining All Three Methods

By combining thickness, value, and tapering, you can convey depth, light, and importance all at once. For example:

  • A line can be thick and dark in the foreground (importance and shadow).
  • The same line can taper and become lighter as it recedes (depth and light).

Practice and Intuition

You don't have to think about these methods constantly while drawing. With practice, you'll start to apply them intuitively. Focus on training specific aspects of line weight, and over time, your drawings will naturally incorporate these techniques.

* * *

Keep experimenting and trying new things. Developing your own style takes time, but by combining these line weight methods, you'll be able to tell more complex stories through your art.
