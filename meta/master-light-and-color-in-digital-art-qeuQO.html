Preparing the Base for Color

I've merged all my layers to simplify my base. Now, it's time to bring in the color.

Applying Local Colors

Coloring the Skin

First, I select the skin. It's not a perfect selection, but I can clean it up later. I use the color layer mode and choose a skin color. It's too saturated at first, but I adjust the intensity to keep the value structure intact.

Coloring the Hair

Next, I select the hair using the lasso tool. Holding alt lets me select straight lines easily. I decide on a blue color for the hair to create a warm-cool contrast. Again, I use the color layer mode and adjust the saturation. I experiment with different layer modes like multiply, overlay, and even vivid light to see which effect I prefer.

Adding Color Variations

To make the skin feel less flat, I introduce some color variation. On a new multiply layer, I add reds to the ears and nose and a bit of yellow to the forehead. I might overdo it initially, but I can always dial it back by adjusting the layer's opacity. Adding cooler tones to the shadows creates more contrast against the warmth of the face.

I repeat this process with the hair, adding some red to the back on a multiply layer to create an ombre effect. It's all about experimenting and seeing what looks interesting.

Refining the Painting

After merging the layers, I continue painting, focusing on lights and darks. I use the smudge tool sparingly to avoid making things muddy. I prefer to have my values more defined before blending.

Adding Specular Highlights

Now, I start adding specular highlights using a screen layer mode. These highlights emphasize the shininess of areas where light reflects more intensely. For example, adding a highlight to the eyelid instantly makes it look shiny. I consider the planes of the face that catch light similarly, like the eyelids, nose, and lips. It's important to keep the underlying form in mind so the highlights make sense.

Incorporating Rim Light

I block in the rim light to enhance the lighting effect. By understanding the forms of the face, I can apply the light appropriately, ensuring it follows the contours rather than just being a straight line down the edge.

Utilizing Subsurface Scattering

To capture the effect of subsurface scattering, I use a color dodge layer. I add saturated reds to areas where light passes through thin skin, like the nostrils and edges of the face. This creates a glowy effect that adds realism.

I remember that surfaces hit by light can reflect color onto nearby areas. For instance, the neck illuminated by the rim light can cast a subtle glow onto the underside of the chin. I use a saturated color rather than pure white to reflect this.

Final Touches

I merge the layers again and continue refining. I might add subtle skin textures like freckles or beauty spots using a multiply layer. Adjusting the opacity helps me control how prominent these details are.

As I refine, I constantly check the overall composition, ensuring the colors and values work together. It's a balance of experimenting and making adjustments to bring the portrait to life.
