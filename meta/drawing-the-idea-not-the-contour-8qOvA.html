The Three Big Questions in Gesture Drawing

To make sense of gesture, I like to break this big category into three different pieces:

  1. Why am I doing it? Or, why are others doing it? Why does that person's gesture look like that?
  2. What am I doing it about? For our discussion relative to figures, I'm going to focus on the eight parts of the body.
  3. How am I going to achieve the type of look I want?

These are the big three questions I consider in any visual pursuit. There's no right way, there's just your way or how you want to organize your thoughts.

Abstraction and Its Importance

Generating an idea is most easily attained in terms of abstraction. Abstraction means to withdraw or remove what's the essence of a subject and put it into its simplest terms. In this case, that could be lines like a straight, a C, an S, or maybe your basic shapes: a sphere, a box, a cone.

In gesture drawing, we're not copying the thing, we're generating an idea about it. These are really confusing concepts. They're always the questions that I get about gesture. Examples from Art History

I want to direct your attention to people who have helped me come to this understanding, people you can also reference. You'll start to see the great degree of similarity that exists in the way many artists define an idea.

The Renaissance and the Vitruvian Man

My favorite thing in art is always seeing the high against the low. An example of a high kind of framing of an idea is the Renaissance. We can understand the Renaissance in the form of one image: the Vitruvian Man.

You've all seen this, I hope. It's a figure suspended between a square and a circle. What does this mean? Well, this exercise was called squaring the circle. Every artist was trying to make the figure dissolve into this harmonious proportion, somewhere between a box and a sphere.

Why? The sphere symbolically represented the idea of the divine, and the box represented the idea of the terrestrial or the earthly plane. The figure was meant to be some kind of synthesis, an aesthetic bridge that connected the divine and the terrestrial.

The goal of the Renaissance was to give rebirth to principles of antiquity that had been lost, such as harmony, and connections between the divine and the terrestrial. This illustration embodies those concepts, distilling the idea into its most simplest terms.

Andrew Loomis and the Line of Beauty

Another example is from Andrew Loomis , whose books are very popular. Look at how abstract his description of line is. He has seven steps, it's very specific, organized, and intentional. Number one is something highly subjective, conveying beauty.

For him, the idea of rhythm is important. This is something I hear talked about all the time in gesture. He discusses basic lines of rhythm and beauty. That's where I would identify his understanding of gesture.

William Hogarth's Serpentine Lines

Loomis references William Hogarth, a British artist who had lots of ideas about a line of beauty. Hogarth would often hide this line in his images, like on his palette or within different aspects of the composition, because gesturally, that's building his concept forward.

He described serpentine lines, talking about them with great intention. They move the eye and create a pregnant moment, which is sometimes really useful for thinking of figures where we're trying to create anticipation.

Nathan Cabot Hale's Curved Lines

Other books, like Nathan Cabot Hale's "Abstraction in Art and Nature", talk about line in a similar way. He describes the curved line as delightful or pleasurable. There's this connection between these authors about line and its pleasurable qualities to the eye.
