How to Sculpt Dynamic Zombie Poses

Copy Code

Welcome to this spooky Halloween sculpting session. Today, I'm diving into creating a Halloween-themed character. I'll be spinning a wheel to decide which character to sculpt, adding an element of surprise to the process.

Materials and Tools

For this quick, fun sculpture, I'm using:

  • Wire armature: A basic framework for the sculpture.
  • Basic sculpting tools: Some of which I made myself.
  • Aluminum foil: To fill out the armature and save on clay.
  • Wax-based clay: Similar to the brown microcrystalline wax I've used before.
  • Character wheel: To randomly select the character to sculpt.

Choosing the Character and Pose

I spun the wheel, and it landed on female zombie. Keeping with the zombie theme from last time, I'll be sculpting a female zombie today.

For reference, I'm using a pose called Felicia 007. The pose feels very zombie-like, with an unbalanced stance and the body twisted in an unnatural way. I plan to exaggerate this pose as much as possible to embody a possessed, out-of-control zombie.

Building the Armature and Posing

First, I capture the pose using the wire armature:

  • Push the gesture further: Exaggerate the pose to enhance the zombie-like qualities.
  • Adjust the armature: Bend the arms and legs to reflect the twisted, unnatural stance.
  • Modify proportions: Push the hips up to shorten the distance between the torso and legs.

This gives me the basic pose, which I can attach to the base using some clay.

Preparing the Clay and Base

I'm using a green-gray wax-based clay, which suits the zombie theme. To make it easier to work with, I warm it up until it becomes softer and more pliable. In the early stages, softer clay helps build up forms faster.

I build a small base for the sculpture and press the armature into it. Then, I fill out parts of the sculpture with aluminum foil to save on clay.

Covering the Armature with Clay

With a basic understanding of the pose and the foil in place, I cover the armature in a thin layer of clay:

  • Start from the feet: Working upward adds stiffness to the lower body.
  • Keep it thin: Allows me to build forms without getting too thick too fast.
  • Use clay pancakes: For larger areas, flattening the clay helps cover more surface.

I make adjustments to the pose as needed, since big changes are easier to make at this stage.

Blocking in the Head

For the head, I use a wedge-shaped block-in, similar to an axe shape. This helps establish:

  • Front face plane: The overall direction the face is looking.
  • Head proportions: A good foundation for adding facial features later.

Refining the Major Forms

Now, I start thinking about the major forms:

  • Rib cage
  • Pelvis
  • Head

I also begin adding anatomy details, especially in thicker areas like the legs. I focus on the front angle and then the side view to see how the rib cage compares to the pelvis. Drawing guidelines helps me keep the forms aligned.

Adjusting Anatomy and Pose

I notice the wrist is bending the wrong way, so I make that adjustment early on. I imagine this zombie was at a swimming pool in a swimsuit, which lets me work out the anatomy without it being indecent.

I modify the pose slightly to improve the design. As artists, we have the freedom to make such changes to enhance the sculpture.

Capturing Gestures and Simplifying

I focus on capturing the gestures:

  • Legs: There's a nice gesture where the muscles wrap into the gluteus medius.
  • Side silhouette: Simplifying the side view helps avoid adding unnecessary clay.

Simplifying the curves and gestures keeps distractions out and emphasizes the overall movement.

Sculpting the Face and Expression

To keep the face feminine, I add hair to soften the features. For the mouth, I decide to open it unnaturally wide, making it uncanny and unnerving, perfect for a zombie.

  • Cheeks tightened over teeth: Gives a skeletal appearance as the hunger sets in.
  • Wide-open mouth with teeth showing: Enhances the horror element.

I adjust the arm and head positions so it looks like she's spotted something to devour.

Refining Details

I continue to refine the sculpture:

  • Anatomy: Adding muscles along the spine toward the trapezius.
  • Hands: Capturing the gesture simply, suitable for a gesture study.
  • Clothing: Indicating a swimsuit helps focus on simplified anatomical forms.

Final Thoughts

This is a quick gesture study, done more for fun and to practice anatomy, gesture, and pose without getting bogged down in details. Quick studies like this are great for learning and improving sculpting skills.

* * *

I encourage you to try your own gesture studies, maybe even with a Halloween theme. It's a fun way to stay creative and productive. Thanks for joining me in this sculpting session, and I hope to see you in another video.
