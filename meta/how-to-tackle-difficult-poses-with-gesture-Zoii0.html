How to Tackle Difficult Poses with Gesture

Copy Code

In this lesson, you'll learn how to tackle poses that aren't exactly friendly, those with challenging perspectives or extreme foreshortening. Instead of developing new techniques for every unusual pose, you can adapt existing methods by flipping or adjusting them to suit the figure's posture.

Flipping Rhythms for Leaning Figures

When drawing a standing figure, you typically organize your rhythms in a certain sequence. For a leaning figure, you can flip this sequence of lines to match the new posture. Begin by taking the standard rhythm lines you use for an upright figure and invert them to accommodate the lean.

For example, if the figure is stretched forward, place the primary line along the back to emphasize the feeling of stretch there. Then, indicate the pinch on the front by adjusting your usual sequence of lines. This approach allows you to use familiar techniques without reinventing your method for each new pose.

Adjusting Lines for Bending Poses

When dealing with poses that collapse, bend, or break the standard norms, you might not be able to rely on large relational measurements, like dividing the body into equal halves. In these cases, consider:

  • Shortening lines to indicate foreshortening.
  • Emphasizing wrap lines to show how forms move toward or away from the viewer.
  • Integrating observational measurements, such as plumb lines, to compare relationships within the figure.

Balancing analytic and observational approaches helps you organize information effectively while adapting to the pose's challenges.

Maintaining Rhythm in Complex Poses

Even in difficult poses, the rhythm and flow of the figure should remain. The lines may become shorter or adjust in direction, but the animated, active, fun, lyrical movement is still essential. Focus on:

  • Keeping your gesture lines fluid and dynamic.
  • Ensuring that the rhythm doesn't get lost as you navigate foreshortening.
  • Thinking about design elements, like line weight, to direct the viewer's eye.

Using Wrap Lines and Shortened Lines for Foreshortening

Foreshortening can be daunting, but you can simplify it by:

  • Shortening your lines to show forms coming toward or moving away from the viewer.
  • Using wrap lines to indicate the volume and direction of forms in space.
  • Practicing drawing ellipses and wrap lines to become comfortable with indicating depth.

When a limb is foreshortened, resist the urge to make it longer. Instead, make the line shorter and use wrap lines to convey the correct spatial relationships.

Introducing Asymmetry in Symmetrical Poses

Symmetrical or straight-on poses can be challenging due to their lack of dynamic movement. To address this:

  • Introduce asymmetry through tilts in the shoulders or hips.
  • Use perspective asymmetry by showing the rib cage moving away in space while the pelvis comes toward you.
  • Emphasize forward and backward projections to create depth even in seemingly symmetrical poses.

Small adjustments can add interest and prevent the figure from feeling static.

Embracing Design and Composition

As you become more comfortable, start treating your gestures as compositions:

  • Use line weight to emphasize important areas and direct the viewer's attention.
  • Think about the overall design, considering how different parts of the figure relate to each other.
  • Incorporate directional lines and shared rhythms to create unity within the pose.

By focusing on design elements, you can enhance the visual appeal of your drawings and prepare for further development.

Adapting and Inventing Poses

Don't hesitate to change or invent parts of the pose to improve your drawing:

  • Adjust limbs or angles to avoid tangents or awkward overlaps.
  • Explore variations that might feel more stable or dynamic.
  • Remember that gesture drawing is notational, giving you the freedom to interpret and modify as needed.

The goal is to create a pose that you enjoy developing and that communicates your intended idea effectively.

Simplifying the Drawing Process

Keep in mind that the gesture stage is about laying a foundation, not rendering a finished drawing:

  • Focus on posing and rhythm without getting bogged down in details.
  • Trust that structure and form will be developed in later stages.
  • Use the gesture to understand the position and set the stage for further work.

By simplifying your approach, you can work more efficiently and keep the drawing process enjoyable.

Encouragement and Mindset

Remember why you're learning these techniques:

  • Mastering difficult poses expands your range and allows you to create more dynamic and expressive figures.
  • The initial challenges are worth the reward of being able to depict complex actions and perspectives.
  • Keep your ultimate goals in mind, whether it's illustrating a character in an intense action scene or simply improving your skills.

Stay patient and persistent, and the effort you invest now will pay off in your future work.

* * *

Tackling complex poses involves adapting existing techniques, maintaining rhythm, and embracing asymmetry and design. By adjusting your approach to leaning, foreshortened, or symmetrical figures you can overcome challenges without needing entirely new methods. Focus on the essentials, shortened lines, wrap lines, and rhythmic flow, and you'll confidently navigate even the most difficult poses.
