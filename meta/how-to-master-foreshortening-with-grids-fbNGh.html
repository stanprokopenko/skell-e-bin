How to Master Foreshortening with Grids

Copy Code

In this lesson, you'll learn two opposite ways of drawing: the blob approach and the grid approach. Both methods can lead to mastery, as seen in artists like Kim Jung Gi. Let's explore how they differ and how you can apply them to improve your drawing skills.

The Blob Approach

Review of the Blob Method

The blob approach is a way to get form fast by:

  • Starting with something and deciding its position.
  • Wrapping a rubber band to point it away or toward.
  • Deciding on a corner that counter-angles the rubber band.
  • Snapping the curved rubber band into two straight lines.
  • Drawing the echoing lines.

This method is a shortcut procedure to drawing form. It's about guessing line angles to build a structure that's solid without much concern for proportion. The lines may not be accurate yet, but it's a process of making decisions and refining them.

If you haven't worked on this, I recommend you rehearse this approach until it makes sense to you or even until it gets easy.

The Grid Approach

Now, let's explore a whole different exercise. Starting with accurate lines from a grid, we compare orthos (orthographic views) to foreshortened views and guess the proportions.

Understanding Grids

Grids can be bad and grids can be good.

Here's where they're good:

  • Grids make important invisible lines visible, establishing space in the picture.
  • They can be guidelines, like scaffoldings, helping you stay on track.
  • Grids help us draw by keeping track of line angles while we work on other things.
  • They structure our imagination, allowing us to create the views we desire.

Here's how they're bad:

  • They can feel restrictive, telling artists where to go.
  • Some artists see grids as shackles, limiting creativity.
  • Artists who don't want to conform to perspective or the observable world may dislike them.

But remember, grids are tools we elect to use when we want to go in a certain direction.

Grids in Practice

Grids don't create artists, artists create grids. We use them for

  • Down views or up views.
  • Simple or complex perspectives.
  • Loose or refined drawings.
  • Compressed space or wide angles.
  • Straight or warped perspectives.
  • Safe or daring compositions.

Grids help by reminding us of turns and directions to hasten our journey.

Foreshortening and Proportions

Learning from Kim Jung Gi

Kim Jung Gi could fill walls with drawings at high speed with nothing to look at. You may wonder, how did he do it?

He tells us his two secrets:

  1. Know your subject's proportions. You've worked on that with orthos.
  2. Fit them into simple structures. These structures give you clues to how much you can dare to foreshorten.

Understanding Foreshortening

Foreshortening makes a thing shorter. In an ortho, a line at its longest is just that, it can never be longer. As soon as a long line goes away or comes forward, it shortens compared to the ortho. It can shorten dramatically, it can turn into a dot.

In oblique views, a line, plane, or even a form will foreshorten a lot or a little. As you learn to draw foreshortened views, you can:

  • Play it safe to begin with minimal foreshortening.
  • Dare to foreshorten and make bold adjustments.
  • Get daring gradually, increasing the foreshortening over time.

However, if you play it safe, a point of view that looks easy on one axis will imply that some other part will angle away severely. That's where we learn about how planes foreshorten, a little or a lot.

It can be scary drawing a line that you know is this long as this short. But when you do this several times, you gain courage to foreshorten until you overdo it and say, "This is a sword."

Project Instructions

Preparing for the Project

Get out your orthos. You're going to use them as reference.

The Project

Redraw your form studies in new positions by looking at their orthos.

  • If you feel confident, redraw them freehand.
  • If you don't feel confident or need guidance, use a grid. They give you a framework.

Now that you know your subject's proportions, see if you can foreshorten them.

  • How tall?
  • How deep?
  • How wide?

Try to envision them onto and into a pre-existing spatial structure for support.

Tips for the Project

  • Grids mind the angles, you judge the proportion.
  • Don't try to measure, guess.
  • Some grids include measured depth that let us draw without guessing, but guessing is why we are doing this.
  • Work with just enough uncertainty to sharpen perception and grow into good guessing.
  • Keep attention on foreshortened planes and forms, ignoring detail.
    • All that detail conforms to the simple form.
  • Look along each of the axes and ask, how much does this portion foreshorten?
  • Choose something simple enough to simplify and do this much more than once.

One perfect drawing where you measure everything doesn't rehearse this imprinting experience. If you're aching to make precise drawings, you'll have your day, I assure you. Keep it loose here.

As your guidelining video teacher, I recommend that you start with more drawings, using the grid as a safety net for line directions.

Experimenting with Distortions

If you enjoy this, have some fun with some deliberate distortions. You can do what you want without even veering off grid. It's a choice, what you conform to.

* * *

This exercise teaches you to see, to notice foreshortening. Look back and forth between the ortho and oblique views.

  • Ask yourself, does this look credible?
  • Do the proportions look off?

Make intuitive guesses about axes and proportions. Use grids to imprint in your brain and to envision and conjure proportions into them.

And if you see grids as shackles, take on the shackles enough to emerge understanding unshackled.
