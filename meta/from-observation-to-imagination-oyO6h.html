Studying My Son's Features

When thinking about my next illustration, I knew I wanted to incorporate AC units to decorate the background. But that wasn't too challenging to research. The real question was, what would be the focal point? What elements could I explore that would be both interesting and meaningful?

I started looking at photos of my son, who was around nine months old at the time. He's now 13 months, but those earlier pictures captured some truly adorable shapes. His head turns and cheek shapes are some of the most delightful forms I've ever seen. I realized I hadn't spent much time drawing him because of other commitments. So, I decided to take this opportunity to draw some fan art of my son.

Observing Ratios in Baby Faces

The unique aspect of his face is, as I mentioned, the shape of his cheeks. I've drawn enough chibi characters to generally know what a small, young person looks like, but in reality, it's a bit different and even cuter.

In this photo, Conan was just getting over a cold, so there are little splashes under his nose. Focusing on the ratios of his face, I noticed that the eye line is almost the midpoint of his face. This is because the face is smaller on a younger person. Observing further, there's an indent at the forehead, and the distance from there to the chin is slightly longer.

I measured with my hands, a technique I learned from observational drawing where you measure exactly to understand proportions. I found that there's a one-to-one ratio between the forehead and the cheeks before it hits the chin shape. The cheeks protrude, creating mirrored bulges on either side.

Breaking Down the Features

  • Head Shape: Almost a square when considering the width and length, but with rounded edges.
  • Eye Line: Right through the middle of the face.
  • Eyebrows: I like to draw them to define the front plane of the face, much like drawing a box.
  • Ears: Define the side plane of the face and are aligned with the side.
  • Eyes and Nose: The width of the eye is similar to the width of the nose.
  • Mouth: Really small and cute, with a little dip and a slight protrusion of the lip.
  • Hairline: Follows different planes, aligning with the top and side planes of the head.
  • Jawline: Starts right by the ear.

By observing and being aware of these details, I organically learn and internalize anatomy.

Studying the Unique Hat

One idea I had was to use a hat he was wearing once. He hated it, but it was so funny! I wanted to study it a bit.

Analyzing the Hat's Shapes

  • Head Shape: The hat follows the same shape as his head but is slightly thicker.
  • Thickness: Exaggerated thickness going all the way around.
  • Top Cone: A small bump at the top, rounded off based on the lighting.
  • Ears: Bisected by the detail inside, resembling a super rounded-off cylinder, connecting towards the back of the head.
  • Connection: The hat connects in the front with a button, about the width of the full strap.

By spending time breaking down the shapes, I gained a basic understanding of the hat's design.

Applying the Design to New Contexts

Now that I've studied the hat, I can think about applying it in different ways. For instance, I could:

  • Draw it from different angles, like from above with the cute little hill at the back.
  • Use it on a different character, not necessarily my son.
  • Place it on an animal or create a new character.
  • Transform it into something else, like the silliest airplane you've ever seen!

Experimenting with the Hat Design

For fun, I decided to turn the hat into a ship:

  • Flapping Ears: Imagine the ears flapping up and down.
  • Front Window: The front of the vehicle could be a window.
  • Incorporate Previous Studies: Borrow elements from other studies, like a vehicle with a wheel.
  • Strapping It Together: Use the button and straps as functional design elements.

This playful approach allows me to experiment and combine shapes I've studied, making it easier and more enjoyable.

Creating Characters Inspired by Studies

I often like to put unique designs onto characters, sometimes even on very different ones, like a sexy girl wearing a cute baby hat. Since I understand the shape, I can do different poses and have fun with it.

Designing the Character

  • Head Shape: Use the rounded box shape of the hat.
  • Hill Shape: Incorporate the little hill at the top.
  • Adult Character: Place an adult character inside, not just a cute baby.
  • Juxtaposition: Create a gritty story where the only thing that doesn't make sense is the cute hat.

By playing with the design in unexpected ways, I aim to evoke reactions from others, whether it's humor, surprise, or intrigue.

Connecting Design Elements

  • Repeated Motifs: Use the cute strap from the hat as a motif throughout the design.
  • Weaponizing Features: Turn the ears into weapons sticking out.
  • Incorporate Details: Include ridges and other small features for added interest.

This process is similar to my work on "Sea of Stars". The game director loved everything I did, which was a dream scenario. The more I had fun and did silly or unexpected things, the more he liked it.

Incorporating Story into Design

When I'm having fun and playing with designs, the story elements come more naturally. As I draw and explore, I begin to imagine:

  • Why the character looks like that.
  • What they do that requires them to wear these things.
  • How they exist in their world.

Connecting the dots between the design and their purpose or narrative helps me make motivated decisions. It ties together the character's appearance with their role and setting.

Embracing Playfulness in the Creative Process

This approach emphasizes:

  • Learning through observation: Studying real-life shapes and proportions.
  • Playing with shapes: Using understood shapes to create new designs with ease.
  • Evoking emotions: Aiming to get reactions from viewers by making them feel something.
  • Storytelling through design: Letting the design process inspire narrative elements.

I find that once I've studied something, it's much easier to play with those shapes and let creativity flow without overthinking. By exploring and combining different elements, I can design characters and stories that are both fun and meaningful.
