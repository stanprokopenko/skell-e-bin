Using Academic Study to Inform Intuitive Drawing

Over years of academic study, I've filled my head with information, and it influences everything I put on paper. Concepts like the C, S, and I line principle - the idea that you can draw anything using these three simple lines - have been foundational for me. Translating these principles into intuitive drawings requires lots of practice, but it is rewarding when those strokes become second nature.

The Value of Embracing Failure

In drawing, there's an impulse to want to create perfect work right away. We have visions in our heads, and it's disheartening when we can't bring them to life. However, the reality is that 99% of drawing is failure. I have hundreds of sketches that didn't turn out as I hoped. But each failure is an opportunity to learn and improve. By analyzing what didn't work, I can incorporate those lessons into my next drawing.

Experimenting and Playing with Art

Trying new materials, like the rustic leather-feel paper I recently found, keeps the process fresh. Experimenting with a chisel tip calligraphy pen adds new dynamics to my strokes. Like Paul McCartney creating music from scratch, I let myself play and see what happens. Most of it might be bad, but some of it turns out interesting. Allowing myself to make mistakes is crucial to my growth as an artist.

The Role of Intuition and Confidence

When drawing from imagination, I find that intuition plays a significant role. It's about reacting to shapes and strokes on the page rather than rigidly planning every detail. Letting my body and brain guide me, I combine the rhythms and anatomy knowledge from life drawing with an intuitive process. Over time, this builds confidence, which is essential for creative drawing. This confidence can't develop without understanding what it means to draw something well and what resonates with me personally.

Balancing Study and Personal Creativity

I believe in a balance between academic study and personal creativity. Spending 30-50% of my time studying, attending figure drawing sessions, studying anatomy, and 50% creating art I love keeps me engaged. Over time, the knowledge from my studies subconsciously influences my intuitive drawings.

Advice for Aspiring Artists

For anyone wanting to dive into sketching or imaginative drawing, my advice is simple, just start. You might feel you're not good enough or don't know enough, but experimentation is key at any level. Draw something you love repeatedly, for me, it's torsos in different poses. This familiar subject provides a safe space to experiment with lines, strokes, and techniques. Remember, the act of drawing doesn't have to be all suffering. Embrace making bad art, it makes creating art easier and more enjoyable.
