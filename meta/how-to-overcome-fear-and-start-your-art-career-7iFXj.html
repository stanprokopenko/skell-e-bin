Moving Beyond the Student Mentality

I've seen many artists who overdo the student mentality. They perpetually take classes and never stop putting themselves under some other artist. While learning is crucial, there comes a time when you need to move on and express what you've learned in your own way.

I believe that sometimes school can be too safe. It's comfortable to stay in a place where you're always learning, but you need to get out there. Facing challenges and even falling on your face is part of the journey to becoming a professional.

My Transition from Student to Professional

When I was 19, I asked my teacher, "When will I know when it's time for me to go out and actually make a living and become an illustrator?" He told me, "When you're consistent."

After about three years in art school, my teacher said I was ready. I got a job working on movie posters. Suddenly, I had to make all this knowledge work right now. It was trial by fire.

I fell on my face a few times in the beginning, but I learned quickly. Getting out of the mindset of being a student into being a professional requires guts. It's about making something out of this knowledge and not just being in school forever.

The Role of Passion in Art

Why did I practice drawing as a young kid, for hours on end? Because I was drawing things I was interested in. I drew Batman, the Batmobile, superheroes, whatever captured my imagination.

Passion drives practice. When you're interested in something, you'll work harder at it and do a better job. I encouraged my daughters to draw things they loved. It wasn't about learning to draw the head, it was about drawing Harry Potter or characters from their favorite shows.

When you draw your passions, you get into it, and that's how you express yourself. Fan art comes from that place of passion, and it leads to improvement because you're invested in what you're creating.

Balancing Passion and Making a Living

I've been approached by art sellers who want me to paint subjects I'm not interested in. They say, "I know I could sell it." But I have no desire to paint contemporary scenes that don't inspire me.

I choose to paint what I love, the Western themes, the costumes, the time period. It's not about getting followers or collectors, it's about doing what I want to do.

Yes, you need to navigate the professional world, but staying true to your interests is essential. If you're passionate about what you're creating, you'll work harder at it, and it will show in your work.

Dealing with Failure and Persistence

Failure is inevitable. Whether you're just starting or you're a seasoned artist, there will be times when things don't go as planned. I've had paintings that didn't sell, even after six months or more of work. It can mess with your head, but you have to keep going.

When you're learning, you'll make mistakes. You're going to fail, and that's okay. It's about having a strong sense of self and not getting discouraged. You have to believe that you'll get there and keep working at it.

Learning and Teaching Art

One of the worst things that can happen is you take an art class and walk away more discouraged than when you started. I believe in breaking down information methodically. Don't throw six balls at a beginner and say, "Here, juggle them."

In my teaching, I start with simple steps:

  • Mapping out the drawing.
  • Separating lights from shadows.
  • Learning to mix an average light and shadow.

We focus on one thing at a time. Once you're handling that well, we introduce the next element. It's about building confidence and skill gradually, so you don't feel overwhelmed.

Applying Fundamentals Across Mediums

The fundamental principles of art stay the same across different mediums. When I started digital painting, I applied the same principles I use in oil painting. I don't rely on digital tricks, I approach it as if I'm working traditionally.

Whether you're painting, drawing, or working digitally, skills like observing tone, edges, shapes, and measuring always apply. Practicing in different mediums validates that these fundamentals work.

Understanding Time Frames in Art

One misunderstanding I've noticed is how different time frames change your approach in art. Starting a 9-hour drawing the same way you start a 20-minute sketch is a recipe for disaster.

In short sketches, you might rely more on memory and knowledge to get it done quickly. But for long drawings, you need to spend more time on mapping, measuring, and observing. Understanding the timing helps you know when to use certain methods, like the Loomis or Riley techniques.

Advice for Art Students

Here are some things I've learned:

  • Practice consistently. Put in the hours, and apply the principles you're learning.
  • Draw what you love. Your passion will drive you to improve.
  • Don't be discouraged by failure. It's a natural part of the learning process.
  • Transition from loving the subject to loving the process. As you grow, you might find joy in the act of creating itself.

If you're thinking about making art your career, remember that it's not always easy. Consider having a backup job to take the pressure off. It's a tough industry, but if you're passionate and determined, you'll find your way.

My Painting Course for Beginners

I've put together a painting course designed especially for beginners. I wanted to give back to those just starting out. Instead of overwhelming you with advanced techniques, I break down the process into manageable steps.

We start with:

  • Drawing and mapping out the subject.
  • Separating lights from shadows.
  • Mixing simple colors.

I use only four paints: white, black, alizarin crimson, and yellow ochre. You don't need expensive materials or extensive knowledge. This course is about holding your hand through each stage, just like my teacher did for me.

I believe that anyone can learn to paint if they have the right guidance. By simplifying the process, you can achieve results you won't believe. It's about building confidence and skill in a way that's accessible and affordable.
