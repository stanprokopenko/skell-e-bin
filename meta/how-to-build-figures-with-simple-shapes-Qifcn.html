How to Build Figures with Simple Shapes

Setting My Goals

I like to stack multiple tasks whenever I draw or paint. I have goals around craft, concept, and aesthetics. In this example, I’m focusing on:

  • Connective strategies (like curves and corners, beanbags and drumsticks)
  • Simple mashup of forms (balls, boxes, tubes)
  • Mark making (clean, beautiful lines)
  • Shape design (strong, clear silhouettes)

These goals give me something to measure my success by. Sometimes I focus on just one or two, other times I stack them all.

Laying Out the Torso

I often start with a T for the shoulders. I’ll compare that T to a vertical, compare the top of the torso to a horizontal, and so on. Each new line I add, I compare to what came before. That helps everything line up accurately.

I usually block in an egg shape for the rib cage. I’m not worried if it’s exactly anatomical. Sometimes I trim the top of this egg to suggest the shoulder girdle. If I need to, I’ll add another egg for the back side of the rib cage or go for a simple two-dimensional shape. As I work, I keep adjusting proportions. It’s a best guess approach, refined as I go.

Adjusting Overlaps

When there’s an overlap, such as an arm covering part of the hip, I want to see the full structure underneath. In a real class, I might shift my head to peek around the model to understand the hidden shape. Then when I sit back down, I can draw both sides of that form and let the arm overlap it as needed.

Refining My Beanbag

My initial torso block-in often looks like a beanbag with two egg shapes. As I refine, I make it less generic, adding slight stomach forms or landmarks like the belly button. Each new element I add, I compare to what’s already there. If I need more clarity, I’ll dust back my lines to avoid confusion and then firm up the improved shapes.

Secondary Forms

Once the big shapes are placed, I can add smaller shapes if needed. For example, I might see a latissimus shape here or a hint of another muscle there. I often treat them as miniature eggs or other simple shapes. This approach stays shape-based instead of going straight into detailed anatomy.

Linking to Rendering

Constructing the shapes well now makes it easier to render them later. If I think of a shoulder as an egg, I can practice rendering an egg in isolation. Then I know how to add tone and light in my final drawing. This process gives me a clear mental model of the forms before I shade them.

Next Steps

That’s how I block in the torso with simple shapes. Next, I’ll work on attaching the legs to the torso so I can move toward a complete pose. After that, we’ll look at how to light and render these forms. I’m excited to take the next step in building our figure drawings. See you soon!
