Mastering Value and Color in Portraits

In this guide, I'll walk you through the process of establishing shadows and light, mixing colors, creating backgrounds, and refining your painting. These techniques will help you achieve a balanced composition and enhance your painting skills.

Establishing Shadows and Light

To start, we use pre-mixed values for basic shadows. This sets a solid foundation for the painting. Next, we add average values for light, which is particularly beneficial when working on skin and flesh tones. This helps in achieving a more realistic look. It's crucial to compare these values to make better judgments about the overall composition.

Color Mixing Techniques

When mixing colors, I begin with ivory black, and mix it with alizarin and yellow ocher. I like to think of this as a ketchup and mustard analogy, a bit more black is added to balance the mix. To create contrast with flesh tones, I adjust the colors to shift towards green by reducing the red. This technique helps in achieving a harmonious color palette.

Background Creation

For the background, I apply random brushwork to give the appearance of a finished sketch. This technique not only adds depth but also makes use of any excess paint on the brush, avoiding waste. You can have fun with this by creating an abstract background. Adding more black and medium can give the background a transparent look, which can be quite striking.

Tools and Techniques

I often use a beat-up old brush for quick and rough application. This allows for a more spontaneous and dynamic approach. It's important to remove excess paint to prevent it from mixing with the flesh colors. I liken this process to using fine charcoal in drawing, where the shadow color acts as charcoal and the average light as a kneaded eraser.

Refining the Painting

The painting process is iterative. I plan to revisit and refine the work, blending edges and making adjustments as needed. This involves a back-and-forth play between shadow and light to refine shapes and achieve the desired effect. While reference photos are used, they mainly serve as a guide for values rather than dictating the entire composition.

By following these steps, you can create a painting that is both dynamic and balanced, with well-defined shadows, light, and background. Remember, the key is to experiment and have fun with the process!
