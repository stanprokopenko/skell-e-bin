Value Thumbnails for Better Compositions

Copy Code

Setup and mindset

I used cool, warm, and red-gray markers plus white, but I stayed focused on value, not color. I kept thumbnails loose on purpose. Clean drawings make me uptight and less willing to explore. Thumbnails should feel free and fast.

Value read of the reference

There’s a bright figure in the middle and a dark background. The top light is cool and the bottom light is warm, but both are equally bright. I ignore temperature and judge only by value. I noticed: - A cast shadow low on the wall (darkest area). - A subtle vignette with darker corners. - The hat’s top light is very bright. - Hands are darker in the reference, but I can “cheat” them lighter if I need them to read.

Shapes and folds

I mapped a quick figure with a hat and outstretched hands. I simplified folds: - Diaper fold between the shoulders. - Vertical folds down the sides. I placed key darks under the jaw, on the chin, and a few fold accents. I kept folds simple and used darks to frame the head and hands.

Three-value thumbnails explored

  • 1) Study the reference as is. Dark cast shadow at the bottom, light center, and simple fold indications. I kept hands lighter than the reference so they don’t disappear.
  • 2) Full dark background. A bold two-value silhouette. Then a mid-value interior for hat, face, and drapery. I added darks around the hands and face to pop them. Very strong.
  • 3) Dark only at the top background. More contrast near the face. I tried hands as bright versus mid. Keeping hands bright worked better unless I pushed more dark around them.
  • 4) Cleaner silhouette with selective darks. I saved the darkest accents for a few places, made more of the figure pure light, and kept hands pure white. The strong silhouette improved.
  • 5) Top light as key, bottom glow dimmed. I lowered the figure to add “air” above for a top glow. I suggested a light shape in the background to justify the extra space.
  • 6) Mixed version with top dark and a mid-value ray. I didn’t like it as much. The earlier strong version won.

I chose the version with a continuous bright shape running up the figure, hands pure white, minimal mids, and selective dark accents framing the head and shoulders.

Larger study with 4 values

I used paper white, a light gray, a darker gray, and black. Marker numbers were reversed from earlier lessons, so I layered to keep the steps clear: - Blocked in the dark background. - Kept the face mostly in shadow for mystery, with small feature accents. - Preserved a large, simple light shape through the figure. - Added a fourth value inside the light to add detail at the focal point without blowing it out. - Layered the darker gray and black to separate close values and popped the corners near the hands with an extra pass of black. - I avoided a fifth value unless it clearly separated something important.

Key takeaways

  • Judge by value, not temperature.
  • A strong silhouette beats busy detail.
  • Connect the light shape for power, splitting it weakens the read.
  • Cheat values to protect important shapes like the hands.
  • Place contrast near the focal point (face and hands).
  • Start loose, explore many options, then commit to the strongest composition.
