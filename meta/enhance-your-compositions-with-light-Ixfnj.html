The Curse of Accidental Success

There's something that many artists hit, which I consider the curse of accidental success. This is when you go to do photography, paint something, or draw something, and 80 to 90% of the time, it doesn't look great. Then sometimes you just hit it, and it looks really amazing. You don't know why, it just does. You think, 'This is great.' As long as I keep brute-forcing my way through by doing hundreds of paintings, sometimes they will look really good.

Unfortunately, a lot of people think when it comes to art that it's some magic thing that happens. That you are just naturally talented. That some people create work magically because they are naturally talented and they're just very lucky.

I would like to challenge this idea. Most artists that make it look easy, it's not about magic. They took the time to learn, what makes a good image. They have taken that knowledge and turned it, instead of just natural talent, into an actual skill that makes it look effortless. They applied the knowledge and the skill by focus. So instead of luck, they took the time, persistence, and effort into growing their skills.

We can get out of the loop of accidental success and actually create more images if we understand the principles of what makes a good image, what makes good composition.

Types of Composition

There are two different types of composition:

  1. Geometric Composition - The placement of objects within your frame. If there's a mountain, if there's a tree, if there's a person, where are they on the screen?
  2. Lighting Composition: Adds on top of geometric composition. This includes contrast, what's lit, what's not lit, what colors are there, and how you use the light.

Most of this talk will focus around lighting-centric composition, but let me briefly touch on geometric composition.

Framing

Another element of geometric composition is framing. This is to keep your eye inside the image and to tell you where to look, what is the focal point of the shot, and how to use what we would consider frames within frames.

You are looking at a square image when you are watching a screen, and sometimes we have framing elements that we put into the composition to help draw our eyes. For example:

  • This door is a frame within a frame and draws us to the characters.
  • In Guardians of the Galaxy, there's clearly a frame within the frame.
  • In The Mandalorian, the cave helps frame the shot.

This type of geometric composition draws our eye to a very particular place. The door provides a frame to have contrast around her face so that I more clearly understand what I'm looking at when I see her face.

Repetition

Another tool of geometric composition is repetition. We want to take elements in a scene to communicate depth and scale. For example:

  • In The Mummy, we see people and small boats, and then those boats are repeated and repeated way off into the distance. They're teeny tiny, which tells us just how big the pyramids are.
  • In Star Wars, if we see these domes repeated in scale and characters standing on them, we can understand just how big this building really is by these repeating shapes.

It's all about visual communication.

Leading Lines

Leading lines are used where the geometry and texture of the objects in our scene draw our eye to what we're supposed to look at. It's all about the focal point of the shot.

  • In Stranger Things, the lines of the wall with the perspective draw us to Eleven, the main character.
  • All these keys, everything just goes to where we should be looking. They're using leading lines to draw us.
  • Looking down the stairs, you can see all of these lines of the perspective.
  • Even in a shot where the characters are not in the center of the frame, the perspective provides leading lines that draw our eye to what is most important. All the chairs and the floor help guide us.

Where do you think these characters are going? That path is a leading line that draws us around to the house. This house is important in the story.

Blocks

  • A big block of grass, a big block of house, and a big block of negative space of the sky.
  • Negative space is a shape that does not have anything in it but helps us understand the shape of something.

In The Mandalorian, the ground is really simplified, and the sky is really simplified. So we basically have one place to look, and those big blocks of value and color help us understand the scene interestingly.

The Rule of Thirds

There is the rule of thirds, something that was invented by the ancient Greeks. They noticed in nature that things split into three was the way nature works. There's something called the golden ratio, a spiral that leads to the one-third. The rule of thirds and the golden ratio are very related, but it's more appealing to our eyes to split the image into threes rather than putting the character in the very center of the frame.
