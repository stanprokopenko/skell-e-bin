The Horizon Line and Eye Level

Choosing the Horizon Line

The horizon line represents the viewer's eye level and is crucial in determining the perspective of the drawing. By choosing the height of the horizon line, you set the stage for how objects will appear in relation to the viewer.

How Eye Level Affects Perspective

Once you've established the eye level, it influences the angles and directions of the other lines in your drawing. Objects above the eye level will show their undersides, while objects below will show their tops. Adjusting the eye level changes the viewer's perception of the scene.

Aiming Receding Lines

Lines Aiming Up and Down

In perspective drawing, there are typically three sets of lines:

  • X-axis lines (horizontal)
  • Y-axis lines (vertical)
  • Z-axis lines (depth)

When receding lines (lines representing depth) aim up as they go away from the viewer, it indicates that the object is below us. Conversely, when these lines aim down, the object is above us.

Remember:

  • Looking down, lines go up (Z and X axes).
  • Looking up, lines go down (X and Z axes).

Understanding Above and Below Views

By thinking in terms of how lines aim in relation to the eye level, you can intuitively place objects above or below the viewer. This approach helps when starting a sketch, even without precisely knowing the vanishing points.

Vanishing Points in Perspective

One-Point and Two-Point Perspective

  • In one-point perspective, one set of lines (typically the depth lines) converges to a single vanishing point.
  • In two-point perspective, two sets of lines (often width and depth) recede toward two separate vanishing points.

The vertical lines in both cases remain parallel and do not converge, as they are perpendicular to the picture plane.

Vertical Lines and Three-Point Perspective

When the picture plane is tilted up or down, vertical lines tilt away from it, resulting in three-point perspective. This introduces a third vanishing point, either above or below the horizon line, where vertical lines converge.

The Flexibility of Vanishing Points

The third vanishing point in three-point perspective is flexible. Artists often take liberties with its placement:

  • You can omit the third point for stylistic reasons.
  • You can bring it closer for dramatic effect.

Understanding this flexibility allows for creative expression without being restricted by strict rules.

Understanding Spatial Axes Over Vanishing Points

Rather than focusing solely on counting vanishing points, it's essential to understand the three spatial axes (X, Y, Z). This approach emphasizes the object's orientation in space and helps in accurately depicting perspective.

The Horizon Line as Eye Level

The Horizon in Landscapes

In landscapes, the horizon is where the earth meets the sky. It's the line where receding parallel lines, that are level to the ground, appear to converge.

The Moon Analogy

An analogy to understand the horizon's behavior is the moon following you as you travel. The moon seems to stay with you because it's so far away that your movement doesn't significantly change its position relative to you. Similarly, the horizon remains at your eye level regardless of your position because it's effectively an infinite distance away.

The Relationship Between Eye Level and Horizon

The horizon line moves up or down in your field of view based on your eye level. This means:

  • If you raise your eye level (e.g., stand on a hill), the horizon appears lower on nearby objects.
  • If you lower your eye level (e.g., crouch down), the horizon appears higher on nearby objects.

Understanding this relationship is crucial for accurate perspective drawing.

Foreshortening Near Eye Level

Angles on Paper

As objects approach the eye level on paper, their planes become foreshortened:

  • In one-point perspective, front corners become acute (less than 90 degrees), while back corners become obtuse (more than 90 degrees).
  • In two-point perspective, front and back corners become obtuse, while left and right corners become acute.

As these planes move toward the eye level, they:

  • Spread out or open up when approaching the horizon.
  • Compress into straight lines when they align with the eye level.

Foreshortening Explained Through the Picture Plane

The picture plane is like a window through which the viewer sees the scene:

  • When objects are above or below the eye level, you can see their top or bottom planes.
  • As objects approach the eye level, these planes foreshorten and eventually compress into lines at eye level.

Understanding how the picture plane works helps explain why objects change appearance based on their position relative to the eye level.

The Picture Plane and Perspective

Side View Explanation

Viewing the scene from the side demonstrates how the viewer's eye level intersects with objects:

  • Objects above the eye level show their undersides.
  • Objects at the eye level compress into lines.
  • Objects below the eye level show their tops.

Top View and Vanishing Points

A top view (plan view) helps in determining the exact placement of vanishing points:

  • The distance between the viewer and the object affects the proximity of vanishing points.
  • Closer objects have vanishing points that are further apart on the horizon line.
  • Understanding this helps in accurately plotting perspective lines.

Skill-Building Project

Drawing Rooms with Eye Level at Different Heights

Practice drawing rooms with the eye level placed at various heights:

  1. Start with the eye level near the center of the picture.
  2. Draw the room in one-point or two-point perspective.
  3. Observe how changing the eye level affects the angles and proportions.

Tips:

  • Begin with five minutes per sketch.
  • Gradually reduce to three minutes, then one minute as you become more comfortable.
  • Speed helps reinforce the concepts into your instincts.

Redrawing Scenes from New Heights

Take existing scenes or photographs and redraw them from different eye levels:

  1. Raise or lower the horizon line in your new drawing.
  2. Adjust the angles of the receding lines accordingly.
  3. Keep the proportions of objects consistent, focusing on how the perspective changes.

Remember:

  • Looking down, lines go up.
  • Looking up, lines go down.

This exercise enhances your ability to visualize and render scenes from various perspectives.

* * *

Understanding how the eye level and horizon line influence perspective is essential for creating accurate and dynamic drawings. By practicing the concepts of receding lines, vanishing points, and the effects of the picture plane, you'll develop a stronger grasp of spatial relationships in your artwork. Keep experimenting with different viewpoints, and soon these principles will become second nature.
