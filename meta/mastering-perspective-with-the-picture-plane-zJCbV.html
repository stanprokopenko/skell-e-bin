Mastering Perspective with the Picture Plane

Copy Code

Drawing in thin air isn't easy. That's why you use a flat surface, a kind of window, to display that delightful illusion of three-dimensionality. This flat surface is known as the picture plane. But the picture plane is more than just a surface to draw on, it is a window that explains how perspective works.

Understanding the Picture Plane

The Picture Plane as a Window

Imagine the picture plane as a window through which you view the world. It helps solve perspective problems by showing how objects appear smaller as they move farther away and larger when they are closer. This is the essence of perspective: closer makes bigger.

From the top view, the picture plane shows why a close object looks wider than the same object farther away. From the side view, it explains how a close object appears taller than a similar or even taller object that is farther away.

This concept demonstrates a fundamental truth about how you see the world: far things diminish, close things enlarge.

Perspective Basics

The picture plane is a powerful tool for understanding perspective. It helps you solve almost any perspective problem, including placing vanishing points precisely.

How the Picture Plane Explains Perspective

By analyzing the picture plane from different angles, you can understand why objects appear the way they do in perspective drawings. It shows:

  • From the top: Why close objects look wider.
  • From the side: Why close objects look taller.

These views help you grasp why perspective lines converge and where vanishing points should be placed.

One-Point Perspective

The One-Point Setup

In a one-point perspective, you look directly at the object face on. Only one set of lines, the Z-lines (lines receding into depth), converge towards a single vanishing point.

From above, you can see that only the Z-lines go away from the viewer or the window (picture plane), leading to a vanishing point in that direction.

The X-lines (horizontal lines) go left and right, parallel to the picture plane, so they remain horizontal in your drawing. Similarly, the Y-lines (vertical lines) go up and down, also parallel to the picture plane, and remain vertical.

Side and Top Views in One-Point Perspective

  • Side View: Shows that the Y-lines do not recede into depth, they are parallel to the picture plane and go straight up and down.
  • Top View: Illustrates that the X-lines are parallel to the picture plane and go left and right.

In a one-point perspective, only one set of lines (Z-lines) recede to the vanishing point, while the other lines remain parallel to the picture plane.

Two-Point Perspective

When Objects Rotate

When the object rotates a few degrees, the X-lines are no longer parallel to the picture plane. They start to veer away from the face of the plane, and the setup changes from one-point to two-point perspective.

In two-point perspective, both the X-lines and Z-lines recede towards their respective vanishing points.

Finding Vanishing Points Exactly

By using the picture plane, you can precisely determine where to place vanishing points for any perspective.

Placing Vanishing Points

The Revelation: Using the Picture Plane to Find Vanishing Points

Here's the key revelation in understanding perspective:

The direction in which lines point will show you exactly where to place vanishing points.

In a top-down view of the scene:

  • Run lines from the viewer's eye parallel to the lines in the scene.
  • Where these lines intersect the picture plane (window) is where the vanishing points are located.

This method allows you to place vanishing points exactly, ensuring accurate perspective in your drawings.

Examples of Placing Vanishing Points

  1. Spinning the Object: You can rotate the object to any position. By projecting lines from the viewer's eye that are parallel to the object's edges, you find where the vanishing points land on the picture plane.

  2. Symmetrical Setup: Placing the object at a 45-degree angle positions the front corner in the center. The vanishing points will then be equidistant from the center, creating perfect symmetrical two-point perspective.

  3. Wide Angle Effect: Moving the viewer closer to the object brings the vanishing points closer together, creating a wide-angle effect. This can result in dynamic and expressive drawings with exaggerated perspectives.

The Effects of Viewer Position

Wide Angle Effects and Field of Vision

Adjusting the viewer's position affects the placement of vanishing points and the overall distortion in the drawing:

  • Closer Viewer: Vanishing points come closer together, increasing distortion and creating a wide-angle effect.
  • Field of Vision: Keeping the object within a 60-degree field of vision prevents distortion. A wider field of vision can introduce distortion, which you can use creatively.

The Value of the Picture Plane

The picture plane is invaluable for understanding the science behind perspective. It shows you:

  • Why you have more than one set of vanishing points when objects rotate.
  • Where the vanishing points go, left and right, up and down.
  • How to draw plan projections, allowing you to take any top, side, or front view of an object and draw it precisely in a three-quarter view.

Drawing Accurate Three-Quarter Views from Orthographic Views

You can draw an accurate three-quarter view from orthographic projections (orthos) in two stages: Plan and Projection.

The Plan Stage

  1. Place the Top View: Position the top view of your object behind the picture plane in any desired orientation.

  2. Set the Viewer Position: Place the viewer far enough back so that a 60-degree field of vision includes the whole object. This avoids distortion.

  3. Project Corner Points: Draw lines (like laser beams) from each corner of the object to the viewer's eye.

  4. Intersect the Picture Plane: Where these lines pass through the picture plane, mark the points. These marks represent where each corner lands left and right on the picture plane.

This stage gives you the lateral (left and right) positions of the points but does not provide the vanishing points yet.

The Projection Stage

  1. Change the View: After completing the plan stage (top-down view), switch to the front view of the picture plane.

  2. Draw the Projection: Transfer the marks from the plan stage onto the front view, bringing down the lateral positions precisely.

  3. Find Vanishing Points: Run lines from the viewer's eye parallel to the object's edges. Where these lines intersect the picture plane in the plan view provide the lateral positions of the vanishing points in the projection stage.

  4. Establish Eye Level and Horizon: Choose your eye level by placing the horizon line. This sets the vertical position of the vanishing points.

  5. Connect the Dots: Draw lines from the object through the vanishing points, constructing the three-dimensional form accurately.

Transferring Heights Using Ortho Views

Since the plan view does not provide height information:

  1. Use Side or Front Orthographic Views: Align an orthographic view (side view or front view) next to your projection.

  2. Transfer Heights: Project the vertical measurements (Y-lines) from the ortho view onto the projection.

  3. Complete the Drawing: Use these heights to accurately depict the object's dimensions in the three-quarter view.

This method allows you to project orthographic views into accurate perspective drawings with no guesswork.
