Why Visual Memory Is Important

Developing your visual memory helps you draw from imagination, draw faster, be more creative, and even improves your ability to draw from observation. Artists like Karl Kopinski, Kim Jung Gi, Peter Han, and Eliza Ivanova excel due to their extraordinary visual memory.

Visual Memory Games

Draw from Memory: Study a reference for a minute or two, then hide it and draw from memory. Gradually decrease your viewing time as you improve. After drawing, check your accuracy and try again to see improvement.

Draw from a Different Angle: Without hiding the reference, draw the subject from a different angle. This enhances your ability to visualize.

Combine Them: Study the subject, then draw it from memory and from a different angle. Starting with a study drawing can help you learn the forms before drawing from memory.

Level Two – Change Proportions: Using figure references, draw from a different angle, from memory, and alter the proportions. Use the reference as inspiration to redesign and create your own version.

Bonus Game – Draw Moving Subjects: Observe a moving subject, like animals at the zoo. Memorize a pose and draw it while the subject continues to move. This challenges you to work with what you've got.

Assignments

Level One: Using provided 3D models, choose from the first three games. Focus on practicing perspective and construct forms from simple shapes. Use the interactive models to check your work by adjusting the angles.

Level Two: Work from figure photos and choose any of the four games, including changing proportions. Simplify poses into mannequins using basic forms like boxes, cylinders, and spheres.

Remember, you can continue these memory games anytime to enhance your visual memory. You can focus on gesture, shapes, colors, shadows—whatever you like.
