How to Draw Blobs with Depth

Copy Code

Why Blobs

You’re going to do a stress-free exercise to practice shading form and focus on edges. There’s no right or wrong. You try things out and see the effects. You’ll draw blobs and shade them to see how different edges change how the forms feel.

Set Up Your Blobs

Start by drawing a collage of ellipses, ovals, and blobby eggs in various sizes and proportions. Keep it loose. It’s very two-dimensional at first, but you can quickly make it feel 3D with a few tools you already know.

Make It Feel 3D

Use these to push it into three dimensions:

  • Overlaps. Decide what is in front and what is behind. Put the front forms overlapping the back ones. You can even add a little wrinkle or compression where they meet. If you reverse the overlaps, it just feels like the viewer is on the other side. Still correct.
  • Cross-contour lines. Wrap lines around the forms to reveal direction and curvature. They don’t have to be perfect cylinders. You can indicate subtle planes if a blob has a little corner.
  • Diminution. Things get smaller as they go back. A sequence of forms decreasing in size suggests depth. Overlaps will confirm it.

Organic forms are forgiving. You don’t need perfect perspective or vanishing points.

Add Shadows and Focus on Edges

This is where it gets super 3D. You’ll focus purely on the edge of the shadow. Not ball shading with all the reflected lights and highlights. Just the edge quality.

Decide the Light

Pick a general light direction. For example, from the top and a little in front. Then:

1) Mass in the shadow shapes. Invent the general shadow areas. Keep it loose at first. 2) Clean up silhouettes. Use sharp edges around the outer shapes. Don’t worry about atmosphere or lost edges for this exercise. 3) Clarify inside edges. Where one form ends and another starts, make that edge sharp if it’s an abrupt connection.

You can put a darker, thicker line on the shadow side. It’s less distracting there. On the light side, keep outlines lighter. This comes from the line weight lessons.

Tools, Texture, and Cleanup

Use what you’re comfortable with. A charcoal pencil can make edge control easier. A kneaded eraser and a pencil eraser help carve sharp edges. If you’re using graphite and the shadow mass looks textured:

  • Define the shape with the tip, then fill the shadow more evenly to remove white specks. Clean shapes make edge adjustments easier.
  • You can lightly even out texture with your finger or a smudge stick.

Don’t let heavy construction lines compete with your forms. Lighten or remove them so the illusion reads clearly.

Shape the Form With Edge Quality

The edge of a form shadow is created by the roundness or hardness of the form. You decide:

  • Softer edge feels rounder, like a big ball.
  • Firmer or sharp edge feels boxier or has a sudden plane change.

You can vary edge quality along a single shadow:

  • Near the top, keep it a bit firmer.
  • As the form turns away from the light toward the bottom, make the edge extremely soft and the value slightly darker.

You can go back and re-sharpen parts of the edge where you want it to feel more precise.

Halftones Are Also About Edges

Add halftone shapes on the light side to reveal more form. Do not mindlessly scribble. Think in shapes and gradations:

  • Place a halftone as a shape, then gradate it to describe the edge and plane change.
  • You can suggest a subtle plane with a firmer or softer halftone edge depending on how you want it to feel.

Cast Shadows Make It Pop

Cast shadows are crisp and sharp. Add them to make the forms jump:

  • From a top light, drop sharp cast shadows down and to the side.
  • Those sharp interior edges will stand out against your softer form edges and make the forms pop.

Don’t obsess over perfect light direction accuracy. Get it close. Focus more on how your edges and planes affect the form. You can cheat light and perspective. If it looks like a plane change, the viewer will believe you.

A Quick Way to Explore

You can stumble into convincing form fast:

  • Pick a blob. Choose one edge to be firm to suggest a sudden plane change.
  • Make another edge soft to slow into the plane change.
  • Add a small halftone gradation to suggest a plane, even if the light direction is not perfect.
  • Clean up texture if it fights the illusion.

Apply This to Previous Projects

Go back to your ginger root and cross-contour on organic forms projects. You already set up the 3D forms with line. Now:

  • Pick a light source.
  • Place the shadow shapes.
  • Decide edge quality for each form shadow based on your cross contours.
  • Add halftones with intentional edges where helpful.

If you don’t want to draw over the originals, redraw them and shade on top.

Assignment

  • Level 1: Invent blobs and shade them. There’s no right or wrong. Include variety. Some forms should have sharper corners, others should be very round and soft. Focus on clean silhouettes, clear shadow shapes, and intentional edge control.
  • Level 2: A more challenging version is in the next video. Do the Level 1 blobs first to get comfortable.

Get blobbing. Experiment. See how edge decisions change how your forms feel.
