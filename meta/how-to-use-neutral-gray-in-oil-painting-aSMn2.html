Environmental Conditions and Painting

It's really hot where I am, even at 9:00 at night, it's still around 90 degrees. The heat seems to have burned off the moisture, even though it's dark outside. I always test my work to ensure it's not going to move, using a fixative. This is crucial for stability in my paintings.

Tools and Materials

I use a gray-toned board to help in assessing mixtures accurately. A white or warm-toned board would make it difficult to judge mixtures. For beginners, I recommend using neutral gray toning to see colors against a neutral background. Testing mixtures on a gray palette ensures no surprises in color appearance.

Painting Techniques

I test by mixing lighter lights to establish contrasts quickly. Highlights on the head and nose are important for contrast. I like the average color and find it easy to lighten. I protect my mixtures to avoid accidentally mixing them into something else. I use a mixture of camp saw and orchid walnut oil, which lasts for a day or two. A rubber lid helps prevent the mixture from drying out.

Paint Thickness and Texture

It's important to keep dark areas thinner in oil painting. Thicker lights in lighter areas create interesting texture contrasts. Thick and thin paint is a tool in the painting toolbox. I avoid making dark areas thick to prevent unwanted highlights. Thick paint in dark areas can create ridges that catch light, altering perceived value. Shadows should feel airy and transparent, not opaque or plastic-like. Thick shadows can make a painting feel less realistic.

Focus and Detail Work

I focus on the eye of the painting, ensuring color and shape accuracy. Using the right brush size is crucial for detail work. I switch to a smaller brush for smaller areas to maintain control. Squinting helps me focus on dark shapes.
