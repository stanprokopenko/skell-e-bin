Great Art Isn't Always Ideal for Learning Perspective

Some of your favorite artworks might not be the best for studying perspective. Kai Nielsen, a Danish illustrator, created beautiful and expressive designs, but his pieces aren't optimal for learning perspective. While his work is powerful and inspiring, it often emphasizes flat shapes over depth.

Observing Albrecht Dürer's Work

Albrecht Dürer's images, like heralds and insignias, are remarkable but offer limited perspective learning. You might find lessons on overlap and anatomy, especially in the feathers and scales he depicts. However, his pieces typically present flattened space, similar to relief sculptures, which isn't ideal for studying depth and perspective.

Perspective in Hokusai and Hiroshige's Art

Artists like Hokusai and Hiroshige incorporated perspective differently. Hokusai's famous horse illustrations show an understanding of form through cross-contours, but his use of medieval perspective isn't the best model for learning vanishing points. Hiroshige's work often employs isometric perspective, which creates optical illusions where objects don't recede realistically into space.

Balancing Flat and Thick Elements

Even when artists know perspective, they might choose to play with flatness and depth. Kai Nielsen skillfully combines flat shapes with areas that display thickness. For example, he uses ellipses at the base of vertical objects to show where planes change direction, adding a sense of depth to his otherwise flat compositions. This deliberate mix enhances the visual interest of his work.

Learning from Virginia Frances Sterrett

Virginia Frances Sterrett, inspired by Kai Nielsen, evolved this style with more refined techniques. Despite her short life, she illustrated old French fairy tales with exceptional control over perspective. By placing elements on the ground plane and understanding how they wrap around forms, she created stunning vertical compositions. Her work serves as a testament to how you can integrate perspective into your art, even within a stylized approach.

Applying These Lessons to Your Art

As you develop your own style, consider how these masters utilized or deviated from traditional perspective. Whether you prefer a flatter tradition or want to explore depth, studying how they manipulated perspective can inform your compositions. You have access to abundant resources and a community of artists to inspire you. Pick the elements that excite you and integrate them into your work.
