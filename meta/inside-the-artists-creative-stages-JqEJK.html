Embracing the Stages of Creation

We're starting right at the beginning, assuming you've got your materials ready. Like Botticelli, who didn't start in one corner and make it up as he went, we work in stages. This methodical approach is essential, whether in classical painting or modern illustration.

My Journey with Advertising Illustration

For most of my career, I did advertising illustrations, airbrushing rendered products before computers were an option. These projects required careful planning under tight deadlines.

For example, working with Bruce Mayo, who provided wonderful comps, I would:

  • Create an accurate line drawing, sometimes multiple versions.
  • Get client approval.
  • Paint the final piece, sometimes overnight.

This process was methodical, with distinct stages: from rough comps to precise drawings to finished art.

Developing the Mock Turtle

When I drew the Mock Turtle from Alice in Wonderland, I started with numerous thumbnail sketches to find a decent design. With honest critiques from my studio friend, I kept refining.

I realized the head's position felt wrong, it needed to tip up more. By analyzing and redrawing the head's center line, I finally got a version I was satisfied with, even if my friend was only mildly impressed.

Understanding Forms Behind the Scenes

Drawing a ball with lines around it, like rubber bands on a sphere, is one of the most useful forms to master. Bending these "rubber bands" teaches you how to draw anything.

These underlying lines, though they don't appear in the finished piece, are crucial. They come from the perspective skill of knowing how to put a line on a ball, relating to boxes and other forms.

Letting Spontaneity Take Over

Not every stage needs to be careful. In fact, early stages should be un-careful. The idea is to let spontaneity flow:

  • Pour out ideas without overthinking.
  • Set time limits to encourage quick thinking.
  • Generate multiple options, hoping some will come alive.
  • Then, choose and refine.

If a drawing doesn't work, start over with vellum. Trace over, make another mess, and do it again. Vellum makes working in stages easier, and while digital tools offer alternatives, I love the tangible feel of paper.

Balancing Method and Improvisation

While a separated, staged process is valuable and common among professionals, it can hinder spontaneous drawing. Spontaneous drawing, like live improv, sharpens instincts but can be challenging.

If you find it difficult, in our next lesson, I'll show you how filling sketchbooks with drawings can help, and share some lessons about taking risks.
