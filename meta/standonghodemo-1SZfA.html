The Importance of Eye Level

When I'm drawing scenes, I always consider the eye level, which is the height of my eyes from the ground when I'm looking straight ahead. This eye level becomes an essential indicator in my artwork. Since I'm the observer, I'm at the center of the scene, and my viewpoint is absolute. By establishing my eye level, I set the foundation for how objects and characters are positioned in the space.

Positioning Characters Based on Eye Level

Let's say I set my eye level at 170cm, which is roughly my height. This means that characters of similar height will align with this eye level when they're at the same distance from me. If I place a shorter character, like a child, or a taller character, like someone close to 2 meters, their positions relative to the eye level change.

By understanding their proportions and positions in relation to the eye level, I can depict them accurately within the space. For example, a child will appear below the eye level line, while a taller person might extend above it. This helps in maintaining correct proportions and creating a realistic scene.

Using Vanishing Points and Lines

Perspective plays a crucial role in making the scene believable. I utilize vanishing points to show how objects recede into the distance. All lines that are parallel in reality converge towards the vanishing point on the horizon line, which is at the eye level. This concept helps in scaling objects correctly as they move farther away.

For instance, when I draw a character further back, they appear smaller due to perspective. By connecting points towards the vanishing point, I can determine the correct scaling and placement of these objects. This technique ensures that the proportions remain consistent, no matter where the characters are positioned in the scene.

Adjusting the Frame and Viewing Angle

The frame, what you see within the borders of your drawing, is determined by your viewing angle and how much of the scene you want to capture. When looking straight ahead, the eye level typically sits at the center of the frame, providing an equal view of the ground and sky.

If I tilt the camera downwards, I see more of the ground and less of the sky. Conversely, looking upwards shows more sky and less ground. Changing the angle affects the composition and can create different moods or focus points within the artwork.

Camera Angle and Direction

The direction of my camera angle is vital. Whether I'm looking straight ahead, upwards, or downwards, it alters the perspective and how objects relate to each other in the scene. For example, drawing from a child's perspective means the eye level is lower, and adults appear taller and more imposing.

Experimenting with camera angles can add dynamism to the drawing. Drawing a hero flying above or viewing a scene from a high vantage point introduces unique perspectives and challenges. It's all about where the eye level is set and how objects align with it.

Practical Tips for Applying Perspective

  • Understand Human Proportions: Knowledge of body proportions helps in accurately placing characters in relation to the eye level.
  • Use Reference Points: Marking key points, like the eye level and vanishing points, guides the placement of objects.
  • Practice with Simple Shapes: Starting with cubes or basic forms can help grasp how objects scale and recede in space.
  • Experiment with Camera Angles: Use a smartphone or camera to explore different perspectives and replicate them in your drawings.
