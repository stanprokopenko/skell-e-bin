How to Render a Realistic Ear in Digital Painting

Copy Code

Congratulations on reaching the final section of the Ear Chapter! In this lesson, I'll guide you through a full color rendering of an ear. We'll work step by step, starting from a color wash and moving towards the final touches.

Starting with a Color Wash

I like to begin my paintings by adding a color wash. I typically choose either cadmium orange or cadmium red. For this rendering, I'll use cadmium red-orange at 96% opacity. This sets a warm base for the painting.

Rough Gesture Drawing

Next, I create a rough gesture drawing. I select a line color that's still colorized, moving the color wheel down to a darker range with about 40% saturation.

I start by drawing the back line of the jaw and cheekbone, where the ear attaches. Then, I sketch the base clef shape to outline the ear. I break down the interior shapes using:

  • A large hook shape for the inner lines of the helix
  • Smaller hook shapes for the antihelix and tragus

I use straights to define the secondary shapes of the ear's silhouette. Combining straights with curves adds a nice sense of structure to the sketch.

I also map out the lights and darks, filling in areas like the sideburns and cast shadows. I roughly indicate the core shadows at the base of the earlobe and sketch in the earrings.

Organizing Layers

I keep the drawing on its own layer. Keeping elements on separate layers allows for quick corrections. In Photoshop, you can check a single layer by holding down Alt and clicking the eye icon next to the layer.

I ensure my line work doesn't have any opaque paint that could obstruct underlying colors. I label my layers for better organization.

Blocking in Flat Local Colors

I create another layer between the wash and the line work. Using a textured square brush, I block out the flat local colors:

  • Skin tone: I find the local color by looking at the midtones. The skin leans more towards ochre than red, so I adjust accordingly.
  • Earrings: I use a darker gold color to stand out from the skin.
  • Hair: It's a dark brown, almost black. I lighten it slightly to allow room for dark accents later.

To match the composition of the reference image, I adjust the canvas to remove unnecessary space.

Separating Lights and Darks

I create a new layer between the local colors and the line work, called Lights and Darks. Here, I focus on the graphic separation of light and shadow without worrying about gradients or highlights.

I start by blocking in the shadow shapes within the ear, which are more saturated and in the reds due to the many blood vessels. I fill in the cast shadows of the rings, sideburns, and strands of hair.

I also block in the shadows for the hair, making them slightly more saturated and dark.

Adding Midtones and Highlights

I create another layer and start pulling out the midtones and highlights:

  • I use the lasso tool to select the highlights in the hair and fill them in, keeping the shapes graphic.
  • I adjust the line work by colorizing and lightening it to match the underlying colors.
  • I begin painting over the line work to make it feel more like a painting.

Refining the Edges

To soften the edges and blend colors, I use a blender brush. This helps transition from the flat graphic shapes to a more rendered look. I also reintroduce some straights to define the structure of the ear.

Adding Subsurface Scattering

Ears have a lot of subsurface scattering due to their thinness and blood vessels. I add this effect by introducing more red and orange hues in areas where light shines through the ear:

  • Around the helix and concha
  • Near the core shadows and cast shadows
  • At the base of the earlobe

This creates hot areas of saturation that mimic light being scattered under the skin.

Adding Dark Accents and Highlights

I add dark accents to deepen the shadows:

  • In the core shadows
  • Under the earlobe
  • In the crevices of the ear

Then, I enhance the highlights:

  • Focusing on areas where the planes are perpendicular to the light source
  • Using cooler colors like cyans and lavenders for contrast

Final Touches

I refine the painting by:

  • Softening edges with the blender brush
  • Adding modeling lines to create more form
  • Balancing large, medium, and small brushstrokes
  • Introducing small details like peach fuzz on the back of the neck
  • Creating a rough border to keep the piece feeling sketchy, as it's a study

Summary of the Process

To recap, the steps we followed are:

  1. Color Wash: Applied a cadmium red-orange wash at 96% opacity.
  2. Gesture Drawing: Created a rough sketch with colorized lines.
  3. Blocking Local Colors: Added flat colors for skin, hair, and earrings.
  4. Separating Lights and Darks: Defined shadow shapes in the ear and hair.
  5. Adding Midtones and Highlights: Pulled out highlights, focusing on key areas.
  6. Refining Edges: Used a blender brush to soften edges and blend colors.
  7. Subsurface Scattering: Introduced reds and oranges to mimic light under the skin.
  8. Dark Accents and Highlights: Enhanced depth with darker shadows and brighter highlights.
  9. Final Touches: Refined edges, added details, and balanced brushwork.

Ear Anatomy Review

To finish, let's revisit the parts of the ear:

  • Helix: The outer rim of the ear
  • Antihelix: The inner ridge parallel to the helix
  • Tragus: The small pointed eminence in front of the ear canal
  • Antitragus: Opposite the tragus, above the earlobe
  • Concha: The hollow next to the ear canal
  • Earlobe: The soft lower part of the ear
