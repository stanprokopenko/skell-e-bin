Lessons from 750 Freelance Jobs

Early Struggles with Airbrush Illustration

At 18, my first attempt at airbrush, a rose, was a disaster due to water-soluble paints and inexperience. Despite hating the complexity, I persevered. At 19, I tried again with an album cover for my brother's band. After initial failure and near despair, I told myself, **"I can do it again." ** I succeeded, creating a piece I was proud of. This taught me the value of perseverance.

Learning from Others

Later, a friend needed an album cover featuring molten gold lettering. My studio buddy, John Dickinson, and I spent a week figuring out how to make gold look like gold. John discovered that reflections were key. Together, we achieved it. Collaborating with someone smarter highlighted the importance of learning from others.

Breaking into the Industry

In my 20s, I improved with airbrush and created portfolio pieces showcasing my skills. Clients began hiring me for technical illustrations of machines. Doing good work led to more opportunities. I became known as the illustrator/photo retoucher of machines.

Diversifying Skills and Styles

Art director Bonnie Toth gave me a significant break. When her regular illustrators were unavailable, she hired me for a complex oxygenator illustration. She was pleased and began assigning me various styles. She once asked if I could emulate an Old Master style. I was thrilled and produced samples inspired by da Vinci. The agency loved it, expanding my range and opportunities.

Adapting to Technological Changes

After completing an airbrush project, I saw Photoshop in action. The ability to create perfect gradations digitally was revolutionary. I soon transitioned to digital illustration, investing in a powerful computer. Photoshop allowed total control and new creative possibilities. Despite the initial cost, embracing digital was essential to stay relevant.

The Unchanging Importance of Deadlines

Regardless of tools, deadlines remained critical. Clients expected timely delivery and high standards. Missing deadlines could be costly and damage reputations. Some projects were grueling, affecting my health. Out of over 750 jobs, a few were so demanding the injuries lingered long after the money was gone.

Reflections on a Changing Landscape

The industry shifted from a few high-paying clients to many clients paying less. Whether working for clients or fans, expectations didn't change. Meeting deadlines and delivering quality work were essential.

Despite technological advances, core principles endure: perseverance, continuous learning, and adapting to new challenges. While tools evolve, the dedication required to succeed as an illustrator remains the same.
