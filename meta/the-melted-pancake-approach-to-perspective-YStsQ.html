Ernest Watson's Teaching Approach

One of you shared Ernest Watson's introduction from his book How to Use Creative Perspective. He recounted how, on his first day of art school, students were asked to draw a pile of wooden boxes without any instruction or perspective theory. This continued for six weeks, with the instructor only correcting their observations and urging them to "use [their] eyes more expertly".

Initially, Watson thought it would have been better to start with some theory. However, when he began teaching at Pratt Institute, he noticed that while students mastered the theory, they still struggled to draw. They lacked the attitude developed through that six-week grind of observation without guidance.

There's a term for this method: "wicked learning". It involves placing students in situations where they must solve problems independently, fostering deeper understanding.

Balancing Theory and Practice

Reading Watson's account resonated with me deeply. It highlighted the dynamic of how we truly learn: by trying, failing, and figuring things out, but also by understanding the underlying theory. One without the other creates trouble.

In our course, I've structured the content to reflect this balance:

  • First Half: Understanding how perspective works. Through assignments like the melted pancakes, you're encouraged to figure things out on your own and start drawing.
  • Second Half: Intensifying the rigor to integrate perspective into both your brain and hand. We'll dive deeper into measuring space and forms.

I'm intentionally yin-yanging these approaches so that each part includes elements of the other. I hope this gives you a clearer sense of our journey together.

Observations from the Melted Pancakes Project

The melted pancakes assignment was designed to be fun and accessible. Many of you excelled, but I noticed a common issue: how the pancakes lay on the ground.

For example, Johannes provided a wonderful diagram with a responsible receding layout without dramatic warping. In contrast, some works had dramatic warping, where objects close to the horizon line appeared too open or too thin inconsistently.

Anke's drawings showed pancakes below us that were quite open but became dramatically thin as they receded. This introduces challenges we'll tackle in Lesson 9.

The Importance of Perspective in Drawing

To enhance the realism of your drawings, remember:

  • Objects closer to us should appear more open.
  • As objects recede towards the horizon, they should become thinner.
  • This helps convey depth and makes it feel like objects are under our feet or further away.

Several of you demonstrated this effectively:

  • Han opened up forms beneath our viewpoint, allowing them to thin naturally as they approached the horizon.
  • Rachel Dawn Owens illustrated how objects transition from being open to closed, enhancing the sense of depth.
  • Ethan went the extra mile by analyzing forms and incorporating the environment with the horizon line.

These examples highlight the importance of combining theory and practice to achieve convincing perspectives.
