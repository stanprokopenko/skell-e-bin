How to Sketch Mechanical Creatures from Nature

Copy Code

In this lesson, we explore how to transform organic forms into mechanical designs using linear perspective. By studying an organic object and reinterpreting it with mechanical elements, we can bridge the gap between natural anatomy and industrial structures. Peter demonstrates this process using a peccary skull as his reference.

Observing the Organic Form

We begin by observing the organic object, in this case, a peccary skull that Peter found in the field. He examines the skull, noting planal structures, angles, and landmarks such as the brow, cheekbone, and nasal cavity. This initial observation helps us visualize how to convert organic shapes into mechanical forms.

Establishing the Basic Structure

Starting with a Plane

To construct the mechanical design, we start by drawing a right-angled plane. This plane serves as the foundation for the entire structure. By establishing a top plane with a square shape, we set the horizon line and determine the perspective.

Finding Landmarks and Symmetry

We find the central line through the plane, which helps us maintain symmetry. By converging lines and connecting them, we map out where features like the nasal cavity and orbital cavity will be. Drawing through the form allows us to visualize the structure in three dimensions.

We also consider the mandible (lower jaw) by connecting it to the main structure. Drawing invisible or hidden lines helps us understand how different parts relate to each other within the space.

Converting to a Mechanical Design

With the basic structure in place, we begin to mechanize the form. This involves incorporating elements like joints, hinges, hydraulics, wires, and gears. Even though these details are not present in the original skull, we use our imagination and visual vocabulary to add them convincingly.

Incorporating Mechanical Elements

Peter starts to draw the orbital cavity and transforms the brow into a mechanical structure. He adds panel lines, shapes, and parts to create the appearance of a mechanical creature. He emphasizes that while the design isn't engineered to function in reality, it should appear believable to the viewer.

Using Visual Vocabulary

Our visual vocabulary , the collection of images and forms we've observed over time, plays a crucial role. Peter draws upon his experiences with mechanical structures from movies, games, and other artists. He mentions that building model kits, like Gundams, helped him understand mechanical joints and details.

Adding Details and Refinements

We continue to refine the drawing by adding details. This includes:

  • Extending proportions or altering the silhouette to enhance the design.
  • Adding exposed frames, showing underlying structures like hydraulics and wires.
  • Including features from the original skull, such as tusks, to maintain a connection to the organic form.

Peter reminds us that this process involves making up parts as we go, relying on our imagination and experience.

Balancing the Drawing

We should move around the drawing, adding details in different areas, rather than focusing on one spot. This approach helps us balance the overall image and prevent overworking one section. By varying line weights, using thick and thin lines, we create depth and emphasis.

Using Markers for Value Shifts

Peter introduces a marker to add value shifts and surface details. While markers can be used initially to plan out a sketch, in this case, we use them to enhance the drawing with shadows and highlights. This helps define the planes and adds dimensionality to the mechanical design.

* * *

By starting with an organic form and systematically applying linear perspective and mechanical elements, we can create dynamic and believable mechanical designs. This process involves observation, structural understanding, and imagination. Through practice and exploration, we expand our visual vocabulary and enhance our ability to visualize complex forms.

Peter's demonstration shows that transforming organic shapes into mechanical designs is not just about perspective, it's also about preparing for whatever we want to create in our artistic endeavors.
