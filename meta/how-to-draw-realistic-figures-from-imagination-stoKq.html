How to Draw Realistic Figures from Imagination

Copy Code

You're well acquainted with proportion. Now, let's tackle something a bit more challenging, drawing realistic forms from imagination. Can it be done? Yes, it can, and you're going to prove it. By taking the proportions you've learned from basic mnemonics and applying realism, you'll unlock the ability to draw anything, from quirky styles to lifelike images and everything in between. Whether you're a comic book artist or an oil painter, this is the stuff that will push your skills to another level.

Building on Proportions and Mnemonics

Using the proportions you've learned, start with the two halves, the upper and lower body. Begin by drawing the clavicles, which sit a bit lower on the female figure. They are not so high up like wings. From there, use the hand length as a guide from the clavicles down to the sternum, and then down to the tenth rib. These measurements help you build a character that is more realistic in proportion and fleshy distribution of muscle, tone, and value.

Think of the egg shape for the upper abdomen. Remember the hierarchy:

  • Upper abs
  • Navel
  • Lower abs
  • Iliac crest

Find the halfway point from the tenth rib to get the top of the iliac crest. Sketch in the underpants area to fit everything in before the body goes back. These are the mechanics, and by understanding them, you can draw with style.

Adding Realism to Your Drawings

Now, add more flesh and thickness to your model to represent something more realistic. Consider how the body is fleshy and how the bones interact to push that flesh into different shapes. With every shape, you embark on a new adventure.

Ghosting Your Drawing

To draw with style, ghost your drawing back. Psychologically, by ghosting your drawing, you're putting the mechanics into the background. You're fading the mechanical lines so you can focus on the form and add style.

Portraying the Female Form

Think of the female body as a landscape of rolling hills with beautiful overlapping forms. On the female figure, the clavicles are lower, and the neck appears longer due to less muscle mass and the lower position of the clavicles.

When drawing the hips, use the bell shape to represent the wider female hips. This shape is unique to the female form and wouldn't look right on a male figure.

Proportions of the Face

For the face, the head is dynamic and dimensional as a proportional tool. Start by finding the halfway point for the eyes. From the eyes, go up to the eyebrows, and halfway down to the bottom of the nose, then to the bottom lip. Notice how you can get the eyebrows and eye distances with one broad pencil stroke.

When the head is tilted down, the ears appear higher. Remember, head down, ears up, head up, ears down.

Using Line Weight and Shading

Use thicker lines for heavy or shadowed areas to show weight, and thinner lines for areas hit by light. This variation in line weight helps create dimension in your drawing. For instance, thicker lines under the breasts show weight, while thinner lines above show where light hits.

Introduce tone and value to bring dimension into your drawing. A simple understanding of light and shadow allows you to place dark and light areas effectively. For example, areas under overhangs like the lower abs will have shadows, while areas catching light will be lighter.

Understanding Compression and Gravity

When drawing the body, consider how compression affects forms. Flesh compresses where it meets other flesh or surfaces, and bones push flesh into different shapes. For example, when a figure is sitting, the gluteus muscles compress against the surface, flattening and changing shape.

Be mindful of gravity's effect on the body. Muscles and flesh have weight, and gravity influences how they hang or rest. For instance, when the arms are raised, the biceps have weight and may appear to hang slightly.

Drawing Dynamic Poses

Don't be afraid to explore dynamic poses, including foreshortened figures. Moving away from the standing figure allows you to inject more life into your drawings. Use the same fundamentals you've learned, such as the egg shape for the upper abdomen and Mrs. V and Mr. T for reference points, to navigate these complex poses.

Utilizing Pinch and Stretch

In dynamic poses, the body experiences pinch and stretch. When the torso twists, one side compresses (pinch) while the other side stretches. Be subtle with these lines to avoid cluttering your drawing, but include enough to convey the motion.

The Importance of Overlapping Forms

Use overlapping forms to show dimension. Overlapping lines help indicate which forms are in front and which are behind, enhancing the three-dimensionality of your drawing. For example, overlapping the ulna over the carpals in the arm adds depth.

Simplifying Complex Anatomy

Complex anatomy can be intimidating, but you can simplify it using structural mnemonics. Think of the ribcage as an egg or the pelvis as a bell. This approach makes the forms less frightening and more manageable. You can add anatomical details as needed, but starting with simple shapes helps keep your drawing loose and fluid.

Drawing Hands and Fingers

Hands can be challenging, but breaking them down simplifies the process. When fingers are extended, you can use sticks to represent them. When fingers are curled towards the palm, consider massing the hand first before adding details.

Remember the hyperthenar eminence (the fleshy mound below the little finger) and the thenar eminence (the fleshy mound below the thumb). The webbing between fingers is also important, especially when fingers are spread apart.

Embracing the Thought Process

Drawing involves constant decision-making. Be open to possibilities and willing to change your mind as you work. Drawing lightly and loosely allows you to adjust and refine your shapes without being confined by heavy lines.

Remember to draw holistically. Always consider how each part of the body relates to the whole. This approach helps maintain proportion and cohesion in your figure.

Practicing Mileage

To understand how to bend and twist forms, you need mileage, lots of practice. Embrace the exploration of shapes and forms. Love the fundamentals and the process of learning. The journey is everything.

Loving the Journey

Enjoy the process and embrace the journey. The more you practice and immerse yourself in drawing, the more confident and skilled you'll become. By applying these techniques, you can draw realistic figures from imagination. Embrace the mileage, love the process, and enjoy the journey. With practice and exploration, you'll push your skills to new levels and unlock the ability to draw anything you envision.
