The Importance of Straight Line Practice

I've noticed that some of you are already excellent at drawing straight lines, perhaps even better than I am! I encourage you to work larger because drawing longer lines is harder. If you can make a straight long line, it will be easier to make straight short lines.

Techniques for Drawing Straight Lines

Pulling vs. Pushing Lines

I find pulling a line toward me much easier to keep straight than pushing it away. Pulling offers more control. I recommend trying both methods to see which works best for you.

Ghosting the Line

Ghosting is when you practice the motion above the paper before actually drawing. It's a way to prepare your hand and mind. Although I often draw directly, ghosting can help reduce mistakes. Try both ghosting and going direct to see which you prefer.

Experimenting with Angles

If you're right-handed, you may find 135-degree lines easier than 45-degree lines, and vice versa if you're left-handed. Practice drawing lines at different angles to improve your skills.

Using Your Non-Dominant Hand

To challenge yourself further, try drawing with your non-dominant hand. It makes the task harder, which can help you improve.

Adjusting Tools and Materials

Using a graphite pencil on wax paper can increase difficulty due to the slipperiness. Conversely, using a charcoal pencil on gritty paper can make it easier because of the increased grip. Experiment with different materials to see how they affect your control.

The "Grandpa Line" Technique

One of you mentioned the "grandpa line" technique, where you deliberately make small jagged movements to focus on straightness over smoothness. This could help you concentrate on keeping the line straight. Alternatively, focus on making one smooth, beautiful line.

Fast vs. Slow Lines

Drawing fast lines can sometimes help keep them straight, but slow lines can work too. Experiment with speed to see which gives you better results.

Journey vs. Destination Focus

You can think of drawing the line as a journey, focusing on the motion and maintaining direction. Or, focus on the destination, keeping your eye on the point you're aiming for. Find out which mindset improves your lines.

Imagining Your Arm as a Robot

Scott Robertson suggests turning your arm into a robot. Imagining your arm moving mechanically, without fear or emotion, can help steady your lines.

Using Metaphors to Improve Technique

Sometimes, inventing a game or metaphor can enhance your performance. Just like telling a child to "be a statue" to help them stay still, finding a metaphor that works for you can improve your drawing.

Challenging Yourself

I encourage you to challenge yourself by working from smaller to larger lines, or from larger to smaller. There's no single right way, the key is to push your boundaries and find what works for you.
