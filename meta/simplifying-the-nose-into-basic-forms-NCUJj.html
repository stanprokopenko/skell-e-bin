Simplifying the Nose into Basic Forms

Reducing the Nose to a Box Shape

In this first section, we're going to focus on reducing the complex planes of the nose into a simple box, a three-dimensional box shape. I want to show you how to do that, before we dive into an exercise that we'll do together. Hopefully, you'll follow along, and we're going to use this reference page that I've included in the upper left corner.

We'll do a series of these exercises, where we just reduce the nose to this box shape. This will help you see through the form and hopefully conceptualize the forms three dimensionally in your mind. Really, that's the key to creating convincing volumetric figure drawings, when you can actually visualize things three-dimensionally in your own mind before you draw it.

Steps to Simplify the Nose into a Box

First, I'll show you where the vertices or points of this box start and end so it's easier for you to follow along.

  • First point: Start where the inner part of the tear duct meets up with the side of the nose.
  • Pull a line down to the side of the nostril, the wing of the nose.
  • Base point: That point at the base will be where this edge starts, the edge of the nostril.
  • Top point: This point here will be where the nasal bone meets up with the glabella, which is that keystone shape between the eyebrows.
  • Use that to find the other point to indicate the top plane of the nose.
  • See this edge here where it shows that transition from the top line to the side plane? We're going to use that to find that line of the box coming down, and then the same on the other side.
  • Simplify the edges of the nostrils and the tip of the nose. Have them flow into each other into this streamlined, flattened S-curve.
  • Indicate the bottom plane of the nose. The nostril has some thickness to it, and it actually hooks back towards the septum, which is this area here. The septum feeds into the philtrum, which is this shape here.
  • Connect those lines together to find the bottom plane of the nose.
  • Draw through the form to make more of a three-dimensional box.

And there we have it, that is the simplified box gesture of the nose shape.

Breaking Down the Anatomy of the Nose

In this section, we're going to break things down even further, finding more of the secondary and tertiary planes of the nose, still trying to keep things somewhat abstract. I thought this was a great reference image for that exercise.

Understanding the Key Anatomical Structures

Let's break down the forms and anatomy that we're looking at.

  • Glabella: The keystone shape between the eyebrows. We'll label it the glabella, and that'll help us find the other point to indicate the top plane of the nose.
  • Nasal Bone: Forms about half of the two-thirds portion of the top plane of the nose. The nasal bone feeds into the glabella. It comes down off to the side and feeds into the maxilla, which is also part of the skull. The nasal bone helps form the top plane of the nose and a portion of the side plane.
  • Maxilla: Feeds into the zygomatic cheekbone. The maxilla helps form the side planes of the nose.
  • Lacrimal Caruncle: The most inner part of the tear duct.
  • Lateral Cartilage: This top portion transitions into the lateral cartilage, which feeds down into the bulb of the nose.
  • Bulb of the Nose: The bulbous part of the nose made of the greater alar cartilage. It consists of two parts, two sides that conjoin in the middle right there.
  • Alar Fat: The wings of the nose, the nostrils, are actually made of fat called alar fat. They attach to the lateral sides of the greater alar cartilage, and their lateral edge feeds into the lateral edge of the bulb of the nose. The base of the wing kind of has this nice hook on the backside. The bottom edge of the nostril has thickness, and it hooks back in, pointing back towards the septum of the nose.
  • Septum: The middle of the nose between the nostrils. The septum comes all the way down and will also have a kind of hook shape that goes back in and feeds into the philtrum.
  • Philtrum: The furrow that's in between the two halves of the upper lip.

Drawing the Nose with Anatomical Awareness

Now we'll try to draw that off to the side and break the planes down, simplifying them still.

Start by drawing the basic structure we learned in the previous section, but break it down more.

  • Nasal Bone: Helps form the top part of the top plane and a portion of the side plane.
  • Lateral Cartilage: Feeds into the bulb of the nose.
  • Bulb of the Nose: Consists of the greater alar cartilage.
  • Alar Fat (Wings of the Nose): Has thickness on its bottom planes that hook back in towards the septum.
  • Septum: Feeds into the philtrum.

By understanding these anatomical parts and how they relate to each other, you'll be able to render the nose more realistically.

Rendering the Nose

Value Sketches in Black and White

Now we're going to do a few more gestures of different perspectives of the nose. We'll focus on black and white renderings to emphasize the planes and lighting.

When rendering, pay attention to:

  • Core Shadows: The darker areas where light doesn't reach directly.
  • Cast Shadows: The shadows cast by the nose onto other parts of the face.
  • Dark Accents: Areas of occlusion where very little light reaches, such as inside the nostrils.

Try to apply what we've learned about the anatomy and planes to inform your rendering.

Color Studies and Subsurface Scattering

Finally, we'll do a full color rendering. We'll take our time to flesh out the drawing and get everything more structurally sound before diving into the painting.

When working in color, remember the importance of subsurface scattering, the phenomenon where light penetrates the skin, scatters, and exits at a different location. This effect is especially noticeable in areas like the nostrils, where the skin is thinner. Using high levels of saturation in these areas can help avoid a deadened look.

Pay attention to:

  • Local Colors: The intrinsic colors of the skin, eyes, hair, etc.
  • Lighting Effects: How light and shadow affect the colors.
  • Highlights and Shadows: Use cooler highlights and warmer shadows to add depth.

Take your time blending and adding details to create a realistic and vibrant rendering of the nose.
