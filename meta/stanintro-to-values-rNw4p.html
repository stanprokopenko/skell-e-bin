What Is Value in Art?

Value refers to how light or dark something is. It's not about worth or cost. By mastering values, you can transform your line drawings into three-dimensional forms and create the illusion of depth and volume.

The Importance of Value

While lines are useful, in reality, we don't see linesâ€”we see light as it affects forms, appearing as shapes filled with colors. Learning to use values effectively fills in the spaces between your lines, making your art feel solid and realistic.

Value vs. Color

Color consists of three properties:

  • Hue: The color family (red, blue, yellow).
  • Chroma: How pure or gray a color is.
  • Value: The lightness or darkness of a color.

As a beginner, focusing on value is crucial. Practicing with values alone helps you understand light and form without the complexity of color.

Organizing Values: The Value Scale

Values can be organized on a scale from 0 to 10, where:

  • 0 is the darkest black.
  • 10 is the brightest white.

This Munsell system measures light, not pigment, and helps you translate subtle shifts in value into your drawings. Simplifying the infinite gradation of values into steps makes it more manageable.

Factors That Determine Value

Four factors affect the values we see:

  1. Light on Form: How light affects different planes of the object.
  2. Local Value: The inherent lightness or darkness of the object itself.
  3. Surface Material
  4. Atmosphere

Focus on the first two:

  • Light on Form: Planes facing the light are lighter; those turning away are darker.
  • Local Value: An object's inherent value, like dark hair versus lighter skin.

Understanding these concepts helps you accurately render shadows and highlights, enhancing the realism of your art.
