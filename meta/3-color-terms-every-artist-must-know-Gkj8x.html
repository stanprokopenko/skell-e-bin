Primary and Secondary Colors

This is where we get into a little bit of color theory. You may have heard of the three primary colors: red, blue, and yellow. We consider those colors primary because back in the day, when you could only mix pigments to paint, those colors could not be created by mixing any other colors. And in fact, any other color could be created by mixing the three primaries.

For example:

  • Mixing red and yellow gives you orange.
  • Mixing red and blue gives you violet or purple.
  • Mixing yellow and blue creates green.

Going back to our color wheel, the hues on the color wheel actually tell you this. As you can see, orange is right there between red and yellow, violet is between blue and red, and green is the bridge between blue and yellow. These three new colors, violet, orange, and green, are known as secondary colors. They are direct mixtures of the primaries.

Digital vs. Traditional Mixing

But enough with basic theory for now, let's get back to actual practice. If you're painting traditionally with acrylics or oils, you have to mix colors on your palette, and your resulting mixture is how you choose your color.

With digital painting though, this is not a requirement. In fact, often it's not even advisable to mix colors this way because most digital painting software doesn't treat colors like real-world pigments. So your mixtures digitally will look a bit different than if you mixed them traditionally.

For example, using real paint, I could mix this blue and this yellow and get a really vibrant green. But if I tried that digitally, the resulting color here is green-ish, but it lacks the vibrancy I just got with traditional paint. With digital painting, if I wanted that nice vibrant green, I would simply pick it.

Saturation

Okay, I've been using the word vibrant. There is an official color term for that. It's called saturation. Saturation is the amount of color in a hue. In other words, how vibrant it is or how much chroma it has. And this is where I want to upgrade our color wheel to this one. I like this color wheel because it shows us two dimensions of color: hue and saturation.

This is where that middle part of our color pickers comes back into play. Any hue can have a variety of saturation in it. For example, I could pick a red that is very saturated, a very vibrant red, or I can go the opposite way and pick a very desaturated red.

Desaturating a color like that introduces a very important aspect of color theory. When you desaturate any hue, you are bringing it closer to gray. And going back to our color wheel here, we can see how important gray is because every hue has gray in common. That makes gray a very convenient way to travel from one color to another.

To quickly intro that idea, let's take a blank canvas and quickly paint a bunch of very saturated colors or hues onto it: reds, yellows, blues, greens. I don't know about you, but to me, this is the visual equivalent of eating too much sugar. Yuck.

That's because the more saturated colors are, the more distance they have from other colors. You can see this on the color wheel. If I plot out some very saturated colors on there, the path to get from one color to the next, especially colors on opposite ends of the color wheel, becomes very long. But if you desaturate these colors and bring them closer to gray, suddenly those paths get a lot shorter.

A desaturated color that is not perfectly gray is often referred to as a neutral color or, as a verb, neutralizing the color. A neutral color can have some saturation to it, but generally it's close to gray. Visually, desaturated or neutralized colors translate into a more pleasing visual aesthetic.

Value

Okay, back to our terminology. We have hue and saturation. It's now time to add a very important third term. That term is value. Value refers to the lightness or darkness of a color. Actually, you don't even need color to discuss value.

Here's the grayscale chart. It's a list of tones from white to black and all the tones in between. These are values seen without color. Value is easily the most important element of your digital paintings because value determines the light and form of what you see. In fact, it's an extremely common practice regimen for an aspiring artist to only work in grayscale or black and white at first, before adding hue and saturation into the mix. Value can be a bit tricky as far as the color wheel goes because the color wheel doesn't show us value.
