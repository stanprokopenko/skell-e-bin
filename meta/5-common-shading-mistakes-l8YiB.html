Misrepresentation of the Core Shadow

Another common issue is misplacing the core shadow. The core shadow should be entirely on the shadow side of the terminator, and none of it should be on the light side. If you've placed your terminator correctly, the core shadow will follow naturally.

When drawing the core shadow, think of it as an ellipse that adds thickness to the shadow side of the terminator. It shouldn't overlap into the light side.

Incorrect Reflected Light Values

I've noticed that some of you are making the reflected light too light, almost matching the values of the halftones on the light side. This can ruin the effect of form and make your sphere look flat or imply a different material, like metal.

The reflected light should never be as light as the halftones on the light side. It should be darker to maintain the separation between the light and shadow families. Uneven Gradations and the Cosine Law

Some of you have a gradation from the core shadow to the light that is too linear. This doesn't apply the Lambert's Cosine Law, which describes how the intensity of light decreases as the surface turns away from the light source.

Most of the transition from shadow to light happens closer to the core shadow, and the transition into light should be faster. If your gradation is too even across the surface, it can make your sphere look flat or less three-dimensional.

Conversely, if the transition is too sudden, it can create the appearance of a sharp edge rather than a smooth, curved surface. The key is to find the balance where the transition is gradual but happens more quickly near the core shadow.

Incorrect Shape of the Cast Shadow

Many of you have errors in the shape of the cast shadow. When a sphere casts a shadow onto a flat surface, the cast shadow should be a perfect ellipse. However, I see teardrop shapes, elongated pills, and other incorrect shapes.

If the cast shadow is on a curved surface, it will adjust accordingly, but for a flat surface, it should be an ellipse. Pay attention to the symmetry and curvature of your cast shadow to ensure it matches the light source and the form of the sphere.

Spotty Halftones and Noise

Some of your spheres have spotty halftones, with patches of light and dark in the wrong places. This can make the sphere look dirty or as if it has smudges, rather than a smooth, three-dimensional form.

While it's okay for your shading to have some texture or noise, especially in quick sketches, you need to ensure that the overall values are correct and transitions are smooth. Avoid placing darker halftones near the center light, or creating abrupt changes in value that don't follow the form.
