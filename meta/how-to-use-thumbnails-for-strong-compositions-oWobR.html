Understanding Warp Perspective

What is warp perspective?

A warp perspective is pretty much when, rather than having a singular point at which all of the lines converge, or several, or even three, which is what a three-point perspective would be, a warp perspective is closest to a one-point perspective but where the lens of the camera is adding a little curve to everything.

I know that what I'm about to say might go over most of your guys' heads, but I had an epiphany the other day that I thought was like, oh my god, I finally understand really how to achieve it in a way that makes things concrete.

When you look at a box that is eye level and perfectly straight on with you, then it will look like a square, because you just don't get an angle on the other side planes.

The reason I was thinking about it in these terms is because I saw a Kim Jung Gi video where he was drawing, and he was pointing out, so this is eye line, and I'm always thinking about what's above eye line and what's below eye line.

And I was like, I understand why that's useful for drawing things correctly, but on the other hand, like kind of so what? Like I didn't really see what I would use that for.

But when I was drawing these AC unit things, it reminded me that anything below eye line means you're going to see the top plane of that box. Anything above eye line, you're going to see the bottom plane of the box. And to the sides, you're going to see the side planes of that box.
