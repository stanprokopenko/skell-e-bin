The Picture Plane: A Window into Perspective

Drawing on air isn't easy. That's why we use the picture plane, a kind of window, to display the delightful illusion of three-dimensionality on a flat surface. The picture plane helps us solve perspective problems by explaining how perspective works.

How the Picture Plane Explains Perspective

The picture plane shows us why a close object looks wider than the same object farther away. From the top view, we see that closer makes bigger. This is a fundamental truth of how we see the world.

From the side view, the picture plane shows us how a close object looks taller than the same or even a taller object that's farther away. We learned at the beginning that far things diminish and close things enlarge.

One-Point Perspective

Let me show you a one-point perspective setup. When you look at an object face-on, one set of lines, the Z lines, recedes to their vanishing point. Here's why,

  • Top View: From above, only the Z lines go away from the viewer or from the window (the picture plane), and vanish in that direction. The X lines (left and right) remain parallel to the picture plane, they don't vanish.

  • Side View: The up and down lines (Y lines) are also parallel to the picture plane. They don't go to vanishing points either.

This is a one-point setup because only one set of lines goes away from the window.

Transition to Two-Point Perspective

As soon as the object spins a few degrees one way or the other, the X lines no longer run parallel to the picture plane. Instead, they veer away from the face of the plane. In two-point perspective:

  • X Lines Shift: The X lines shift from width to depth, creating a new setup compared to one-point perspective.

  • Vanishing Points: The direction these lines point will show you exactly where to put vanishing points.

Finding Vanishing Points

When you look straight down on the scene, you can:

  • Run lines from the eye of the viewer parallel to the lines in the scene.

  • These lines land on the picture plane left and right where the vanishing points will be.

This is a breakthrough discovery. If you spin the object to any position, projecting a parallel line from the viewer will find the vanishing point where it intersects the picture plane.

Symmetrical Perspective

If you place the object at 45 degrees so the front corner lands in the center, the vanishing points will be the same distance from the center. This creates perfect symmetrical two-point perspective.

The Effect of Viewer Position

  • Moving the Viewer Closer: If you move the viewer up close, the vanishing points come closer together. This gives that wide-angle effect.

  • Field of Vision: Placing the viewer far enough back so that a 60-degree field of vision includes the whole object protects from distortion.

The Value of the Picture Plane in Technical Drawing

This method is far from freehand drawing, but it's the science behind accurate perspective. The picture plane:

  • Explains from the side how you might look down at a scene or up at a form.

  • Shows why we have more than one set of vanishing points when objects spin.

  • Shows where the vanishing points go left and right, and up and down.

  • Allows you to draw plan projections, meaning you can take any top, side, and front view of an object and draw it precisely in three-quarter view.

Drawing Accurate Three-Quarter Views from Orthographics

Here's how you draw an accurate three-quarter view from orthos. It happens in two stages: Plan and Projection.

Plan Stage

  1. Place the Top View: Position your top view behind the picture plane in any way you like.

  2. Set the Viewer: Place the viewer far enough back so that a 60-degree field of vision includes the whole object.

  3. Project Points: Run lines (imagine laser beams) from the corners of the object to the viewer's eye.

    • Each corner places a dot left and right where the laser beam goes through the picture plane.

  4. Understand the Process: Take a moment to think about what you just did.

    • You placed crucial points like corners where they land left and right on the picture plane.

    • Press pause if you need to. Be sure you understand this before moving on.

Projection Stage

  1. Change the View: Now, change the view from looking down (plan view) to looking at the picture plane directly.

  2. Draw the Front View: Draw that front view separately, usually below the plan.

  3. Transfer Marks: Bring these marks down to place, transferring lateral positions (left and right) precisely.

    • Top-Down Orthos show width and depth.

    • When depth swivels, it has a little width on the picture plane left and right.

  4. Set Eye Level: Choose the eye level, which means placing a horizon.

    • This places vanishing points at their correct height.

  5. Connect the Lines: Fill it all in by connecting dots as the lines going off to the left and right go their respective ways.

  6. Add Height: Since the plan view doesn't show height, line up an ortho, a side view or front view that shows Y lines.

    • Paste it on the window to transfer those height lines into the projection.

    • This tracks those unforeshortened heights into that world so they look true back there.

Completing the Drawing

You've now projected the plan below you into a three-dimensional drawing. This method allows you to draw with accuracy and precision, projecting orthos into three-quarter views with no guesswork.
