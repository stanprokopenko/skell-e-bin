How to Capture Weight in Gestures

Copy Code

Starting with Gesture and Idea

Now that I've set up the big, broad strokes, I focus on the gesture and think about the idea as prominent, not about the subject as something to be copied or captured. I aim to communicate the essence, not just replicate what I see.

Introducing Weight and Balance

As I progress, I shift towards the what of what I'm seeing. If I'm trying to capture a feeling or a sense of realism about the figure, I consider principles that will get me closer to the mark. For me, the next focus is weight and balance.

My idea is often about communicating the naturalistic realism of the body. Your idea could be entirely different, but it's essential to pair your concept with appropriate execution.

The Eight Parts of the Body

To study weight and balance, I'd like to take you through a proportion chart. This chart helps us organize and understand what I'll describe as the eight parts of the body:

  1. Head
  2. Rib cage
  3. Pelvis
  4. Spine
  5. Two arms
  6. Two legs

I'm not going into strict detail about landmarks or where muscles go. Instead, I want to give you the big ideas of these parts and their organization.

Creating a Proportion Chart

Here's how I like to create a proportion chart:

  1. Start with a line for the head and another for the feet. This creates an arbitrary scale.
  2. Divide the distance between the head and feet into halves and thirds. I use halves and thirds because that's about all my brain can handle mathematically.
  3. Halve the space to find the midpoint of the figure.
  4. Divide each half into halves again, giving you four sections.
  5. Halve each of those sections once more to get eight equal parts. This represents an eight-head figure, an idealized proportion.

Front, Side, and Back Views

I like to draw three views:

  • Front view
  • Side view
  • Back view

Labeling these helps organize my thoughts and allows me to see how the parts relate in different perspectives.

Understanding the Rib Cage and Pelvis

Between the head and the midpoint, I divide the space into thirds:

  • The rib cage starts at the first third and ends at the second third.
  • The pelvis is generally one head tall and two heads wide.

To determine the width of the rib cage:

  • For the front and back views, it's about two heads wide.
  • For the side view, it's the width of one head.

I also consider the tilt of the rib cage and pelvis:

  • The rib cage often has a slight tilt backward.
  • The pelvis tilts forward, especially in women, creating an asymmetrical balance.

The Spine and Its Curves

The spine is crucial for weight and balance. It has three parts:

  1. Cervical spine (7 vertebrae) – pushes the head forward.
  2. Thoracic spine (12 vertebrae) – angles backward.
  3. Lumbar spine (5 vertebrae) – angles forward into the pelvis.

I visualize the spine as an S-curve, with each section contributing to the body's balance. This natural imbalance creates the upright posture we have.

Importance of the Spine's Curvature

Understanding the spine's curvature helps explain:

  • Movement and balance in the figure.
  • The relationship between the masses (head, rib cage, pelvis).
  • How to create naturalistic and lifelike drawings.

Weight and Balance in the Body

The body is a chaotic tower of balances. Our upright qualities come from a constant state of imbalance. Here's how different parts contribute:

  • The head leans forward due to the cervical spine.
  • The rib cage angles backward to counterbalance the head.
  • The lumbar spine curves forward to balance the rib cage.

This asymmetrical balance is essential for creating a sense of naturalism in our drawings.

Proportions of the Legs

Moving into the legs:

  • The great trochanter (hip joint) is about two-thirds down the pelvis.
  • The knee is located at about five and a half heads down (in a seven and a half head figure) or at six heads down (in an eight-head figure).

The femur (thigh bone):

  • Has a J-curve, especially noticeable from the side.
  • Is longer than in most mammals, making humans slower but allowing upright posture.

The lower leg:

  • Forms an S-curve with the femur, acting as a shock absorber.
  • The ankle ends at the last half head, which is further divided into thirds for detailed proportions.

Asymmetry and Design in Limbs

The limbs exhibit asymmetry:

  • The leg can be seen as a lightning bolt shape, with in and out movements.
  • The arm has a carrying angle, more pronounced in women, causing the wrist to align with the great trochanter.

Understanding these asymmetries helps in creating dynamic and realistic figures.

Observations on Gender Differences

There are notable differences between male and female proportions:

  • Women tend to have:

    • A greater tilt in the pelvis.
    • A vertical belly button due to the angle and abdominal stretch.
    • A more pronounced carrying angle in the arms.

  • Men tend to have:

    • A less tilted pelvis, resulting in a horizontal belly button.
    • Less pronounced asymmetries in certain areas.

These differences can affect the overall story and character of the figure.

The Importance of Understanding Form

By understanding these principles:

  • We see that the body's form reveals functionality.
  • We can create drawings that are lifelike and natural.
  • We avoid drawing figures that are stiff or symmetrical, which can look unnatural.

Applying Proportions to Gesture Drawing

When doing gesture drawings:

  • Keep in mind the big ideas of placement and proportion.
  • Use curves, rhythms, and asymmetries to organize the figure.
  • Don't get overburdened with details, focus on the overall balance and movement.

Contour and Anatomy

The contour of the muscles and bones reflects the underlying asymmetry:

  • Active and passive muscles create variations in the contour.
  • Even the arm's musculature shows asymmetrical relationships.
  • Recognizing these helps in drawing more convincing gestures.

Common Proportion Errors

Be aware of common mistakes:

  • Drawing the gluteus maximus (the largest muscle in the body) too small or too high.
  • Making hands too short , they should end halfway down the length of the femur.

* * *

Understanding weight, balance, and proportions is essential in figure drawing. By studying the eight parts of the body and their relationships, we gain insights that enhance our gestures and overall drawings. Always focus on the why behind your drawing choices, and let the underlying structure inform your art.
