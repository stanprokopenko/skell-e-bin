Painting Process Overview

I took a break, got some water, and returned to work on the painting before bed. It was important to me to complete the painting before calling it a night. I focused on the drapery and vest details because leaving them unfinished was bothersome.

Color Selection and Adjustment

I had already established the colors for the scarf and green shirt. However, I needed a darker color for the vest. I decided to use black with a bit of red to warm it up, aiming for a dark brown. This process involved some trial and error to get the right color. Once the color looked close to what I wanted, I decided to go with it.

Managing Unfinished Areas

I considered that the background and vest might blend, which was fine with me. I adjusted the scarf to bring in the vest instead. It's crucial not to leave sour notes in a painting, as they could bother you later. Unfinished details could be the reason for disliking a painting years later. Completing these details helps others relate to the painting rather than being puzzled by unfinished areas.

Adding Details and Character Identity

I aimed to quickly indicate details to establish the character's identity. In my experience, collectors appreciate a complete story rather than an unfinished study. I tend to tell the whole story in my paintings, for better or worse. I questioned whether adding more details made the painting feel more complete. I wasn't doing this to draw attention but to avoid distractions from the main focus.

Artistic Decisions and Techniques

I took artistic license to suggest the character's head was casting a shadow on a nearby wall. I played with shadows to create lost edges and bring focus to the face. I revisited the original color mixture to maintain balance and avoid excessive deviations. I added surface texture with a palette knife for contrast with brushwork. Working over thick paint required adding more texture, enhancing the painting.

Achieving Realism

I discussed the principle of achieving a glowing effect through smooth transitions. I reflected on the process from starting with a graphite drawing to adding shadows and light. The initial flat, posterized look was necessary for building realism. I emphasized the importance of observation and getting proportions right. I explained the transition from flat to realistic through stages of adding halftones and edges. I used my finger to soften edges, which made a significant difference. I added color variations to flesh tones to avoid a plasticky look. I encouraged playing with edges and colors while maintaining control.

Reflection and Future Plans

I aimed to simplify the painting process without overwhelming with too many fundamentals. I suggested that following this process could lead to a decent painting, even without advanced drawing skills. I proposed trying the process again with a different model to reinforce learning. I concluded for the night, planning to reassess the painting in the morning and possibly add more.
