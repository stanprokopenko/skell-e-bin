Observing Water Shapes and Perspective

When you look at Hiroshi Yoshida's "Rapids," notice how water arranges itself into roundish shapes when viewed from above. However, when you look along them, as in this piece, they compress into ellipses. Understanding ellipses is crucial here.

Observe how Yoshida treats the top plane of the waterfall. He depicts it as a plane with varying heights, breaking into a sloping front plane with a slight curve. This demonstrates his awareness of planes in perspective.

Composition and Cross Contours

Yoshida creates a rhythm of light areas with dark patches and dark areas with light patches. While this is a compositional choice, he doesn't sacrifice perspective to achieve this rhythm. Instead, he uses cross contour lines to enhance both composition and perspective.

Even in portions of the waterfall, he treats them as segments of an interior cylinder. The lines crowd together at the edges, as they would on the inside of a cylinder, and spread out as they face toward you.

Scale and Depth

Notice how the froth and wavelets decrease in size as they recede into the distance. The larger shapes in the foreground contrast with the smaller, intricate patterns in the background, enhancing the sense of depth.

Perspective Mastery

Yoshida's ability to control the convincingness of water through perspective is remarkable. His work is both stylized and realistic, showcasing his mastery of perspective. As you study perspective, find images like this that demonstrate skills you wish to develop. Analyze them to deepen your understanding and apply these principles to your own work.
