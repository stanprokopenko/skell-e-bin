Drawing Diggers: Side and Front Views

To draw diggers, start by looking at them and sketching a side view. Then, draw a front view to see where the boom, arm, and bucket are positioned left to right.

Using Blobs to Create 3D Forms

Next, use a blob to represent the digger in three dimensions. The front end of the blob is closest to you, representing the front of the digger. To define the form:

  • Wrap a rubber band around the blob. This helps establish the orientation.
  • Slice off the front plane to make it clear where the front is.

If the top part is closer to you, do the same:

  • Wrap a rubber band around the top.
  • Slice off the top plane.

The point is, if a corner is closest to you, the lines that go away to your right and left go up to your right and left. This is useful information, and the rubber band helps you visualize it.

Slicing Planes and Understanding Perspective

Using rubber bands and slicing planes might seem like the long way around, but it's a way to get a three-dimensional form to sculpt into. Starting with a blob in position gives you a general placement, helping you know where the front, side, and top are.

Drawing Diggers from Different Angles

Try drawing a digger from an up view, as if it's floating above you. Begin with a blob:

  • The end closest to you is the front.
  • Draw the equatorial lines to define the sides and underbelly.

These lines aim down to your left and right, determining the perspective. We first do it generally, then gradually learn to do it precisely.

Exploring Multiple Vanishing Points

Consider the platform and cabin of the digger:

  • They might be lined up, but what if they're not?
  • You can place the platform in two-point perspective and have the cabin aim right at you in one-point perspective.
  • Alternatively, both can be in one-point, or both in two-point with different sets of points.

This shows that a picture doesn't have to be in just one or two-point perspective. Things spin in reality, and each object can have its own vanishing points.

The Limits of Blobs

Blobs aren't very precise, and they may give you trouble when placing characters into environments. For example, a character's feet might not look right without a grid on the ground. Drawing a block of ground with a more open grid helps position the character correctly.

Combining Forms in a Scene

Remember that:

  • The street, the digger, and the character's feet can each be their own individual unit.
  • Cartoonists and animators often use blobs.
  • Upper body, lower body, limbs, and even fingers can go in different directions.

Wrapping a rubber band around each shape turns it into a form. That's the Looney Tunes maxim: **"Put a striped sock on everything, you can take it off later." ** It makes it clear whether a blob is coming toward you or going away.

Creating Characters and Sketching Ideas

If you feel like creating characters, sketch all sorts of crazy ideas. Don't give up out of fear that the first idea might not be perfect. If you're not sure about the shape:

  • Put it in a pure front view or pure side view.
  • Maybe even draw the back, top, and bottom.
  • Make a little orthographic drawing to force decisions about the shape.

Understanding Perspective in Cartoons

Not sure about tumbling the character? Some cartoonists prefer not to have too much perspective in their work. They feel that once you begin to draw sophisticatedly, it's hard to return to naive drawing. They're correct.

However, even if it's hard to get into naive mode when you know too much, it's harder to use something you never learned how to use. Personally, I like cartoons that use perspective. Early 20th-century cartoonists almost all worked in this tradition. They had the skills that you're working on here, and this is one of them.
