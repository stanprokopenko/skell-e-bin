Understanding Memory in Art

I've always been fascinated by memory, and how it impacts our ability to draw. One story that highlights this is that of Henry Molaison. In 1953, a surgeon named Dr. William Scoville operated on Henry's brain, hoping to alleviate his convulsions. During the procedure, a little-known part of the brain called the hippocampus was removed. This tiny section is crucial, it's our brain's internal art engine, responsible for storing short-term memories and deciding which are important enough to become long-term memories.

Without his hippocampus, Henry could only remember things for about 15 minutes. Anything beyond that vanished. Yet, something remarkable happened. Despite his inability to form new long-term memories, Henry improved at tasks he practiced repeatedly. This phenomenon revealed the power of procedural memory, stored in other parts of the brain like the basal ganglia and cerebellum.

Mnemonics and Memory Clues

I've discovered that using mnemonics, strange or unusual memory clues, helps the hippocampus deem information important. For instance, I remember my sixth birthday vividly because I received a giant button with the number six on it. It was such an unusual gift, my brain decided to keep that memory.

In drawing, I use visual mnemonics to retain complex anatomical details. Thinking of the clavicles as bicycle handlebars or a skydiver's arms makes them easier to recall. The gluteus muscles become an upside-down butterfly. These odd associations make the information stand out, and the hippocampus stores them as important.

Repetition and the Art Engine

Repetition strengthens our internal art engine. By drawing the same figure or shape multiple times, we are telling our brain that this is important. This method doesn't just rely on mnemonics, it taps into procedural memory. Our hands develop memory, improving motor skills through practice.

This concept was evident in Henry's case. Researchers had him draw a star by looking at its reflection in a mirror, a challenging task. The first attempts were poor, but with repetition, he improved, even though he couldn't remember doing the task before. His hands had learned the movement.

The Memory in Your Hands

When we draw repeatedly, our hands begin to "remember" the motions. This muscle memory is why artists can sketch complex forms without constant reference. It's stored in parts of the brain unaffected by short-term memory loss.

I often recommend drawing from reference and then attempting the same drawing from memory. This practice leverages the short-term memory stored in the hippocampus and reinforces it through repetition, moving it into long-term storage.

Overcoming Educational Challenges

I once believed I had a bad memory, especially when it came to math. Numbers didn't stick unless I attached them to visual images. For example, to remember the number one, I'd think of a bun and imagine what I needed to recall nestled inside it.

My educational environment wasn't ideal. Schools didn't prioritize art, and I wasn't exposed to it beyond the first year. Despite this, I found ways to nurture my passion. I realized that regardless of our beginnings, we can be successful in art by picking up the pieces later and finding methods that work for us.

Practical Tips for Enhancing Memory

  • Use Mnemonics: Create strange or vivid associations with the material you want to remember. The weirder, the better.
  • Repeat Tasks: Draw the same subject multiple times. Repetition signals to your brain that this information is important.
  • Engage in Motor Skills: Practice drawing techniques to develop muscle memory. Your hands can learn movements independently of conscious memory.
  • Write Notes: Jotting down important points reinforces memory. It tells your brain that the information is valuable.
  • Draw from Short-Term Memory: After studying a reference, put it away and redraw it from memory. This exercise strengthens recall.

Passion and Persistence

Art isn't just about talent, it's about passion and persistence. I found solace in drawing, even in challenging environments. Whether you're making a living from art or doing it for personal fulfillment, the act of creating is beneficial. It not only produces something beautiful, but also keeps your mind active and engaged.

Remember, feeding your internal art engine is a continuous process. Embrace mnemonics, practice regularly, and let your passion guide you. By doing so, you'll enhance both your artistic skills and your memory.
