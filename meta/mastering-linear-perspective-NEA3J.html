Drawing by Hand and Using Your Brain

Drawing in perspective requires both knowledge and skill. While you can draw by hand, using tools helps you achieve precision that's challenging to attain otherwise. Let's explore the essential tools that will aid you in mastering perspective drawing.

Basic Drawing Tools

Pencils

To learn perspective, you need only one sharp pencil. An HB pencil is common and balances between light (hard) and dark (soft). Mechanical pencils are recommended because they're inexpensive, reliable, and widely available.

  • 0.5 mm leads are great for detailed, small-scale work.
  • 0.7 mm leads are slightly thicker, allowing you to press harder.

Choose the pencil that feels right to you. If it makes a clean line, it'll work. Pencils also allow for erasing, which is helpful when you're learning.

Paper

Two kinds of paper are useful: bond and transparent.

  • Bond paper is standard printer paper and works for most exercises.
  • Transparent paper, like tracing paper, lets you start clean when a drawing gets messy.

The best tracing paper is vellum. It's white but becomes transparent when laid over an image. It's durable and holds up well when erasing. If vellum is too expensive, you can tape bond paper to a lightbox or a window to make it transparent.

Erasers

Two types of erasers are essential:

  • Plastic erasers are firm and good for precision. They have sharp edges that you can recut when they become dull. You can also get them in holders like pencils for even more precision.
  • Kneaded erasers are soft and moldable. You can shape them for precision, but they're floppy. They're great for gently lightening areas by dabbing or rolling them over the drawing.

Straight Lines: Freehand and Ruled

Drawing Freehand Lines

Drawing perfectly straight lines freehand is challenging, but you can get pretty close with practice and some secrets to improve. Developing this skill enhances your hand-eye coordination.

Using Rulers and Straight Edges

When you need precision, use a ruler or straight edge. This isn't cheating, tools are there to help you. Rulers give you straight lines, but they don't control angles by themselves.

Understanding Angles with Protractors

Measuring Angles

Every angle has a specific measurement, and a protractor helps you find it.

  • A circle is divided into 360 degrees.
  • A straight line across the circle measures 0 to 180 degrees.
  • A right angle is 90 degrees, perpendicular to the base line.
  • Angles less than 90 degrees are acute angles (sharp).
  • Angles greater than 90 degrees are obtuse angles (blunt).
  • Parallel lines run in the same direction and never meet.

Knowing these angles is crucial. In professional drawing, you often need to draw lines that are parallel, perpendicular, or at a specific degree to another line.

Introducing Drafting Instruments

T-Squares and Triangles

A T-square slides up and down the side of your table or drawing pad, keeping every line horizontal. Triangles slide along the T-square to help you draw vertical lines and specific angles.

There are two common types of drafting triangles:

  • 45-degree triangle: useful for creating 45-degree angles.
  • 30/60-degree triangle: provides both 30-degree and 60-degree angles.

These tools make drawing common angles easy and fast.

Axonometric Perspective

In drafting, triangles help create axonometric projections, like isometric, diametric, and trimetric perspectives.

  • Isometric projection: all axes are equal, and you can measure along any axis.
  • Diametric and trimetric projections: axes have differing scales.

These methods don't use vanishing points. Lines recede but remain parallel, so your drawing can extend infinitely without disappearing.

Linear Perspective and Vanishing Points

While axonometric perspective is useful, linear perspective allows you to create more realistic depth.

  • Vanishing points help draw lines that appear to recede into space.
  • Drawing lines to vanishing points requires both brain skill (knowing where to place them) and hand skill (drawing them accurately).

Practice aiming lines toward vanishing points to improve your perspective drawing.

Developing Hand and Brain Skills

Aiming Lines Freehand

The goal is to draw lines toward vanishing points freehand, combining your brain and hand skills. This allows you to:

  • Invent any point of view or position.
  • Exaggerate forms.
  • Build complex worlds.
  • Place the viewer inside your scenes.

Mastery Over Tools

Masters use various tools, but they also know when to rely on their skills. Developing the ability to draw lines without constant assistance from tools enhances your capabilities.

Keeping It Simple

You don't need expensive tools to learn perspective. A pencil, paper, eraser, and a few basic instruments are enough.

  • Practice with what you have.
  • Introduce new tools as needed to solve specific problems.
  • Focus on learning linear perspective before moving on to painting or rendering.

By keeping your tools simple, you develop both your brain and hand skills. Remember, it's about learning to draw with your mind and your hand.

Next Steps
