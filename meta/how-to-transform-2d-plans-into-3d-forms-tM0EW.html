How to Transform 2D Plans into 3D Forms

Understanding the Plan

Your final project is to create a series of puzzles based on one plan. This plan shows how wide and deep the object is but tells you nothing about its height. This lack of height information allows you to invent the heights any way you want.

Exploring Heights and Elevations

You can:

  • Make positive parts into negative parts or vice versa.
  • Squash or stretch the heights of any portion.
  • Float one portion above or below another, connecting them with wires to clarify their positions.

Be creative in how you assign heights to different parts of the plan. This exercise is excellent for your brain and isn't too hard if you use a template.

Using Templates and Height Lines

Use the provided templates to assist you. When drawing, use a height line to track the elevations. Proceed slowly, every corner counts, and you need to think about which lines are on the other side. Fade back the lines that are on the far side, to enhance the illusion of depth.

Drawing Orthographic Views

To ensure you're understanding the spatial relationships, draw the side and front elevations (orthographic views). You can do this before or after drawing the oblique views:

  • If you draw the orthos first, conform your oblique view to them.
  • If you draw the oblique view first, conform your orthos to it.

Drawing the other views stretches your awareness and helps you know the true proportions of the object.

Enhancing Spatial Awareness

To exercise your spatial reasoning:

  • Pause and study images of the puzzles until they make perfect sense.
  • Try different starting points: use a side view to play with width or a front view to play with depth.
  • Remember, a draft view is one flat view that needs more information to complete.

Simplifying with Isometric Graph Paper

You can simplify the task by using isometric graph paper:

  • It has angled lines at 60°, 90°, and 120°, eliminating vanishing points.
  • You can count units to maintain proportions.
  • Foreshortening is predictable, and you always know how to put a plan into perspective.

However, isometric perspective doesn't provide the realism of vanishing points and limits forms to a few fixed positions.

Moving Forward with Custom Forms

If you understand the concept:

  • Create new elevations based on the provided plan.
  • Design your own forms from these plans.
  • See how many variations you can create while keeping them right-angled for now.

Some may attempt complex forms with bevels, but it's better to start simply. We'll use this thinking in the next lesson.

* * *

The three basic views on the three basic axes provide the fundamental truths of how an object occupies space. Draw from these plans, or if you're ambitious, create orthos of anything you want to learn to draw from imagination. This practice will enhance your spatial understanding and drawing skills.
