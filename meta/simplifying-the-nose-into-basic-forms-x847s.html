Simplifying the Nose into Basic Forms

When drawing the nose, I like to start by breaking it down into its most simple shapes. By reducing the complex planes of the nose into a simple box shape, we can internalize its basic forms and develop a shorthand technique for quick gesture drawings. This approach helps us visualize the nose three-dimensionally.

To simplify the nose into a box:

  • Identify Key Points:

    • The glabella: the keystone shape between the eyebrows.
    • Where the inner part of the tear duct meets the side of the nose.
    • The wings of the nose (nostrils).
    • The tip of the nose.

  • Construct the Box:

    • Connect these points to form the top, side, and bottom planes.
    • Simplify the edges of the nostrils and the tip into a streamlined, flattened S-curve.
    • Indicate the bottom plane by showing how the nostrils hook back towards the septum, which feeds into the philtrum (the groove between the upper lip).

By doing this, we create a simplified box gesture of the nose, making it easier to see and draw its basic structure.

Exercise: Reducing Various Noses to Box Shapes

Practice this technique with different nose types:

  • Narrow noses
  • Wide noses
  • Upturned noses
  • Downturned noses

By applying the box method to various noses, you'll understand how to adjust the basic structure to fit different shapes and perspectives. This exercise trains you to see beyond contours and focus on underlying forms. Remember, the goal is to conceptualize the nose as a three-dimensional object.

Understanding the Anatomy of the Nose

Knowing the anatomy helps us understand the nose's forms and how they relate. Here are the key anatomical parts:

  • Glabella: The keystone shape between the eyebrows.
  • Nasal Bone: Forms the upper portion of the nose's bridge.
  • Lateral Cartilage: Connects to the nasal bone and forms the middle section.
  • Greater Alar Cartilage: Makes up the bulbous tip of the nose.
  • Wings of the Nose (Alar Fat): The sides of the nostrils composed of fatty tissue.
  • Septum: The cartilage that separates the nostrils and feeds into the philtrum.
  • Philtrum: The groove between the two halves of the upper lip.
  • Maxilla: The bone forming the side planes of the nose and connecting to the cheekbones.

Understanding these components allows us to break down the nose into more detailed planes while keeping the overall form cohesive.

Applying Anatomy to Drawing

When drawing, start with a light gesture of the glabella to center the nose. Build upon this by adding the nasal bone, lateral cartilage, and greater alar cartilage. Be mindful of:

  • Indicating Thickness: Show the thickness of the nostrils and how they hook back toward the septum.
  • Defining Planes: Use topographical grid lines to visualize the planes and volumes.
  • Capturing Relationships: Note how the nasal bone feeds into the glabella and how the maxilla forms the side planes.

By applying anatomical knowledge, we create a more accurate and lifelike representation of the nose.

Shading and Rendering Tips

Understanding the nose's planes is crucial for effective shading. Here's how to approach it:

  • Core Shadows: Identify where light transitions to shadow on curved surfaces, like the bulb of the nose.
  • Cast Shadows: Notice where the nose casts shadows onto the face, such as under the nostrils.
  • Dark Accents: Emphasize areas with little to no light, like the nostril interiors and creases.
  • Subsurface Scattering: Use higher saturation in shadows to convey the translucent quality of skin.

When rendering:

  • Separate Lights and Darks: Start by defining the main light and shadow areas.
  • Build Values Gradually: Work from mid-tones to highlights and deep shadows.
  • Highlight Interaction of Light: Pay attention to how light interacts with different planes.
  • Use Color Thoughtfully: Incorporate warmth into shadows and cooler tones into highlights for realism.

Practice with Black and White and Color Studies

Begin with black and white sketches to focus on the nose's planes and values without the complexity of color. This helps in mastering:

  • Form
  • Light and Shadow
  • Contrast

Once comfortable, progress to color studies:

  • Emphasize Saturation: Increase saturation in occluded areas like the nostrils.
  • Avoid Flatness: Use subsurface scattering techniques to add depth.
  • Balance Warm and Cool Tones: Create a dynamic and lifelike appearance.

* * *

By simplifying the nose into basic forms and understanding its anatomy, we can draw it more effectively. Regular practice with different nose types and perspectives enhances our ability to depict this complex feature. Remember to focus on the underlying structure, apply anatomical knowledge, and pay attention to light and shadow. With these techniques, your nose drawings will become more convincing and expressive.
