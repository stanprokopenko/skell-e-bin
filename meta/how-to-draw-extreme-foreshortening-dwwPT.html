How to Draw Extreme Foreshortening

In this lesson, I want to push more into the extreme cases of foreshortening and how to handle them in our figure drawings. We'll explore the use of shortened lines and how to emphasize wraps to convey depth and perspective. Even in challenging poses, it's crucial to maintain the rhythm and lyrical movement of the figure.

Shortening Lines and Using Wraps

When dealing with foreshortened poses, I focus on shortening the lines to make parts of the body appear to recede in space. For example, if the rib cage shifts away and the pelvis pushes back, we have more in-and-out movement. By shortening these lines and emphasizing the wraps, we can effectively represent this depth.

For a foreshortened arm, the length might only be a fraction of what we'd expect. The lower arm, for instance, might be much shorter due to the perspective. By shortening the lines and considering the perspective wraps, we can achieve a more realistic depiction.

Building from the Inside Out

My intention is never to close off shapes when I'm drawing. I focus on the inside lines, and I anticipate building out to the contour. When I was learning, I challenged myself, you can't draw the contour unless you know what it is. The outside isn't just a shape, it is an extension of everything you know on the inside.

So, whenever I'm drawing these lines, I'm anticipating that my build will end somewhere out there. I can slowly creep up on the contour because it's challenging, and sometimes I need that forgiving aspect in a drawing.

Maintaining Rhythm in Foreshortened Poses

Even when dealing with challenging, foreshortened poses, it's crucial to maintain the rhythm of the figure. The animated, active, fun, lyrical movement hasn't left, it's just gotten knotted. I'm working slowly and shortening these lines, but at no point is my rhythm going away. It's still very much foregrounded, and it's important to keep that in mind.

Strategies for Straight-On and Symmetrical Poses

One of the most common questions is: How do I handle straight-on poses? Well, there are lots of options. You can:

  • Draw it as is
  • Push asymmetry in the back and forth, creating 3D asymmetry
  • Change it by turning it a bit or creating some movement

If you're committed to the straight-on structure, focus on showing the forward and backward projection of form. The rib cage might go away in space while the pelvis comes toward you. This creates asymmetry in space, which is good because our bodies rely on asymmetrical balance to stay upright.

You can also play with asymmetry in the tilts of the shoulders. Including a tilt line for the shoulders in your gestures can add expressive quality and make the pose more interesting. Try experimenting with that, I think it's really fun.

Avoiding Common Mistakes in Foreshortened Poses

When dealing with reclining poses or extreme foreshortening, I often see people making lines longer instead of shorter, which is the opposite of what we need. Be aware that you should be shortening the lines to represent foreshortening effectively. Instead of extending limbs, make them appear to come toward or away from us by making the lines shorter and using wraps.

Understanding Weight and Balance

In poses where weight distribution is complex, think of the body as a structure that needs support to maintain balance. For example, if you have a mass leaning in one direction without something to support it, it's going to fall over. Sometimes it's helpful to consider how weight works in your poses and how the figure maintains its balance.

Consider the weight-bearing parts of the body, like the pelvis or the feet. Understanding where the weight is distributed will help you create more believable and dynamic poses.

Using Ellipses and Technical Drawing Skills

Getting comfortable drawing ellipses can be incredibly helpful when dealing with foreshortening. Practicing ellipses and incorporating them into your wraps helps convey depth and form. You might:

  • Start with a plane, angle it, and build ellipses in.
  • Practice drawing ellipses that gradually open up.
  • Use foundational exercises to improve your ellipse drawing skills.

I'm relying quite a bit on this technical aspect of drawing to get the figure forward and back in space.

Incorporating Line Weight and Design Elements

As you get more comfortable, start treating your drawings as compositions. Use line weight to direct the viewer's eye and emphasize important areas. By pushing darker lines in certain places, you can convey what's important and guide the viewer through the drawing.

Think about design elements and how different parts of the figure flow together. Consider how one arm might flow into another and how that creates a rhythm and continued interest in the piece. Incorporating these elements makes your drawing not just a study but a potential plan for a developed work.

Encouraging Invention and Personal Exploration

Don't be afraid to invent and adjust poses to suit your design needs. Invention doesn't have to originate entirely from your head, you can invent a different position based on elements from various references. The history of painting is full of artists who have taken existing compositions, and riffed off those figures and compositions. Painters like Cezanne or Manet weren't accused of plagiarism, they were taking and playing with conventions.

By exploring and adjusting, you might come up with a pose that feels more stable or dynamic, depending on your intent. This exploration ensures that your work isn't just an isolated exercise in a vacuum but has practical applications in your art.

Understanding the Purpose of Gesture Drawing

Remember, gesture drawing is not the final drawing. It's a notational tool to get you started and to develop your ideas. People often get stuck because they think the gesture is the end of the drawing, but that's too much pressure. There's a whole list of steps that come after this, where you take your loose lay-in and develop it into a finished drawing.

When the drawing is notationally done for you, it's done. There's no "gesture police" who'll say you need to do more. When you feel like it's good, it is good. The goal is to understand the position and build from there.
