Master Ear Placement for Realistic Portraits

Starting with a Light Gesture

When drawing the ear, I begin with a light gesture. I start by sketching a horizontal line to represent the brow line, which aligns with the top of the ear. A vertical line indicates where the ear attaches, just behind the back of the jaw and the zygomatic arch.

Blocking in the Ear Shape

Using a simple C curve, I block in the basic shape of the ear. I then use straight lines to define the interior shapes, keeping the gesture light and flexible.

Thickness Variations

It's important to pay attention to thickness variations within the helix. Notice the small, medium, and large shapes, which often widen toward the base as they transition into the earlobe.

Detailing Ear Anatomy

I block in the key anatomical features:

  • Helix
  • Antihelix
  • Triangular fossa
  • Tragus and antitragus
  • Concha
  • Earlobe

Adding rough values helps establish depth and form. I emphasize the thickness by shading around the earlobe and the backside of the helix.

Adding Value and Texture

I apply a light value wash to avoid working against a stark white background. Pulling out the lights allows me to highlight the thickness variations, especially between the helix and antihelix. Avoiding uniform thickness prevents the ear from appearing flat or unnatural.

Learning from the Old Masters

Studying classical sculptures, like Michelangelo's David, provides valuable insights. Observing how the masters designed the shapes of human anatomy enhances our understanding and improves our drawing skills.

Placing the Ear on the Head

Using the Loomis Method

I often use the Loomis method to construct the head:

  1. Start with a light sphere for the cranium.
  2. For profile views, add thickness to the front for the face.
  3. Cut the sphere in half vertically and horizontally to find the brow line and top of the ear.
  4. Block in the neck and shoulders.

Simplifying the Ear's Gesture

For the ear's basic gesture, I use a simple C curve or a bass clef symbol. The interior shapes are added with:

  • A big hook (helix)
  • A smaller hook (antihelix)
  • An even smaller hook (tragus)
  • A triangle for the triangular fossa

Important Proportions

Remember:

  • The top of the ear aligns with the brow line.
  • The base of the ear aligns with the base of the nose.
  • From a profile view, the ear sits in the back third of the skull.

Placing the ear incorrectly can make a character look alien or unnatural.

Ear Placement in Different Views

Profile View

In the profile view, the ear is placed on the back third of the head. The sternocleidomastoid muscle connects to the mastoid process just behind the ear.

Three-Quarter View

For a three-quarter view:

  • Use the Loomis method to find the side plane.
  • Place the ear in the lower back quadrant.
  • Ensure the ear aligns correctly with the facial features.

Front View

From the front:

  • The ears angle out slightly before curving back in at the earlobes.
  • Indicate the helix, antihelix, and tragus for a simplified ear.
  • Maintain symmetry by aligning the tops and bases of the ears with the brow line and nose.

Enhancing Character Design with Ear Anatomy

Understanding ear anatomy greatly enhances portraits and character designs. Knowing where the planes are and how light interacts with the ear adds depth and realism. Even in stylized or cartoony characters, informed anatomical knowledge grounds your work and makes your designs more believable.
