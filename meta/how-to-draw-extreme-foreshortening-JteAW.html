How to Draw Extreme Foreshortening

Embracing Extreme Foreshortening

When tackling extreme foreshortening, I focus on shortening lines to convey depth. By intentionally reducing the length of lines, I push forms away or pull them towards the viewer. For example, if the rib cage shifts away and the pelvis pushes back, I shorten the torso lines accordingly and emphasize the wraps to enhance the sense of depth.

Maintaining Rhythm and Gesture

Even in complex poses, it's crucial to maintain the rhythm and flow of the gesture. The figure should still feel animated and lyrical, regardless of foreshortening. I ensure my lines convey movement, and I avoid losing the foundational rhythmic quality that brings life to the drawing.

Using Wraps and Ellipses

To depict forms moving in space, I rely on wraps and ellipses. Practicing ellipses helps in showing how forms turn in space. I use wrapping lines to indicate the direction of the form, this is especially helpful with limbs coming toward or moving away from the viewer.

Dealing with Overlapping Forms

Overlaps can make poses feel more challenging. When limbs overlap or obscure parts of the body, I focus on the gesture and the flow of lines. I use shorter lines for foreshortened limbs and emphasize the overlapping wraps to convey depth and spatial relationships.

Introducing Asymmetry in Symmetrical Poses

Symmetrical or straight-on poses can feel static. To add dynamism, I introduce asymmetry through perspective. I might show the rib cage receding while the pelvis projects forward, creating a spatial imbalance that adds interest. Sometimes, I'll tilt the shoulders or adjust the pose to introduce movement.

Adjusting and Inventing Poses

If a pose feels too stiff or challenging, I don't hesitate to change it. There's nothing wrong with adjusting the pose to suit the drawing better. I might turn the figure slightly, introduce a tilt, or exaggerate certain aspects to enhance the composition and expressiveness.

Considering Weight and Balance

Understanding how weight is distributed is essential. I think about where the figure's weight rests, whether on the feet, the pelvis, or the hands. In reclining poses, I visualize how the body's mass interacts with the ground, and I ensure the pose feels stable and believable.

Incorporating Design Elements and Line Weight

I use line weight to direct the viewer's eye and emphasize important parts of the drawing. By darkening lines in key areas, like weight-bearing limbs or significant gestures, I create a visual hierarchy. Incorporating design elements such as shared rhythms or directional lines enhances the overall composition.

Practicing with Challenging Poses

I encourage practicing poses that push your comfort zone. Drawing figures with extreme foreshortening, overlaps, or unusual perspectives builds confidence and skill. Systematically working through these challenges helps you understand how to apply gesture and form in any situation.

Remembering the Purpose of Gesture

Finally, I remind myself that gesture drawing is notational. It's a starting point, a way to capture the essence of the pose quickly. The gesture isn't the final drawing, so I stay loose and avoid getting bogged down in details. This approach keeps the process enjoyable, and allows for greater creativity in subsequent stages.
