How to Draw an Anvil from Eye Level

Setting Up the Grid and Horizon Line

I start by drawing the horizon line in my image. To make the grid less prominent, I place two pages of vellum over it. This way, the grid becomes a whispered guide in the background.

Constructing the Box

I choose a front corner to keep the box within the picture frame. By setting the height of the box from this corner, I establish where my vanishing points are. With the three axes in place, I need to decide on the width and depth of the box.

I aim for a rectangle that's higher than it is wide. Since it's a wide-angle view, the front might appear skinnier. I test this by running the corners back to the vanishing points. After adjusting, I create a back rectangle that's tolerable in proportion.

Defining the Anvil's Base and Proportions

Using the ortho (orthographic projection), I find the halfway point in the box. I notice the base of the anvil sticks out in front of the halfway point. I estimate the position of the back and front feet of the anvil.

By running lines to the vanishing points and connecting dots, I check the proportions. The base forms a rectangle that's deeper than it is wide. I find the center of this rectangle using the X method and lift it up to the height of the base.

Building the Anvil's Body and Horn

In the center of the base, I construct a block that represents the elevated body of the anvil. I mark the position of the hanging end, which is about half the distance left to right in the center.

Using the ortho, I see that the body extends beyond the halfway point. I connect lines to form the shoulder and the horn. The horn reaches its peak slightly below the top and curves forward.

I use straight lines as guides but adjust them to reflect the curves of the anvil. The horn's center line isn't visible because it's slightly above us, so I focus on the contour lines.

Refining Curves and Details

I add cross-contour lines to define the curves of the anvil's body and horn. I pay attention to the point where the surface transitions from side to top, known as the terminator. This helps me capture the subtle changes in form.

At the base, I adjust the lines to reflect how the anvil curves down and spreads out. I erase unnecessary lines to reduce confusion and focus on the essential shapes.

Finalizing the Drawing

I darken the lines I intend to keep and erase the ones I don't need. This clarifies the form and makes the anvil stand out against the grid.

Throughout this process, I relied on the orthos and constructed the anvil within the box, navigating around the grid in 3D. This approach allows me to draw the anvil from eye level without directly referencing it in that exact position.
