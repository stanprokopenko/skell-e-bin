Understanding the Intermediate Plateau

The intermediate plateau is a phase many artists experience. We've moved past the beginner stage, but we find ourselves stuck and not improving. It's that point where the last 5-10% of mastery feels extremely difficult to achieve. Many of us might feel we're putting in the effort, but we're not seeing the progress we'd hoped for.

Common Traps That Keep Us Stuck

Frustration and Boredom

Two words often come up when discussing the intermediate plateau: frustration and boredom.

  • Frustration happens when we realize we are not getting better, even though we are trying. But being frustrated can be a good sign, it means we're aware of our shortcomings and are prepared for instruction. We know there's a problem, and we are ready to fix it.

  • Boredom can set in when we're not challenging ourselves. If we're always drawing the same thing, or not taking risks, we can become bored. This boredom might be a sign that we're not pushing ourselves out of our comfort zones.

Complacency

Complacency is a dangerous trap. Sometimes, we're content with our current level and don't see that we're lacking in certain areas. We might think we're doing fine, but in reality, we're not advancing.

  • It's important to be self-aware and recognize that there's always room for improvement.
  • Without acknowledging our weaknesses, we won't take the necessary steps to grow.

Developing Bad Habits

Bad habits can keep us from progressing.

  • We might be doing things consistently wrong without realizing it.
  • These habits can be difficult to break on our own.
  • Having a mentor or seeking feedback can help us identify and correct these bad habits.

Not Setting Clear Goals

Without clear goals, our practice lacks direction.

  • We might have a general idea of wanting to be a better artist, but that's not enough.
  • We need to set specific and achievable goals.
  • Clear goals help us focus our efforts and make deliberate practice possible.

Strategies to Move Beyond the Plateau

Embrace Challenges and Take Risks

To grow, we need to step out of our comfort zones.

  • Try new subjects, techniques, or mediums.
  • Be willing to experiment and fail, because failure is a part of the learning process.
  • Taking risks can reignite our passion and lead to breakthroughs.

Seek Feedback and Mentorship

Having someone else evaluate our work can provide invaluable insights.

  • A mentor can help us identify mistakes we might not see ourselves.
  • They can prioritize areas for improvement and guide us through challenges.
  • Don't underestimate the value of constructive critique.

Focus on Fundamentals

It's tempting to jump ahead to the flashy techniques, but fundamentals are essential.

  • Continually practice core skills like anatomy, perspective, and shading.
  • Even advanced artists revisit the basics to keep their skills sharp.
  • Avoid skipping ahead without solidifying these foundational elements.

Adjusting Our Mindset

Our attitude towards learning can make a significant difference.

  • Working in a relaxed state can facilitate growth.
  • Avoid becoming the dabbler who quits when it gets hard, the hacker who settles for good enough, or the obsessive who burns out chasing perfection.
  • Embrace a balanced approach that combines hard work with enjoyment.

Deliberate Practice with Clear Intent

Practicing with intent means focusing on specific areas for improvement.

  • Identify specific mistakes and work on correcting them.
  • Set goals like, "I want to improve my understanding of lighting within the next month."
  • Deliberate practice leads to more effective learning.

Find Inspiration and Rediscover Enjoyment

Energy and enthusiasm are vital.

  • Pursue passion projects that excite you.
  • Reconnecting with what you love about art can boost your energy levels.
  • Remember why you started creating art in the first place.

Recognize You're Not Alone

Understanding that the intermediate plateau is a common experience can be reassuring.

  • Many artists go through periods of stagnation.
  • Naming the problem can help us objectify it and plan a way forward.
  • Remember that this phase can either drag us down or spark us to new heights.

When to Consider Quitting or Pivoting

Sometimes, it's worth evaluating whether to continue on the same path.

  • Quitting isn't always negative, it can free us to pursue something more fulfilling.
  • Alternatively, we might pivot and take a new approach or direction in our art.
  • Assessing our situation honestly can help us make the best decision for our growth.

* * *

Overcoming the intermediate plateau involves a combination of self-awareness, clear goal-setting, and maintaining our passion for art. By embracing challenges, seeking feedback, and focusing on deliberate practice, we can reignite our energy and continue progressing. Remember, maintaining a positive attitude and high energy levels can make all the difference in our journey to mastery.
