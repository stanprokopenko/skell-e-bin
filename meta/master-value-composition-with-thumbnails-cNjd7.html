Master Value Composition with Thumbnails

Copy Code

Let's practice everything you've learned about value composition in the past few lessons. In this project, you'll take reference images and explore several variations of value compositions.

Project Instructions

  1. Get the Reference Images

    • Two photos are provided in the downloads.

  2. Create Thumbnail Sketches

    • Do thumbnail sketches the size of your thumbnail or palm, or anything in between.
    • Simplify each sketch to just three values.
    • This is similar to the previous master studies project, except now you are the composer.

  3. Use Your Creativity

    • Use your creativity muscles to find the versions of the value compositions that you like best.

  4. Start Small and Simple

    • Begin with small and simple thumbnails to capture the main idea.
    • Play with that for a little.
    • If you attempt small and simple but end up with large and detailed, try again and simplify.
    • Sometimes it's easier to simplify on the second attempt after you've gotten that urge out to get all those details in.

  5. Avoid the Temptation to Finish

    • I know the temptation to try to make a really nice finished piece.
    • The benefit of thumbnails is you can try many ideas quickly and not go down a long path you end up not liking.
    • Spend a little time thinking through the big idea first.

  6. Explore Variations

    • After you finish that first thumbnail, do more quick iterations to explore value ideas.
    • Try to do at least five iterations of each photo.
    • Play around by modifying groups of values.

  7. Don't Fall in Love with Your First Idea

    • The first several ideas are usually not the best.
    • You gotta get them out.
    • Of course, there will be some cases when you hit it on that first try, but that shouldn't prevent you from exploring alternatives.
    • Don't fall in love with your first idea.

Tips for the Project

Freedom in Portraits

  • In a portrait, you usually have a lot of freedom to do what you want with the background or the clothing.
  • Consider these questions:
    • Do you want the background to be darker to emphasize white hair?
    • What if the shirt and hand were both a mid value to reduce contrast?
    • What if you rearrange the value shapes for a totally different pattern?
    • What if, what if, what if?

Keep Edges Hard

  • Sometimes we might be tempted to add a gradation or a soft edge.
  • Hard and soft edges have an effect, but let's keep them all hard for now.
  • We will focus on edges later.

Simplify Environments

  • You don't have to add environment details or invent environments at all.
  • Just consider various abstract shapes that those environments would simplify to.

Consider Cropping

  • Consider how you want to crop your thumbnail.
  • You don't have to stick to the way the photo is cropped.
  • Maybe you want to add a bunch of empty space above.
  • What effect does that have on the portrait?

Tool Suggestions

Using Pencils

  • Use a Dull Pencil
    • You might want your pencil to be kind of dull.
    • It will help you draw with larger value masses while avoiding details.
    • It's that fine pencil tip that might lead you to unnecessary details.
  • Benefits of Pencils
    • Erasable: You can adjust your shapes and values as you go.
    • Control: Pencils can give you more control of your shapes.
    • Texture: Pencils pick up the texture of the paper, which some people find more aesthetically pleasing.
    • Practice Pressure Sensitivity: They force you to practice that pressure sensitivity to get the value that you want.

Using Markers

  • Great Time to Use Markers
    • If you've got some markers, this would be a great time to whip them out.
  • Benefits of Markers
    • Faster: The thick chisel or brush tip allows you to fill in large shapes very quickly.
    • Value Separation: Because markers have predefined values, it's easier to get clear value separation.
    • Simplification: Markers force you to think within the limited number of values and organize the values into those limited groups.
    • Avoid Details: Because the markers are thick, it's much harder to control small detailed shapes, so markers force you to simplify your shapes and think of the bigger idea.
  • Practice with Both
    • You can choose to use pencils or markers for this project or try both.
    • Each has its benefits.

Level Two Instructions

  • New Reference Photos
    • You get two different photos in the downloads.
  • Start with Level One as a Warm-Up
    • You can start with the level one photos as a warm up.
  • Explore Many Versions
    • After exploring many versions, if you fall in love with one of your thumbnails, now's the time to do a bigger thumbnail with a little bit more details and with the option of a fourth and fifth value.
  • Size of Thumbnails
    • These could be the size of your hand or at most the size of two hands.
    • We still want to keep them relatively small to just practice the value composition.
  • More Challenging Photos
    • The level two photos are a bit more challenging.
    • There's more going on and there's more that you can explore, which means more that can go wrong.

Final Advice

  • Don't Add Pressure
    • Don't add that additional pressure on yourself to get these beautiful drawings.
  • Simplicity and Clarity Over Accuracy
    • Simplicity and clarity are more important than accuracy.
    • I don't care if your proportions are correct.
    • I don't care if you captured the likeness or the fancy details.
  • Focus on Value Groups
    • I care if you made deliberate and confident decisions about your value groups and then clearly executed them in your small sketches.
  • Remember Key Principles
    • Simplify for clarity.
    • Control your value range and key.
    • Create a clear hierarchy to guide the viewer's eye.
  • Good Luck!
