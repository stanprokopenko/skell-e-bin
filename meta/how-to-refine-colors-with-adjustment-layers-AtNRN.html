How to Refine Colors with Adjustment Layers

Copy Code

After finishing the flats, I move on to the adjustments stage of my coloring process. This is where I choose the final colors and make holistic adjustments to ensure everything reads clearly and fits within the desired color space.

The Adjustments Stage

In this stage, I might:

  • Use adjustment layers for broad changes.
  • Alter specific colors for better readability.
  • Add variations, like introducing different shades of green to plants.

I've been subtly adjusting colors as I choose flat colors, but now I focus on refining them. For instance, I initially made all the buildings the same color for speed. Now, I darken buildings closer to us due to atmospheric diffusion, distant objects like mountains appear lighter because of air molecules scattering light.

Using Adjustment Layers

Adjustment layers let me manipulate colors without altering the original layers. Since everything is organized, this process goes smoothly. I might:

  • Make foreground elements slightly darker.
  • Adjust the greens so plants closer to the viewer are darker.
  • Add variety to plants to prevent them from looking like one type.

Considering Light Source

At this point, I start thinking about the light source. I decided earlier that light comes from the left to illuminate my main character's face. With flats, I make micro-decisions to account for lighting changes, like:

  • Making one side of a building's plants brighter.
  • Adjusting gray tones on AC units for separation.

Enhancing Contrast and Separation

I look for areas needing more tonal contrast:

  • Adjusting sidewalk colors to make elements pop.
  • Tweaking skin tones to add variety and make the main character stand out.
  • Ensuring legs and background elements have clear separation.

Masking Techniques

I use masks to apply adjustments to specific areas:

  • Masking the background to adjust it separately.
  • Using overlay, soft light, or hard light blending modes to affect overall tones.
  • Creating smoother transitions between foreground and background with airbrushed masks.

By masking the foreground and background, I can:

  • Make the background appear more blown out.
  • Darken the foreground subtly.
  • Increase saturation in the foreground for depth.

Previewing Different Color Schemes

Adjustment layers allow me to experiment with different atmospheres:

  • Warming up the scene for a cheerful look.
  • Cooling down colors for an overcast feel.
  • Quickly shifting the entire palette without altering individual elements.

For example, changing the sky's color can dramatically alter the scene's mood. I can then fine-tune adjustment layers to achieve the desired effect.

Refining Adjustments

Sometimes, adjustments affect elements I want to keep unchanged. If an adjustment layer dulls the red of my main character's outfit, I can:

  • Mask out the red areas from the adjustment layer.
  • Rearrange layers to limit the adjustment's impact.
  • Tweak opacity and blending modes for subtle effects.

* * *

The adjustments stage is all about refining colors and ensuring the artwork feels cohesive. By using adjustment layers and masks, I can make broad changes quickly and focus on enhancing the overall composition.

Next up, I'll be adding shadows, lighting, and highlights to bring more depth to the piece. I'll also add finishing touches like coloring lines or subtle glows, depending on what the artwork needs.
