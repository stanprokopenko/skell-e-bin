How to Draw Blobby Heads

Copy Code

Goal

You’re moving from level 1 blobs to something with a bit of right and wrong. Draw real heads, but keep them as blobby heads. You have room for error. The goal is to think three dimensionally, simplify into big masses, and focus on the edges.

You’re not drawing flat shapes. You’re drawing 3D blobs with plane changes and cross-contours you can feel.

Start Simple: The Egg Version

Begin with the simplest version to train your eye and brain. You’re inventing a lot here, which can be harder than copying.

  • Start with an egg for the whole head.
  • Place the light. For example, from the top right.
  • Fill a clean tone on the shadow side. Keep the top softer.
  • Add a blobby nose. Give the nose edges more firmness than the egg.
  • Add the bottom plane of the nose. Also firm.
  • Draw the cash shadow coming off the nose. Keep that edge sharp.
  • Adjust the edge of the big face shadow:
    • The forehead is very rounded, so keep it very soft.
    • As it goes down toward the chin, make it firmer.
    • Let it transition from soft to firm.

These edges reveal the forms. It’s not the model. It’s a simple form with the same angle and light as the model.

Build the Blobby Head

Use a reference head (like Yoni), but keep it simple. You can push proportions. You’re not using the Lumous method here, just stacking blobs.

  • Cranium: A rounded mass. If the person’s cranium is squarer at the top, add corners. Think cross-contour wrapping around.
  • Jaw: A separate blob that extends from the cranium. Indicate plane changes with edges. Think in 3D.
  • Nose: A very simple, almost Elmo-ish triangular blob. Proportions don’t matter. Just think 3D and how edges will act in light.
  • Brow ridge (optional): Sometimes a big mass with a sudden plane change at the eyebrows. Forehead pops out, then back in under the eyes.
  • Eyeballs: Two spheres dropped into the eye sockets. No eyelids or creases. They will look weird. That’s fine.
  • Muzzle: No lips. Imagine a tuna can with a chunk chopped off. It comes forward and also out to the sides. It’s a bloated, rounded mass over the lips.
  • Cheekbones/Cheek mass (optional): Little ovals for the whole cheek mass. Sometimes fat makes it more prominent.

Think in 3D

  • It’s not a flat ellipse around every mass.
  • Find the strong plane changes and indicate them with edges or subtle cross-contours.
  • Use rhythms to follow how forms connect.

Shade the Blobs

You’re not copying shapes from the photo. Imagine how your blobs would look if lit the same way as the model.

Cranium and Brow Ridge

  • Start with the big form of the cranium.
  • As the cranium meets the brow ridge, place the shadow that runs toward the nose.
  • That edge at the brow ridge is firm where the plane turns suddenly.

Eyeballs

  • With light from the top right, the eyeball is lit on the top-right.
  • Keep the silhouette edges of the eyeball sharp.
  • Inside the form, keep transitions soft so it feels round.
  • Where the eyeball meets the cheek, show the difference:
    • Very rounded plane change in some spots.
    • Firm plane change in others.
    • Sometimes it’s in between, not sharp or soft.

Cheekbone

  • Light from top right puts the bottom-left of this blob in shadow.
  • That shadow edge is firm, but softer than the bridge of the nose.
  • It’s actually very soft on rounded forehead and cheek areas.

Muzzle and Nose

  • The muzzle wraps around into a side plane and has a small bottom plane.
  • Mark the bottom edge of that “can.”
  • Draw the cash shadow off the nose. Keep it sharp.
  • Even if the start of the muzzle edge is covered by the cash shadow, continue the soft transition beyond it.
  • The area under the nose and under the can edge stays in shadow.
  • If your nose blob ends up wider than the model’s, that’s fine. You’re using the model as inspiration for your blobby dude.

Jaw

  • Much of the jaw will be covered by eyeball, nose, cheek, and muzzle blobs.
  • The small exposed part low on the jaw is pretty firm.
  • A very little piece of the bottom might be visible in shadow.

Other Side and Halftones

  • Round forms stay soft. Revisit edges and lighten lines that are too dark.
  • Replace heavy construction lines with half tones and very soft transitions.
  • As forms wrap to the left, keep it very soft.
  • Drop in the other eyeball. Don’t draw eyebrows. Place and fix blobs as needed. Use an eraser to sharpen edges or correct perspective.
  • Hair may cover forms, but you can still add a gentle half tone wrapping around the cranium.
  • The jaw plane can be a little darker with a very soft edge into the cheek, and a more sharp edge where the jaw is firmer.
  • Even in half tone areas, vary the edges:
    • This part is a little sharper.
    • This is soft.
    • This is even softer.

Practice

  • Use the provided portrait photos. Download them and do both versions if you want: the egg and the slightly detailed blobs.
  • Make it harder: keep the same shapes and same angle, but change the light direction. Now you have to invent.
  • You can’t rely on the reference for the shadows, but it will be easier after your study because you understand what each blob is doing.
  • Adjust all the shadow shapes and edges to fit the new light.

Key Reminders

  • Keep forms super simple.
  • Always think three dimensionally.
  • Use cross-contours and plane changes to guide your edges.
  • Control your edges: soft, firm, and sharp.
  • You’re not trying to draw the person. You’re building a blobby version with the same angle and light.
  • Do your best and focus on the edges.
