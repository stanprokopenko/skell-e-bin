Eye Placement and Basic Construction

When placing the eye in a portrait, we use facial landmarks as guides:

  • The wing of the nose relates to the corner of the eye
  • The corner of the mouth often aligns with the center of the eye
  • Use the existing proportion line for the center of the eye top to bottom

To begin constructing the eye:

  1. Roughly sketch a sphere for the eyeball
  2. Look for opportunities to overlap the sphere with the socket for a more form-based relationship

Eye Anatomy and Structure

The eye is held in place by the canthral ligament, which creates a boxy area at the corner of the eye. This ligament attaches to the inside of the temple.

Tarsus plates form the eyelids:

  • They have a planar, somewhat sharp surface
  • Corners of the plates are often areas for highlights

The canthral tilt is crucial for customizing an eye:

  • It refers to the two-dimensional axis of the eye across
  • The tilt can be low to high or high to low
  • A low to high tilt (higher on the outside) is often considered more aesthetically pleasing

When drawing eyelids:

  1. Start from the canthral ligament
  2. Trace the form of the eye sphere
  3. Return to the initial alignment of the canthral ligament and tilt

Remember that eyelids are asymmetrical. The upper lid's peak is usually higher closer to the nose, while the lower lid's peak is higher further from the nose.

Fat Pads Around the Eye

Several fat pads surround and protect the eye:

  1. Nasal fat: Located in the inner corner, pinched by the canthral ligament
  2. Pre-tarsus fat: Rare, sits on the eyelid (as seen in actor Steve Buscemi)
  3. Lateral fat: On the outer edge of the eye
  4. Upper eyelid fat (roof fat): Can impact the appearance of the upper eyelid
  5. Lower eyelid fat (souf fat): Important in aging, covers the cheekbone

These fat pads are held in place by the orbital septum, which weakens with age, leading to changes in appearance.

Eye Socket Form

The eye socket is not a flat plane. To depict its form:

  1. Use an S-curve to show the twisting of the socket
  2. The inner part of the socket recedes, creating more shadow
  3. The outer part becomes more prominent near the temple

Drawing Techniques

  • Use organic details like eyebrows to suggest underlying structure
  • Choose strategic points to change line direction, signaling plane changes
  • Balance between finding all planes and artfully hiding them in the final drawing

Aging and the Eye

As we age, several changes occur:

  • Fat pads shift and sometimes herniate
  • Underlying bone structure becomes more visible
  • Wrinkles form perpendicular to muscle movement

To depict an older eye:

  1. Show more prominent bone structure
  2. Indicate dropped fat pads, especially under the eye
  3. Add wrinkles sparingly and strategically

To de-age an eye:

  1. Raise the malar (cheek) fat
  2. Soften plane changes
  3. Reduce the visibility of underlying bone structure

Practical Application

When drawing eyes:

  1. Start with basic placement and construction
  2. Add structural details based on anatomy
  3. Consider the subject's age and adjust accordingly
  4. Use line weight and placement to suggest form without overworking

Remember, understanding these structures helps create more realistic and age-appropriate drawings, even when depicting younger subjects.
