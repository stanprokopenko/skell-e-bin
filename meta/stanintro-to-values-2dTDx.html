What Is Value?

In art, value refers to how light or dark something is. It's not about monetary worth; it's about the shades that create the illusion of depth and form. By adding values to your line drawings, you can transform them into three-dimensional artworks.

Lines vs. Values

Lines are useful tools for outlining shapes, but in reality, we don't see lines—we see light affecting forms. Lines are abstractions. What you truly perceive is an arrangement of colors and values filling in shapes.

The Components of Color

Color consists of three properties:

  • Hue: The color family, like red, blue, or yellow.
  • Chroma: The purity or grayness of a color.
  • Value: The lightness or darkness of a color, from white to black.

While all three are important, value is crucial for creating form and depth in your drawings.

The Building Analogy

Think of a drawing like a building:

  • Lines are the steel framework.
  • Values are the walls that fill in the framework, making it solid.
  • Texture and Color are the decorations that add feeling.

Values are essential—they create the illusion of form, depth, and volume.

Start with Values

As a beginner, focus on practicing with values before introducing color. This helps you understand light and form without the complexity of hue and chroma. It's tempting to jump into color, but mastering values first will greatly improve your art.

Organizing Values: The Munsell Scale

Artists use value scales to organize and communicate values effectively. The Munsell scale ranges from 0 (black) to 10 (white). While reality presents an infinite gradient of values, breaking it down into steps makes it more manageable.

You don't have to use all values in every drawing. You might choose a limited value range to create specific effects, like using only darker values for a moody atmosphere.

Light on Form and Local Value

The values we see are determined by two key factors:

  1. Light on Form: How light affects an object's planes. Areas facing the light are lighter; areas turning away are darker.
  2. Local Value: The inherent lightness or darkness of an object, regardless of lighting.

For example, a red apple and a yellow apple under the same light will have different values due to their local colors. Focus on observing how light interacts with forms and local values to accurately depict your subject.

* * *

Understanding and mastering values is essential for creating realistic and three-dimensional art. By concentrating on values, you'll improve your ability to portray form and depth, setting a strong foundation before diving into color. So grab your pencils and start exploring the world of values!
