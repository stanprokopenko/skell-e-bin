General Approach to Drawing Features

When drawing facial features, focus on a softer interpretation of features and their placement. Maintain the same parts perspective setup throughout the drawing. Use hatching primarily to maintain a sense of play rather than heavy light and shadow.

Drawing the Eye

  1. Place the eye shape and consider moving the nose if necessary.
  2. Consider the canthal ligament and how it attaches.
  3. Think about the center split and how it interacts with the eye.
  4. Start with an average plane and build up or down for thickness.
  5. Wrap a band across a ball or a long box across a ball, developing a corner on it.
  6. Combine the plate, fat, and skin to create a fuller shape.
  7. Observe how light and shadow react to the fuller shape.
  8. Interpret the shape as a spiral or twisting plane pushing back into the face.
  9. Consider the top of the brow area and its exaggerated structure.
  10. Look for the eyebrow and think about it in perspective.
  11. Move across the face and adjust features like the keystone and saddle.
  12. Finish the eye by placing the ellipse of the iris and pupil.
  13. Use a target to place the gaze and ensure eyes are aligned.
  14. Design the lower lid to reflect the presence of the eye.
  15. Connect plane and value to lead into shading.

Drawing the Nose

  1. Transition to the nose by considering the drop back of the socket and the curve up to the saddle.
  2. Understand the plane of the nose and its cartilage.
  3. Use highlights to indicate plane changes.
  4. Develop the nose by considering the septum and the under plane.
  5. Think about the malar fat and how it spreads across the face.
  6. Consider the denture sphere and its form line.
  7. Observe the sharp change at the end of the nose and the highlight area.
  8. Work inside the under plane of the nose and consider the septum's flow.
  9. Use lines to respond to the plane and avoid overthinking.
  10. Develop the nostril and its depth and thickness.
  11. Make plane differences clear to show the buildup of perspective.
  12. Consider cast and form shadows in light and shadow relationships.

Drawing the Mouth

  1. Think about the parts of the mouth and their relation to other features.
  2. Relate the mouth to the denture sphere and cheek.
  3. Develop the philtrum shape and its relation to the ball.
  4. Consider the lower lip's forward and back movement.
  5. Think about the chin's perspective form and its projection.
  6. Push the chin in and curve it for a less boxy look.
  7. Revisit and adjust features for proportion and placement.

Additional Considerations

  1. Consider larger deposits of fat and their impact on the face.
  2. Design the ear with basic shapes and planes.
  3. Think about the ear's internal structure and its relation to the head.
  4. Address contours from the head to the neck.
  5. Check shapes around the mouth and consider muscles of chewing.
  6. Use the masseter muscle to add form to the back of the jaw.
  7. Develop an understanding of surface and exaggeration of features.
  8. Prepare the drawing for more concrete ideas of lighting and rendering.
  9. Focus on connecting the understructure, surface development, and architecture of the skull.
  10. Build an understanding of the form of features and their complex surfaces.
