Breaking Down Figures into Interconnected Shapes

Copy Code

In this lesson, I dive into the part-to-part based approach to gesture drawing, inspired by Hampton's Dom Riley method. If you haven't tried your hand at this method yet, I encourage you to give it a shot with the provided images before reading further.

Modifying Basic Shapes

When starting with the head, I often adjust the basic shape to better match the model's features. Instead of the average head shape, I might round it out or soften certain lines to capture the unique body type. This early modification helps in pushing towards a more accurate description without turning it into caricature.

Connecting Parts for Spatial Understanding

Next, I focus on the neck and shoulders, ensuring they're connected in a way that makes spatial sense. I consider the overlaps and wrapping lines to establish depth and perspective. The torso is approached with gestures that reflect its lean or twist, and I might adjust its shape to reflect the model's physique, such as giving it an hourglass figure.

Utilizing Perspective and Overlaps

Adding perspective-driven lines across the forms helps in conveying volume. For instance, when drawing the torso, I use three light lines to represent its dimensionality. Overlaps are crucial, they show which parts are in front and which are behind. This adds to the three-dimensionality of the figure.

Drawing Arms and Legs with S and C Curves

When drawing limbs, I decide between using S-curves and C-curves or combining straights and curves. For example, an arm might start with an S-curve to capture its flow. I pay attention to the relationship between parts, ensuring that the upper arm connects naturally to the forearm and so on. The legs might involve arrow shapes or pinch forms, depending on their position and movement.

Addressing Foreshortening by Breaking Up Parts

Foreshortening can be challenging, but by breaking up the limbs into parts, such as separating the upper and lower legs, it's easier to convey depth. Shortening the length of a part and opening the wrapping lines can suggest that it's coming towards or away from the viewer.

Comparing with Previous Gesture Methods

I find that this part-to-part approach allows for a more deliberate and slower mindset compared to the fluidity of traditional gesture drawing. It helps in understanding the pose more deeply, especially with regards to perspective and spatial relationships. Unlike the six-line Riley figure, which I always struggled to memorize, simplifying it into tubes and shapes makes it more approachable.

Practice and Self-Assessment

I encourage you to practice this method and compare your attempts with mine. Pay attention to how you attach one part to the next and how the overlaps and perspectives affect the overall pose. This approach can significantly enhance your ability to set up figures quickly and accurately for more detailed drawings.

Remember, the key is to understand the connection between parts and how they move in space. By focusing on these relationships, you'll develop a stronger foundation in figure drawing.
