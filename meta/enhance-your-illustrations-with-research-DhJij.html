Enhance Your Illustrations with Research

Copy Code

In this article, I'll walk you through my process for creating a Spirited Away fan art piece. I'll share how I approached research, initial sketches, thumbnails, and the steps I took to finalize the illustration.

Research and Initial Sketches

Before diving into the final piece, I spent time familiarizing myself with Miyazaki's art style and the characters from Spirited Away. I hadn't drawn anything from this film before, so I needed to understand the design elements and aesthetics.

I started by sketching the characters, even though my first attempts didn't look great. It's important to push through early sketches that don't meet expectations. By continuously drawing, I began to get more comfortable with the characters and the style.

I focused on drawing Haku, the dragon character, as I found him fascinating, cool and a bit scary, but not frightening. Practicing these sketches helped me capture the mood I wanted for the piece.

Exploring Ideas and Inspiration

As I sketched, I allowed myself to explore different characters and elements. Sometimes, drawing without a concrete plan can lead to unexpected inspiration. I was reminded of other characters and scenes that I enjoyed, and I considered including them in my artwork.

This phase is also about evoking a feeling. For me, it was about channeling the emotions and atmosphere from Spirited Away and personal experiences that resonated with the film.

Thumbnails and Composition

Next, I moved on to creating thumbnails to plan the composition. My initial idea was a horizontal orientation focusing on Sen in a close-up shot. However, I realized that I wanted to include more characters and Easter eggs in the piece.

I experimented with different poses and compositions, eventually deciding on a scene where Sen and Haku are flying over a night market. This idea was inspired by a personal experience at the Raohe Night Market in Taiwan, which reminded me of Spirited Away both in atmosphere and in a humorous incident where we lost and found family members.

Line Art and Flats

With the composition decided, I moved on to the line art. I didn't start with a pencil sketch, instead, I went straight into drawing the final lines. My goal was to create a piece that looks like a Spirited Away scene, but also includes elements from other Studio Ghibli films.

In the flat color stage, I was already thinking about the color palette. I wanted to keep the colors cohesive, so I chose colors that were close to each other in tone. For background characters, I adjusted their colors to fit within this palette, avoiding a rainbow effect that might distract from the main focus.

Adding Lighting and Shading

I then added lighting and shading, bringing depth to the illustration. By applying a highlight pass, I unified the elements with a consistent light source, the glow of the night market. I used gradients to draw the viewer's eye toward the center, and to balance the composition.

At this stage, I also adjusted the values to ensure that the main characters stood out against the background. It's crucial to control contrast to guide the viewer's attention.

Finalizing the Piece

In the final version, I further refined the color palette to enhance the nighttime atmosphere. I compressed the background colors to a middle-tone purple, allowing the main characters to pop. The line art served as the darkest value, defining the forms amidst the subdued colors.

My aim was to create a piece that feels cohesive and captures the essence of Spirited Away, while also including subtle nods to other Studio Ghibli films. From a distance, it appears as a straightforward scene, but upon closer inspection, fans can discover the hidden details.

* * *

Creating this piece was a journey of combining personal experiences with inspiration from the film. By infusing my own memories and emotions, I felt more connected to the artwork, and I believe that connection translates to the viewer.
