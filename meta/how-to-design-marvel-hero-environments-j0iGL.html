How to Design Marvel Hero Environments

Copy Code

It’s time to make some environments. You have a solid understanding of basic perspective and you’ve seen how Daniel Warren Johnson takes those core skills and transforms them into creative environments. Now it’s your turn to make your own!

Interior Environments

Create an interior space owned by a Marvel hero. Think about what you can do to give this space personality and a lived-in feel. Choose one of the following options:

  • Tony Stark’s high-tech workspace where he can test his latest Iron Man armor prototype.
  • Scarlet Witch’s study room where she meditates and researches the occult.
  • Matt Murdock’s law office in New York City.

Include the hero associated with this room to help set the scene and so we understand the scale of the environment. Remember to:

  • Think on the character: Consider their personality and how it influences the space.
  • Make a map: Plot the space out before choosing the shot for your final illustration.
  • Choose the shot: Decide on the best angle and composition for your final piece.
  • Scope the small details: Add elements that make the space feel lived-in and authentic.
  • Have research handy: Use references to ensure accuracy and depth in your design.

Exterior Environments

Draw one of the exteriors of a Marvel Universe location listed below. Make sure you draw the environment at a different angle from any references you see here or online. If you’re just copying another artist’s interpretation, you won’t be practicing all the skills you need for this assignment.

  • Avengers Mansion
  • Doctor Strange’s Sanctum Sanctorum
  • The Baxter Building, headquarters of the Fantastic Four
  • The Daily Bugle

Full Scale Environments

Draw the exterior of one of these locations from the Marvel Universe. This will be a more zoomed-out view than our previous focus on just the building:

  • Atlantis, the underwater kingdom.
  • Golden City, the capital of Wakanda.
  • Krakoa, a living island for mutants.
  • Castle Doom in Latveria.

Remember to think carefully about the shape, size, and readability of all these environments. Work out the composition in at least three different thumbnails before you choose your shot. If the image works at the size of a postage stamp, it will work when you move to the full-size panel. Keep Daniel’s perspective tips in mind before you start, because that will make or break your drawings.
