How to Achieve Realism with Three Values

Copy Code

Welcome! In this lesson, you'll learn how to paint using three values only - a white, a mid-value, and a black. These aren't exact, there can be some variation between them. The key point is to have three distinct values that you can easily distinguish. By matching these simplified values accurately, you will inevitably achieve a realistic impression. This method streamlines the painting process, and ensures success if you follow it.

Simplifying Your Subject with Three Values

When faced with a complex reference photo, it's easy to feel lost and confused. To make things simpler, start by turning your photo black and white. This reduces distractions and narrows down your focus to values instead of colors. But we'll take it one step further by reducing the number of values in the image.

Using Photoshop to Reduce Values

Here's how you can simplify your reference photo using Photoshop:

  1. Convert the Image to Black and White: This eliminates color and lets you focus on value.
  2. Go to Image > Adjustments > Posterize: This tool reduces the number of values in the image.
  3. Choose the Number of Values: Decrease the value to simplify the image. Start with two and adjust accordingly.

When posterizing, look for the number of values that keeps the story intact. For example, two values might be too simple, but three values often tell a good story. You can experiment with four values and see if it makes a significant difference.

Sampling Your Values

Once you've chosen the number of values, sample them:

  • White: This will be your paper white.
  • Mid-Value: A middle-of-the-road value, not too dark or light.
  • Black: Your darkest value.

Remember, you don't have to stick to pure black or white. The most important thing is having three distinct values.

Preparing to Paint

Mixing Your Three Values

Use a neutral color like Payne's gray, but any color will work as long as the values are correct. Mix your paints to create:

  1. White: Leave the paper untouched.
  2. Mid-Value: Achieve this by using a thin consistency of paint with plenty of water.
  3. Almost Black - Mix a very dark value, you won't need much since these are smaller sections.

Starting with the Main Subject

Begin by focusing on the part of the painting that interests you the most. For instance, you might start with a car that's the center of attention. By starting here, you can build everything else to support it.

The Painting Process

Painting the Mid-Values

  • Observe Your Reference: Use the simplified image to determine where your mid-values are.
  • Connect Shadow Sections: Paint the areas that share the same value in one go.
  • Leave Highlights: Skip areas where you see highlights to let the paper white show through.
  • Focus on Shapes: Think in terms of abstract shapes rather than specific details.

Adding the Darkest Values

Once the mid-values are in place:

  • Add Details with Dark Values: This includes windows, tires, and other dark elements.
  • Simplify Background Elements: For distant cars or objects, simplify them by using your dark value and leaving a few highlights.
  • Use Shapes to Define Forms: Don't worry about lines, focus on filling in the shapes you see.

Painting the Ground

  • Adjust the Ground Value: Darkening the ground can give more meaning to the highlights and bring the whole painting together.
  • Mix Enough Paint: Ensure you have enough mid-value paint to cover the ground.
  • Avoid Scrubbing: Apply the wash gently to prevent reactivating previous layers.

Enhancing Mid-Values with Context

By adding darks and adjusting the ground, the mid-values start to make more sense:

  • Add Small Details: Include indications of bricks or patterns with simple strokes.
  • Limit Detailing: Only add details where necessary to avoid overcomplicating the painting.

Final Touches

Adding Dark Details for Depth

  • Paint Windows and Shadows: Use your darkest value to bring out these elements.
  • Create Contrast: The darks will enhance the highlights and mid-values.
  • Use a Bigger Brush: This helps cover larger areas and keeps the process efficient.

Incorporating Highlights

  • Use Gel Pens or Opaque Paint: If you lose a highlight, you can bring it back with these tools.
  • Add Thin Highlights: Small touches can make a big difference in realism.

Managing Shadows and Edges

  • Add Cast Shadows: This frames the scene and adds depth.
  • Soften Edges if Needed: You can loosen up edges to blend shapes together.

* * *

By following this process, you can create a realistic painting using just three values. This method simplifies the complex task of painting and provides a reliable blueprint for achieving realism. Remember, while there are more skills to learn, like color, temperature, and composition, this approach gives you a solid foundation. You now have a foolproof method for creating the sense of realism you desire. Use it, build upon it, and make it your own.

When you're ready, move on to your next lesson and keep practicing!
