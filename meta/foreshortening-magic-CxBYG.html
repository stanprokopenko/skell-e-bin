Welcome back! This week, we're diving into foreshortening, one of my favorite ways to draw. Foreshortening brings rhythm and dynamism to figures, moving away from stiff poses to ones that are frolicking and full of life. Shorter distances in foreshortening make it easier to navigate proportions. Let's explore the wonder of drawing with rhythm and end with some flights of fantasy.

Drawing with Rhythm and Broad Strokes

Techniques for Using the Pencil

I like to use the underside of the pencil to create broad strokes. Here's how:

  • Sharpen the pencil in a curved manner.
  • Place all fingers and palm on the page.
  • Glide across the page with your hand close to the surface.
  • Use the broad side of the pencil for mass and the tip for fine lines.

Drawing this way frees you up to make beautiful marks quickly. The broad strokes allow you to capture the gestures and mass of the figure efficiently.

The Beauty of Gestural Drawing

Gesture Over Accuracy

When drawing foreshortened figures, proportions can be tricky. But that's the beauty of it, nobody can call you out on incorrect proportions because they're difficult to measure in foreshortening. Accuracy isn't the key to beautiful drawings here. Instead, focus on the rhythm and gesture. I prefer gestural drawings over strict academic ones because they convey more life and energy.

Drawing with rhythm allows you to connect different parts of the figure smoothly. Think of curves followed by straights, and how forms overlap and wedge into each other.

Approaching Complex Poses

Managing Foreshortened Figures

Foreshortened and twisting figures can be challenging but rewarding. Here's how I approach them:

  • Start with key features, like the head or torso.
  • Use simple shapes to build the figure.
  • Think about how forms overlap and interlock.
  • Don't worry about accuracy, enjoy the exploration.

If you find yourself struggling, don't hesitate to go back and practice earlier stages. Take notes on what you find tricky and focus on strengthening those areas.

Building Confidence Through Practice

Overcoming Challenges

Drawing is about practice and persistence. If you're feeling unsure, remember:

  • There are no bad drawings , each one is a learning experience.
  • Drawing without erasing lets you see your exploratory lines.
  • Confidence grows with each stroke you make.
  • Draw at your own pace , don't rush to keep up with others.

The Value of Traditional Art

Human Touch vs. AI Art

In today's digital age, the value of traditional art stands strong. While AI can produce impressive images, it lacks the human touch that makes art special. Knowing that a human hand crafted each line adds soul and connection to the artwork. Traditional art remains valuable not just monetarily but emotionally.

Developing Your Unique Style

Learning from Other Artists

I've been influenced by many artists, like Andrew Loomis, Jeff Watts, John Buscema, and Frank Frazetta. Studying their work helps me grow, but it's important to bring your own style into your art. When learning from others:

  • Acknowledge your influences.
  • Use their techniques as a foundation.
  • Infuse your work with your unique aesthetic.

Don't just copy, interpret and adapt what you learn to develop your own voice.

* * *

Drawing should be a joyous and fulfilling experience. Embrace the challenges of foreshortening, and gestural drawing. Push boundaries and don't be afraid to break the rules. The more you draw, the more confident you'll become, and the more your unique style will shine through. Remember, every line you make is your line, you're in charge of your art. So pick up that pencil, and let's create something wonderful together!
