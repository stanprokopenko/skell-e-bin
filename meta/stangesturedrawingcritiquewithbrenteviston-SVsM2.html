What is Gesture Drawing?

Gesture drawing is a simplified form of figure drawing that captures the action of the pose. It's about creating an armature that represents what the figure is doing, using simple lines and shapes. Our goal is to convey motion, energy, and the essence of the pose without getting bogged down in details.

Why is Gesture Drawing Important?

Gesture drawing is the essential first step in figure drawing. It helps us simplify the complex human figure and establish the foundational action before adding details. By starting with gesture, we can:

  • Prevent stiffness in our drawings
  • Capture the figure's vitality and movement
  • Avoid proportional mistakes that are harder to fix later

Key Principles of Gesture Drawing

Simplicity

We should keep our gesture drawings simple. Use as few lines as possible to represent the pose. Simplifying allows for easy adjustments and prevents us from getting attached to incorrect proportions or details early on.

Capturing Action

Focus on the movement and direction of the figure. Use action lines to depict the flow of the pose. These lines are not about the contours of the body but represent the direction limbs are moving.

Practicing Gesture Drawing

Mindset for Practice

  • Draw freely and expressively: Gesture drawing is our place to experiment.
  • Don't make the drawings precious: They're not meant to be perfect or finished.
  • Focus on the process, not the outcome: Each drawing is practice for the next.

Drawing from Life and Photos

While photos are helpful, drawing from life pushes us to capture poses quickly and embrace the fleeting nature of action. Live models can't hold poses indefinitely, encouraging a dynamic drawing approach.

Exaggeration and Believability

Exaggeration can make our drawings more dynamic and lifelike. Surprisingly, aiming to exaggerate often leads to capturing the pose more accurately:

  • Accuracy is overrated: A perfectly accurate drawing can appear stiff.
  • Exaggerate up to about 10%: Pushing curves and angles slightly enhances the sense of movement.
  • Maintain believability: Even when exaggerating, ensure the figure could exist realistically.

Common Beginner Mistakes

Starting with Details

Avoid beginning with anatomical details or shading. Focus on the overall action first. Adding details too early can make it difficult to adjust proportions and can lead to a stiff drawing.

Being Too Rigid

  • Stiff drawings lack life: Drawings that are too precise can appear lifeless.
  • Overcoming the tendency to stabilize: We often subconsciously stiffen dynamic poses. Intentionally exaggerate to counteract this.

Tips for Dynamic Drawings

Leave Pentimenti (Process Lines)

  • Embrace multiple attempts: It's normal to have several lines before finding the right one.
  • Don't erase early lines: Keeping them shows our thought process and adds vibrancy.

Use Variations in Line Speed

  • Draw lines at varying speeds: Faster lines can convey energy, but it's not about rushing.
  • Lines drawn more quickly feel different: They contribute to the sense of motion.

Think of the Figure as Alive

  • Consider functionality: Ensure our drawing represents a figure that could move and live.
  • Avoid impossible anatomies: Exaggerate within the limits of a believable human form.

* * *

Gesture drawing is a fundamental skill that brings life and movement to our figure drawings. By focusing on simplicity, capturing action, and embracing exaggeration, we can create dynamic and lifelike drawings that serve as a strong foundation for more detailed work.
