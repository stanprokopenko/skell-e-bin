How to Combine Structure and Gesture

Matter and Energy

I always think of structure as the solid, three-dimensional form, like the architecture of whatever I'm drawing. I pair that with gesture, which is the fluid, lifelike energy flowing through everything. I call it the matter and energy of art. It's a balance of corners and curves, form and fluidity. This resonates with how our universe is built, matter plus energy. When I respect both in my drawing, I get the solidity of life and the flowing grace of life.

Why Connection Matters

Art’s superpower is its ability to connect ideas. For example:
- Foreground can connect to background through linear perspective.
- Light and shadow connect through gradual changes in value.

These strategies help me convey depth and unity. No matter how complex something is, I can find ways to connect the big shapes with subtle transitions.

Simple Shapes and Curves

To make drawing more approachable, I distill it to a few childlike ideas. I often start with simple geometric shapes, such as boxes, tubes, eggs, or teardrops, to represent an arm, a torso, or any object. Then I add gesture by curving the long axis of those shapes. I connect structure and gesture early, so the drawing has both solidity and movement.

When I first draw a part of the body, I replace it with a basic three-dimensional form and then give that form a curve that suggests how it moves. Having both shape (for structure) and curve (for gesture) keeps it lively and believable.

Seeing Like an Artist

Reality can look incredibly complex. I’m not born with the ability to see the underlying shapes and curves, it’s something I wire into my brain through practice. Anyone can learn this. It’s the same idea as learning a sport or a musical instrument. Consistent exposure and effort help me see the world in simpler terms, and I can draw it with clarity.

Building Value

Once I have my structure and gesture in place, I can start playing with value, light and shadow. I like to keep it simple by making shadow shapes darker, and light-side shapes lighter. I’m not worrying about every law of light right away. Just blocking in shadow shapes and light shapes gives me a strong illusion of form. Later, I might refine those transitions or add half-tones and subtle gradations. But I don’t need all that complexity at the start.

Putting It All Together

My process is about layering simple ideas: 1. Draw a gesture line or curve along the long axis.
2. Add structure using basic forms (two-dimensional or three-dimensional).
3. Block in the shadows and lights with simple value shapes.

When my structural forms feel solid, and my gestures feel fluid, the drawing starts to look alive. From there, I can refine details, push realism, or stylize my work. It all begins with connecting gesture and structure. That’s the foundation I build on each time I draw.
