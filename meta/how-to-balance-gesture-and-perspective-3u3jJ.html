How to Balance Gesture and Perspective

Copy Code

In this critique session, we focus on gestural architecture drawings, specifically distorted houses and mansions. The key challenge lies in balancing creative distortion with maintaining a convincing 3D form. This balance is crucial for creating visually interesting and structurally sound architectural designs.

Maintaining 3D Form in Distorted Drawings

When creating distorted architectural drawings, it's essential to maintain clear plane changes and corners. This helps preserve the three-dimensionality of the structure, even when pushing the boundaries of realistic representation. Avoid creating flat shapes that lack depth. Instead, use overlaps to indicate depth and maintain the illusion of a 3D form.

Example: Saschu's Work

Saschu's drawing demonstrated an intentionally bloated effect. However, it lost three-dimensionality due to the absence of clear corners and plane changes. This resulted in the drawing appearing as flat shapes rather than a 3D form. To improve this, we demonstrated how to bloat a house while maintaining its 3D form:

  • Push the roof corner in the opposite direction to maintain foreshortening
  • Consider the entire form, not just individual lines
  • Use overlaps to create depth

Balancing Gesture and Structure

In gestural architecture, aim for a balance of 80% perspective and 20% gesture for buildings. This ratio helps maintain structural integrity while allowing for creative expression. It's important to follow perspective rules while adding organic tilts and angles.

Example: Lasuli's Submission

Lasuli's work was praised for achieving a good balance between gesture and structure:

  • Followed perspective rules while adding organic tilts and angles
  • Maintained clear plane changes and tapering
  • Incorporated fun details that adhered to the perspective

Deforming Forms vs. Individual Lines

When distorting architectural forms, focus on deforming entire forms rather than individual lines. This approach helps maintain the overall structure and believability of the design.

Example: Matias' Work

In Matias' submission, the middle house lost its form in an attempt to implode it. The right house maintained perspective but had lines deformed independently. We advised:

  • Deform forms, not just individual lines
  • Maintain the overall structure of the building

Subtle Deformations for Personality

Subtle deformations can be highly effective in giving personality to architectural designs while maintaining believability.

Example: Johannes' Submission

Johannes' work was highly praised for its subtle yet effective deformations:

  • Fun details in roof tiles and wooden boards
  • Subtle deformations gave personality while maintaining believability
  • Achieved a storybook feel

Handling Extreme Distortions

Even when pushing distortions to the extreme, it's possible to maintain depth and 3D structure.

Example: Jayoshi's Work

Jayoshi's castle drawing pushed distortion to the extreme but still worked effectively:

  • Maintained depth and 3D structure despite heavy distortion
  • Spent 7-8 hours on each drawing, showing dedication to the craft

Common Issues and Solutions

Several common issues emerged during the critique:

  • Random distortions losing clarity
  • Inconsistencies in the thickness of elements
  • Losing perspective in melted appearances

Example: Diana's Submission

Diana's work illustrated some of these issues:

  • Houses looked melted, losing too much perspective
  • Unclear forms in some areas
  • Tree house was interesting but difficult to show perspective due to melted appearance

To improve, we suggested:

  • Maintain clear plane changes
  • Use overlaps and thickness variations to indicate depth

General Advice for Improvement

To improve your gestural architecture drawings:

  • Focus on deforming entire forms, not just individual lines
  • Maintain clear plane changes and corners for a 3D effect
  • Balance between gesture and structure (80% perspective, 20% gesture for buildings)
  • Use subtle deformations to create personality
  • Consider the entire form when making distortions
