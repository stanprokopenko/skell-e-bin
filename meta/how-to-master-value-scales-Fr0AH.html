Difficulty in Distinguishing Adjacent Tones

Many of you mentioned that it's hard to see the difference between adjacent mid-tones. Is it normal to struggle with this? Yes, it is. There are several reasons for this:

  1. Practice Makes Perfect: It takes time to train your eyes to see subtle differences in value. This assignment is designed to help you adjust your mind to perceive minor subtleties.

  2. Physiological Adjustments - Your eyes physically adjust to different lighting conditions. Throughout the day, you're exposed to a wide range of values, from the brightest lights to the darkest shadows. When you focus on a smaller range of values in your drawing, your eyes need time to adjust.

  3. Compressed Mid-tones in Your Drawing: If your mid-tones are too similar, it's harder to distinguish between them. This often happens when you don't use the full value range in your scale.

Importance of Using the Full Value Range

One common issue is not establishing your full value range, especially the darkest darks. When you don't go dark enough, you compress all your mid-tones into a smaller range. This makes it difficult to create distinct steps between values.

Establishing Value Zero and Value Five

  • Value Zero: Start by achieving the darkest dark possible with your medium.
  • Value Five: Familiarize yourself with the true middle value. Practice shading a value five on a new piece of paper to solidify it in your mind.

By anchoring your scale with the darkest dark and the middle value, you ensure that your values are spread out evenly across the full range.

Overcoming the Fear of Dark Values

Many artists have a fear of going too dark. Even a value three can feel overwhelmingly dark on the page, especially if you haven't established your darkest value yet. You might worry that you can't erase it or adjust it later.

Remember: Being comfortable with dark tones is essential. Don't be afraid to stretch your value range all the way to black. It's better to have the full range available than to limit yourself to lighter tones.

Paper and Pencil Considerations

The Impact of Paper Texture

The texture of your paper can greatly affect your shading:

  • Textured Paper: Rough surfaces can cause your shading to appear noisy or grainy, especially with soft pencils. The grooves in the paper prevent the graphite from filling in evenly.
  • Smooth Paper: Provides a more even surface for shading, making it easier to achieve smooth tones.

Choosing the Right Pencils

Using a range of pencil hardness can help you achieve the full value range:

  • Hard Pencils (H, 2H): Good for lighter values.
  • Soft Pencils (2B, 4B, 6B): Essential for darker values.

Tip: If you're using a soft pencil on textured paper, consider switching to smoother paper or adjusting your technique to achieve the desired effect.

Practicing with Mid-Tones

It's important to get comfortable with mid-tones:

  • Practice Hitting Value Five: Shade a piece of paper to match the true middle value. This helps you understand where your mid-tones should be.
  • Adjust Your Values: Be willing to go back and darken areas that are too light. It's normal to adjust your values multiple times.

Patience and Persistence

Creating value scales can be time-consuming and requires patience. Don't rush the process. Take your time to adjust your values properly. It's normal to feel frustrated, but remember that each adjustment improves your ability to see and render values accurately.

Working with Different Mediums

Challenges with Charcoal

Charcoal can be harder to control than graphite:

  • Softness of Charcoal: It's very soft and releases a lot of pigment, making it difficult to achieve lighter values.
  • Paper Choice: Charcoal works best on soft, pillowy papers like newsprint. Avoid using it on regular sketchbook paper if you're struggling with control.

Tips for Charcoal:

  • Use the overhand grip to shade with the side of the charcoal.
  • Keep your charcoal pencils sharpened well.
  • Practice on appropriate paper designed for charcoal.

Importance of Proper Lighting

Lighting plays a significant role when drawing and photographing your artwork:

  • Consistent Lighting: Ensure your workspace is evenly lit to see your values accurately.
  • Artificial vs. Daylight: While daylight is great, you can use high-quality artificial lights with good CRI ratings to maintain consistency.
  • Avoid Reflections: When photographing graphite drawings, be mindful of glare and reflections that can alter the appearance of your values.

Adjusting Images Post-Processing

If your photos aren't capturing your artwork accurately:

  • Use Editing Software: Adjust the levels in applications like Photoshop to correct lighting issues.
  • Maintain Value Relationships: Be careful not to overexpose or underexpose parts of your image during editing.

Getting Comfortable with Dark Values

It's essential to overcome the hesitation of using dark tones:

  • Test Your Limits: Try pushing your values darker than you're comfortable with to see how it affects your work.
  • Balance: Find the right balance between avoiding values that are too light and not going too dark too fast.
  • Develop Confidence: The more you practice using the full value range, the more confident you'll become.

Summary of Key Points

  • Use the Full Value Range: Don't be afraid to go all the way to black.
  • Establish Reference Values: Set your darkest dark and middle value to anchor your scale.
  • Practice Seeing Subtle Differences: Adjust your values incrementally to train your eyes.
  • Choose Appropriate Materials: Select the right paper and pencils for the effect you want.
  • Be Mindful of Lighting: Proper lighting helps you see and capture your artwork accurately.
  • Be Patient: Take your time to get the values right. Adjustments are a normal part of the process.

Remember, improving your value scales is a journey. Each time you practice, you're training your eyes and hands to work together more effectively. Keep experimenting, stay patient, and you'll see progress over time.
