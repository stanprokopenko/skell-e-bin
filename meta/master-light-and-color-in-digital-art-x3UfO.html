Adding Specular Highlights

I think of lighting typically in two ways: matte finish and specular highlights. The matte finish is our base, and the specular highlights represent the shininess of the object that's reflecting the environment around it, often a light source.

For example, we can quickly make the eyelid look shiny by adding a specular highlight. That's what everyone wants to draw, the fun thing that really makes things pop. But without an underlying understanding of the form, it won't make any sense and it might not look quite right.

We're looking for similar planes that are going to reflect the light. So, the plane on the eyelid might be similar to planes on the nose, the corner of the lip, or the cheekbone. This is how you start to get a cohesive feeling.

Not everything is going to be super shiny, but there are parts of the face I like to punch up with specular highlights, like the inner eye section or the eyebrows. Understanding Light Interaction and Subsurface Scattering

Everything that a light source touches becomes a light source itself, and it also takes on the properties of that thing. For example, light reflected from skin becomes a skin-colored light. That's going to reflect onto other areas, like under the chin, where we might get more saturated colors.

That's a little tip that has really helped me make light feel that little bit more realistic. Instead of just doing a white light under the chin, which can wash it out, I'd like to see something way more saturated.
