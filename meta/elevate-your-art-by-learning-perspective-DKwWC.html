Learning from Modern Artists

Let's explore how early 21st-century artists work and learn a few lessons from them.

Wes Wheeler: Blending Drawing and 3D Modeling

Wes Wheeler is a 3D artist who draws before modeling, then draws over the models to make them look drawn again. This approach cheats time and benefits the viewer. Importantly, he works out multiple options before committing to one.

Understanding 3D Modeling and Perspective

Building 3D models requires knowledge of a 3D program, not necessarily perspective. However, the program uses perspective, so it's important to think like a 3D program. Learning to sketch with 3D awareness enhances your skills.

Polina Ristova: Combining Traditional and Digital Methods

Polina Ristova designed Dracula's carriage by studying funeral cars, drawing thumbnails, and creating orthographic drawings. She then built it in Maya to make her own custom reference and drew a new version on paper. She says, "We use 3D programs to get to our result faster, not as an excuse not to learn the basics."

Vance Kovacs: Exploring Options with Digital Tools

Vance Kovacs, a concept artist for movies, often starts with rough sketches to work out composition and perspective choices. He explores options and doesn't settle for the first try. Digital tools allow him to track stages and adjust as needed.

Israel Sanchez: Different Approaches to Perspective

Israel Sanchez works in illustration, children's books, advertising, comics, and TV animation. His work shows two approaches to perspective: traditional perspective that looks right and whimsical perspective that appears childlike. Knowing perspective allows you to defy it when appropriate, enhancing the desired effect.

Choosing Your Approach

Decide how you work based on what you're trying to achieve. Whether you want your art to look playful or convincing, working in stages helps solve one problem at a time. Professionals like Israel, Vance, Polina, and Wes are not held back by lack of skill because they've built a strong foundation.

Dan Fraga: Mastery Without Planning

Dan Fraga demonstrated drawing without any planning, showcasing his skill developed over years in comics. While impressive, this off-road approach is dangerous for beginners.

* * *

Remember, we're here to learn perspective. Focus on building your skills first. Mastery leads to masterpieces later. Skills come before creating great art.
