The Make or Break Phase in Drawing

Copy Code

Starting with Big Shapes

When drawing a portrait, I begin by focusing on the big shapes. I like to work from big shapes to small shapes, starting with an envelope or scaffolding shape. This helps me establish everything that's going to be included in the drawing.

I ensure the height-to-width ratio of my initial shape matches that of my subject. Taking quick measurements, I compare the width and height to maintain accurate proportions.

Separating Hair and Face Shapes

Next, I separate the hair shape from the face shape, thinking of them as silhouette one and silhouette two. I keep these shapes general, avoiding details like individual strands of hair. Instead, I aim for a big, general Lego hair wig shape.

Establishing the Center Line and Features

I then find the center line, separating the left and right sides of the face. I look for comparison measurements, such as how distances on one side relate to the other.

I divide the face into perfect thirds:

  • Brow Line: Top of the eyes.
  • Nose Line: Middle third.
  • Chin: Bottom third.

Using rhythms popularized by Frank Riley, I incorporate placeholder lines for features without drawing them in detail. These rhythms help simplify the head's structure.

Adjusting for Specificity

With the basic structures in place, I adjust the contours to make the drawing look more specifically like the subject. I look for specific angles, slight tilts, and placements of features like the ears and mouth. For example, the mouth often falls about a third of the way from the bottom of the nose to the chin.

The Two-Value Phase

Moving into the two-value phase, I squint at the model to simplify the values into light and dark shapes. Using the side of my pencil, I keep it very general and focus on what I see when I squint. This phase is crucial because it defines the drawing, it is the make-or-break phase where you can't let anything slide.

Refining with Additional Values

After establishing the two values, I start dividing the dark and light shapes into two or three more values. This helps me build up to a five or six value drawing, which is usually enough. I avoid over-rendering and focus on making a powerful statement with the values I choose.

Artistic Influences and Advice

Some of my significant artistic influences include Jeff Watts, Phil Hale, Glen Orbik, and James Bama. They've shaped my approach to drawing and painting.

For aspiring artists unsure about their professional path, I recommend focusing on the fundamentals and developing a broad skill set. Trust the path and listen to those who have walked it before you. Remember that achieving greatness often requires significant sacrifices.

Final Touches

As I near the end of the drawing, I focus on the focal point, often the eyes, and add more detailed values there. I might build up the halftones and push into a full value range, especially around the focal point to draw attention.

Finally, I remove some of the construction lines that may age the subject and refine any highlights or details needed. I aim to create a balanced drawing that captures the essence of the subject without overworking it.
