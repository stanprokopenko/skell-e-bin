How to Draw 3D Letters in One-Point Perspective

Project: Simple Extrusion on the Z-Axis

Your task is to put your name in perspective. There are two ways to approach this project, and it's recommended to try both.

Method 1: Precise Illustration

Draw your name using one-point perspective to create a 3D effect. If using your name feels too self-focused, choose the name of someone you appreciate. Here's how:

  • Use a ruler for accuracy.
  • Draw each letter twice: once for the front plane and once for the back plane.
  • Connect corresponding corners to create the illusion of depth.
  • Think of it like drawing two squares and connecting them to form a cube.
  • Determine the size of the back letters by drawing lines to the vanishing point.

Method 2: Rapid Iterations

Practice by sketching your name or any shapes freehand, without a ruler.

  • Complete 20 quick sketches this week.
  • If you enjoy it, aim for 100 sketches over the next two weeks.
  • Experiment with different vanishing point placements to see various effects.
  • This method helps you strengthen your idea-generating muscles and improves freehand line skills.
  • Use any shapes or letters, the goal is to understand perspective, not lettering.

Understanding One-Point Perspective

Limits of One-Point Perspective

  • Placement of the vanishing point affects the drawing:
    • Too close to the center: the extrusion may not be visible.
    • Too far to the side or above/below: creates distortion.
  • When distortion occurs, it may be necessary to introduce a second vanishing point.

Tips for Working with Vanishing Points

  • Work larger to manage lines converging at the vanishing point.
  • Lighten your lines as they approach the vanishing point to reduce clutter.
  • Be aware that with long words or shapes, lines at the ends become almost horizontal, making them tricky to draw with a ruler.

Enhancing Your Skills

  • Completing finished illustrations helps you become comfortable with drawing instruments.
  • Experiment with vanishing point positions:
    • Above or below introduces vertical positioning (eye level).
    • Left or right introduces lateral positioning.
  • Understanding these concepts prepares you for more complex perspectives, like five-point perspective.

Inspiration and Next Steps

  • Explore different alphabets and typefaces for ideas. Dover Publications offers excellent resources.
  • In our next lesson, we'll explore drawing objects with no perspective. Start thinking about simple to complex objects you'd like to draw.
  • For now, focus on extruding letters into space and enjoy the process of bringing flat shapes into the third dimension.
