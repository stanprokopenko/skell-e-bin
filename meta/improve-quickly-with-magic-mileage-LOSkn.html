Embrace Your Role as the Artist

You've been drawing all your life. It's time to take agency and embrace the fact that you are the boss of your artwork. Confidence in your hand comes from recognizing the skills you've developed over time. Remember, sometimes not knowing can lead to a refreshing take on art. Keep what works for you, discard what doesn't, and see what you gain from continuous practice.

Learn by Observing and Interacting

When you engage with other artists, your skills can skyrocket. Watching and listening to those who know more can teach you invaluable lessons. But remember, you bring something unique to the table. Sharing your perspective enriches the artistic community and helps everyone grow together.

Understanding Anatomy Without Over-Reliance

Start thinking about taking agency in your drawings. Move away from relying on arrows and indications for landmarks. Instead, find them yourself. Get acquainted with the terminology: acromion process, sternum, iliac crest, and more. By internalizing these concepts, you'll strengthen your understanding and improve your drawings.

The Iliac Crest and Body Perspective

When the body moves away from you, the iliac crest becomes a significant feature. Think of it as the top of a tilted tub. From different angles, it appears as a curve or an arc. Understanding this helps you capture the body's depth and form accurately.

Facial Features and Angles

Drawing faces, especially from challenging angles like near-profile views, can be tricky. Remember to think in halves and keep features aligned:

  • Eyes, nose, and lower lip should be aligned.
  • The upper lip and eyebrows can change angle.
  • The muzzle denotes youth. Add softness to the face where needed.

Depicting Breasts Accurately

Breasts are often misunderstood in figure drawing. They are soft, malleable, and affected by gravity. Remember:

  • They change shape depending on the body's position.
  • They're not always symmetrical.
  • Observe how they wrap around the ribcage and hang when the body leans forward.

Overcoming Challenges in Drawing Hands

Hands can be complex, but understanding their structure simplifies the process.

Avoiding the "Catcher's Mitt" Hand

Be cautious of drawing hands where all fingers are equal and lack gesture. Instead:

  • Fan out the fingers for rhythm.
  • Use the longest finger as a reference point.
  • Think of the hand as a mitten with sticks coming off it.

Utilizing Tools and References

Consider using an articulated hand model to understand tricky positions. This can help you visualize and twist the hand into the desired pose.

Use Your Tools Wisely

Keep your tools well-maintained. A dull pencil hampers your drawing ability. Also, prefer kneadable erasers over hard ones, especially on fragile surfaces like newsprint. They gently lift graphite without damaging the paper.

Embrace Asymmetry and Lost Edges

Asymmetry adds interest to your drawings:

  • Use thick and thin lines next to each other.
  • Incorporate lost edges to create depth.
  • Remember that asymmetry can be in value, line, and shape.

Handling Mistakes and Building Confidence

Celebrate your successes and learn from what's not working. Don't fixate on mistakes. If you're struggling:

  • Take a break and return with fresh eyes.
  • Work on something you're good at to boost confidence.
  • Remember, every artist has off days.

The Importance of Taking Breaks

Drawing requires mental energy. Give yourself time to recharge:

  • Step away when needed.
  • Breaks help prevent frustration.
  • You'll often return with new insights.

Finding Inspiration and Using References

Draw inspiration from various sources:

  • Movie stars can serve as templates for characters.
  • Observe how actors age to understand changes in anatomy.
  • Use references, but don't let them control your drawing. At the end of the day, focus on your artwork.

Sketching and Filling the Page

Experiment with sketch jams, where you fill a page with multiple sketches:

  • Helps overcome the intimidation of a blank page.
  • Encourages creativity within confined spaces.
  • Allows you to play with composition and flow.

Value of Community and Continuous Learning

Surround yourself with like-minded artists:

  • Join studios or art classes.
  • Share insights and learn from others.
  • Even professional artists continue to learn and attend classes.

Embrace the Journey of Artistry

Remember, drawing is a continuous journey. Be fearless in your approach:

  • Enjoy each drawing, regardless of the outcome.
  • Practice leads to improvement.
  • Your style will evolve over time.
