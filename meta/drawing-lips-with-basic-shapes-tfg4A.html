Drawing Lips with Basic Shapes

Copy Code

In this session, I'm going to do three gesture drawings from reference images of people with a variety of ethnic backgrounds and age demographics. The goal is to explore different lip types and shapes, and to share my process of approaching gesture, especially when it comes to mouths.

Gesture Drawing 1: Front View with Goatee

Starting with the Denture Sphere

I like to begin by drawing an egg shape, almost like an elongated sphere. This represents the denture sphere, where the teeth are. The lips are not flat, they wrap around the roundness of this denture sphere. Including this helps me keep the perspective of the lips in mind, as they wrap around.

Establishing the Mouth

Next, I roughly indicate the corners of the mouth and connect them with a straight line. I go in very soft, focusing on nailing the tilt of the lip line. In the reference image, the lips are tilted upward along with the nose, so I make sure to capture that with the initial line.

I then roughly establish the lip line where the lips meet, and add a little dash to show the center of the upper lip. This also acts as the beginning of the midline that I use to divide the face in half vertically for symmetry.

Defining the Lips

I establish the cupid's bow, noticing that the lip line is a bit more flat and doesn't match the cupid's bow as much as in previous models. There's a lot of variation with lips, so it's good to memorize the canon so you know what you're deviating from. This helps when you're trying to push certain features in caricatures or characters.

I rough in the middle line of the lower lip and pretend I can see that upside-down keystone shape below the lower lip line, adding a bit of value there. I put the upper lip in shadow, and add little circles to suggest the donut hole indentation of the corners of the mouth.

Simplifying Facial Hair

The model has a goatee. Instead of drawing individual hairs, I simplify the overall silhouette of the goatee before breaking it up into smaller shapes. Once that's done, it's easier to start adding smaller details. I recommend simplifying complex textures like hair into larger shapes first.

Adding Facial Features

I indicate the septum, a little tie shape, that feeds up into the base of the nose. I draw a horizontal line to find the base of the nostrils, and I make sure it matches the perspective of the mouth. I block in the nasolabial folds, and I give thickness to the base of the wings of the nose.

Even though the lip line seems pretty flat, I still break up that straight line slightly with a suggestion of the middle tubercle. The plane turns inward quicker with his lips, it is not as rounded as on previous models.

I punch that line just above the goatee, and notice a gradient in the goatee where it's darker towards the bottom. I roughly indicate that as the planes turn away from the light source, with lines feeding into the jowls.

Refining and Correcting

I break up the goatee into clumps rather than individual hairs and erase some construction lines to clean up the drawing. I add a ball to suggest the volume of the chin, and add a few modeling lines to give it more form.

I realize I made his nose too wide and consequently made his right cheek too wide, so I adjust that. It's important to constantly check your proportions and make corrections as needed.

Key Features to Note

  • Philtrum: Feeds up into the septum.
  • Dark accents at the corners of the mouth.
  • Cupid's bow.
  • Tubercles.
  • Nasolabial fold.
  • Upside-down keystone shape: Recedes back and feeds down into the top of the chin.

Gesture Drawing 2: Profile View of an Older Woman

Starting with the Denture Sphere

Again, I start with a simple denture sphere. From the profile, the lips have an overall slanted orientation, so I add a little mark to get started.

Establishing the Lips

I find the lip line, noting that the bottom lip recedes further back behind the upper lip. I make sure to capture that. I add the upside-down keystone shape. She has a rounder chin with more fat, so we don't see that sharp edge that the skull usually provides.

Adding Facial Features

I indicate the corner of the mouth and lightly block out the bulb of the nose. The philtrum runs up towards the septum of the nose, which is that middle part between the nostrils. From the profile, when the head's not tilted, you can see a decent portion of that nostril being revealed.

We have the alar fat forming the wing of the nose, and her nose is a bit more bulbous, so I add more volume to that. Remember, the bulb is comprised of two pieces of the greater alar cartilage.

I go back to the top of the upper lip and add the cupid's bow, which swoops back into the corner of the mouth. I hold that in shadow.

Capturing Age and Weight

She has more pronounced jowls, being older, with a bit more weight and fat. I add the cast shadow on the lower lip, and feed it into the core shadow there. Her chin is not very round, so I rough in some value there. I add the core shadow on her cheek, and I'm about done with this one.

Adding the Neck and Finishing Touches

I add her neck, and can see the maxilla feeding into the nose. I block out the shape of the cheek and throw that into shadow. I darken the drawing a bit more to add contrast, making the neck and other areas in shadow pop.

Key Observations

  • Mouth Placement: The side of the mouth doesn't go back that far. If you draw a line from the back of the nostrils to the corner of the mouth, it's not that far back. It typically lines up close to the front of the eye. Amateurs might draw the mouth going too far back, which looks unnatural.
  • Cupid's Bow - Has the end flattening out, feeding into the corner of the mouth. The line steps up, slips up, and comes down a bit. Its overall orientation is an upward angle, the profile of the mouth.
  • Philtrum and Nasolabial Fold: The philtrum feeds up to the septum. The crease of the nasolabial fold feeds down into the jowls. Heavier models or older ones will have a more pronounced nasolabial fold. Thinner and younger models have less definition, blending more with the muzzle and cheek.
  • Overall Flow: Remember the overall flow of the mouth shape from the profile, it steps in, comes out, steps in, and comes out a bit, then recedes back to the neck.

Gesture Drawing 3: Profile with Fuller Lips

Starting with the Nose and Denture Sphere

For our final gesture, I start off with the nose and then draw a light denture sphere to give me a place to work off of.

Establishing the Lips

I lightly lock in the general direction of the shapes of her lips. She has a very subtle smile, with the corners of the lips turned up slightly. The perspective is top-down slightly because we can see the upper lip overlapping the lower lip.

Drawing a line connecting the bottom and upper lip, I see they're fairly close in vertical alignment. Her chin recedes back quite a bit before meeting the bottom plane of the jaw.

Emphasizing Tubercles and Overlaps

You can really see the tubercles, they are very pronounced. The tubercle on the upper lip that's closest to us, overlaps the middle tubercle. Remember, they're soft ovoid volumes. I add a core shadow because that side of the lip falls into shadow.

There's a slight plane that cuts in and recedes back, and we have the front of the middle tubercle. I pull that core shadow forward a bit more and block in the area of occlusion for the corner of the mouth.

Capturing Lip Shapes and Planes

Her upper profile has more of an S-curve, so I capture that. Her cupid's bow has a different shape this time, with a longer tail that arches back at a more flattened slope into the corner of the mouth.

There's a receding plane under the lip, which I cast into shadow, along with a cast shadow under the nose. I'm trying to get the thickness of the wing of the nose.

Here, we can see the planes turning inward, getting sucked into the corner of the mouth. There's a slight indication of where the wing conjoins with the ball of the nose, so I indicate that.

Key Details to Note

  • Thickness of the Wing of the Nostril: Always indicate this, we don't want paper-thin nostrils.
  • Septum: There's a little plane that upturns slightly and can catch more light. It's good to accentuate or downplay this as needed.
  • Cupid's Bow: This one has a longer tail, arching back into the corner of the mouth.
  • Lateral Tubercle Overlapping the Center Tubercle: Adding this overlap adds depth and volume to the lips.
  • Upper Lip Overlapping the Lower Lip: This overlap also helps add depth.
  • First Plane Under the Lower Lip: It recedes back into the top of the chin.

* * *

These gesture drawings help us observe and capture the variety of lip shapes and features across different individuals. By paying attention to anatomical landmarks, overlaps, and planes, we can add depth and realism to our drawings of mouths.

Now, we're ready to move on to the color render, and then onto the most exciting part, the full portrait, where we'll apply everything we've learned to the whole face with all its features.
