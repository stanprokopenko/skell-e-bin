Transform Your Drawings with Values

Values are awesome! If you want your drawings to look three-dimensional and create the illusion of form, you need to start adding values to your line drawings. Lines are great and they get us really far, but learning how to effectively use values can transform your art. Later, when you combine them with edges, it will feel like magic!

When we talk about value in art, we're not referring to its 'worth' or how much money it costs. A value is simply how light or dark something is. If we take a photo and make it grayscale, you'll see that the skin is lighter than the lips. Even though the color of the lips and the shadows are quite different, their values are much more similar, they're both kind of dark.

Value and Color

We learned a lot about lines, but I hate to break it to you, lines aren't real! We don't see lines. As artists, we use lines as a placeholder for the edges of shapes. Lines are a very useful tool for communicating ideas, but they are not what we actually see. It's an abstraction of what we see. ** In reality, we see light as it affects forms. We see that light as an arrangement of colors**, shapes filled in with colors.

Color is made up of hue, chroma, and value.

  • Hue is the family of colors it belongs to: yellow, red, blue, and so on.
  • Chroma describes how pure or gray the color is. You can lower the chroma on a blue color and it's still blue, but it's lower in chroma, a little more gray.
  • Value is how light or dark the color is, from white to black.

You can make any color lighter or darker by shifting its value. The relationship between these three properties of color can get complicated, and that's beyond this course. The most important part of color is value, at least for artists it is. Value is so important, actually, that artists often categorize it separately.

Let's imagine that a drawing is like a building. Each individual steel beam is like a line. We put lines together to make shapes, and that's like the steel framework of the building. Values are like the walls. ** They fill in the spaces between the framework, making it solid. Values create the illusion of form, depth, and volume. Beyond that, you have things like texture and color, which are like the decorations in the rooms, wallpaper, furniture. They're details that add to how your art feels.

All these elements are important. If you take any one of them out, you don't really have a building you would want to live in. But the walls, the floor, the roof, those are really important.

As a beginner, it's wise to practice drawing without color at first. Just practice using value to focus on light and form. Once you're comfortable with values, then you can introduce the other elements of color to add more emotional impact. I know it's really tempting to jump to color, but if you want the time you spend practicing to have the biggest impact, start with values.

Value Scale

Imagine a scale from 0 to 10, where 0 is the darkest black and 10 is the brightest white. This can seem a bit counterintuitive if you think of value as pigment. When we draw, we start with white paper and add darker values with the pencil. But in this course, we'll be borrowing from the Munsell Color System. It's a traditional way of organizing color and value. Instead of measuring pigment, we're measuring light.

When we're drawing, it's not very useful to think of the pigment. It's much more practical to think about the subject we're drawing and how the light affects that subject. A value of 10 means that area is getting the most light, while a value of 0 means it's in complete shadow and we can't see anything.

In reality, the value scale is a smooth gradient with an infinite number of values between black and white. But trying to work with infinity is not very practical for drawing and organizing values. That's why artists typically break it down into steps of values, usually about 10. This is more manageable and makes it easier to communicate. If I say "value number 3," you can imagine where that is on the scale.

You might have noticed that 0 to 10 is actually 11 total values in the Munsell value scale. Some people squish it to 10 and flip the order. But these numbers are just systems. The most important thing to understand is that there is a gradation from white to black, and you can split that into as many steps as you want.

You don't always have to use all 10 values in every drawing. Sometimes, you might choose to work with a limited value scale. Maybe you decide to use 5 values, just enough to represent the main elements of light and shadow.

You can also play around with different value ranges within the scale. Maybe your drawing only uses values from 3 to 8, with no deep shadows or bright highlights. Or you could go for a moody drawing using only darker values from 0 to 5.

We'll learn more about value ranges in another lesson and we'll practice with projects. Our goal is to train your eye to see these subtle shifts in value and then translate them into something you can work with on paper.

Sources of Value

There are four things that determine the values we see:

  1. Local Value
  2. Light on Form
  3. Surface Material
  4. Atmosphere

For beginners, I highly recommend focusing mostly on those first two, Local Value and Light on Form. The second two are a little bit more advanced, and you should come back to them later. Surface Material is what the object is made of, it could be matte or glossy or highly reflective, and that could greatly change the values that we see even if the light and the form are the same. Atmosphere refers to the effect of the air on how we perceive things far away. I mentioned this earlier in the course as atmospheric perspective.

Local Value

The value of my hair is darker than the value of my skin, that's local value. It's the inherent lightness or darkness of the thing, regardless of the light you shine on it. The local value of something isn't always consistent, and flat. An apple might have a lot of variety in its local value, and a general gradation from light to dark.

Light on Form

Even though my hair is darker than my skin, the value of the shadow on my skin is darker than the highlights on my hair, that's light on form. When you shine a light on something, it makes it brighter. The value goes up because there is more light. But the value doesn't go up evenly on the entire object. It gets distributed based on the form, where the planes are facing. The part of the object that faces towards the light gets affected the most. But as these planes start to turn away, they get affected by the light less, and so they get darker.

Local value and light on form act together. Both of these apples are hit by the same light, but the light side of the red apple is darker than the yellow apple. And the same with the shadow sides. The value of the light and shadow are both shifted based on the local value.
