Overview

I've been exploring filters and iterations to reshape forms, and see their potential. Now I want to push this further with what I call mashups. A mashup is simply taking a shape or form, like an egg, box, or tube, and creatively blending it with another form. This helps me evolve my shapes organically, without having to understand every architectural detail of the subject.

What Are Mashups?

A mashup means I start with one idea, like a tube, and modify it by adding or subtracting shapes. Maybe I carve out a notch or add a box halfway. I transform a basic shape into something more characteristic. I can decide to stay two-dimensional in some areas and turn three-dimensional in others. It’s all about playful mixing.

Combining Shapes for the Head

Let’s say I’m drawing a head and start with an egg for a face. It might feel too simple. I could: - Add another egg behind it for the back of the head.
- Evolve that into a boxy shape if the hairstyle has sharper angles.
- Turn it into a tube and cut off sections to shape the skull.
- Mash up the head by blending a tube with a box near the back, creating a partial box where it needs more structure.

This approach keeps the design flexible. I can shift from 2D silhouettes to 3D forms and back as needed. If it becomes too complicated, I simplify again by reverting to a basic shape.

Hands and Other Body Parts

I use the same idea for hands or any other body part. I might start with a simple two-dimensional shape for the palm, then mash up a boxy ending for the knuckles. If I want more definition, I round off one side to suggest a bulge and leave the other side sharply angled. Sometimes I add balls for the knuckles or even a “chew bone” shape for the fingers. If it gets overwhelming, I dial back to the most basic form and try again.

Evolving Forms in 2D or 3D

I can switch between a purely 2D silhouette or a clearly 3D construction whenever I want. I might carve out or add bulges as if I’m a sculptor. This gives me endless possibilities to explore shape, proportion, and gesture. If I need a stronger gesture, I might push a long curve on one side and keep the other side straight.

Iterations and Extremes

One powerful way to use mashups is to vary different filters: - Shape: egg, box, tube, or hybrids.
- Value: shift from light to dark.
- Color: go from warm to cool.
- Proportions: stretch one dimension or compress another.

I can gradate from simple to complex, from big forms to small forms. Sometimes I push extremes in one area and keep another area tame. This keeps my designs playful and imaginative.

When In Doubt, Simplify

If I hit a roadblock, I remind myself to go back to basics. Whether it's lines, shapes, or forms, simplification resets my focus. Then I can slowly add those more interesting, characteristic touches again.

* * *

Mashups let me experiment with my drawings in small, manageable steps. By pairing simple shapes and forms, I can create fresh variations that tap into both 2D and 3D thinking. I encourage you to try out these shape mashups on heads, hands, and the entire figure. Have fun and let each mashup spark the next idea!
