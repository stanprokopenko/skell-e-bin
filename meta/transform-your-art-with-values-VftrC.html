The Importance of Value

Values are essential in making your drawings look three-dimensional and creating the illusion of form. While lines are useful, learning how to effectively use values can transform your art. A value is simply how light or dark something is.

Value and Color

In reality, we see light affecting form, represented by colors made up of hue, chroma, and value.

  • Hue: The color family, like yellow, red, or blue.
  • Chroma: How pure or gray the color is.
  • Value: The lightness or darkness of a color from white to black.

For artists, value is the most important component of color.

The Value Scale

Imagine a scale from 0 to 10, where 0 is the darkest black and 10 is the brightest white.

  • Munsell Value Scale: A system measuring light to organize color and value.
  • Artists break down values into steps to make them easier to manage.

You can use a limited value scale in your drawings, using fewer values to represent light and shadow.

Value Sources

Four factors determine the values we see:

  1. Local Value: The inherent lightness or darkness of an object.
  2. Light on Form: How light affects an object based on its form.

As a beginner, focus on local value and light on form.

Local Value

Local value refers to the inherent value of a thing, regardless of lighting. For example, hair is typically darker than skin.

Light on Form

Light affects an object unevenly based on its form. Parts facing the light become lighter, while parts turning away become darker. Together with local value, this creates the illusion of depth.

Practice Tips

Start by practicing drawing without color. Focus on using values to represent light and form. Once you're comfortable with values, you can introduce color to enhance your art.
