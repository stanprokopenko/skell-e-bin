Seeing Subtle Differences in Mid-Tones

Many of you mentioned difficulty in distinguishing subtle differences between adjacent mid-tones. This is normal. With practice, your eyes will get better at detecting slight value changes. Remember, our eyes physically adjust to different value ranges. When you're working on your drawing, give your eyes time to adapt to the values on your paper.

Establishing a Full Value Range

A common issue I noticed is not establishing the full range from darkest dark to lightest light. Often, there's a big jump from the darkest value to the next, making mid-tones compressed and hard to distinguish. To fix this:

  • Start by achieving the darkest possible value your medium allows.
  • Familiarize yourself with a middle value (value five).
  • Ensure each step between values is consistent.

By doing this, you'll have more room to distribute your mid-tones evenly.

Avoiding Big Jumps in Value Steps

Pay attention to the steps between your values. If the jump from one value to the next is too big, especially from your darkest value to the next, you'll have trouble distinguishing the mid-tones. Aim for even, subtle transitions between each value.

Using the Right Materials

Your choice of paper and pencil affects your shading:

  • Soft pencils on textured paper can lead to noisy, uneven shading.
  • If you want smooth tones, use smoother paper or adjust your pencil hardness.
  • Be aware that charcoal pencils are softer and harder to control on regular paper.

Overcoming the Fear of Going Dark

It's common to feel hesitant about using darker values. Many of us avoid going too dark, fearing it's irreversible or too bold. However, using the full value range, including the darkest darks, adds depth and contrast to your drawings. Don't be afraid to push your darks.

Patience in Shading

Shading requires patience. If you find yourself getting impatient, remember that taking your time leads to better control and smoother transitions. Even if it feels tedious, the effort is worth it.

The Role of Lighting

Proper lighting is crucial when drawing and photographing your work:

  • Good lighting helps you see values accurately.
  • When taking photos of your drawings, ensure your light is even to avoid reflections and shadows.
  • Artificial light can be as good as natural light if you use quality bulbs.

Techniques for Charcoal and Graphite

Working with charcoal can be challenging:

  • Charcoal is softer than graphite and can be harder to control, especially on regular paper.
  • Use softer, pillowy paper like newsprint for charcoal to get lighter values.
  • Sharpen your charcoal pencils properly for better control.

Creating Values with Ink and Markers

With mediums like ink and markers, you have to create values differently:

  • Use texture and line density to simulate different values.
  • Experiment with hatching, cross-hatching, and other mark-making techniques.
  • Remember, it's about the ratio of paper showing versus ink.

Photographing and Scanning Your Artwork

To present your artwork well:

  • Adjust levels in photo editing software to represent your values accurately.
  • Be mindful of reflections, especially with graphite.
  • Ensure your lighting setup minimizes glare and shadows.

Practice Makes Perfect

Remember, improving your value scales takes practice. Don't be discouraged if you need to adjust your values multiple times. Each correction helps you train your eye to see subtle differences. Keep pushing yourself, and you'll see progress.
