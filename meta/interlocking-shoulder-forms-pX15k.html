Overview

I’m focusing on the deltoid mass and its interaction with surrounding muscles on black paper. This approach helps me practice both topographic lines (wrapping lines that show form), and halftones (showing subtle value transitions). Working on black paper forces me to think in reverse, using white pencil for highlights and allowing the black to serve as shadows.

Deltoid and Trapezius

The deltoid has three heads, front (anterior), middle, and rear (posterior). When the arm lifts, each head overlaps slightly, creating pockets or wedges in the figure,

  • Rear Deltoid: Slips further back, creating a pocket behind the middle deltoid.
  • Middle Deltoid: Sits on the top and sides of the shoulder, often showing unique muscle striations in a flexed pose.
  • Acromion Process: Bony landmark where the deltoid and trapezius meet near the top of the shoulder.

The trapezius (trap) is prominent in shoulder movement. It bunches and compresses when the arm lifts, forming a bulge from the neck to the shoulder:

  • As it travels up toward the skull, the trapezius leaves a column of raised muscle that you can follow visually with halftones.
  • The seventh cervical vertebra sits in the small depression between the two trapezius columns at the base of the neck.

Scapula and Rotator Cuff

Beneath the deltoid and trapezius lie the scapula (shoulder blade) and a group of rotator cuff muscles (infraspinatus, supraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis). When the arm lifts, the scapula fans around the ribcage:

  • Fanning Movement: This provides a critical range of motion for the arm. Tightness or injury (like “frozen shoulder”) can limit this sweeping motion.
  • Teres Major & Minor, Infraspinatus: These lie close together on the back of the shoulder blade. Often, subtle shadow shapes indicate where they attach.

Drawing Technique on Black Paper

I typically layer white pencil for highlights, letting the black paper serve as the shadow. Key points:

  • Halftones: I soften or lighten certain transitions so the black remains a subtle shadow.
  • Topographic Lines: Wrapping lines follow the curves of the trapezius, deltoid heads, or scapular muscles, revealing the form’s 3D structure.
  • Building Up Highlights: I use different softness levels of white pencils (like Prismacolor and Verithin) to push certain muscle shapes forward.
  • Lost and Found Edges: Sometimes I let an edge fade into the black paper if the light dissipates there.

Observations and Tips

  1. Stay Loose at First: My underdrawings are often rough, because I know I’ll spend more time refining and designing on top.
  2. Use Personal Knowledge: Past injuries or physical therapy can deepen your understanding of scapular motion and rotator cuff function.
  3. Constant Learning: No school covers everything, so integrate knowledge from diverse experiences (like CPR training or athletic pursuits).
  4. Value Families: Keep half tones in the light family lighter than any shadowed areas. This separation maintains the illusion of form.
  5. Experiment with Lines: Sometimes I draw cross-contours or “draw-through lines” that wrap over the shape, purely because they look engaging and clarify form.

Shoulder Close-Up Example

When viewed from above, I see the top of the middle deltoid with a tiny sliver of the rear deltoid behind it. The trapezius can roll over and obscure part of the rear deltoid:

  • Traps Compress: When the arm is lifted or the shoulder is shrugged, the trapezius “bunches up” near the neck.
  • Deltoid Highlights: Each head of the deltoid may have different highlights and halftones, showing a “pinching and rolling” effect.
  • Peck/Deltoid Intersection: Where the pectoralis major runs into the deltoid, there’s often a small separation or crease, visible as a distinct halftone boundary.

Practical Application

I first used this black paper/white pencil approach working on movie poster comps. Even though most work now is digital, practicing with traditional media:

  • Sharpens hand dexterity and tonal control.
  • Forces you to think about form rather than relying on layers or photo manipulation.
  • Helps you see design opportunities (like showing rolling forms or striations in creative ways).

* * *

Working on black paper is a powerful exercise for learning shoulder anatomy, particularly how the deltoid, trapezius, and scapula interact. By mapping highlights and halftones, I train my eye to see form continuity and muscle transitions more clearly. If this feels too advanced, doing the same studies in charcoal on toned or white paper is a perfectly fine step. But I encourage you to push forward, experiment with line direction, vary your pencil pressure, and continually integrate new information about shoulder anatomy into your figure drawings.
