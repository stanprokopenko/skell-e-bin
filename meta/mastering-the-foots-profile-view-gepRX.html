Mastering the Foot's Profile View

Copy Code

I want to focus on how the foot looks and behaves from a side view. When we consider the foot in profile, especially the weight-bearing foot, we need to understand how it grounds and supports the rest of the body. Whether I’m carrying groceries, hiking with a backpack, or just standing in place, the weight funnels down into the foot’s structure and disperses out through the arch.

The Silhouette

From a profile, the foot’s outer shape (or silhouette) stays similar whether I’m looking at the little toe side or the big toe side. However, there is a key difference with the ankle bones (malleoli): - On the big toe side, the malleolus (tibia) sits higher.
- On the little toe side, the malleolus (fibula) sits lower.

This side-to-side change affects how the arch looks. From the front, if I could slice off the toes and just see the mid-foot, I’d notice the arch forming a half-circle. That arch carries the load from above and helps distribute the weight out toward both sides of the foot.

Weight and Compression

The arch is more than just a curve. It’s a shock absorber. When I place my weight fully on the foot, the arch compresses slightly: - The foot flattens where it contacts the ground.
- The little toe side can spread out more to form a small platform.

When the foot isn’t carrying much weight, like in a seated pose, it can cup a bit because the arch isn’t forced down. Sometimes I even see a peek of the foot’s underside from the side view if the arch hasn’t compressed.

Three Key Pieces

I like to break the foot into three pieces: 1. The main wedge (mid-foot), connecting to the ankle and leg.
2. The toes, often grouped together like they’re wearing a sock.
3. The heel, a smaller wedge behind.

From the side, I imagine those wedges with right angles and trimmed tips. The ankle bone can be high or low depending on which side we’re seeing, and the arch can appear or stay hidden based on whether there’s weight pressing down.

Stability and the “Tripod” Idea

The foot’s shape reminds me of a camera tripod in how it helps me balance: - The heel wedges back a bit, letting me rock backward without toppling.
- The toes and ball of the foot widen in the front to stand firmly.

Having two feet on the ground, often one forward and one back, keeps me stable from all sides. That’s why athletes plant their feet this way, for better support.

Subtle Cupping

A relaxed foot without direct weight doesn’t always lie flat. Often, it will cup up a bit: - The arch remains raised. - The little toe side might lift away from the ground.

This cupping disappears once all my weight bears down and flattens the foot.

Next Steps

That wraps up the profile view. Next time, I’ll turn the foot around and show you the back view, which can be intimidating because of how the form changes from behind. Understanding this final view is crucial to finishing any figure drawing with confident, well-constructed feet.
