Simplifying Values in Composition

When analyzing a composition, start by simplifying it into basic values. This helps you understand the underlying structure and visual hierarchy.

Using Two Values

Begin by breaking the image into two values: dark and light. For example, consider a composition with a dark silhouette of a horse and rider against a lighter background. The dark shape stands out prominently, creating a strong focal point.

  • Dark Silhouette: The horse and rider form a bold, dark shape.
  • Light Background: The surrounding area is a lighter value, allowing the silhouette to pop.

Introducing a Third Value

Adding a middle value introduces depth and complexity.

  • Middle Value Background: Most of the background shifts to a middle tone.
  • Lightest Light: A light cloud or dust cloud behind the focal point creates the most contrast.

Creating Dynamic Compositions

The Power of Diagonals

Incorporate diagonal shapes to add energy and movement.

  • Dynamic Silhouette: The dark shape moves diagonally, avoiding static verticals.
  • Avoid Symmetry: An unbalanced shape is more engaging and dynamic.

Exploring Negative Shapes

Pay attention to negative shapes, the spaces around your subject.

  • Background Shapes: Observe the shapes formed by the background between limbs and body parts.
  • Silhouette Design: Use these shapes to enhance the overall composition.

Focusing on Abstract Shapes

When sketching thumbnails, think in terms of abstract shapes rather than detailed forms.

Simplifying the Subject

  • Loose Mapping: Sketch lightly to adjust shapes easily.
  • Avoid Details: Don't worry about anatomy or small features at this stage.

Emphasizing Important Elements

  • Key Details: Include essential elements like the spear to maintain context.
  • Context Matters: Critical details prevent oversimplification that can lose the story.

Grounding the Subject

Connecting to the Ground Plane

Prevent your subject from appearing to float by grounding it.

  • Blending Feet: Merge the feet into the cast shadow to connect with the ground.
  • Value Gradation: Use a gradual shift from dark to middle values at the base.

Adjusting Background Values

  • Merging Values: Consider how the foreground and background values interact.
  • Ground Shape: Adding a darker tone at the bottom can ground the silhouette.

Experimenting with Values

Balancing Light and Dark

  • Value Relationships: Explore how dark, middle, and light values coexist.
  • Contrast: Use the lightest lights to create focal points.

Observing Artist Intentions

  • Shape Design: Notice how masterful compositions use shapes and values intentionally.
  • Small Shapes: Delicate details in small areas can draw the eye effectively.

Noticing Subtle Details

Value Adjustments for Clarity

  • Overlap Elements: Adjust values where elements overlap for clarity.
  • Spear and Hand: Making the spear lighter over the hand ensures it remains visible.

Enhancing Depth

  • Separating Forms: Use mid-values to separate parts of the subject, adding depth.
  • Foreground and Background: Subtle value changes can bring elements forward or push them back.

Embracing the Thumbnail Process

  • Experiment Freely: Thumbnails are for exploration, don't hesitate to test ideas.
  • Focus on Composition: Prioritize overall composition and value structure over details.
  • Refine Shapes: Simplify shapes to their essence for a stronger visual impact.

By concentrating on these principles, you can create compositions that are simple, elegant, and dynamically engaging. Remember, the goal is to convey the essence of the scene with clarity and impact.
