Head Construction Method

When constructing heads, it's crucial to understand the structural differences between various character types. This knowledge helps in creating accurate and varied character designs. The process involves laying in the basic shapes and then refining them to match the desired look.

Male vs. Female Likenesses

There are key differences in the head structures of males and females. For females, subtlety is essential. We avoid bony protrusions and prominent areas, focusing instead on softer and more delicate features. Males, on the other hand, have more angular and pronounced structures.

Exaggerated Examples

Using exaggerated features can help illustrate these differences. For instance, the "Handsome Squidward" character is an exaggerated example of masculine features, with a straighter jaw and more projection in the chin.

Modifying Lay-In for Different Styles

There are many opportunities to adjust the lay-in for various looks. Customizing head construction for likeness involves making corrections and adjustments throughout the process. This flexibility allows for a wide range of styles and character types.

Female Head Construction

For younger females, we aim for a less prominent bone structure. Here are some key points:

  • Forehead: Straighter and less angled.
  • Jaw: Subtle shaping with less angle difference.
  • Cheekbones: Less aggressive and more subtle.
  • Chin: Softer with a more passive movement back.
  • Malar Fat: Higher and fuller, concealing the skull.
  • Eyebrows and Forehead: Subtle and covered by fat.
  • Eyes: Less aggressive planes with more subtle wrapping lines.
  • Nose: Subtle with more curves.
  • Ellipse and Lips: More curves and less aggressive drops.
  • Buccal Fat: Noted for softer curves.

Male Head Construction

Male head construction emphasizes broader and more angular features. Key points include:

  • Jaw: Broader and straighter with more projection in the chin.
  • Cheekbones: Lower and more angular.
  • Forehead: More reclined and diagonal.
  • Brow: More exaggerated development.
  • Nose: Broader and more developed.
  • Eyes: Smaller due to bone development.
  • Cheek and Jaw: Wider and more vertical.
  • Denture Sphere and Mouth: Broader and more angular.
  • Super Ciliary Arch and Temple: More prominent.

Adjustments and Corrections

A process-oriented approach allows for corrections and mistakes. Making adjustments is a natural part of achieving an accurate likeness. This flexibility is crucial for refining the head construction to match the desired character.

Structural Differences

Understanding the structural differences is key. These include:

  • Jaw Angle and Width
  • Chin Angle
  • Brow Heaviness
  • Forehead Plane

Age and ethnicity also affect head construction, adding another layer of complexity to the process.

Hair Shape and Form

When constructing heads, it's important to think about the structure and form of the hair. This adds to the overall character design and helps in achieving a more complete and accurate representation.
