Overcoming the Challenge of Visualizing Different Angles

One common struggle is drawing something in one position, and then depicting it in other positions without looking at a reference. It's a difficult exercise, but with practice, we can learn to spin objects around in our minds and draw them from any angle. Early on, I was like everyone else, I drew from a particular angle and got comfortable with it, often relying on references for different views. Over time, I realized that to gain control, I needed to practice visualizing and constructing internally.

Starting with Simple Shapes and Stick Figures

When tackling this challenge, I focus on building from simple shapes. I often start with a stick figure that has an awareness of space. People say they can only draw stick figures, but that's actually a great starting point. Using basic lines and anchor points, I map out the gesture and proportions. This skeletal structure helps me establish the pose and spatial relationships before adding more complexity.

Practicing Drawing from Different Angles

I decided to sketch a humanoid creature in one pose, and then draw it from various angles. The intention isn't to produce a pretty illustration, but to explore and understand. I allow myself to be loose and intentional, not worrying about perfection. I start with the initial pose, using gesture lines and simple shapes to build the form. Then, I try shifting the "camera" to different positions, behind, to the side, from above, or below.

For each new angle, I consider the spatial relationships of the body parts. I think about what's in front, what's behind, and how overlapping occurs. Cross-contours help communicate the three-dimensional form, allowing me to perceive what goes back and what comes forward. It's essential to be aware of the ground plane and how limbs relate to each other in space.

Recognizing Weaknesses and Iterating

Not every sketch will be perfect, and that's okay. I might notice inaccuracies or things that don't feel right. It's important to recognize weaknesses and be willing to repeat sketches to improve. By doing multiple iterations, I get closer and closer to accuracy. Sometimes, I isolate specific sections to problem-solve a particular area without getting overwhelmed by the whole drawing.

The Importance of Sketching and Thumbnailing

In my process, I separate sketching, thumbnailing, and drawing. Sketching is about understanding something, it is the exploration phase. Thumbnailing is associated with sketching, but it focuses on exploring ideas and compositions. Drawing is taking the information understood and putting it into the final piece. By defining these stages, I can focus on each part of the creative process, without rushing.

Building a Visual Library

Over time, by practicing these methods, I've built a visual library in my mind, a Rolodex of different angles and poses. I think of the character or object as a puppet that I can manipulate, and I visualize limbs and joints from various points of view. This experience allows me to draw more freely, without having to construct everything meticulously each time.
