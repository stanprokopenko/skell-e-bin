How to Create Value Composition Thumbnails

Copy Code

Starting with a Basic Thumbnail

I'm diving into some value composition studies, focusing on thumbnail variations. I like to begin with a simple, "boring" thumbnail that's close to the reference photo. This acts as a warmup and helps me understand the major shapes and value groups without making significant changes right away.

I sketch out the big shapes, the head, the hands, and the general silhouette. I'm not concerned with details at this stage. I focus on simplifying and getting my simplifying muscles warmed up. I fill in the mid-tone background, ensuring it's not too clean or too messy. The goal is to communicate the value clearly, without spending too much time.

I pay attention to the dark areas, like the subject's shirt, which is very dark in the photo. I fill it in, recognizing that working small can be beneficial. It allows me to design shapes efficiently without getting bogged down by filling large areas.

Experimenting with Value Variations

In the next thumbnail, I decide to change the value of the shirt. Instead of keeping it dark, I make it lighter to see how it affects the composition. I introduce shadows within the shirt, like those under the collar and from the hands. I minimize contrast in certain areas to direct attention where I want it.

I also play with the background values, adding dark shapes behind the head to frame the subject. I adjust the values in the hair and face, deciding whether to keep them similar or differentiate them. I'm exploring how these changes influence the overall feel of the image.

Modifying the Background and Hair Values

I experiment further by making the hair lighter and the background darker to make the hair stand out. I fill the entire background with a dark tone, which creates a strong contrast with the light hair. I adjust the face's values, making it mostly mid-tones with fewer highlights.

I realize that the hands should be mid-value to prevent them from drawing too much attention away from the face. I'm aiming for a low-key drawing where the emphasis is on the hair and face, surrounded by darker tones.

Emphasizing the Hands

I want to explore a composition where the hands are more prominent. I consider increasing the contrast around the hands but don't want to diminish the importance of the face. I experiment by adding mid-tones around the hands to frame them without introducing too much contrast.

I adjust the shadow shapes to create a diagonal movement that leads the eye towards the hands. I play with the values, ensuring that the dark areas don't feel distracting or out of place.

Playing with Cropping and Composition

Finally, I focus on the cropping and framing of the composition. I add more space above the subject, which drops him lower in the frame and creates an interesting dynamic. Alternatively, I add more space below, bringing the hands to the center, which also works well.

I consider using a gradation in the shirt, transitioning from dark to mid-value, and adjust the background accordingly. I explore various shapes and edges, understanding that even with hard edges, I can imply soft transitions in value.

* * *

Through these thumbnail studies, I see how many directions a composition can take. By adjusting values, shapes, and cropping, I can dramatically alter the focus and feel of the piece. It's a process of exploration, and even though I could spend more time refining, these studies provide valuable insights into value composition.
