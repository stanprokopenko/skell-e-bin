Copy Code

I'm Eric Gist, though you might know me as EMGIST. Over the years, I've worked on various covers for Marvel, including Fantastic Four, Iron Fist, Wolverine, and Hulk. My career spans about 15 years in comics, with the last five focusing on Marvel projects.

My Favorite Covers

Choosing a favorite cover is tough because I like them for different reasons. My Hulk cover stands out because it features a classic fight between Wolverine and Hulk, with a twist—zombie Wolverine. It was exciting to blend action with the horror elements I'm known for.

The Fantastic Four cover was another favorite. It involved more storytelling, incorporating multiple characters and their family dynamics into a single image. Capturing their unique characteristics was a rewarding challenge.

My Path into Comics

From a young age, I was passionate about comics and wanted to draw interiors. I submitted work to companies regularly and collected plenty of rejection letters. Eventually, I realized I had a knack for single-image storytelling, which led me to focus on covers.

In 2016, I started working with Marvel, beginning with covers for the Haunted Mansion series. My first cover featuring a core Marvel character was for the anniversary issue of Crazy in 2019.

Developing My Style

I'm often recognized for my deep shadows, high contrast, and moody, noir-inspired themes. I'm drawn to darker, atmospheric imagery that conveys emotion and storytelling. Lately, I've been branching out into brighter pieces, especially when working with characters like the Fantastic Four or Iron Fist, who aren't traditionally dark or moody.

I admire artists who balance iconic imagery with storytelling. Artists like Norman Rockwell, Bouguereau, Caravaggio, and Rembrandt inspire me. Their ability to create unforgettable images that also tell a deeper story is something I strive for in my work.

Advice for Aspiring Artists

Specialize or Be Versatile?

I recommend specializing at first. Master one area where your natural abilities shine. Once you're established, branch out and develop a well-rounded skill set. It's better to be exceptional at one thing than mediocre at many.

Getting Better at Making Covers

Study artists who excel at cover art, both contemporary and historical. Work on your fundamentals—figure drawing, anatomy, composition, color theory, and perspective. Most importantly, create covers. Make bad ones, learn from them, and keep improving.

Mastering the Fundamentals

Focus on mastering the figure and composition. A dynamic figure can carry a piece, even if other elements aren't perfect. Ensure you're competent in other areas so nothing distracts from your work.

Embracing Limitations

Limitations can breed creativity. Constraints force you to think creatively and find unique solutions. When working on covers, push yourself to come up with original ideas by doing multiple thumbnails. Often, your first ideas are influenced by others, so dig deeper.

Be Yourself

Avoid forcing a style. Let your style emerge naturally from your influences and experiences. Don't try too hard to be someone else; authenticity resonates more with audiences.

What I Wish I Knew Earlier

I wish I knew how good you have to be to succeed. When I was younger, I submitted work that wasn't ready, but that ignorance kept me motivated. Now, I understand the importance of continually improving, especially when my work sits alongside that of industry giants. Ultimately, our job is not just to create art but to sell comics, and being the best we can be helps achieve that.
