Transform Your Drawings with Values

Adding values to your line drawings transforms them, creating the illusion of three-dimensional form. While lines are great, values take your art to the next level.

What Is Value in Art?

In art, value refers to how light or dark something is, not its monetary worth. For example, in a grayscale image, skin might appear lighter than lips, even if their actual colors differ.

From Lines to Shapes Filled with Color

In reality, you don't see lines, you see shapes filled with colors. Color consists of three properties

  • Hue: The color family (red, blue, yellow).
  • Chroma: How pure or gray the color is.
  • Value: How light or dark the color is.

As an artist, value is the most important of these. Focusing on value helps you create depth and form.

The Importance of Value

Think of lines as the framework of a building, and values as the walls that fill in the spaces, making it solid. Values:

  • Create the illusion of form, depth, and volume.
  • Are essential for realistic drawings.

As a beginner, focus on drawing with values before adding color. This approach helps you understand light and form without the complexity of hue and chroma.

The Value Scale

Imagine a scale from 0 (black) to 10 (white). This is borrowed from the Munsell Color System. Although there are infinite values between black and white, artists simplify this into manageable steps, typically 10.

You don't need to use all 10 values in every drawing. You might:

  • Work with a limited value range (e.g., values from 3 to 8).
  • Choose values from 0 to 5 for a moodier effect.

The goal is to train your eye to see subtle shifts in value and translate them into your drawings.

Four Sources of Value

There are four factors that determine the values you see:

  1. Local Value
  2. Light on Form
  3. Surface Material
  4. Atmosphere

Focus on the first two for now.

Local Value

Local value is the inherent lightness or darkness of an object, regardless of lighting. For example, hair typically has a darker local value than skin.

Light on Form

When light shines on an object, it affects its value based on the object's form:

  • Planes facing the light are brighter.
  • Planes turning away are darker.

Local value and light on form work together. Two objects with different local values will reflect light differently. For example, a red apple and a yellow apple under the same light will have different values on their light and shadow sides.

* * *

Understanding and using values is crucial for creating realistic and engaging drawings. Practice focusing on value to capture light and form effectively. Before diving into color, mastering value will provide a strong foundation for your art. Get ready to apply these concepts in upcoming exercises and projects!
