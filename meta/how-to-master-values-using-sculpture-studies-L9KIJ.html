How to Master Values Using Sculpture Studies

Copy Code

Welcome to Section Two of the portrait painting chapter. In this section, we'll dive into some sculpture studies. I strongly recommend doing these studies, especially if you're a beginner and the thought of painting a full-color portrait feels intimidating. This is a great segue because we'll focus on values, simplifying the process.

The old masters did a fantastic job of simplifying forms, making them easier to identify and well-designed. They also excelled at designing hair into large shapes and clumps. We'll focus on that in our first sketch.

These studies will help us bridge the gap to full-color portraits, which we'll tackle in the following sections.

Gestural Studies

First Gesture Study

For the first study, we'll focus on gesture. I like to use a rough Loomis approach:

  • Block in the cranium with a sphere.
  • Indicate the brow line.
  • Cut the head in half vertically to find the center.
  • Pull out the front of the face mask.
  • Roughly block in the neck and hair shape.

I use graphic straight lines to ensure the angles are correct. Then, I add small dashes to indicate features like the eyebrow, eye corner, and nose gesture. Starting with basic shapes serves as placeholders that I can refine later.

Remember: Always start with the larger shapes and work your way down to the smaller details. This approach gives a strong foundation to build upon.

I'm indicating strong cast shadows with thin, crisp lines and core shadows with thicker, softer lines. I keep the shadow shapes graphic by adding a flat value, focusing on the modeling in the light areas.

I also pay attention to line variety, using a mix of thick and thin, soft and crisp lines to add depth and vibrancy to the sketch.

Learnings from the First Gesture

Observing the Reference Image

I love how the artist organized the structure of the hair in the reference sculpture. They created large masses and designed the shapes into big, medium, and small clumps. This creates an overall flow that makes the hair feel attached to the scalp.

When drawing hair, following consistent flows and rhythms helps it feel more natural and connected to the head.

Studying Planes and Lighting in Sculptures

Studying sculptures makes it easier to see the planes of the face. The eyes, lips, and nose are simplified, allowing us to see where edges need to be sharper or softer. It's also easier to study lighting because the core shadows, cast shadows, and midtones are more apparent.

This is why sculpture studies are a great exercise, they help us learn the planes of the face and anatomy in general.

Black and White Value Study

Second Study: Benjamin Franklin Bust

For our second study, we'll work on a black and white value study using the bust of Benjamin Franklin. The lighting on this sculpture is appealing, and we'll focus on the anatomy of lighting, separating lights and darks, and designing shapes in an appealing way.

Starting with black and white is a great first step for those learning painting. It teaches you how to control your values, which translates directly to color painting.

Blocking In the Gesture

I center my reference image and start by:

  • Drawing a light circle for the cranium.
  • Adding to the front of the circle to capture the mass of the face.
  • Blocking in the brow line.
  • Adding a smaller circle to describe the side of the head.

I represent corners with straight lines to graphically design the shapes before rendering. Watching my angles and points of comparison is crucial for capturing a likeness. I use straight lines to connect points and ensure features align correctly.

I block in features like the nose, eyes, chin, and lip line, drawing parallel lines to maintain symmetry. I also use alignment techniques, like drawing a line from the tear duct down to the neck, to place features accurately.

Developing the Value Study

After refining the gesture, I:

  1. Flatten the sketch and darken the composition slightly.
  2. Graphically separate the lights and darks using a textured brush.
  3. Carve out shapes, editing as I paint in the values.
  4. Fix the eyes, as they're crucial to the portrait.
  5. Chisel in the planes, softening edges with a blender brush.
  6. Block in midtones, adding depth to the face.
  7. Use a multiply layer to ensure the darks are darker than the lights.
  8. Squint my eyes to check the separation of lights and darks.

I add dark accents in the areas of occlusion, places where light can't reach, like under the nose and between the lips. Then, I flatten everything and start adding the highlights, which are like the icing on the cake.

Importance of Value Control

Controlling values is essential. When you squint your eyes, you should see a clear separation between the light shapes and dark shapes. Avoid letting values in the shadow areas become as light as those in the lit areas, as this can make the image look muddy.

Final Notes and Takeaways

Separating Lights and Darks

A strong separation of lights and darks is vital. When I squint, the lights should merge into one shape, and the darks should do the same. This clarity makes the painting read well from a distance.

Use of Core Shadows, Cast Shadows, and Occlusion Shadows

  • Core Shadows: Softer shadows where the form turns away from the light.
  • Cast Shadows: Hard-edged shadows cast by an object blocking the light.
  • Occlusion Shadows: The darkest areas where light can't reach at all.

Understanding and applying these correctly adds depth and realism.

Variety in Line Work and Brushstrokes

Using a variety of brushstrokes and line work, thick and thin, soft and crisp, adds energy and vibrancy to the piece. Larger brushstrokes for large shapes and finer details for small areas create a pleasing hierarchy.

The Importance of Hierarchy in Detail

Design your painting with large areas of rest and small areas of detail. This balance keeps the viewer's eye engaged without overwhelming them.

Effective Painting Process Overview

  1. Block out local values first.
  2. Separate your lights and darks graphically.
  3. Define your core shadows and cast shadows.
  4. Add gradients where necessary.
  5. Save dark accents and highlights for last.

This approach builds a strong foundation and makes the painting process more manageable.

Next Steps

With the value studies complete, we're ready to move on to the full-color portrait painting. These exercises have prepared us by reinforcing the importance of values, shapes, and the efficient structuring of a portrait.
