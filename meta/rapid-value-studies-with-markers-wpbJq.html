Rapid Value Studies with Markers

Copy Code

Hello everyone! In this session, I'm exploring value compositions by creating thumbnails using markers. I'll be working from a reference of a sculpture, probably Greek, of a man wearing a robe. This time, instead of pencils, I'm using markers to experiment with different value arrangements.

Materials

I'm using warm gray markers in values 9, 5, and 3. I chose warm grays because the subject feels warm to me, but you can use cool gray or neutral gray, whatever you have on hand. The key is to have a few options of value to play with.

I also consider the white of the paper as an additional value, giving me a total of four values to work with. This setup allows me to manipulate the value range in my thumbnails.

Setting Up the Value Range

With these markers, I can adjust the range of values in my sketches. For some thumbnails, I might use:

  • A 9, 5, and the paper for a broader range.
  • A 9, 5, and 3 to shift everything towards the darker values.
  • The paper, 3, and 5 to keep everything lighter.

This flexibility lets me explore different atmospheres and moods in my compositions.

Starting the Thumbnails

I plan to create thumbnails quickly, focusing on the major value masses without getting into details. I'll start with the 3 marker to lay down initial shapes.

When I squint at the reference, I see two main groups of light:

  • The face.
  • The cloth on the shoulder.

The darks are:

  • The beard and hair, framing the light of the face.
  • The dark cloth on the other shoulder.
  • The background, which is more of a mid-tone.

First Thumbnail: Replicating the Photo

I begin by sketching the head and the triangular shape of the shoulder cloth. I use the 3 marker for mid-tones and quickly lay in shapes using both the brush tip and the chisel tip for variety.

I realize that the mid-value I've chosen is too light, so I switch to the 5 to darken certain areas, like the eye sockets and the beard. This first attempt feels a bit boring and strange, so I decide to try something different.

Experimenting with Value Arrangements

Thumbnail Two: Emphasizing the Face

In this version, I make the face the only light shape, with the shoulder in mid-tone and everything else dark. I draw the head larger, filling the background with dark to make the face pop. I realize the composition needs more space above the head, so I adjust the framing.

Thumbnail Three: Framing the Head

I focus on the shape of the hair, which flows diagonally and creates interesting shapes. I decide to make the hair stand out more by leaving it as a mid-tone against a light background. I add space above his head and include some triangular shapes in the drapery to create tension.

Thumbnail Four: Adjusting Shapes and Tension

I continue to adjust the shapes, trying to frame the head better. I experiment with adding dark shapes to balance the composition but realize that sometimes unbalanced compositions can be more dynamic.

Thumbnail Five: Lightening the Cloth

I play with the lighting by making both shoulders light, reducing shadows on the drapery. I keep the composition tall and adjust the mid-tones to bring everything together. I add some dark shapes within the mid-tones to create a checkerboard effect, grouping elements and adding interest.

Thumbnail Six: Strong Diagonal Dark Shape

I attempt to create a strong diagonal dark shape across the composition by making the cloth dark. I realize that adding dark shapes elsewhere can detract from the main focus, so I keep the rest simple.

Thumbnail Seven: Simplifying and Emphasizing Contrasts

I revisit the idea of a strong diagonal dark shape but keep the background light, allowing the dark shape to stand out. I ensure that the area with the most contrast is around the face, where the viewer's eye is naturally drawn.

Analyzing the Thumbnails

Looking back at the thumbnails, I notice that the markers allowed me to move through ideas quickly. The initial thumbnails weren't great, but they helped me figure out the shapes I'm working with.

My favorite thumbnails are:

  • The third and fourth ones, where I adjusted shapes and created tension.
  • The seventh one, where I emphasized contrasts and simplified the composition.

I realize that sometimes you need to get the bad drawings out of the way to discover the good ones.

Playing with Value Range

Thumbnail Eight: Low-Key Composition

I decide to play with the value range by using darker markers. I use a 7 for the darks and make sure nothing is as light as the paper. This creates a low-key composition, where the lightest value is still relatively dark.

I introduce a fourth value, a lighter tone, only in specific areas like the face to create focal points. This experiment shows how adjusting the value range can dramatically change the mood of the composition.

Choosing a Favorite and Next Steps

Sometimes, picking a favorite composition is hard. I'm torn between three of my thumbnails:

  • The third and fourth ones, where the shapes and tension work well.
  • The seventh one, which has a strong diagonal dark shape and emphasizes contrasts.

If I were to take this further into a larger project, I'd enlarge my favorite thumbnails and create small color studies in oil paint or digitally. This would help me decide which composition works best before committing to a final piece.

* * *

This exercise shows how you can expand your creative bubble by experimenting with values and compositions. Values make a huge difference, and it's up to you how you manipulate them.

Remember, even if certain elements like the head have to stay recognizable, you have creative freedom with everything else. Play with shapes, values, and compositions to see where it takes you.

I hope you enjoyed this exploration, and I'll see you in the next one!
