Pushing the Extremes of Foreshortening

When tackling foreshortening, I like to push the extreme use of shortened lines and their impact. For example, if the rib cage shifts away and the pelvis pushes back, I shorten the lines to emphasize this in-and-out movement. By doing so, I can convey depth and perspective more effectively.

Shortening Lines and Emphasizing Wraps

To depict foreshortening, I focus on shortening the lines of the limbs or torso that recede into space. Coupled with this, I emphasize the wraps, the way forms curve around in space. This combination helps create a sense of volume and depth.

Maintaining Rhythm and Gesture

Even when dealing with complex poses, I ensure that the rhythm and gesture of the figure remain intact. The animated, active movement hasn't left, it's just become more intricate. I avoid losing the flow by keeping my lines dynamic and connected.

Using Asymmetry in Space

In poses that appear symmetrical or straight on, I introduce asymmetry in space. This could mean showing the rib cage going away in space while the pelvis comes toward the viewer. This spatial asymmetry prevents the figure from feeling static or flat.

Drawing from the Inside Out

I always draw from the inside out, focusing on the underlying forms rather than just the contour. By understanding the internal structure, I can create more accurate and dynamic drawings. My lines anticipate building out to the contour, ensuring the figure feels solid and three-dimensional.

Handling Challenging Poses

For reclining poses or limbs coming directly at the viewer, I remain aware of common pitfalls. Often, we unintentionally make lengths longer when we should be shortening them. Practicing ellipses and being mindful of line length helps me depict these poses accurately.

Utilizing Line Weight and Design Elements

I use line weight to emphasize important areas and guide the viewer's eye. By darkening certain lines, I can highlight where the weight is distributed or bring focus to specific parts of the figure. Incorporating design elements and thinking about the overall composition enhances the drawing's impact.

Gesture Drawing as Notation

Gesture drawing is a notational tool, a way to plan and understand the figure. It's not the end of the drawing, but a starting point. I remind myself that there's no pressure for the gesture to be perfect, it's there to inform the next stages of development.

Embracing Forward-Backward Projection

When figures lean forward or backward, I convey this by adjusting the forward-backward projection of forms. The rib cage might tilt away while the pelvis comes forward. By showing this, I can depict balance and movement even in static poses.

* * *

I hope this approach helps you feel more comfortable tackling challenging poses. By pushing foreshortening, maintaining rhythm, and thinking three-dimensionally, you can bring more dynamism to your drawings. Remember, gesture is about capturing the essence of the pose, so keep practicing and experimenting.
