The Challenge of Measuring Proportions

We are trying to find where the highest point of the couch lands on this leaning ladder. It could be easy if the couch is lined up conveniently, and the base may be just right in this case, but it's hard to see over there through all those lines.

And here is a second issue, the high part of the couch is not directly over that line. So we've gotta be careful to get this right, even if it means tracking a lot of lines.

Let's work up here where it's easier to see. We're trying to get the height of the couch over to here and run it back to the ladder, but getting the height of the couch over here can go wrong, and it did.

Let's see what's going on. If we find a lowest point on the floor and bring it to the baseline of the ladder, then run the highest point of the couch over there, it won't work because we need a height point directly above the base point. We would have the same problem if we started with the height, where is the point on the floor directly below it?

To get these points, we must take rides on the three line systems to find unknown points with connections from known points.

Let's try again to move that high point there, so that it's directly above this corner touching the floor. We must find a way to bring it by way of lines, or in some cases, complete sections, so we know exactly where to stop, up to the same proximal position. Then we can work confidently with reliable height, run it across, run it back to the base of the ladder, and across to the leaning edge.

And gosh, that was hard, so many lines just to find the height of the couch on the ladder. But we found it.

Could we have made it simpler? Yes, if the couch was simpler and didn't lift off the floor, or if we had the orthos so we could be sure of the couch parts' proportions to each other. It would take far fewer moves.

Applying Perspective in Storytelling and Comics

Now we'll end this response to the rooms by noting what William Montalvo said about comics in one of the comments. This is important for storytellers.

Spike Lee, in his MasterClass trailer, mentioned that whether you look up at a character or down at a character affects the feeling. He particularly mentioned that typically when we look up at a character, we feel like that character has more power than us and could be a threat, but not necessarily.

It can be the opposite of that. People who do children's books know this. I had a teacher who did many children's books, and he pointed out that typically you put the eye level of the child into the book so that the child looks at it and says, "This is the way I see the world", the gum under the table, looking up into the grownups, because that lets them know that this is for me.

But not necessarily, kids like to be lifted up and flown over their environment. And so you get the Where's Waldo approach to looking down on everything.

There is no rule about this, and it is not our problem here for what height is best. But here's my question: How can an artist use their talent and wisdom for how high or how low to place the viewer without either knowing this, if they're drawing, or at least struggling with it to heighten awareness that it is a view from a point of view?

You are working here to set up your skills in placing a view. Wisdom for where to place it can come later.
