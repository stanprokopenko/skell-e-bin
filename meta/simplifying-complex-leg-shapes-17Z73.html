Simplifying Complex Leg Shapes

Copy Code

I like to think of the leg as more than just two simple forms connected by the knee. The knee area itself needs to be its own structure. This strategy makes all the forms fit together better and gives the leg a more natural flow.

Establishing Three Key Forms

I start by viewing the leg as three main sections: 1. Upper Leg (Thigh) 2. Knee Structure 3. Lower Leg (Shin and Calf)

Seeing the knee as its own form, like a small “Coke can,” box, or wedge, helps me integrate the thigh with the lower leg in a clearer, more connected way.

Using Tubes and Tapers

I begin with the idea of a tube for both thigh and lower leg. A tube will often taper as it nears the ankle. From the side view, I also allow for gentle curves that suggest muscle shape: - The tibialis anterior can create a slight bulge on the front, near the shin. - The calf curves outward in the back, then narrows into the Achilles tendon before reaching the heel.

Introducing S-Curves

I use S-curves to show the flowing transition from one form to the next. These curves bring a “watery” design, which suggests the natural flexibility of the leg. Even small changes in direction, like a subtle S-curve in the shin or calf, help the leg look more alive and less stiff.

When the upper leg and lower leg move in similar directions, I connect them with a curve around the knee. If the angles differ more, there may be one or more corners at the knee joint. Corners or curves depend on the pose, but I aim for enough flow to show that the knee can bend.

Refining the Knee

The knee structure is more than just a kneecap. It includes all the subtle shapes around the knee, the outer bulges, the interior forms, and any overlaps. These bulges and overlaps are key for believable articulation, - I often sketch this area as a small, rounded block or a bulge with corners. - Once the knee is set, it creates a more convincing place to start the lower leg.

Variations and Overlaps

I love to experiment with different shapes for thighs, knees, and shins: - Straight or Bowed Shin: Some shins are fairly straight, others bow out slightly. I often exaggerate the bow to keep the drawing fluid. - Bulged Calf: The calf usually curves outward, creating that strong shape on the back of the leg. - Overlaps: Overlapping lines around the knee give a strong sense of connection from thigh to shin.

Trying out boxy, egg-shaped, or teardrop forms keeps my approach flexible. These simple shapes can be combined in many ways to capture different leg types and poses.

* * *

Focusing on the knee as its own structure, using tapered tubes, and introducing S-curves and overlaps are all great ways to bring the leg to life. Even though it’s a simple approach, the details of these smaller forms, like bulges in the shin or subtle corners at the knee, make a big difference. By exploring variations and iterating on these shapes, I get a stronger sense of structure and flow in my leg drawings.
