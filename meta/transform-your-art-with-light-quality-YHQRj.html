What Is the Quality of Light?

As an illustrator, you need to understand the quality of light. This term doesn't refer to good or bad light, but to its characteristics, how light behaves and affects your subject. The size and type of your light source dramatically change the quality of light in your scene.

Small vs. Large Light Sources

  • Small, bright light sources (like the sun on a clear day) create sharp shadows and a small terminator angle (the edge where light meets shadow).
  • Large light sources spread light around objects, resulting in softer shadows and a broader terminator edge.

Choosing the Right Quality

Decide what look you want for your illustration:

  • Sharp, high-contrast images with direct light and strong shadows.
  • Soft, low-contrast images with diffused light and gentle shadows.

Use the size of your light source as a tool to achieve this.

Primary and Secondary Light

  • Primary (specular) light comes straight from the source to your subject.
  • Secondary light is bounce light that reflects off surfaces, becoming more diffused and changing the quality of light.

Using Bounce Light

  • Bounce light as key light: The main light comes from reflected surfaces, giving a softer illumination.
  • Mixed keys: Combine direct light and bounce light for dramatic effects.

Examples from "Klaus"

In the film "Klaus":

  • Direct light: Characters lit by candles or sunlight directly, creating sharp lighting.
  • Bounce light: Light reflects off walls or floors onto characters, softening the light.
  • Mixed lighting: Some characters are in direct light while others are lit by bounce light, enhancing storytelling (e.g., showing a character's transition by lighting them half in shadow, half in light).

Live-Action Film Techniques

Indirect Lighting

Cinematographers use bounce light to soften scenes:

  • Bounce off walls: Light reflects onto characters, wrapping softly around faces.
  • Diffused through curtains: Creates mood and soft shadows.
  • Bounce from tables: Common in interrogation scenes, adding mystery and soft illumination.

Diffusion in Harsh Sunlight

To avoid harsh shadows in bright conditions:

  • Diffusion cloths ("fly swatters"): Large fabrics used to soften sunlight on actors' faces.
  • Example: In "Jumanji," actors are lit with diffused light to prevent overexposed or harsh shadows.

Golden Hour and Blue Hour

Golden Hour

  • Occurs just before sunset when the sun is low.
  • Allows for backlighting with rim light and avoids overhead shadows.
  • Creates long shadows and a warm glow.

Blue Hour

  • Happens after sunset when the sun is below the horizon.
  • The sky emits a soft, cool light.
  • Provides gentle, diffused illumination with a unique mood.

Applying These Concepts

When lighting your illustrations:

  • Consider the size and type of your light source.
  • Use bounce light to soften and diffuse.
  • Experiment with different times of day for varied lighting effects.
  • Employ cinematic tricks like diffusion to control light quality.

Understanding and manipulating the quality of light enhances your artwork and supports your storytelling.
