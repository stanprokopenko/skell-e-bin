Balancing Simplicity and Character

I like to start by aiming for a simple structure, and I make sure it still captures essential characteristics. If we go too realistic, we risk overcomplicating, but if we make it too simple, we get a snowman-like figure. My goal is to find a balanced approach that’s quick to sketch, yet reads as a believable torso right from the start, before any rendering details.

Building Blocks

I begin with a basic rectangular or tubular shape for the torso. This shape should be waist-wide so I have room to adjust later. If I’m looking for slightly more nuance, I’ll define two main sections:

  • Rib Cage: Often about three units tall and two units wide.
  • Pelvis (hips): About two units tall and three units wide.

By separating these two sections, I keep the construction simple. It is also more characteristic. I also maintain a small gap between rib cage and pelvis for the waist, unless the figure is bending and those masses pinch together on one side.

The “T” for the Torso

When I face the figure head-on or from the back, I usually find it helpful to establish a “T”:

  1. Shoulder line running horizontally.
  2. Center line running vertically down the torso.

Those lines help orient the major masses. If the pose is more dynamic, I check how the shoulders tilt and how far the center line deviates from vertical. It’s easy to overestimate these angles, so I compare them to a simple mental “grid” and adjust accordingly.

Front and Back Views

This approach works whether I’m drawing a front view or back view:

  • Front View
    I place the rib cage form, then create the slightly bulging pelvic box below. The waist area is a narrower connection between them.

  • Back View
    I watch carefully for the in-and-out “accordion” effect of the spine. The rib cage often arches back from the neck, then transitions to a flatter section at the waist, and bulges again at the hips if the figure is leaning. I still separate rib cage from pelvis, and then connect them with simpler lines or bulges that indicate where the spine is pushing outward or curving inward.

Adjusting Proportions

I keep my lines light so I can tweak them on the fly:

  • Start with a waist-wide tube.
  • Decide where the rib cage ends and the hips begin.
  • If needed, shift the center line or expand or narrow the torso before committing to darker lines or refining.

By drawing lightly, I can fix mistakes simply by drawing on top. I often wait to see proportions in context before finalizing.

Bending and Twisting

When the figure leans to one side or twists more dramatically, I track where the shoulder line and hip line tilt. I still use the same shapes, but I allow them to:

  • Pinch on one side (where rib cage and pelvis come closer).
  • Stretch on the other (where there’s more space).

I rely on quick horizontal and vertical checks, asking myself, “Is that line truly horizontal or vertical?” to keep the alignment in perspective.

* * *

With these simplified masses for the torso, I can quickly capture a wide range of poses. By thinking of the rib cage and pelvis as separate boxes or tubes, and by paying attention to tilts or bends, I get a strong base structure. Then I can add details like shoulder girdle or muscles as needed. This approach helps me focus on clear proportions and convincing 3D form right from the start.
