6 Steps from Gesture to Anatomy

Starting with the Gesture

I begin by capturing the gesture of the figure, a quick, flowing sketch that represents the movement and energy of the pose. In complex poses, like a forward lean with a slight bend, it's crucial to keep lines light and airy, and use minimal pressure. This approach helps when you add more details later on.

Indicating Landmarks

Next, I indicate key landmarks using simple shapes:

  • Spine
  • Sacrum
  • Scapulae
  • Pelvis
  • Joints

These landmarks help me understand the proportions and alignment of the figure. I use circles, triangles, and straight lines as shorthand, which simplifies the process and keeps the focus on the overall structure.

Considering Perspective

Understanding the perspective of the figure is essential. I visualize the rib cage and pelvic box in three dimensions, noting how they're oriented in space. This helps in accurately placing muscles and understanding how they wrap around the forms.

Adding Muscles and Anatomy

With the structure in place, I begin adding muscles over the forms. I think of muscles as tumbling shapes, especially in the limbs. For example:

  • The gluteus maximus is like a butterfly shape that wraps around the pelvis.
  • The hamstrings can be simplified into three shapes.
  • The calf muscles are higher on the outside and lower on the inside, reflecting their anatomical structure.

I pay attention to the contours and how they move across the surfaces, which conveys the form without needing to draw every detail.

Emphasizing Pinches and Stretches

Muscles reflect the body's pinches and stretches:

  • On the pinching side, muscles are shorter and compressed.
  • On the stretching side, muscles are longer and elongated.

This understanding helps me design the shapes and lines that represent muscle forms accurately, enhancing the figure's dynamism.

Rendering Surfaces and Light

When rendering, I consider how light interacts with the forms:

  • Core shadows indicate where the form turns away from the light.
  • Cast shadows show where one form blocks light from another.

By focusing on the geometry of the forms, I render them more effectively, making them look three-dimensional without heavy shading. It's about conveying the surface and volume through thoughtful line work.

Working Holistically

I ensure to work through the entire figure rather than fixating on one area. This holistic approach keeps proportions consistent and the drawing unified. It prevents overworking parts of the figure and maintains a cohesive style throughout.

Designing with Gesture

Anatomy isn't just about accuracy, it's also about design and movement. I use the gesture to inform,

  • The shapes of the muscles
  • The flow of lines across the forms
  • The overall rhythm of the figure

By integrating gesture and anatomy, I create drawings that are both structurally sound and expressive. This approach allows me to convey not just the figure's appearance but its energy and motion as well.
