Anvil Drawing: From Orthographic to 3D

I started by placing the horizon line in the image. To keep the grid subtle, I used two sheets of vellum over it. I chose a front corner for the box, marking a point to ensure it stays within the picture. This point determined the height of the box.

Knowing the vanishing points, I established the three axes needed. I had to decide how wide and deep the box should be. I aimed for a front rectangle that's higher than it is wide. In a wide-angle view, objects closer to us appear narrower due to perspective.

I tested the proportions by running the corners back to the vanishing points. The back rectangle seemed square, but acceptable. This box serves as a guide to place the anvil within it.

Using the orthographic projection (ortho), I found the halfway point. Noting that the base of the anvil extends in front of the halfway point, I estimated the position of the back foot. I used the X method to find the center, observing how things compress in a wide-angle view.

I constructed the base, a rectangle deeper than it is wide. From its center, I lifted a vertical line and this established the height. Being close to the horizon line made side-to-side and front-to-back lines appear nearly the same, but I carefully distinguished them.

Next, I focused on the anvil's body. Drawing from the base up to the waist, I used the center line to find the front and back limits. I divided the width into quarters to get the right proportions.

For the hanging end, I noted it's about halfway across the base but doesn't reach as far forward as the front foot. I plotted its position, ensuring it aligned correctly in perspective.

The anvil's horn extends beyond the halfway point, matching the width of the face. I connected points with straight lines to guide its shape, then transformed them into curves based on the ortho. I added the step down and found the center where the horn points.

Using cross-contour lines, I defined the curves of the horn and waist. I paid attention to how lines change direction, aiming down as they move away when above the horizon line. This helped in capturing the anvil's form accurately.

Throughout, I erased unnecessary lines to clean up the drawing. By darkening the lines I wanted to keep, I clarified the anvil's structure. I constantly referred back to the ortho, ensuring symmetry and proportion without directly viewing the anvil in this position.

In the end, I navigated the grid in 3D, constructing the anvil from eye level. This method relies on understanding perspective, using grids and orthographic projections to accurately render complex forms.
