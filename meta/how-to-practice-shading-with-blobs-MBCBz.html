How to Practice Shading with Blobs

Copy Code

I like to think of this exercise as a quick way to explore shapes and shading. I grab a pencil (or whatever tool I’m using) and craft little blobs. I try to keep them organic, combining circles, ellipses, curves, and even boxy edges. The goal is to practice shaping forms, lighting them from different directions, and building confidence with edges.

Building Basic Forms

I usually begin by sketching a simple outline of a blob. It might start as a curvy bean shape, a blob with two bulbous ends, or a squarish form connected to a rounded mass. I focus on making it look organic by varying the dimensions and contour. There’s no single correct starting shape, any blob is fair game.

Adding Light and Shadows

Once I have my blob, I decide where the light is coming from. I often imagine a basic top-left light source, but sometimes I place the light behind the object for variety. From there:
- I fill in shadow areas where the form curves away from the light.
- I pay attention to any cast shadow if one part of the blob might block light onto another part.
- I think about a simple core shadow and soften or firm up the edges around it.

Key Edges

Edges help me describe how the form is turning. I like to mix:
- Sharp edges: Sudden plane changes or where the form overlaps another part of the blob.
- Firm edges: Transitions that aren’t razor-sharp but still clearly indicate a turn in the surface.
- Soft edges: Smooth, gradual transitions that help the blob look round and organic.

Halftones

Halftones appear between the lit surface and the dark core shadow. I add a soft gradation if the form is smoothly curving. If it’s a firmer turn, I keep that middle value more abrupt. Sometimes I skip halftones to focus on the main shapes of the shading.

Speed Drills

To keep it fun, I time myself. I might give myself five minutes to resolve each blob, or even just two minutes for a quick challenge. Speed forces me to make clear decisions and stay loose. The results may not be perfect, but these quick drills build skill and confidence.

Practice and Repetition

Honestly, the real benefit is in repetition. Trying different light sources, practicing a wide range of edges, and mixing shapes is how I hone this technique. I see this as a great warm-up before diving into more complex drawings, like portraits or full scenes. It loosens my hand and mind, it encourages more inventive shapes, and lets me explore edge variety without stress.

That’s it! They’re just blobs, but they’re an excellent way to master shading and build up my skill set.
