How to Draw Extreme Wide-Angle Perspective

Copy Code

I want to show you how I set up a wide angle perspective for a simple object I often call a “widget.” I’ll walk you through how I find key intersection points, establish the vanishing points, and place a viewer very close to the object to give it an extreme look. This approach can feel like a complex crossword puzzle, but it’s a great exercise in spatial awareness.

Setting Up the Scene

I start by placing top, front, and side views (orthos) of the object. I use these to figure out the height of important features when they touch the picture plane.

I mark the base, the transition line where a slope meets a flat plane, and the highest point of the widget on the picture plane.

Then I decide how close the viewer should be. If the viewer’s point is too close, the effect becomes very distorted. If it’s too far, I don’t get that wide angle I’m aiming for. I find a middle ground that still gives a strong convergence in perspective.

Finding the Vanishing Points

I draw lines from the viewer’s point to key corners of the object: - One set of lines defines the right vanishing point.
- The perpendicular set defines the left vanishing point.

Both vanishing points end up fairly close together, which adds to the super wide angle look.

Projecting the Corners

I connect the far left and far right corners of the object back to the viewer on my diagram, then note where these lines intersect the picture plane. These intersections become the object’s corners when I drop them vertically. I’m careful to keep lines straight and consistent either to the vanishing points or vertically down.

I also place the horizon line very high, aiming for a severe downward viewpoint.

Constructing Heights and Slopes

Next, I identify the height lines by projecting them back to the correct vanishing point. Where these lines meet the back edges determines the upper and lower shapes of the object.

For the sloped part of the widget: 1. I track the slope and where it intersects the front and back edges.
2. I connect those slope corners to the vanishing points.
3. I drop any verticals needed to see where they fit into the final shape.

I double-check how close lines meet and where they converge. Minor variations are normal.

Finishing the Drawing

After the slope is in place, I finish the plateau on top of the widget: - I project a horizontal line from the slope’s highest point. - I connect it back to the left and right vanishing points. - I confirm all intersections match up to complete the final shape.

The result looks stretched and warped. The front corner juts toward the viewer, creating acute angles where I’d normally expect obtuse ones in a standard two-point setup. The deep sense of space comes from the viewer being so close.

* * *

This sort of extreme wide angle perspective is fun and reveals how perspective can become distorted when the viewer is placed near the subject. It also trains me to track multiple lines, keep intersections organized, and appreciate how a simple shape can turn into something wild in a drawn perspective.

I encourage you to try placing a viewer very close to your own object and building an exaggerated two-point perspective. Yes, it’s a bit of a puzzle, but it’s a valuable exercise in understanding and controlling your drawing’s sense of depth. Good luck!
