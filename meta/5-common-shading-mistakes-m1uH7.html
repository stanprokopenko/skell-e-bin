5 Common Shading Mistakes

Hey everyone, let's dive into shading spheres and address some common mistakes I've noticed in your work. Overall, you did fantastic, but there are a few recurring issues we can work on together.

Incorrect Placement of the Terminator

One common mistake is the incorrect placement of the terminator, the line where the light transitions to shadow. I've seen the terminator placed too far into the light side, and this makes the lit area too small.

How to Fix It:

  • Find the Equator: Imagine the sphere's equator, where the terminator should typically be when the light source is distant.
  • Draw a Proper Ellipse: Ensure the terminator cuts the sphere into two equal halves with a smooth ellipse.
  • Consider Light Source Distance: Remember, if the light source is far away, the light rays are nearly parallel, and the terminator is near the center. If the light source is close, the terminator shifts but indicates a specific light setup.

Incorrect Shape of the Terminator Ellipse

Sometimes, the terminator isn't shaped correctly, it might not curve properly or resemble an ellipse.

How to Fix It:

  • Practice Ellipses: Ensure your terminator is a true ellipse that wraps around the sphere.
  • Check Symmetry: Both sides of the terminator should be symmetrical, dividing the sphere evenly.
  • Visualize the Sphere's Surface: Imagine wrapping a band around the sphere to help place the ellipse accurately.

Reflective Light Too Light

Another issue is making the reflected light too bright, matching or exceeding the halftones on the light side. This flattens the form and can make the sphere look metallic.

How to Fix It:

  • Subdue the Reflected Light: Keep the reflected light darker than any area on the light side.
  • Maintain Value Hierarchy: Ensure the shadow areas remain darker than the light areas.
  • Reference Value Studies: Revisit value charts to understand proper value relationships.

Incorrect Value Transitions (Halftones)

Many of you have a gradation that's too linear from the core shadow to the light, or the transition happens too quickly, making the sphere look flat or faceted.

How to Fix It:

  • Apply Lambert's Cosine Law: The transition to light should happen more quickly near the core shadow, with the light halftones occupying more space.
  • Adjust Your Gradients: Concentrate darker halftones closer to the terminator and transition smoothly into lighter halftones.
  • Avoid Uniform Transitions: The shift from dark to light shouldn't be even across the surface.

Spotty Halftones and Dirty Appearance

Some spheres have spotty halftones, with inconsistent patches of light and dark that don't align with the form, making it look dirty or irregular.

How to Fix It:

  • Smooth Out the Halftones: Aim for gradual and consistent transitions in your shading.
  • Keep Values Consistent: Ensure lighter areas are where the light hits most directly, and there's no unnecessary variation.
  • Clean Up Unwanted Marks: Eliminate random dark spots or smudges that can disrupt the illusion of smooth form.

Incorrect Shape of the Cast Shadow

The cast shadow is often drawn with the wrong shape, sometimes it's too narrow, too wide, or resembles a teardrop instead of an ellipse.

How to Fix It:

  • Draw the Cast Shadow as an Ellipse: Since the sphere is on a flat surface, the cast shadow should be a perfect ellipse.
  • Maintain Consistent Proportions: Ensure the cast shadow isn't disproportionately thick or thin at any point.
  • Consider the Surface: If the shadow is cast on a curved surface, the shape changes, but for flat surfaces, stick to an ellipse.

Final Tips

  • Identify Your Mistakes: Review your drawings to see which of these mistakes you might have made.
  • Practice Deliberately: Focus on correcting these specific areas to improve your shading skills.
  • Understand the Science of Light: Remember that shading is about how light interacts with form.
  • Start with the Basics: Master shading basic forms before adding complexity to your work.

Let's keep practicing and refining our skills. You've got this!
