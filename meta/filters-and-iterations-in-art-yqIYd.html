The Holistic Approach

I believe in combining science and intuition to create art. Science isn’t wrong. Anatomy, perspective, and the laws of light aren’t wrong. But there’s also creativity, emotion, and personal ideas. It’s not one or the other. We need balance. That’s what composition is about: balancing craft and imagination so they work together.

Seeing Like an Artist

It’s said that 90% of our brain’s power goes to visual data. We’re visual creatures, so it’s no coincidence that we’re passionate about the visual arts. When we draw while taking notes, we’re more likely to remember what we learn.

Learning to see the world through an artist’s eyes is the real challenge. We can pick up drawing skills relatively quickly, but seeing the world in a fresh way and then getting that down on paper is a lifelong journey. Our goal is to make that process feel exciting rather than frustrating.

Creativity and Education

Around ages four to six, children’s creativity plummets worldwide when they enter public school. They’re often taught that there’s one right answer for problems. Meanwhile, an artist might see an infinite number of ways to paint the same sky. This contrast drives us to balance the facts of art (shadows need to be darker, etc.) with the imaginative freedom to decide how dark or where to place those shadows.

Filters and Iterations

My strategy centers on filters and iterations. These concepts help us master skills by breaking them down and repeating them in simple, engaging ways.

Filters

A filter is any idea or aspect of art. It could be: - Shape design
- Line quality
- Value (light vs. shadow)
- Composition
- Emotional or conceptual intent

We focus on one or two filters at a time instead of trying to master everything at once. If it’s too complicated, we simplify the challenge. Maybe we work on line and shape alone, not worrying about tone or proportion yet. Each filter becomes a separate learning goal.

Iterations

Once we pick our filter, we do it again and again, iterations. Baseball players hit balls over and over. We do the same in art. Repetition burns the skill into our muscle memory. We might repeat a drawing 48 times until we feel that little improvement, that triggers our excitement. That dopamine rush encourages us to come back and do more the next day. Over time, these small gains build into strong drawing habits and personal style.

Connection and Disconnection

Art often explores connection and disconnection. We’re here, and we want to be there. In spiritual paintings, we see a divide with heaven up high, earth below, and a figure bridging both. Even if we’re not painting anything religious, we still convey emotions by positioning elements to show unity or separation.

This connects directly to filters like color choices, lighting, composition shapes, and the gestures or poses in our work. Whether we paint a grand spiritual scene or a portrait of grief, each detail is chosen with purpose. With immersion and repetition, we learn to use these details to communicate ideas and emotions effectively.

Next Steps

I want to help you quickly gain fluency with basic materials so they won’t slow you down. Then, we’ll move into deeper, more creative challenges. Our big goal is to create beautiful, compelling art. We’ll do that by practicing filters, building skills through iterations, and seeking a balance between knowledge and self-expression.

Thanks for being passionate about art and for joining me. I’m excited to explore how we can bring more beauty and connection into our work. Let’s keep going.
