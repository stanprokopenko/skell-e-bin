How to Show Age Without Wrinkles

Copy Code

Drawing an aged face requires more than just adding wrinkles. It's about understanding the underlying structure and changes that occur with age. This guide will help you create more realistic and compelling aged faces in your artwork.

Basic Approach to Drawing an Aged Face

When drawing an aged face, start by laying in simple representations of larger planes in the skull. This approach helps you focus on the overall structure rather than getting lost in details. It's crucial to avoid overreliance on wrinkles, which can become noisy and hurt the illusion of form. Instead, concentrate on the underlying changes that occur with age.

Key Areas to Focus On

When drawing an aged face, pay special attention to these areas:

  • Eye sockets
  • Nose shape
  • Mouth
  • Cheekbones
  • Jaw
  • Temple

Understanding how these areas change with age will help you create more realistic aged faces.

Age-Related Changes in Facial Structure

Cheek Area

The cheek area undergoes significant changes with age. In younger faces, this area is smoothed out due to fat coverage. As we age:

  • The cheekbone becomes more prominent as fat shifts
  • The malar fat (cheek fat) slides down and settles near the nose
  • The transition from side to front of the cheek becomes more pronounced

Eye Area

The area around the eyes is particularly affected by aging:

  • Fat around the eye is affected by gravity
  • This creates pinches and wrinkles

Forehead

With age, the forehead undergoes changes:

  • It becomes more prominent
  • Fat underneath pulls tighter against the skull

Mouth and Jaw

The lower part of the face also shows signs of aging:

  • The buccal fat (near the mouth) and jowl fat are affected by gravity
  • Breaking up the straight line of the jaw suggests a later stage of life

Ears

Interestingly, ears tend to get larger with age. Keep this in mind when drawing older characters.

Importance of Head Construction

Remember that head construction is a means to an end, not the final result. Consider how much detail is needed to move to the next step, whether that's rendering or painting. The contour of the face is a result of internal forms, not a thing in itself.

Lighting and Shading Techniques

Dramatic Lighting

Lighting can be used to tell a story, not just render form. The video uses an example from "The Godfather" to illustrate dramatic lighting.

Basic Shapes in Head Construction

Use basic shapes like spheres, cylinders, and boxes to construct the head. This simplification helps in understanding the overall structure.

Edge Treatment

Pay attention to edge treatment, as it's important in conveying the turn of surfaces.

Value Distribution

When shading, consider how light dissipates as it moves away from the source. Use three main values: light, shadow, and an average value. For focal points, develop either half-tones in light or darker half-tones in shadow.

Practical Application

When applying these principles:

  • Use your understanding of form to guide your shading
  • Focus on the contrast between bone and muscle/fat, which becomes more apparent in older faces
  • Remember that the basic shapes and construction are tools to help you understand the form, not the end goal

By focusing on these aspects, you'll be able to create more realistic and compelling aged faces in your artwork.
