How to Bring Life to Figure Drawings

Copy Code

Welcome back! In this lesson, we'll work with two simple shapes - bells and tubes - to create figures filled with life and movement. We'll explore how to run gesture between them and dive into the idea of animation in a still drawing. With some simple ideas and golden secrets, we'll transform lifeless figures into ones that are full of life. So grab your pencil, watch, listen, draw along, and repeat until you're drawing with all the confidence in the world. Let's get into it!

Tools and Materials

We'll be working with newsprint and a 1710 Conte de Paris charcoal pencil. These tools are great for quick, loose sketches and allow for easy adjustments.

Using Simple Shapes: Bells and Tubes

Mr. T and Mrs. V Mnemonics

We'll start by using the mnemonics Mr. T and Mrs. V to simplify the torso. Mr. T represents the line from the collarbone down the sternum, acting as a central bar. Mrs. V represents the pelvic area, forming a V shape. By identifying these key landmarks, we can quickly establish the figure's structure.

Halves and Quarters

Break the torso into halves and quarters:

  • From the shoulders down to the tenth rib is half.
  • From the tenth rib down to the iliac crest (top of the hip) is another half.
  • From the iliac crest down to the pubic triangle is half again.

Think of this area as a big pair of underpants, a mnemonic to help visualize the shape.

The Skydiver Mnemonic

Visualize the torso as a skydiver:

  • The collarbones are the arms.
  • The little hand lengths represent the body and legs.

This helps in understanding the proportions and placing the sternum and linea alba (the line running down the abdominals).

Drawing Faces Quickly

When drawing faces quickly, use these proportional guidelines:

  • Link one eye to the other.
  • Ensure there's five eyes distance across the widest part of the skull.
  • From the eyebrow to the bottom of the nose is half.
  • From the bottom of the nose to the bottom lip, and then to the chin, divide the lower half accordingly.

Keep the lines subtle and light to expedite your drawing, especially during quick sketches. Use the sternal muscle of the neck to indicate slight turns.

The Importance of Quick Drawings

Quick drawings, or short drawings, prepare you for any eventuality. They may seem similar, but there are huge differences when you look closer. It's not about speed, it's about the power of decision-making in your own time. These quick sketches help embed foundational lines into your subconscious. This allows you to draw confidently from life or imagination.

Ghosting Back with a Chamois

Using a chamois leather cloth, you can ghost back your drawing. This means lightly erasing to soften the lines, allowing you to adjust and refine without starting over. A chamois takes away a lot, giving you a fresh start whenever needed.

Adding Dimension with Line Thickness

Utilize the broad end of your pencil to create varying line thickness. Thick lines suggest areas that are closer or heavier, while thin lines indicate areas further away or in light. This technique adds dimension to your drawings without extensive shading.

Pinch and Stretch

Understanding the concepts of pinch and stretch is crucial:

  • Pinch occurs where muscles and flesh compress.
  • Stretch happens on the opposite side where muscles extend.

For example, when a leg moves, it can create a bulge where there's a pinch. Recognizing these areas adds realism and fluidity to your figures.

Rendering: Pros and Cons

While rendering can add depth, be cautious not to get bogged down in it. Over-rendering can make your drawing muddy, obscuring the foundational lines and proportions. Render is most effective when the foundation is solid. Use it to enhance, not to hide mistakes.

The Role of Anatomy

Understanding Muscles and Bones

A solid grasp of anatomy elevates your drawing:

  • Know where the bones are to establish structure.
  • Understand how muscles like the brachialis, brachioradialis, and magnus adductor interact.
  • Recognize that flesh is thick and heavy, affecting how muscles and bones are depicted.

The Female Figure

The female figure is especially challenging due to its subtlety:

  • Extra layers of subcutaneous fat make bones harder to find.
  • Landmarks like the clavicles, navel, and crest of the hips are essential.
  • Consider the softness of features like the breasts, which sit atop the hard ribcage.

The Magic of Mileage

Mileage refers to the practice gained from numerous drawings. The more you draw, the more these methods become second nature. This allows you to:

  • Draw confidently from imagination.
  • Make adjustments on the fly.
  • Develop a sense of rhythm in your work.

Drawing from Imagination

With practice, you can:

  • Change shapes around as you see fit.
  • Use the tube and bell method to move the top egg (ribcage) around.
  • Experiment with different body types and poses.

Mileage helps you internalize proportions and shapes, freeing you to create dynamic figures.

Creating the Illusion of Movement

To make a figure appear animated:

  • Incorporate pinches where movement compresses the body.
  • Use gesture lines to link structures fluidly.
  • Shift the weight balance to suggest motion.

This technique brings stillness in motion, making your figures look alive.

Balancing Gesture and Structure

While gesture adds fluidity, structure provides stability. Balance them by:

  • Starting with simple shapes like tubes and bells.
  • Adding anatomical details to areas like the forearm, which can't be left tubular.
  • Using gravity to inform the pose and weight distribution.

Building Confidence Through Practice

Practice allows you to:

  • Internalize the mechanics of drawing.
  • Draw faster and more accurately.
  • Focus on expression rather than perfection.

Remember, there's no such thing as a perfect drawing. The goal is to express and communicate through your art.

Embracing Personal Style

Learn from others but bring in your unique take:

  • Take inspiration from various teachers.
  • Imbibe what resonates and discard what doesn't.
  • Experiment to find your own style.

Avoid being a clone of any artist. Your individuality makes your art special.

* * *

By using simple shapes like bells and tubes, understanding anatomy, and practicing diligently, you can create figures filled with life and movement. Embrace the methods, but remember they're not rigid rules. Let your mileage guide you, and don't be afraid to bring in your unique style. Keep drawing, keep experimenting, and most importantly, enjoy the journey of becoming the magician of art.
