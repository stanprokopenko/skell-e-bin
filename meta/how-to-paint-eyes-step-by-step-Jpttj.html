How to Paint Eyes Step by Step

Starting the Lay-In

I'm starting my full-color rendering of the eyes, applying the knowledge and tips we've covered so far. I begin with the gesture and lay-in, focusing on the keystone shape between the eyebrows, the glabella. I rough in the eye line and adjust the tilt of the eyes to match my reference. It's important to block in the underlying spheres of the eyeballs to build three-dimensional eyelids that wrap around them.

I use vertical curved lines to indicate where the pupils will be on these spheres. Then, I sketch the gesture of the eyelids and erase any unnecessary construction lines. I take my time to ensure accuracy, knowing that initial lay-ins don't have to be perfect. Adjustments can always be made along the way.

Understanding Eye Anatomy

I pay close attention to the negative shapes surrounding the irises, as they help me place them accurately. The cornea, the lens that bulges out on top of the iris and pupil, affects the shape of the eyelids. Typically, the apex of the eyelid aligns with the center of the pupil. I notice that the pupils aren't always centered, they might favor one side depending on the perspective.

Blocking in Eyelashes and Eyebrows

When drawing the eyelashes, I focus on creating flat, graphic shapes instead of individual hairs. This simplifies the process and allows for more detailed rendering later. I also block in the eyebrows, treating them as solid shapes to establish a good construction drawing to work with.

Preparing for Painting

In Photoshop, I organize my layers by collapsing all line work into one layer and removing any white areas from erasing and painting. I label my layers for clarity. I colorize the line layer, choosing a reddish-orange hue, and set it to multiply over my wash layer.

I create a wash layer underneath, using a red-orange color wash to eliminate the intimidating white canvas. This wash prevents white from poking through unpainted areas and provides a mid-tone value to work from.

Blocking in Local Colors

I add another layer for local colors, filling in areas with flat values without worrying about gradients or shadows. I find the local color of the skin by looking at the midtones and fill the entire area. For the sclera (white of the eye), I select the flesh color and desaturate it, avoiding pure white.

I fill in the irises with desaturated blues and the pupils with a saturated red-orange rather than black to keep them lively. I block in the eyelashes and eyebrows as graphic shapes, and add the tear ducts with a more pinkish tone.

Separating Lights and Shadows

Next, I create a layer to separate the lights and darks in a graphic way, similar to comic book coloring. I select a good shadow color for the flesh and fill in the shadow areas as flat shapes. I simplify the shapes into graphic forms without focusing on gradients yet.

I apply the same process to the whites of the eyes, separating the light and dark areas. This step helps establish the foundational value structure of the painting.

Blending and Creating Gradients

I merge the layers onto a new layer (keeping the originals for selection) and use the blender tools to create soft edges and transitions. I leave cast shadows sharp to maintain variation between hard and soft edges, which adds visual interest to the painting.

Adding Highlights and Dark Accents

I add highlights by using cooler pinks to create variation between warms and cools, making the features pop. I adjust the opacity of the highlight layer for subtler effects. To add stronger highlights, I use lighter, desaturated blues for the brightest spots, like the tip of the nose or the edges of the eyelids.

For dark accents, I use saturated cadmium reds and oranges to keep the shadow areas lively. I carefully place these accents in the corners of the eyes and areas of occlusion.

Refining Details

Now, I move into the refining phase, adding variation in brushwork, color, and edge sharpness. I go back and forth between different areas, adding nuances and more definition. This "noodling" phase is where the painting starts to come to life.

Final Touches and Tips

I push the cast shadows by adding saturated colors to make them pop. I vary my brushwork, incorporating smooth gradients and textured strokes for interest. I also add a subtle secondary light source, keeping it subdued so the primary light source remains the focus.

Throughout the process, I focus on maintaining a balance of warm and cool tones, hard and soft edges, and light and dark values. By building a strong foundation with careful lay-ins, thoughtful layering, and attention to detail, the final rendering achieves a realistic and dynamic look.
