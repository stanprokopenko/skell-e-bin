How to Use Box Logic for Foot Drawing

Why the Back View Is Challenging

I find the back view of the foot to be one of the toughest angles to capture. We need to consider the high arch on the inside (big toe side), the wider base on the outside (little toe side), and how everything overlaps as it recedes from us.

Overlaps

Overlaps help me show which parts are closer or farther in space. I make the heel the foremost shape, then ankle and calf, then the rest of the foot and toes. Each shape overlaps the next, like a stack moving away from the viewer.

Box Logic

I rely on box logic to avoid flattening the foot. The clearer I make my corners and planes, the easier it is to see what faces me versus what angles away. Boxiness lets me establish a solid structure that I can refine or round off later. It also helps me compare tops, bottoms, sides, and fronts of each part.

Breaking the Foot into Three Pieces

I break the foot into three main wedges: 1. The arch wedge (the midfoot).
2. The heel wedge (the back portion).
3. The toes (the front).

From the back, the heel wedge is key. It’s often a slightly boxy or bulbous shape that supports the Achilles tendon and connects up to the ankle.

Shaping the Heel

When sketching the heel, I sometimes imagine a teardrop or a rounded oven-like shape with a little chimney for the Achilles tendon. Whether I keep it squarer or more rounded, I emphasize that it’s a flat, weight-bearing base that meets the ground. Then I decide how wide or narrow to make it compared to the ankle.

Connecting Calf to Heel

The calf and ankle can be simplified into a tapering tube or a teardrop shape. I often leave these parts “floating” as I block in the larger shapes. Then I gradually connect the Achilles tendon down into the calcaneus bone (heel). This approach helps me keep track of layers and avoid confusion with overlapping forms.

Putting It All Together

Once I establish the heel, I figure out how the foot widens toward the toes. Like the chin versus the jaw on a face, the rear of the foot is narrower than the front. That extra width on the little toe side flares out for stability. Even with a slight outward angle of the feet, I can see some of the inside and the outside planes of the foot.

I also remind myself not to rush. The foot has many planes and knobby protrusions, and each should read clearly. I keep my lines light at first, so I can adjust proportions or perspective.

* * *

This back view tests my ability to manage overlaps, compare planes, and keep the structure clear. I break it into three main wedges and use box logic to find each plane. Next time, I’ll focus on the toes and how they connect to the rest of the foot.
