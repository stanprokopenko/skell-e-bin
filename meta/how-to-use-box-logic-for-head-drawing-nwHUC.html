How to Use Box Logic for Head Drawing

Copy Code

The Back View

When I draw the head from behind, I start by looking at the symmetry. I imagine a ball for the skull and notice how the neck and erector muscles attach. The erector muscles, or “shrugging muscles,” run in two cables along the back of the neck to keep the head upright. They attach high at the base of the skull.

The ears often stick out from the sides of the head. From a direct back view, I see the ears poking out on either side, with a bit of the face’s silhouette in between. Even though the back of the head is more rounded and lacks the clear “box corners” of the face, I still use structural thinking to place the ears and sketch the basic shape of the skull.

I also pay attention to hairstyle, which can either hide or reveal parts of the neck and skull. If the hair is full or curly, it helps to reduce it to simple forms, either box-like or tube-like, so I can see the main shape before adding any wave or highlight details.

The Three-Quarter Back View

When the head rotates to a three-quarter back view, I see part of the back and side of the head. The ear moves closer to the front of the face as the head turns away. So, I observe how close the ear is to the silhouette of the jaw and the sideburn area, then adjust my proportions accordingly.

Using Box Logic

I often apply box logic to keep the perspective consistent. I imagine corners on the head, even if they’re curved. The corners give me reference points to measure angles and proportions. For example:

• If I treat the top of the hair as a box, I can outline a top plane, a side plane, and a back plane.
• Lines connecting these planes will appear to converge slightly, depending on the tilt of the head.
• This creates an easy way to check if the head is angled up, down, or to the side.

Practical Tips

Checking Proportions

• Ear Height: In a three-quarter back view, the ear often appears higher because I’m looking slightly under the form. I’d rather place it a bit too high than too low.
• Jaw vs. Ear: The jaw should sit in front of the ear. It’s easy to accidentally shade or place lines that push the jaw behind the ear.
• Comparisons: I compare every new line, such as a hairline or eye socket, against the positions of the ears and skull. This helps me keep everything in proportion.

Emphasizing Structure

I try to see the fullness of the head even when I only see partial shapes. By visualizing all sides of the skull, I can persuade the viewer that the form is complete and three-dimensional. Even a slightly rounded head can be drawn with boxy guidelines to maintain clarity in how it’s oriented.

* * *

Using box logic on the back and three-quarter back views helps me see the head as a clear, architectural structure. Once I set up those simple building blocks, I add any unique details, like intricate hair shapes or strong jawlines. This process keeps everything in proportion, and makes the anatomy feel solid and convincing from any angle.
