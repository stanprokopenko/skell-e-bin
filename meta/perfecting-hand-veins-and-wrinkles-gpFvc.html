Techniques for Adding Veins

To create veins, take small pieces of clay, coil them out, and lay them on the surface. It's better to use smaller pieces of clay and vary their width. This technique, recommended by sculptor Eric Arneson, helps create natural-looking veins. Veins should have variation in their shape and some breakage in the line to mimic how they go in and out of the skin's surface.

  • Use small coils of clay to build out one vein.
  • Vary the shape and width of the coils.
  • Check your own hand often to ensure veins are in the right place and direction.

Blending Veins into the Surface

Once the veins are placed, use a tool, like a small metal spatula with a rounded end, to blend the edges into the surface. This process involves pressing down the edges repeatedly to create a soft transition.

  • Blend edges to avoid sharp transitions.
  • Press down the edges to make veins look like rolling hills on the surface.
  • Work perpendicular to the length of the vein for a natural blend.

Even for someone with prominent veins, the transition between veins and skin should be very soft. If a vein is too raised, you can remove some material with a loop tool and blend it again.

Importance of Hierarchy in Forms

Always maintain a hierarchy of forms:

  1. Primary forms: The most important shapes, like the overall shape of the hand.
  2. Secondary forms: Smaller shapes, like muscle bumps.
  3. Tertiary forms: Details like tendons and veins.

Each layer should build on the previous one. Ensure primary forms are accurate before moving to secondary forms, and so on. This hierarchy ensures that textures and details blend seamlessly into the established forms.

Adding and Refining Wrinkles

When adding wrinkles, focus on the important ones. For example, the wrinkles between the thumb and the hand are crucial and change based on the thumb's position.

  • Build walls of clay to represent wrinkles.
  • Blend edges of the wrinkles into the surface.
  • Press in between wrinkles to create valleys.

Use a loop tool with a rounded end to press in between the folds of the wrinkles. Always observe your reference and adjust based on the thumb's movement.
