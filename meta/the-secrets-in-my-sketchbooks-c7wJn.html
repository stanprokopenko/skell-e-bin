The Dynamic Bible Sketchbook

I want to start by sharing an old favorite of mine, the Strathmore watercolor sketchbook. Back in 2016, this sketchbook became the foundation for what would become The Dynamic Bible, a collection of my lecture notes, visuals, and teaching methods that I've curated from my classes at Art Center and other schools. For about six months, I filled this sketchbook with pen and watercolor drawings and scanned them to create the book. Originally, I didn't intend to publish it, it was a personal project to see if people understood what I was teaching. But after encouragement from friends and followers, I launched a Kickstarter, edited the text, and successfully published it. This experience showed me how a sketchbook can have a specific purpose beyond just drawing, it can be used to produce something meaningful.

Travel Sketchbooks

The Traveler's Journal

Another sketchbook I cherish is the Traveler's Journal by Midori (now known as Traveler's Company). This leather-bound book is my go-to for traveling. I use it to collect everything, drawings, stickers, stamps, and even natural materials like birch bark and flora. Whether I'm sketching fountains in Italy, studying pitcher plants at the Arboretum, or documenting a taxidermy workshop on insects, this sketchbook becomes a visual journal. It's not just about drawing, I write down notes, observations, and experiences. It's a companion that goes with me everywhere, capturing moments from Australia to New Zealand and beyond.

Accordion Sketchbooks

I also enjoy working with accordion sketchbooks, which present a unique challenge. Since the pages unfold continuously, I think about how each image transitions to the next, ensuring a seamless flow. My first accordion book focused on aquatic themes, but as I continued, it evolved to include studies from Paris, dinosaurs, and historical figures like Abraham Lincoln. The continuous format pushes me to consider the design of the entire spread, not just individual compositions. It's a fun and creative way to explore different subjects and see how they connect.

Larger Format Sketchbooks

Discovering New Materials

I love experimenting with different sizes and types of sketchbooks. One of my favorites is a large-format sketchbook I found in Austria with paper that lays completely flat. The paper quality is fantastic, and I filled it with pen and marker studies ranging from Mongolian culture to figurative work and mechanical designs. Another is a handmade book from Italy with ragged, hand-pressed paper. Using these sketchbooks allows me to focus on single imagery and dive deeper into illustration, rather than just sketching.

Capturing Experiences

These larger sketchbooks often become a canvas for significant experiences. For instance, during my trips to Africa, I sketched live observations of lions and cheetahs in the Masai Mara. The energy and speed of these animals in the wild are incomparable to seeing them in a zoo. I also documented moments like the passing of Kim Jung Gi, capturing emotions and reflections through my drawings. These sketchbooks become deeply personal, filled with illustrations that hold special meaning to me.

The Importance of Observation and Variation

Throughout my sketchbooks, you'll find a vast array of themes and subject matter, from creatures and animals to vehicles and machines, from stylized cartoons to realistic portraits. It's not the subject itself that draws me in, it's the act of drawing - the process of visualizing and putting ideas on paper. Whether it's observational studies at museums or imaginative explorations, I embrace the evolution of process in my work.

A Personal Relationship with Materials

I have a profound relationship with my sketchbooks and materials. Each sketchbook is unique, often with a character of its own, like the old, raggedy book I found in Utah that smelled like a basement. The pens, the paper, the textures, all of these elements contribute to the experience of drawing. They speak to me, and influence how I express my ideas. Collecting and using these sketchbooks isn't just about art, it's about building a connection with the materials and the moments they represent.

* * *

Every sketchbook I fill is more than just a collection of drawings, it's a personal journey. They're companions that document my growth, experiences, and thoughts as an artist. Looking back at them, I remember where I was, how I felt, and what inspired me at the time. They hold immense value, not monetarily, but personally, and they continue to motivate me to observe, explore, and create. This is me, and these are the stories my sketchbooks tell.
