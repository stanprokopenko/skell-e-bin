Understanding Lines Like Scientists

The purpose here is to understand straight lines not like artists but like scientists. Labeling every position in space helps you to truly understand space.

The Three Sets of Lines

The first ones are the easy ones. You shouldn't have trouble with these. Every line is one of three sets, and you can name them anything as long as you don't confuse their names.

But here's where it takes a little concentration.

Perception of Width and Depth

The person at the end of a table will say this is the width, and the table is deep. The person at the side of the table will say this is the width, and the table is shallow. Whatever the case, sketch it, identify the lines, spin it, sketch it again, relabel the lines, and here's the big deal: as you sketch, name the lines out loud. That digs it in.

Understanding Diagonal Lines and Bevels

Now for the bevels, the diagonal lines. Any line lying level but not a pure X or Z has got to be both. Any line aiming up even slightly has got to have Y in its name. If it leans front or back, YZ makes sense. If it leans side to side, XY makes sense.

Three-Way Diagonals

Now for the three-way diagonals. You could cheat on this one, just label every line X, Y, and Z, and you'll pass the test, but you'd miss the point of the line. Imagine this straight line, a mere angle on your flat paper, as part of this awesome illusion. What is a line but a dot that, when it moves, makes a trail? Straight lines are the simplest trail.

Some lines move on only one axis, X, Y, or Z. Some move on two, for example, any floor plan, wall plan, or ceiling likely has lines that move on only two axes. But some lines move on all three, which are the most complex non-curving lines. Draw, name, and imagine writing these lines left-right, up-down, front-back, and combinations.

If you take time to observe and label their three variations, you build your skill to imagine lines as more than just graphic elements but as directions in space.

Approaching Lines in Space

See Simple First

Here is a hint for how to approach it. See simple first, just like with shapes where we want to see simply. Studying forms, we keep it simple by keeping right angles in mind. In fact, draw them out. The more you can compare diagonals to right angles, the more you understand where the diagonal is leaning from.

Starting with a Base or Wall

Here's another hint, more advanced. If we start with a base and build up, the base helps us solve every width and depth point. Each point is sure to be on a height line above it, so travel on Y, and you may overshoot it or you may undershoot it, but you won't go off track.

What you learn is there is a logic to lines, and establishing a floor creates a foundation for all X and Z angles.

Sometimes it's best to start not with the floor, but the wall. A wall helps us solve every height and, say, depth point. Width lines in this case are easy, they just go across. Take a ride on width, and we may pass our point, but we're at least on the right track.

The floor or the other wall gives us a marker on depth to find the point we seek. On the width, it's just bound to that.

Sometimes it's best to define a floor, wall, or ceiling, and then put things on them or in them.

Building Complexity from Simplicity

We're doing this simply to start, in case you're a beginner. This spider was not simple, but I did it the same way. I made myself sure of the width and depth, we call that a plan. The height and depth came from a wall, we call that a side elevation. The height and width also came from a wall, another elevation, and lots of time connecting lines.

That's pretty much it for those of you who want more complexity. This is the secret: complex form comes from combining three simple axes. But we're getting ahead.

The Importance of This Groundwork

You may wonder, why all this fuss? "I just want to draw free and loose." You will. This prepares your awareness so that even in your loose sketching, you will see hidden planes. This is groundwork. It is supposed to be simple but thoughtful. Start here as you like.

Starting Your Drawings

Two more reminders:

  1. You don't have to begin with straight lines. You can start with blobs before getting to the straight lines.

  2. Best to start lightly. Light lines will fade back as you find and correct them.

However you do it, once you've got straight lines, name out the lines for the directions they move.
