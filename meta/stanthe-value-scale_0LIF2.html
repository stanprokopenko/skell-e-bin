Value is a fundamental concept in art that often goes underappreciated. While color might get all the glory, it's value that does the heavy lifting in creating the illusion of form and depth in your drawings. Let's dive into what value is, why it's crucial, and how to use it effectively in your artwork.

What is Value?

Value in art refers to how light or dark something appears. It's the tonal quality that helps create the illusion of three-dimensional form on a two-dimensional surface. When you add value to a simple line drawing, suddenly parts of the body appear to dip or bulge, muscles become visible, and the overall form becomes more tangible.

Value vs. Color

While we see the world in color, every color has an inherent value. For example, a bright yellow typically has a lighter value than a deep ultramarine blue. In drawing, we often separate value from color to focus on tonal relationships without the added complexity of hue.

The Structural Framework

Think of value as the steel framework of a building. The exterior might be colorful and eye-catching, but it's the internal structure (value) that gives the building its form and stability. Similarly, in art, a strong value structure ensures your drawing or painting "holds together" visually, even if you were to convert it to black and white.

The Value Scale

To make the infinite range of values in nature more manageable, artists often use a simplified value scale.

Creating a Value Scale

  • A typical value scale ranges from 0 (black) to 10 (white).
  • There are various types of scales: some use 18 values, others 10, 7, or even just 5.
  • The traditional Flemish scale has 9 values: 4 light, 1 mid-tone, and 4 dark.

The key is to have a gradation from white to black, with enough steps to represent light, mid-tones, and shadows effectively. A smaller range simplifies your drawing into basic shapes of light and shadow, while a wider range allows for more detailed rendering.

The Relative Nature of Value

One of the most crucial aspects of understanding value is recognizing its relative nature. Values don't exist in isolation â€“ they're always perceived in relation to surrounding values.

Optical Illusions and Value Perception

  • A mid-gray square can appear light when surrounded by darker values, or dark when surrounded by lighter values.
  • Two identical gray squares can appear different when placed on contrasting backgrounds (e.g., white vs. black).

These illusions demonstrate why it's essential to always compare values to their surroundings rather than making assumptions based on what you think you know about an object's inherent lightness or darkness.

Value Keys

Value keys refer to the overall tonal range used in a drawing or painting. Understanding and using value keys can help create mood and focus in your artwork.

Types of Value Keys

  1. High Key: Mostly light values (7-10 on the scale), with darker values as accents.
  2. Mid Key: Centered around middle values (4-7), with some lighter and darker accents.
  3. Low Key: Predominantly dark values (0-3), with lighter values as accents.

When working within a specific value key, it's crucial to maintain consistency. Adding a value that's too light or too dark for your chosen key can break the illusion of form and look out of place.

Creating Value Studies

Practice creating value studies of simple objects to understand how different lighting conditions and surfaces affect value relationships. For example:

  • An egg on a white surface will have a limited value range, mostly in the light to mid-tone area.
  • The same egg on a dark surface will have a broader value range, spanning from light to dark.

The Importance of Value in Artwork

Value is the glue that holds a piece of artwork together. Even with well-drawn lines and shapes, if the values are incorrect, your drawing won't read properly. As you continue to study art, you'll learn how factors like light direction, light strength, and local color affect the values you see.

Creating Your Own Value Scale

To better understand and apply value in your artwork, try creating your own value scale. This exercise will help you recognize and reproduce a range of values, improving your ability to add depth and form to your drawings.

Remember, mastering value is a fundamental skill that will enhance all aspects of your artwork. Practice observing and reproducing values in your everyday sketches, and you'll see a significant improvement in the realism and impact of your drawings.
