Example: Hermes Running

Here's a cool one of Hermes running. There's a lot of drama and movement in it.

  • Observation: The pose feels a bit static.
  • Goal: Make it feel like there's more movement or drama in the way he's running.

Steps to Enhance Movement

  1. Initiate Rhythm on a Diagonal: When making gesture lines, place them dramatically on a diagonal to force a back and forth.
    • This over-exaggeration anticipates the stiffening that happens when forms are added later.
  2. Emphasize the Thrust: Look for two diagonals, the thrust of the ribcage forward and the pull of the pelvis back.
    • Keep it loose and avoid getting too contoured, which can take away rhythm.
  3. Use Wrapping Lines: For foreshortening or indicating cylinders going back in space, use wrapping lines as shorthand.
    • Think of angling a cylinder (like a toilet paper roll) relative to a limb to match its direction.

Adjusting the Limbs

  • Shorten Lines for Foreshortening: Make lines shorter and push the degree of wrapping to create a sense of depth.
  • Chain Rhythms: Use curves to connect one part of the body to another, like bumping into the lower leg and back into the foot.
  • Match Angles for Unity: Tweak limbs to match the direction of others to build a sense of unity and movement.
    • Avoid horizontal lines that can make the pose feel stiff.

Connecting Limbs and Creating Flow

A common issue is how to connect limbs into the starting pathways of rhythms from the torso.

  • Strategize Connections: Decide which lines you'll use to help draw the eye out or make limbs feel integrated.
  • Avoid Contour Dependency: Don't rely solely on contours, think of abstract diagrams and relationships between body parts.
  • Emphasize Rhythmic Bridges: Ensure there's a flow from one area to the next without abrupt breaks.

Using Long Lines for Passive Limbs

  • Passive vs. Active Sides: Differentiate them by creating longer flows for passive limbs.
  • Show More with Less: Use economy in your lines to convey movement efficiently.

Handling Back Views and Symmetry

Back Views

  • Opposite Wrapping Lines: For back views, the wrapping lines are opposite to front views.
    • The pelvis comes back at us, so wrapping lines go accordingly.
  • Integrate Limbs: Use asymmetries in the limbs once you get into them.

Symmetrical Poses

  • Challenging Symmetry: Symmetrical poses can feel static.
  • Use Shape if Necessary: Sometimes, you may need to use shape to convey the figure when rhythm isn't enough.
  • Introduce Diagonals: Even in symmetrical poses, add diagonals with arms or legs to bring interest.

Emphasizing Story and Design

When drawing, think beyond just capturing the twist or foreshortening.

  • Focus on the Story: What's the big idea of the pose? What's compelling about it?
  • Use Rhythm to Convey Emotion: The movement and positioning can tell a story.
  • Design for Unity: Match limbs' angles, create parallels, and think about the overall composition.

Example: Adam and Eve

  • Design Choices:
    • Use diagonals to show movement.

Tips for Practicing Gesture Drawing

  • Avoid Adjusting for Body Type Early On: Treat every body the same in gesture stages.
    • Customize body types with shape later.
  • Focus on Posing and Position: Leave details like muscle shapes and proportions for later stages.
  • Stay in the Flow: Your attention should constantly redirect from one area to the next without interruption.

Common Issues to Avoid

  • Symmetry in Limbs: Beware of making limbs too symmetrical, which can stiffen the pose.
  • Isolated Lines: Don't let lines exist too far from one another, move them up to create rhythm.
  • Attachment Points: Avoid sticking limbs on as shapes, integrate them into the figure's flow.
  • Overemphasis on Contour: Refrain from focusing too much on outer shapes at this stage.
