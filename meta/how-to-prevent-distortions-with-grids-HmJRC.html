Welcome to our review of your work on using grids to help with line directions while focusing on proportions from your orthos. Let's dive into the key lessons from your submissions.

Grids and Line Directions

Grids are meant to guide us with line directions, but some of you got creative and faced challenges. Remember, grids can restrict us, but that's the point, they help maintain accuracy.

Tip: Always ask yourself which part is closest and which is distant, including up and down, to ensure your lines converge correctly.

Common Mistakes

Some of you inadvertently used the grid backward, causing lines to diverge instead of converging toward a vanishing point. If lines are going away from the closest corner, they should aim toward a vanishing point, not away.

Daring to Foreshorten

I encourage you to dare to foreshorten. It's challenging because it goes against what we know, but it's essential for creating dynamic drawings.

For example, drawing objects like wire cutters or cannons in a foreshortened position can look odd if not proportioned correctly. When an object is close to us, the parts closer should appear larger, and those further away should appear smaller. Ensure your boxes or grids reflect this to avoid distortion.

Understanding Perspective and Distortion

Be mindful of wide-angle perspectives and the field of vision. When vanishing points are too close or grids are too wide, distortion occurs.

Tip: If your drawing looks distorted, check if your vanishing points are too close. Spreading them out can reduce distortion.

Estimating Proportions

Learning to see proportions by comparing back and forth between your drawing and ortho references is crucial. It's similar to how we learned to walk, through trial and error. Trust your intuition, if it doesn't look right, adjust it.

Remember: You're not measuring proportions yet, you're estimating them as a preparation for measuring.

Drawing Through and Rough Sketches

Don't be afraid to draw through your forms. Drawing the parts that are not visible helps you understand the object's structure better.

Also, embrace rough sketches and preliminary lines. They are essential steps in refining your drawings.

Handling Complexity

If you're tackling highly developed or complex objects, know that it's a tough challenge. You might encounter more mistakes, but that's part of the learning process. Consider starting with simpler objects to build your foundational skills.

Dealing with Grids

Grids can be overwhelming if they have too many lines. You only need enough lines to guide you. If the grids confuse you, focus on the essential lines that help with direction.

Tip: Ignore unnecessary grid lines initially and refer to them when you need guidance.

Embracing the Learning Process

Learning to draw accurately involves trial and error. Don't be discouraged by mistakes. Every attempt improves your perception and skill. Remember, even if it feels challenging now, with practice, it will become easier.
