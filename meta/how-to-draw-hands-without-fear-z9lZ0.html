Overcoming the Fear of Drawing Hands

Drawing hands can be intimidating, the horror, the fear, the panic. But it doesn't have to be. By drawing lots of hands, you will overcome the anxiety and develop confidence. The hand is powerful and elegant, virtually a figure in itself. It can convey emotion just like the entire human figure. So let's take that fear away, and learn to love drawing hands.

Simplifying the Hand Structure

Basic Shapes and Proportions

Keep the hand as simple as possible. Think of it as a continuation of a hinge-like joint from the arm, then bulge it out on the sides. Remember to make it thicker, especially at the thumb, the thenar eminence.

Comparing Fingers to Legs

Consider the fingers as akin to legs hinging from the metacarpals (the top of the hand). Just as the lower leg hinges from the knee joint, the fingers rock back and forth on the metacarpals. This analogy can help you visualize their movement and structure.

Understanding Knuckles and Phalanges

Knuckles vary in size. The middle finger has the largest knuckle, and they decrease in size toward the little finger, whose knuckle is sometimes barely noticeable. Use a one, two, three counting method to determine finger lengths, with the middle finger being the longest.

Natural Rhythm and Movement of the Hand

Closing and Opening the Hand

Observe the natural rhythm when closing your hand: the little finger curls first, followed by the ring finger, middle finger, and index finger. The thumb folds in last. When opening, the thumb releases first, and the fingers uncurl in reverse order.

The Role of the Middle Finger

The middle finger is the main player in plotting out the other fingers. It's the longest and often the straightest, with other fingers curving toward it. Use it as a reference point, especially in complex poses.

Techniques for Drawing Hands

Using Sticks and Tubes

Start with simple shapes like sticks for the fingers: straight on top, curved underneath. This approach prevents you from getting bogged down in detail. Think of fingers as tubes to help capture their three-dimensionality.

Fanning Out the Fingers

Fingers naturally fan out. When drawing an open hand, use a big fan shape to place the knuckles. Remember, when fingers are spread, the ring and little fingers drop down faster than the other two.

Simplifying Complex Poses

When unsure, be schematic. Capture the characteristic shapes without focusing on details. This method helps you avoid getting lost in complexity and allows you to refine your drawing later.

Drawing Different Types of Hands

Drawing Female Hands

In female hands, knuckles are less pronounced. To convey femininity, taper the fingers from wide at the base to slim at the tip. Minimal emphasis on knuckles and tendons keeps the hand looking delicate.

Drawing Creepy or Gnarly Hands

For a creepy effect, exaggerate features. Elongate the metacarpals and phalanges, emphasize knuckles, and create gaps between fingers. Use pentimento - overlapping lines - to add a sense of vibration and unease.

Tips for Improving Your Hand Drawings

Avoiding Common Mistakes

Don't draw faster than you think. Rushing leads to errors that compound as you try to fix them. Instead, draw slowly and thoughtfully. Be mindful of tangents, points where lines just touch without overlapping, as they can flatten your drawing.

Leveraging Anatomy Knowledge

Remember, there are no muscles in the fingers, only tendons extending from the knuckles down. Understanding the anatomy, like the unique articulation of the thumb due to the radius bone, enhances the realism of your drawings.

Considering Perspective

Think of fingers as tubes in space. Pay attention to how curves indicate perspective. It's easy to misplace curves, so practicing with tubes can help you grasp how fingers foreshorten and overlap.

Practicing and Enhancing Skills

Draw hands from every angle. When you feel overwhelmed, sketch something you're comfortable with to relax your mind. Puzzling out hand drawings keeps your mind keen and prevents cognitive decline.

* * *

Treat the hand as a living thing, personify it. Think of hands that are angry, gentle, or threatening. This approach adds emotion to your drawings. Embrace the challenge, keep practicing, and you'll find that drawing hands becomes a fun and rewarding part of your artistic journey. **Never fear hand day! **
