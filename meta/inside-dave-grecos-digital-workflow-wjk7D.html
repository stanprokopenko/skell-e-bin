Inside Dave Greco's Digital Workflow

Copy Code

Starting with Thumbnails

I've been a concept artist for over 23 years, currently working as a senior character concept artist for World of Warcraft at Blizzard. When tackling an illustrative piece, like one I did for Magic the Gathering, I begin with thumbnails. I usually submit three to four thumbnails to the art director, aiming for compositions that read well and fit the required ratio. Once a thumbnail is approved, I enlarge it to the canvas size I want to work on.

Refining the Sketch

The initial sketch is often a total mess, it's loose and chaotic. To make sense of it, I start working over it with a cleaner line drawing and figure out all the moving parts. I'm not too concerned about exact anatomy or final rendering at this stage. It's about getting the perspective, the background elements, and the overall layout in place.

Building Layers and Blocking In

I work in small steps, layering elements even if I don't know all the colors yet. For complex pieces with many components, like crowds or effects, I use separate layers to block things in. This approach allows me to adjust elements easily and ensures I'm not wasting time rendering parts that might change later.

Collaborating with Art Directors

Throughout the process, I frequently send stages over to the art director. It's crucial to get feedback on the layout and placement of elements before diving into detailed rendering. This collaboration helps avoid unnecessary work on areas that may need significant adjustments.

Working with Reference

Getting certain elements right, especially hands, is vital. I shoot a ton of hand reference myself, my phone is filled with photos of my own hands in various poses. If something feels off, I make sure to find reference instead of trying to make it up. It's important to spend the time to get these details correct, it shows dedication and improves the overall quality of the piece.

Balancing the Composition

I strive to work on the entire piece simultaneously, balancing all elements as I go. I pay attention to how the viewer's eye moves around the piece, ensuring there's a good flow and that the shape design is appealing. I also look for interesting negative space and avoid awkward tangents between elements.

Adding Effects Carefully

When adding effects, like magical energies or environmental elements, I proceed with caution. Effects are placed on their own layers so I can adjust them without affecting the rest of the piece. It's easy to overdo effects, so I focus on enhancing the piece without losing focus on the main subjects.

Final Adjustments

As I near completion, I make final tweaks to enhance the piece. Using tools like Curves adjustments in Photoshop, I can punch up the values to make the image pop. I look at the overall value range to ensure there's a good spread between the darkest darks and lightest lights, without overexposing the image.

The Importance of Iteration

Throughout the process, if something doesn't feel right, I keep coming back to it until it does. I remind myself of artists like John Singer Sargent, who would redo a hand or finger multiple times to get it right. Stopping to fix issues, no matter how small, is a big part of achieving a successful piece.

Considering Composition for Different Formats

When creating art for different formats, such as card games versus large prints, I adjust my composition choices. For smaller formats like Hearthstone or Magic the Gathering, it's important that characters are instantly recognizable, with clear silhouettes and dynamic poses. For larger pieces, there's more flexibility to render characters in detailed and nuanced ways.

* * *

My workflow is about taking small, manageable steps, continuously refining, and not being afraid to fix what's not working. By balancing all elements, collaborating with art directors, and paying attention to details like anatomy and composition, I navigate through the challenges of creating concept art from sketch to final illustration.
