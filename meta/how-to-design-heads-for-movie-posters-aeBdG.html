Why Design Is Important

You might be wondering, "Why design? Why is design important? I just want to draw Tom Cruise, why is design important?" Well, in this business, every movie poster has to really grab your attention, and design is the main tool to do that.

As artists, we're required to be not only very good at drawing faces and characters, but we also have to be very fast. It's a high-demand business, and design is the main tool to help me be fast, accurate, and get that killer likeness. Our main responsibilities as artists are to get the likeness and communicate the story and the feeling of our movie.

Design is simply the ability to organize shapes. When we're designing heads and faces for movie posters, there are really three main shapes that I look at:

  • Primary shape
  • Secondary shapes
  • Detail shapes

Understanding Shapes in Head Design

Major Shapes

The major shapes are things like the outline, the silhouette, and the contour. That's really important. Things like the head type, we want to make sure we get that to look and feel like our character or actor.

Secondary Shapes

Secondary shapes are things like planar shapes, the shape of the eye socket, and maybe a little bit of the underside of the nose. The nose itself could be a big secondary shape, and also the shape of the hair relative to the shape of the face, and things like costume pieces. Those are the big secondary shapes.

Detail Shapes

Finally, the detail shapes are the smaller shapes, specifically shapes of value that I look for, light and dark, specifically things that tend to be very dark naturally. For example, the eyebrow, the pupil, the shadow under your nose, and the underside of your lips. All of these together can help us create the full picture of the portrait that we're trying to draw.

Homework Exercises

Now it's your turn. To help you improve these skills, here's some homework exercises I recommend.

Warm-Ups: Sketch Head Types and Head Shapes

Before you do every exercise, do some warm-ups. Just sketch some head types and head shapes, and you can exaggerate and play with them, just to get yourself warmed up.

Study the Contour: The Silhouette

Learn to train your eye to see the outer shape, the silhouette. Remember, that's the most important foundation of our drawing. So, study the contour. You don't have to draw any detail. You don't have to do any finished drawing. I recommend doing them fairly small so you can do a lot. You can jump on a Pinterest page or a folder of your own reference that has a bunch of faces, a bunch of actors or characters, and just start to practice and get a good head type first, and exaggerate and get the nuance and character of each one.

'Poster Vision' Exercise

The third exercise is what I call poster vision. This is where you start to get into the detail shapes. Once you become familiar with doing the outer shape, the outline, and the silhouette, and getting a good head type, now you want to come in and start doing the detail shapes. These will be the light and dark shapes, mostly things like the brow, the eyebrow, the little shadow under the nose. You can start to get a little bit more specific. Again, don't worry too much about the detail. Just learn to train your eye first. Make them very simple, only black and white. No need to do full-finished drawing or any shading.
