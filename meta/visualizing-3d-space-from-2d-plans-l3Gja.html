The Exercise: Transforming Plans into Elevations

Your final project is a series of puzzles based on one plan. This plan shows you the width and depth but tells you nothing about the height. Your task is to make up the heights any way you want, which can be straightforward or more creative.

Creative Freedom with Heights

You can:

  • Invert positive parts into negative spaces and vice versa.
  • Squash or stretch the height of any portion.
  • Even make one portion float above or below another, connecting them with little wires to clarify their positions.

This exercise is excellent for your brain, and it's not hard if you use a template. Templates are provided to help you get started.

Tracking Heights and Enhancing Illusions

When drawing, use height lines to keep track of elevations. Take your time, every corner counts. Think carefully about which lines are on the other side, and fade them back to enhance the illusion of depth.

Drawing Orthographic Projections

To ensure accuracy, draw the side and front elevations (orthographic projections). You can do this either before or after your oblique views. If you draw the orthos first, conform your oblique view to them, and vice versa. Drawing multiple views stretches your awareness and helps you understand the true proportions.

Exploring Different Views

By changing your starting point, you can explore how variables affect other dimensions:

  • If using a side view, play with the width.
  • If using a front view, play with the depth.

Remember, a draft view is one flat view that needs more to complete it. This is pure three-dimensional thinking.

Practice and Mastery

If you find this challenging, start by copying examples. As you progress:

  • Try creating new elevations based on the given plan.
  • Create your own forms from these plans.
  • See how many variations you can develop.
  • Go fully independent and make up your own plans, but keep them right-angled for now.

Start simply before attempting more complex forms. While some may dive into complicated attempts with bevels, it's beneficial to begin with simple shapes.

Using Isometric Graph Paper

To simplify further, use isometric graph paper:

  • No need for vanishing points, lines are at 60°, 90°, or 120°.
  • Foreshortening is predictable - you can count the units.
  • However, isometric perspective lacks the realism we get with vanishing points and only creates forms in a few fixed positions.

Templates and Next Steps

Until you learn to place a plan in perspective on your own, use the provided templates. They fix the position as it spins left to right, allowing you to focus on creating flexible heights.

We'll use this thinking in our next lesson.

Back to Orthographic Views

The three basic views on the three axes are the basic one side at a time truths of how a thing occupies space. Draw from these plans, or if you're ambitious, create an ortho of anything you want to learn to draw from imagination.
