How to Pick the Right Reference Photo

Copy Code

When it comes to painting, selecting the right photo is crucial. Many beginners make the mistake of choosing photos that are too difficult to paint, which can complicate their learning process. It's important to choose photos that make painting easier and more enjoyable. I've spent a lot of time working with lighting models for painting, and I always consider whether a setup will be both enjoyable and feasible to paint.

Challenges with Complex Photos

Beginners often choose challenging photos, which can make their learning process harder. Professional photos might use multiple lights and tricks to make subjects look good, but these techniques don't always translate well to painting. Even experienced artists like myself avoid overly complex lighting situations. In art school, we learned to recognize which photos were suitable for drawing and painting. Drawing and painting require clear, definable light sources. Multiple light sources can confuse shapes, values, edges, and colors, making it difficult to create a successful painting.

Characteristics of a Good Reference Photo

A good reference photo should have clear, definable light sources. Photos with clear light and shadow patterns are ideal. A single light source with distinct edges and shapes makes it easier to judge light direction and see edges. This helps in creating a painting with depth and interest. For example, John Garrity's portrait was painted with a single soft light, providing clear light and shadow separation. Sargent's paintings often used lighting that allowed for artistic interpretation rather than copying every detail.

Common Mistakes in Photo Selection

One common mistake is choosing photos with evenly lit faces, which lack the separation of lights and shadows, making it difficult to create depth and interest. Photos with multiple light sources can create a confusing mix of light and shadow, making it hard to analyze and paint. Artificial lighting can overpower natural light, eliminating shadows and making it impossible to paint effectively. Flat lighting with multiple light sources results in a lack of clear shadow patterns, complicating the painting process.

Tips for Selecting Photos

When selecting photos for painting, look for clear shadow patterns and avoid those with half the face in shadow. Large reference photos are preferable as they allow for better analysis of light and shadow when squinting. Overhead lighting can create interesting shadow patterns, such as shadows in eye sockets, adding mystery to the painting. Easy-to-paint photos should have clear lighting and shadow patterns, making the painting process enjoyable.

Differences Between Photography for Art and General Photography

Good photography for artists differs from photography for photographers. It focuses on clear light and shadow patterns rather than just aesthetic appeal. A photo might look good but not have the right lighting for analysis and painting. Sargent's use of lighting allowed for artistic interpretation rather than detail copying. Effective lighting for painting should not cut the model's face in half with shadow and light but should provide a balanced light-to-shadow ratio.

In conclusion, choosing the right photo for painting is about finding one with clear light and shadow patterns. This makes the painting process easier and more enjoyable, allowing for artistic interpretation and creativity.
