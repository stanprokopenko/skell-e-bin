The Mnemonic Approach

When I need to draw something unfamiliar, I like to use a mnemonic approach. This means I liken pieces I don't understand to things I'm familiar with. It makes it easier to grasp what I'm looking at and helps me remember the shapes when I'm drawing.

Breaking Down a Bike into Familiar Shapes

Bikes are so hard to draw. I really don't enjoy drawing bikes, but they look cool and add a lot of personality and atmosphere to a piece. So let's give it our best shot.

The Front as a Helmet with Antlers

In the photo I'm working from, the front of the bike looks like the front of a biker helmet to me. Thinking of it that way, like a sharp helmet with horns or antler ears sticking out, helps me remember the shape. If I can just get something close, that's great.

So I imagine an alien helmet with antler ears. That's a shape I can remember because I've thought of it in terms I already know. If I ever want to draw a helmet again, I know I'm just drawing a human head shape with those large ears and antlers.

Humanizing the Bike's Body

Continuing that metaphor, I think of the neck and chest of this character. The license plate becomes the collarbone, and the lights are like the pecs on a chest. On these speedier-looking bikes, the chest comes to a more flat face.

I start thinking about how the design is implemented on a bike. The piece of machinery that connects to the wheel is like the stomach of our armor that's been hollowed out. When I'm drawing, I remember that I'm drawing this piece with the intention of a wheel being either laid into it or jutting out from it.

Understanding and Simplifying Complex Parts

The Wheel as Underwear

From another angle, the shape looks like underwear. So the bike's body is like the torso, and the area where the wheel fits is like underwear that houses the wheel. I'm not going to do anything with the wheel, come on, guys, I can only go so far!

I know from experience that on top of the wheel, there's usually a cover. That's not conceptually difficult for me to understand that needs to happen. So from the top view, the shape looks like this, and that's underwear. These are legs, and the long crotch leads up to the torso. This is my Titan.

Detailing Tires and Wheels

Tire textures are generally all the same insofar as, for an illustration, we only really need to remember that it has a pattern and usually a middle area. I'm not doing a fantastic job here, but this is research where I'm noting these are the things to remember to include.

Filling in the Gaps

Both of these bikes have a really big butt for storage, I assume. It's just this suitcase thing connected via some rods. Sometimes, we don't see enough to make educated guesses at what it should be, but knowing that the shape ends with a somewhat larger rounded-off box might be enough.

If I'm drawing it from an angle and think it just ends in a big box, as well as some kickstand-type pieces, that might be sufficient. I might assume there's a wheel much like the front one on the back. I'm missing the middle section, but if I literally just connect the dots and fill in this middle area with pieces of what I'm seeing, I get the rough idea of a bike, and that might be enough for my drawing to be convincing.

The Importance of Research

That was a breakdown of how I do research. Just remember, guys, that this is a step you don't want to skip because it's building that foundational knowledge you're going to use to play with when you get to the illustration. It's actually fun to draw from imagination.
