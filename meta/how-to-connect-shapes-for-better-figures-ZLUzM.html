How to Connect Shapes for Better Figures

Stacking Goals

I like to stack a few goals whenever I draw. I focus on:
- Connective strategies (curves and corners, beanbags and drumsticks)
- Simple forms (balls, boxes, and tubes)
- Mark making (aiming for more beautiful lines)
- Strong shape design

These goals help me measure my success. For example, if I’m working on connecting forms, I might not worry as much about the prettiness of my lines at first. But once I feel good about one area, I add on another focus.

Basic Construction

T for the Torso

I often start by drawing a T-shape for the shoulders. I compare this shoulder line to a vertical or horizontal guide to keep it aligned. This helps me build a solid foundation before adding detailed shapes.

Egg for the Rib Cage

Then I draw an egg shape for the rib cage. It doesn’t need to be anatomically perfect. I might trim a bit off the top where the shoulder girdle meets the rib cage. Sometimes I visualize two eggs for each side, or I start with a two-dimensional side shape and refine it later.

Defining the Waist and Stomach

As I move down, I note the waist, the stomach, and where these meet the rib cage and hips. Little markers, like the crotch or belly button, help me stay accurate. I lighten earlier lines if they get messy and adjust as needed.

Using Overlaps

When a limb or another form overlaps the torso or hips, I try to see the complete form behind it. I sometimes lean or move to a different vantage point so I can visualize the full shape. Even if it’s hidden, drawing it lightly helps keep my perspective correct.

Refining Shapes and Proportions

I treat each region as simple forms (eggs, tubes, or boxes). Once I have these, I compare each new piece to the others. I look for overlaps and corrections to maintain good proportions:

  • Egg for the shoulder
  • Tube for the arm
  • Beanbag for the torso

With every layer of detail, I refine shape and proportion a bit more.

Adding Character

After laying in generic forms, I start adding characteristic details. A slight bulge for the stomach or a clearer waistline helps the figure feel more natural. This also helps me check if my proportions match the pose I see.

Visualizing Complete Forms

When I imagine each segment in 3D, it becomes easier to render or shade later. For instance, if I’ve drawn the shoulder as an egg, I can practice shading a single egg to improve my light and shadow skills.

Next Steps

Now that I have a torso structure, I’m ready to attach the legs. I’ll keep building toward a full pose and, eventually, explore how to light and shade these forms.
