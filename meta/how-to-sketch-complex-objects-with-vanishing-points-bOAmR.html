How to Sketch Complex Objects with Vanishing Points

Setting Up the Grid

I'm going to make the anvil float by using an up view in three-point perspective. I start with a grid where lines going away to the right vanishing point go down, and lines to the left vanishing point also go down. The vertical lines are slightly angled due to the third vanishing point placed high above. This setup creates the illusion that we're looking up at the anvil.

Establishing Vanishing Points

To create a mild three-point perspective, I placed the third vanishing point way up on my coffee table. This allows the lines going away to the vanishing points to go slightly up, giving depth to the drawing. By adjusting the position of the third point, we control the degree of the up view.

Constructing the Box

I begin by building a box for the anvil using the grid lines:

  • I decide on the proportion of the front face.
  • I run lines back to the vanishing points to establish depth.
  • I find the centers of the side planes using diagonals.

This helps me locate the center coronal section, the halfway point from front to back and top to bottom.

Finding Key Proportions

Focusing on the transverse section, I:

  • Determine it's slightly above halfway up the box.
  • Use the center lines to establish a square in perspective.
  • Estimate the square by eye, knowing practice improves accuracy.

This square represents the waist of the anvil and is crucial for the overall proportions.

Drawing the Base

To draw the base:

  • I find the halfway point of the front face.
  • Place the front foot slightly in front of this line.
  • Position the back foot appropriately to maintain credible proportions.

Using the grid, I ensure the spacing and sizes of the feet are accurate. The rectangle formed by the feet should be deeper than it is wide, matching the anvil's proportions.

Adding the Feet

I draw the feet by:

  • Connecting the corners to create the base rectangle.
  • Ensuring the other feet are slightly in front of the halfway line.
  • Checking placement with grid lines to maintain accuracy.

This step brings solidity to the anvil's base and grounds it within the perspective grid.

Shaping the Horn

To draw the horn:

  • I find the center top of the box.
  • Mark where the horn touches, starting below the top face.
  • Use a center line and consider the horn as a series of ellipses to capture its roundness.

I draw cross-contour lines to define the form and ensure the widest part of the horn aligns with the anvil's body.

Final Touches

I refine the curves and planes:

  • Emphasize key contours and clean up extra lines.
  • Ensure all lines follow the grid and perspective rules.
  • Add cross-contour lines to enhance the three-dimensional form.

By understanding the planes and using the grid, I make the anvil appear credible and three-dimensional. This method, while complex, becomes intuitive with practice and helps in drawing objects convincingly in perspective.
