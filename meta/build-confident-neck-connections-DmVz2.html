Build Confident Neck Connections

Copy Code

Overview

I like to think of the head and neck as basic shapes fitting onto the torso. Sometimes I use an egg or capsule shape for the head. When it moves into profile, though, I shift to a boxy shape so the face can drop down and the skull can move back without a weak chin. I’ll often add a sailboat triangle at the back of the skull to show that higher back of the head. All of it is fluid and can be rounded off or chiseled later as I refine the head.

Front and Profile of the Head

When I draw from the front or a three-quarter view, I keep the head shape symmetrical. From a profile, I carefully separate the forehead, face, and back of the skull. I sometimes follow a slight curve in the face and skull to maintain a fluid look.

Adjusting Chin and Skull

• I keep the chin defined by a slightly boxy edge if needed.
• The skull is higher at the back than at the front.
• I can adjust curves or corners to achieve a fluid or chiseled look.

Neck, Throat, and Pit of the Neck

When placing the neck, I pay attention to the throat moving forward from the pit of the neck in profile. I visualize a tube or cylinder for the neck, and I find its center line. For a male, it’s wider, for a female or child, it’s thinner. The pit of the neck is usually lower than the shoulders, and the collarbones slope up or swing in small arcs.

Torso Connection

I like to connect the neck to a tube or “coke bottle” torso shape. The shoulders are often wider than the waist, so I see three main segments: 1. Neck tube.
2. Chest and rib cage (the bigger tube).
3. Waist area (which narrows).

I draw through the arms or shoulders if necessary to ensure I understand the complete structure underneath. Then I can place the shoulder girdle and arms on top.

Putting It All Together

In a front view, I keep the arrangement of the neck to the torso simple and symmetrical, adjusting later if the pose shifts. In a profile, I extend the center line straight down through the torso, even if part of it is hidden by the arm or chest. I can play with the shrugging muscles or the sagging triangles around the neck and shoulders, marking out the trapezius or collarbone area.

As I refine, I chisel out planes and shapes, always checking proportions and relationships. I don’t have to make these lines final or perfectly clean right away. It’s enough to feel the main forms and know how they stack. Then I simplify or stylize as needed, whether it’s for a more heroic figure or a softer look.

That’s the process I use to build a wide range of poses, starting with the underlying structure and adding complexity step by step.
