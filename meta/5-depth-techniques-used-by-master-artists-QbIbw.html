Gannon Beck's Norman Rockwell Analysis

Let's look at Gannon Beck's analysis. There's so many good slides here. If you are involved in your own little in-person community where you spend an hour or three going through these on your own with your friends, Gannon posted a Norman Rockwell piece with comments.

Shuffleton's Barbershop by Norman Rockwell is a painting that is many people's favorite, approaching perfection in both concept and execution. While I could go on all day about what I think this painting is saying about the importance of art, and in this case, music, and the human experience, I'm going to get on with the analysis.

Form Analysis

Convergence: 5 out of 5

This painting is set up on a one-point perspective grid. Rockwell placed objects throughout the painting to reinforce the grid, from the magazine rack on the left, the rugs on the floor, and the boards on both the ceiling and the floor. Interestingly, he didn't place the vanishing point at the center of interest, he placed it inside the part of the room we don't see. It creates a bit of mystery. Are there other musicians in the room? Are they playing for somebody? We're left to wonder.

The forms within the shapes all overlap in accordance with the perspective established, and the same goes for the foreshortening.

Value Analysis

The value design of this is brilliant. The light and the shadow are distinctly organized. Look at Rockwell's book Rockwell on Rockwell: How I Make a Picture, and he talks about how much work he puts into planning his value arrangements. It goes beyond perspective, but yes, good observation.

The focal point of the musicians is far from the viewer, and Rockwell avoided the darkest values in this area to help push the figures to the back, that's atmosphere. He's making it so that there's some thickness to that space with the particles in the air, even though it's rendered small.

Compared to the size of the painting, we are drawn to the figures in the light. They are like jewels against a whole bunch of stuff that is not as valuable.

Shape Analysis

Overlapping: 5 out of 5

Rockwell gives us lots of things to look at, the barber's tools, the chairs, the benches, the stove heater, the bucket, the rifle and fishing gear on the shelves, the comic books on the magazine rack, the poster on the wall. Just as there are layers to the painting, Rockwell is making the point that there are layers to this barber. He's an outdoorsman, he is considerate to children, he is patriotic, and he is a musician that likes to make music with his friends after hours.

The framing of the musicians within the doorframe is an inspired choice. He could have filled up the canvas with the figures, as he has in other paintings, but he didn't do that here. Just as the room is the inner sanctum of this shop, we get the sense that music is the inner sanctum of these musicians.

Moreover, Rockwell made the additional choice to show the windowpane. He could have chosen not to do this. This makes the viewer seem a bit voyeuristic. Perhaps the viewer was running an errand on a cold night, we know it's cold because the heater is glowing. Whatever the reason, the viewer was walking by the barbershop, came across a mini concert, and took it in, enjoying music that perhaps wasn't being made for him or her but was found all the same. And though this may be the inner sanctum of this barber, he left the door open.

A lot more can be said about this, but I digress.

Additional Thoughts on Values

There have been a number of things here, but I want to mention another thing about the values. Dark values mean something in a light environment. Light values mean something in a dark environment. You can say that the dark background brings out the light foreground. In this case, he worked the opposite of that. He has mainly darker foreground, and then it is the background that becomes light.

It's as if when you're shining a spotlight on a stage, don't shine it on the stage during the day. N.C. Wyeth did a similar thing like this in one of his Treasure Island illustrations, where the treasure is seen because there is a figure in the foreground who is in such shadow that it accentuates the light and the treasure.

We are moving into compositional studies, that is where we're headed in the next year or three. As you master perspective, you are mastering a technical discipline, and where you go with any technical discipline is to use it creatively. Rockwell is using this creatively.

Diminution

The figures are about the same size as the comics on the magazine rack. Although the figures are the focal point of the painting, they are painted quite small. Rockwell gives us lots of things we are familiar with for size comparisons and to just look at.

No more to say until now, good observations, Gannon.

Another one of you pointed out how having a large object in the foreground gets us into the action. And yes, he could have left out the pane, but that tells us we could reach out and touch it, and we can go all the way through that space.

Danny's Question on Atmosphere

Danny asked for help: "I underperformed on the original gathering assignment, so I gathered some more for this one. I'm not sure I understand atmosphere. Is it when air and space is represented and implied with the horizon?"

Let me tell you this: It is not dependent on the horizon. It can happen in a steam room or in a walk-in freezer within just a few steps.

At its simplest, atmosphere is the amount of fog, or at least the practical effect of it. Let me elaborate. Atmospheric perspective has three specific components, and they all start with the letter C.

  1. Contrast: If you've got a light background and you have a dark thing, and you move back far enough, that darker thing will fade into the background. The same thing happens in the opposite. If the background and the thing in the foreground are different enough in contrast, it will create that illusion of thickness.

  2. Color Changes: When you have green mountains with green trees on them, and you get far enough back from them, they don't look green anymore, they look lavender. The same thing will happen with a mountain of yellow dirt. It's not about the thing changing color, it's about what happens in the air between.

  3. Clarity of Detail: You can see the blades of grass that are right there near your feet, but you cannot see the blades of grass that are on the other side of the yard.

Now, back to Danny's posts. I don't see any difference in the contrast or color saturation from the front to the back. This is an example where you are using perspective and then choosing to leave one of them out.

Sometimes, when there's a fog, a spotlight cuts right through it, that's an exception. But look at how high contrast Hergé, in this Tintin image, made the background. That is a choice. It changes the tone of a picture. It makes it so that instead of having everything fade back, you decide to emphasize shape. But there is also no detail. So, they did not fade back for contrast, they did fade back for the amount of detail we can see in them.

I think there's an analogy in music. Some recording engineers and producers hate reverb on a singer's voice, just let the singer be how they sound. Folk music likes that kind of thing. But then there's another kind of music, where you want that kind of effect. This background is so high contrast and so assertive, it has qualities similar to what heavy metal did. You don't have syrupy backgrounds that connect it all together. You have assertive shapes, assertive sounds that pop out and create a rhythm.

Thank you for your question that led us into more components.
