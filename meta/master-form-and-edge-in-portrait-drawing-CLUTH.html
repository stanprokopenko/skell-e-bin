Transition from Lines to Values

In nature, we don't see aggressive lines; instead, we see transitions of value. When moving from linear drawings to value-based drawings, think of every area of line as one of three types of forms: box, cylinder, or sphere. Each of these forms is associated with a type of edge:

  • Soft edges for spheres
  • Firm edges for cylinders
  • Hard edges for boxes

Understanding Basic Forms and Edges

Recognizing and using basic forms and their corresponding edges is crucial:

  • Box: hard edges
  • Cylinder: firm edges
  • Sphere: soft edges

For example, parts of the face can be visualized as different forms: - The head as a ball - The nose as a box - The eye as a ball

Types of Light and Shadows

Lighting involves understanding various types of shadows and their effects on forms:

  • Form shadows: the darkest part of the shadow caused by light hitting the form.
  • Core shadow: describes the form and its turn.
  • Cast shadows: create sharp points and abrupt separations.

There are eight conditions of light to consider: 1. Highlight 2. Crest Light 3. Halftone 4. Core Shadow 5. Shadow 6. Reflected light 7. Accent 8. Cast

Application to Facial Features

When applying these principles to facial features, consider the following:

  • Forehead: Seen as a cylindrical form; track the light-shadow difference.
  • Eye socket: Visualize as a box; keep edges sharp to get a sense of turn.
  • Nose: Can be seen as a cylinder; soften edges to avoid harsh lines.
  • Cheekbone: Interpreted as a cylinder or box; separate light from shadow with a gradation.
  • Chin: Visualized as an egg with a core shadow blending into the jowl fat.
  • Jaw: Shadow feels more cylindrical and abrupt.
  • Lips: Map with form and cast shadows in mind.

Techniques for Realistic Light and Shadow Effects

To create realistic light and shadow effects, follow these techniques:

  • Use edges to move the viewer's eye across the surface of the form.
  • Design shapes based on the underlying structure.
  • Light the bigger forms first before focusing on smaller details like the pupil and iris.
  • Gradual gradation indicates slower timing and softer form.
  • Lost edges occur where light weakens, creating atmospheric or compositional effects.
  • Focus on big forms and core shadows before detailing.
  • Value composition involves mapping large values relative to form and lighting.
  • Avoid big outlines; use edges to define form.
  • Detail one eye to create a focal point, keeping the rest softer.
  • Consistent shadow mapping around the neck and face can include weaker cast shadows.
  • Final drawing should have ghosted lines and planes, leaving good scaffolding for tone decisions.

By understanding and applying these principles, you can create drawings with depth and realism, effectively transitioning from lines to values and mastering the use of light and shadow.
