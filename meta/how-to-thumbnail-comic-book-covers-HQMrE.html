How to Thumbnail Comic Book Covers

Copy Code

My name is Eric Gist (or E.M. Gist, as you'll often see me credited). Today, I'm working on the ideation or idea development phase of a comic book cover. You'll often hear this called thumbnailing because these are tiny sketches, like a thumbnail, focusing on the design problem more than anything else.

At this stage, I'm not too worried about drawing, anatomy, or even perspective, though there's a bit of perspective involved. Instead, I'm aiming to capture what's in my mind's eye in a very simple, three-value design. I keep coming back to design and graphic design, it's about shape dynamics, silhouette, and telling the story we're trying to tell. I'm using these silhouettes as a vehicle to tell the story, focusing on how light against dark can pop one thing in front of another, or push it behind.

Setting Up the Template

I'll be working in Procreate, and I already have a template for the cover. I may manipulate it a bit because they give you some freedom, especially on variant covers. They've also provided me with a character sheet to ensure we're using the correct designs, the most contemporary or modern designs from the actual book.

The characters have specific patterns on their costumes, which will influence the overall value breakdown or design of the image. For instance, their shoulder pieces, boots, and belts will affect how I approach the light and dark patterns in the composition. I consider all these elements as I start designing because they play a crucial role in how the characters stand out against different backgrounds.

Defining the Story and Composition

I start by creating a layer to draw on and select the Peppermint pencil under sketching. It's the one that feels most like a real pencil and allows me to transition into digital from my traditional background as smoothly as possible.

My initial idea is to exhibit the individuality of each of the Fantastic Four while also showing how they work together as a team. I want to illustrate how each member's unique personality and characteristics enhance the whole, the whole being more than the sum of its parts.

Rough Perspective

I don't worry too much about perspective, but I want a rough feel of what I'm working with. The image I have in my mind is kind of a three-point perspective, so I loosely get this in. One of the nice things about Procreate is that I can set up a perspective grid under the canvas settings, which is really handy.

Placing the Characters

I start to figure out where everyone is in the scene:

  • Reed Richards (Mr. Fantastic): Up front piloting the Fantasticar. He's the leader of the family, and I want to reflect that.
  • Sue Storm (Invisible Woman): At Reed's right side in the right pontoon, symbolizing her role as his wife and partner.
  • Ben Grimm (The Thing): In the back, covering everyone's butt. I like the idea of him relaxing with his feet up, showing his laid-back personality and his large size.
  • Franklin Richards: In the near-side pontoon, serving as the catalyst of the action in the piece.
  • Johnny Storm (Human Torch): Off on his own, flying alongside the Fantasticar, emphasizing his rebellious nature while still being part of the family unit.

Working with Values

I keep the sketches small because I want the design to read at the smallest possible size. People might see it as a small icon on the Marvel Unlimited app, so it needs to appeal on a very simple design basis.

To keep things simple, I limit myself to three values:

  • Light Value
  • Medium Value
  • Dark Value

With white, this gives me four values to work with. I create little value squares in the corner of my canvas to remind me, and I use the color picker to select them as needed.

Painting the Background

On a new layer, I use the lightest value to paint the background buildings. I leave the top planes of the buildings white, which helps establish the perspective. I'm not getting too detailed, just enough to give the impression of a city in three-point perspective.

Silhouetting the Fantasticar

On another layer, I use the medium value to silhouette the entire Fantasticar. This helps it stand out against the lighter background and establishes the main shapes in the composition.

Adding the Human Torch

I include Johnny Storm flying around the Fantasticar, following the same direction but off on his own. This emphasizes his independent, rebellious nature while still being connected to the family.

Refining the Design

Now, I start to tighten up the drawing and include more storytelling elements. I move from thumbnail to a slightly more detailed sketch to provide more information and make decisions about the composition.

Storytelling Elements

  • Franklin: He's the catalyst of the action. I have him losing his ice cream cone due to Reed's crazy driving. He's open-mouthed, screaming in surprise.
  • Reed Richards: Piloting the Fantasticar with one hand, while his stretchy arm reaches back to catch Franklin's ice cream cone. This shows his multitasking abilities and his role as a caring father.
  • Sue Storm: She's amused by the situation, smiling as she observes her family. Her hair is blowing across her face.
  • Ben Grimm: Relaxing in the back with his feet up, possibly munching on popcorn. This shows his laid-back personality and adds humor to the scene.
  • Johnny Storm: Flying around the Fantasticar, possibly leaving a trail to show his path. He's high energy and always moving.

Enhancing Silhouettes and Shadows

I focus on silhouetting the characters within their compartments. The dark portions of their costumes, like the shoulder pieces, help them stand out against the lighter parts of the Fantasticar or the sky.

I use cast shadows to create depth and enhance the silhouettes. For example, Reed's arm casts a shadow that conveniently connects with the dark part of his costume, helping it stand out.

Adjusting Values

I consider making Sue's hair white to help it pop against the background. This also creates a connection between her and Johnny, who appears as a bright flame. I adjust shadows and highlights to ensure each character reads clearly and stands out in the composition.

Balancing the Composition

I like the balance of curvilinear shapes with rectilinear shapes, and the dynamic juxtaposition of the Fantasticar not aligning perfectly with the city. The city provides stability, while the Fantasticar adds movement. This balance of unity and diversity creates a dynamic and exciting composition.

* * *

Now that I bring the title back into the composition, it looks like a cover to me. The dynamics, storytelling elements, and movement are all present. It captures what I saw in my mind's eye, and I'm happy with how it's turned out.

There's:

  • Excitement and Movement
  • Family Dynamics
  • Individual Personalities and Powers
  • Iconic Elements like the Fantasticar and the Baxter Building

Most importantly, it reads strongly, which is exactly what I'm looking for in my thumbnails. If I chose to develop this further, I could enhance it with more drawing, adding highlights, reflections, and details to make it pop even more. But for now, it serves its purpose as a solid foundation for a cover.
