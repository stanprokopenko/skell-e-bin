The Role of Exaggeration in Figure Drawing

Every single drawing I do uses some form of exaggeration. The vast majority of figurative art created by masters, whether it's classical drawing or painting or contemporary comics and illustration, employs exaggeration in some way. Exaggeration adds drama, dynamism, and movement to a drawing, making it more engaging and expressive.

Starting with the Primary Action Line

I begin with the primary action line, intentionally making it more extreme than what I see in the pose. For instance, I'll start with the line from the top of the rib cage down to the leg projecting backward on our right. But I don't simply capture the direction of the back leg; I extend it out further and push it in a more extreme direction, as if the model were deepening her lunge even further.

Remember, the tendency of the human brain is to stiffen the pose. Often, when we feel like we're exaggerating, we're simply capturing what's actually there.

Making Selective Exaggerations

It's important to remember that not every part of the figure needs to be exaggerated. You want to make decisions about which elements to push and which to keep as they are. For example, I might draw the width of the torso and the bowl of the pelvis pretty much as I see them. The torso will already seem exaggerated because I tilted the rib cage back. I don't need to also exaggerate the tilt of the pelvis.

This isn't a hard and fast rule; it's just how I'm reacting to a particular pose. That's why it's critical to practice gesture drawing a lot—to familiarize yourself with the body, to experiment, and to see what kinds of exaggerations will work.

Experimentation and Practice

As I'm fleshing out the torso and moving on to other parts of the body, if a part is a little curved, I'll make it much more curved. For example, the arm in the reference photo may be a little straighter, but in my drawing, it curves much more. I might also extend the legs, making them longer than they appear in the reference photo. Extending the length of the legs is incredibly common in both fashion design illustration and comic book drawing.

These kinds of gesture drawings should not be thought of as precious drawings that need to be finished or resolved. They are experiments. Some of them will work, and some of them won't, and that's perfectly fine. But the more of these kinds of exercises you do, the more comfortable you'll become with exaggeration. You'll be able to tell in advance what kinds of exaggerations will work in a drawing and which should be avoided. There's no concrete list; it's a very subjective process.

Extended Gesture Lines for Movement

I often overemphasize certain features, like the curvature of the clavicles, and extend the gesture lines well beyond the figure. Extending gesture lines is an interesting kind of exaggeration. It's a technique I use to suggest movement, fluidity, and dynamism outside of the figure. Extended gesture lines operate almost like motion blurs. They can give the viewer a dramatic sense of time and movement. Even in my more realistic figure drawings, I tend to leave these kinds of extended gesture lines ghosted in the background.

Examples of Exaggeration in Drawings

Exaggerating Curves and Angles

In one of my quick studies, which took somewhere between 7 to 10 minutes, I drew the leg on our right extending back much further than it actually appeared, and I curved it much more. Real human legs don't curve this way, or at least not this dramatically. I've tried to exaggerate the curvature just enough to add drama and dynamism to the drawing without making the leg appear broken.

Similarly, the arm coming in front of the torso has dramatized and exaggerated curves. The forearm has a bend that's not naturally found in real people. Real forearms curve somewhat, but I've exaggerated this curve for dramatic effect. Is it realistic? No. Is it dramatic? Yes.

The Impact of Quick Experimental Drawings

Because this is a quick study, I feel much more free to experiment. Some of these experiments work, and others don't—that's to be expected. But what I've found overall is that these kinds of quick, experimental gesture drawings yield some of my very favorite results. These drawings have a vitality to them that you just don't find in more finished work. In fact, the unfinished quality of these drawings is what makes them so compelling to viewers.

When we look at this quick study up close, we can see how rough some aspects really are. But we can also see numerous dramatic enhancements:

  • Every curve in the outer contour is curved just a little more than observed in reality.
  • The contrast between light and shadow is increased, dramatizing the overall lighting effects.
  • Nearly every line in the drawing was drawn quickly and passionately.

This is important because most viewers can tell whether a line was drawn quickly or slowly. Viewers tend to prefer lines that were drawn quickly; they evoke a more emotional response.

Enhancing Viewer Engagement with Unfinished Elements

Leaving parts of a drawing unfinished invites the viewer to participate in the illusion. Most viewers love the feeling of their minds assembling images of a figure from pigments smeared across a page.

In a more finished drawing that took between 30 and 45 minutes, I used these same techniques. The arms are slightly elongated, the curvatures of the limbs and contours have been enhanced, and the light and shadow have been dramatized to make the musculature appear more dramatic. Wild gesture lines flow freely around the drawing, giving it an enhanced sense of movement and dynamism.

Personal Reflections on Exaggeration

As someone who absolutely loves drawings, I actually prefer dynamic drawings like this to more finished ones. Often, a drawing that's too finished or too realistic appears uninviting to me. This is purely subjective, but ask yourself: Would your drawings be improved by having more drama and more dynamism? If your answer is yes, then experiments with dynamic exaggeration are an excellent way to achieve that.

Encouragement to Experiment

I wanted to give you a sense of some of the creative things you can do with gesture drawing. Experimenting with gesture drawing, using different materials, and drawing the figure in new ways are all excellent approaches to have new experiences with the figure. The lessons you've learned should allow you to push your figure drawings to new creative and expressive areas.

Exaggeration can dramatically improve your figure drawings by adding dynamism and action. But there's so much more to learn if you want to draw dynamic figures. Practice, explore, and most importantly, experiment to develop your unique artistic voice.
