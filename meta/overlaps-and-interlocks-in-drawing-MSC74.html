Overlaps and Interlocks in Drawing

Understanding Connective Strategies

I like to think of beanbags and drumsticks as more sophisticated ways to connect forms in figure drawing. Before diving into these, I usually start simple with curves and corners:

  • Curves when forms flow in a similar direction along their long axis.
  • Corners when there's a big directional change.

Examples:
• Hips and rib cage often connect with a curve.
• Shoulders and upper arm can connect off a corner if they turn sharply.

What Are Beanbags?

A beanbag groups two or more shapes in a shared “sleeve” that blends on one side and separates on the other. This creates an overlap, which helps show depth. I imagine “bags” containing different forms, where they merge smoothly on one side and pull apart on the opposite side. That overlap gives my drawings a sense of three-dimensionality.

Introducing Drumsticks

The drumstick approach goes beyond overlaps, and creates an interlock. One form doesn’t just sit in front of another, it intrudes into it. I can think of a ball-and-stick combination,

  • A ball (the drum) in front of another shape.
  • A tube (the stick) inserts partly into the ball’s contour.

It’s like fitting a key into a lock, some of the tube’s sides break the ball’s outline, so they become interlocked.

Overlaps and Interlocks

With overlaps, I see one shape simply covering part of another, which convinces the eye there’s depth. With interlocks, the shapes share space more dynamically. This is harder to master, but it’s very effective for understanding and depicting strong three-dimensional structure.

Using Deep Perspective

When the perspective is shallow, sometimes simple bumps with curves and corners are enough. But when forms are drawn in deep perspective, overlaps and interlocks become invaluable. One shape is significantly closer, and the other is much farther back. The inserted form reveals the contour of both objects more clearly.

Variations and Figure Examples

I can treat the rib cage, waist, and hips in many ways:

• Female figures often show a narrower waist interlocking with fuller hips.
• Male figures may feature a broad rib cage that tapers toward the waist.

I might imagine a big egg for the hips and a smaller egg for the rib cage, with a tube (curved or straight) interlocking those two. I can also switch to box-like shapes if that better describes the forms. Sometimes I see these shapes in the organic joints of trees or in figure drawing references (Frank Frazetta’s women are good examples, and Bridgman’s books show similar form ideas).

Putting It All Together

My goal is always clarity. If I’m confused, I go back to curves and corners to keep it simple. But if I need a stronger sense of depth, I’ll use beanbags or drumsticks:

• Beanbags: Overlapping shapes in a single “sleeve.”
• Drumsticks: One shape interlocking with another, giving a deeper perspective.

These strategies help me build figures with a vivid sense of structure and space. By practicing them, maybe sketching torsos from reference, playing with perspective, and watching for how one form folds into another, I can capture the figure’s volumes more convincingly.
