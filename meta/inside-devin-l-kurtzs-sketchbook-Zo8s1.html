Inside Devin L. Kurtz's Sketchbook

Studying from Reference

My sketchbook is my practice zone, different from an art sketchbook where I work on finished pieces. Here, I focus on studying from reference to memorize material. I fill it with sketches of plants, motorcycle gear, animals, and everyday objects. I draw from life, like my 40 house plants, and from resources like Pinterest and specialized Instagram accounts.

Building a Visual Library

I believe in drawing many versions of the same thing to expand my visual library. This helps me avoid drawing generic objects. For example, I don't want to draw just a chair, I want a chair that has attitude, a history, and tells you something about the character. To achieve this, I study various lamps, chairs, glass bottles, and other items by browsing furniture stores, collecting references.

Telling Stories Through Objects

Objects can say so much about a character. I explore how different designs can reflect personality. For instance, I love drawing chairs because they can reveal a lot about who uses them. What kind of chair would you sit in if you were an animated version of yourself? I think about these details to enhance the storytelling in my work.

Learning from Other Artists

I study the works of other artists, like Milt Kahl's hands from classic animations and Glen Keane's Tarzan hands. Hands can express so much emotion and weight. I do studies in pen because its permanence makes me think carefully about each line. Mistakes become learning opportunities, helping me understand where I can improve.

Embracing Mistakes with Pen

Working in pen forces me to accept mistakes and move on. By leaving errors on the page, I can look back and identify patterns in my drawing that need attention. It encourages me to be less finicky and more decisive with my lines.

Practicing Compositions

Once I feel confident drawing an object, I create compositions with it. I practice arranging objects, considering big, medium, and small shapes, and varying their height, width, sharpness, and roundness. Composing a collection of objects is similar to arranging a lineup of characters. It's about creating a pleasing and balanced arrangement.

From Studies to Imagination

I love drawing reptiles, especially when turning them into dragons for my books. Studying animals like geckos, bearded dragons, and sailfin lizards informs my creature designs. By understanding their shapes and forms, I can draw them from imagination, pose them dynamically, and inject personality into them.

Storing Knowledge Internally

I don't often look back at my sketches. Instead, I focus on storing the knowledge internally. The process of drawing helps me build muscle memory and a mental library that I can draw upon when creating original work.

* * *

My sketchbook is a place for exploration and learning. By filling it with diverse studies and embracing the process, I expand my abilities and bring more depth to my art. Whether it's through studying objects, learning from other artists, or turning reptiles into dragons, each sketch helps me grow as an artist.
