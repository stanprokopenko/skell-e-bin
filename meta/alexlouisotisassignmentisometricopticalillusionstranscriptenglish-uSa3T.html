Simple Geometric Illusions

Optical illusions often play with our perception using simple shapes and lines. For example, two lines may look different in length but are actually the same length. Recognizing these patterns helps you appreciate and create your own illusions.

Transforming Shapes into 3D

Start with basic shapes like six triangles meeting at a point. By thickening certain lines, you can make a flat shape appear three-dimensional. Adjusting line contrasts tricks the eye into seeing depth where there is none.

Flipping Perspectives

Changing which lines are emphasized can make a shape flip its orientation. A block can appear as if you're looking down on it or up at it, depending on the line contrasts. This works well with isometric perspectives where there are no vanishing points.

Drawing Techniques

  • Use tools like a T-square, triangles, or graph paper to keep angles precise.
  • Rough it out first to avoid mistakes, it spares you pain.
  • Trace over your outlines, swapping which lines overlap to change the visual effect.
  • Try using bond paper with a light box or vellum for easy tracing.

Exploring and Creating Illusions

Study artists like Sandro del Prete, who create complex optical illusions. Analyze their work to understand how they manipulate perspective. Experiment with isometric and oblique projections used in architecture for more ideas.

Tips for Success

  • Don't multitask , this work requires concentration.
  • If you get mentally exhausted, take a break and revisit later.
  • Play and experiment - it's like solving a visual puzzle.
  • Remember, rough it out first, it spares you pain.
