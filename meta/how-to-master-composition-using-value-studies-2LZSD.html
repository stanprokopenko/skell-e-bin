Just to remind you, the assignment was to take these master paintings and study their compositions by simplifying the values to just two or three: light and dark or light, mid, and dark. For level two, you were to set up your own still life and do the same from that.

Let's explore how to approach value simplification and analyze some examples to understand the common challenges and how to overcome them.

Simplifying Shapes and Values

The key to this assignment is to simplify your shapes into big, abstract areas defined by clear value groups. Think of it as reducing the image to its most basic components without getting caught up in the details.

When simplifying:

  • Focus on big areas, not individual objects.
  • Abstract the shapes to basic angular changes and two-dimensional forms.
  • Avoid drawing specific elements like cows, people, or sails. Instead, see them as shapes of light and dark.

Example: Interpreting a Master Painting

When analyzing a painting, squint at it to see the major value groups. For instance, in a painting with a dark shape in the middle surrounded by lighter areas, simplify it to:

  • A big dark shape representing the central focus.
  • Surrounding light or mid-tone areas representing the background or less important elements.

This approach helps capture the overall composition and the big idea of the painting.

Importance of Value Thresholds

Understanding and choosing the right value thresholds is crucial. Value thresholds determine how you group the various tones in the painting into your simplified values.

  • Two-Value Simplification: Decide which areas are light and which are dark. You might have to make choices about what to include in each group based on the painting's context and your interpretation.
  • Three-Value Simplification: Introduce a mid-tone to add more depth. Carefully assign areas to light, mid, or dark values to preserve the painting's intent.

Adjusting Thresholds for Better Interpretation

Sometimes, you might need to shift values to better communicate the painting's story, especially when translating color into grayscale:

  • Shift lighter values darker or darker values lighter to emphasize certain elements.
  • Consider the painting's context and what elements are most important to highlight.

Balancing Details and Abstraction

It's easy to get lost in the details, but for this exercise, you need to fight the urge to include too many details.

  • Start small: Use thumbnails to plan out the composition.
  • Flip the reference upside down: This helps you see shapes and values rather than specific objects.
  • Think abstractly: Focus on the distribution of values and the composition's overall design.

Avoiding Distractions

Details can be sneaky. They might look cool in the reference, but including them can dilute the impact of your value study.

  • Keep your shapes simple and bold.
  • Limit floating shapes: Too many small shapes can make the composition feel scattered.
  • Ensure cohesiveness: Make sure the darks and lights group together in a harmonious way.

Considering Hue and Saturation in Value Studies

Remember that color has three properties: hue, saturation, and value. When simplifying to just values, you can lose some information conveyed through hue and saturation.

  • Bright colors might have the same value as dull colors but look different.
  • Translating color to grayscale requires careful value grouping to maintain the painting's impact.
  • Adjust values if necessary to capture the contrast lost when removing color.

Example: Emphasizing Important Elements

In a painting where a bright sail stands out because of its color, you might need to:

  • Shift the sail's value to make it stand out in grayscale.
  • Adjust surrounding values to ensure the sail remains a focal point.

Strategies for Effective Value Studies

Start Small and Avoid Distractions

Working on smaller thumbnails helps you stay focused on big shapes and values without getting bogged down by details.

  • Use a medium that allows for bold shapes, like a marker or brush.
  • Avoid using tools that make it hard to achieve true darks or lights, like a 0.5 mechanical pencil on large paper.

Flip Reference Images

Flipping your reference image upside down can help you:

  • See abstract shapes instead of recognizable objects.
  • Focus on value distribution without the influence of the subject matter.

Use Gradient Maps for Analysis

A gradient map is a helpful tool to:

  • Quickly analyze the value structure of a painting.
  • Adjust thresholds to see how different groupings affect the composition.
  • Understand how values are distributed across the image.

Common Issues in Value Simplification

Distraction by Details

Including too many details can:

  • Dilute the impact of your value study.
  • Distract from the main value groups.
  • Make it harder to see the overall composition.

Incorrect Value Grouping

Mistakes in grouping values might include:

  • Merging areas that should be separated, losing important silhouettes.
  • Failing to capture the painting's mood, such as making a dark painting too light.

Misinterpreting Value Thresholds

Not setting the correct thresholds can lead to:

  • Loss of important contrasts, like a bright window or a focal point.
  • Overcomplicating the study with too many value groups.

Importance of Overall Composition and Design

Focusing too much on individual elements can:

  • Cause you to miss the big picture.
  • Lead to unbalanced compositions.
  • Neglect the intended visual flow of the painting.

Key Takeaways

  • Simplify aggressively: Focus on big shapes and clear value groups.
  • Choose the right tools: Use mediums that allow for bold value statements.
  • Adjust value thresholds to maintain the painting's intent.
  • Avoid getting lost in details: Keep your focus on the assignment's objectives.
  • Use analysis tools like gradient maps to check your work.
  • Consider the painting's mood and story when grouping values.
  • Practice discipline: Staying focused on specific aspects like value helps improve overall skills.

By applying these principles, you'll improve your ability to simplify complex compositions and understand the underlying value structures that make master paintings so effective.
