The Nine Positions of Viewing an Object

Let's start with a famous chart making the point that there are nine positions from which we can view an object:

  • Directly in front of us, an ortho view
  • To our left or right
  • Above or below
  • Above to our left or right
  • Below to our left or right

That point is pretty much the main point this chart makes. It does not make the point that the degree of tilt could be different. You might want to learn from that.

Observing How Angles Change in Perspective

Observe how the right angles change. When you see two planes, the front corners become acute, the back corners obtuse. When you see three planes, side angles become acute and the front and back become obtuse. Ponder that.

Adding the Depth Axis: From Flat Shapes to Three-Dimensional Forms

Moving from Blocks to More Complex Forms: Arrows

Let's move from blocks to more complicated forms: arrows. Storyboard artists use arrows to show how something will move in the frame.

A flat arrow works for movement from side to side, but if the subject moves directly toward or somewhat toward or away from the camera, it takes an arrow in perspective.

Flat Arrows vs. Oblique Arrows

The flat arrow does not take perspective. It shows movement across the screen, but not toward or away from the camera, just across and up or down or any diagonal.

Oblique arrows can point toward us, whether to left or right, above or below. We are applying what we learned from those nine blocks to a more complex object.

It's more complex because if these are just blocks, we only need two kinds to flip around through all the variations because they're just blocks. But an arrow's point and tail distinction requires two for the going away arrows and two more for the coming toward arrows to get all the variations.

Reviewing the Basic Positions

Let's go through them again:

  • Flat, not coming toward us or going away
  • Coming toward us
  • Going away from us
  • Going away from us at an angle
  • Coming toward us at an angle

Practice: Drawing Arrows in Every Direction

Now here's what I recommend for you. Draw these arrows and name the positions:

  • Above left
  • Above right
  • Above
  • Below left
  • Below right
  • Below
  • At our left
  • At our right

Don't try to get them exact, we'll deal with that later. Instead, pay attention to the direction the X, Y, and Z lines point in each position, so that when you're sketching from imagination, you can call on these basics.

Whatever you draw is likely to be in one of these general positions. Get to know them. Then, when you learn how to spin forms, you can do variations of more and less tilt.

Building a Foundation Before Advancing

But I recommend you get your foundation before going to the next level, which in our case is placing the eye level with a horizon line.
