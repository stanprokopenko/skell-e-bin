How to Draw Characters from Any Angle

Copy Code

Drawing a figure or object from different angles without relying on reference images is a challenge many students face. How can we visualize and render a pose from a new perspective purely from imagination? It's a difficult exercise, but with practice and the right approach, it's possible to gain control over this skill.

My Approach to Visualizing Different Angles

When I started, I was much like everyone else. I would draw from a particular angle, get comfortable with it and repeat it many times. If I needed a different angle, I relied on reference images, if I could find the exact shot. Often, I didn't draw from those different angles at all, and I preferred straight-on views because it allowed me to show the character or object fully without losing information.

I realized that my brain was fighting against positioning the camera differently because it meant losing some visual data due to foreshortening or overlapping forms. To overcome this, I had to accept that changing the viewpoint would inherently alter how much of the subject is visible.

Building a Visual Library

Over time, I built a mental catalog, a visual library, of poses and angles. I think of the character or object as a puppet, and I can manipulate it. By understanding its structure, I can visualize how it looks from different viewpoints. This mental model allows me to draw the same pose from various angles, without reference.

Practicing Different Perspectives

The best way to practice is by constructing and building the figure internally. Initially, this involves a lot of structural drawing and understanding of forms. As you accumulate hours of practice, you'll start to predict how the figure looks from different angles, enabling you to draw it freely without as much construction.

Starting with a Base Pose

I began by sketching a humanoid creature in a particular pose. The goal wasn't to create a finished illustration but to explore and understand the form. It's important to remind ourselves that in practice, the focus isn't on perfection but on learning.

I started with a stick figure, a skeletal structure with lines and anchor points to map out the pose. For this creature, I decided on extended arms, a weird-shaped head, and altered proportions. I used simple lines to represent the spine, torso, limbs, and joints.

Adding Form to the Stick Figure

Once the basic structure was in place, I added forms to give it volume. I transitioned from 2D shapes to 3D forms by incorporating cross-contours. These lines wrap around the forms, giving a sense of depth and dimension. I emphasized areas like the torso, limbs, and head, thinking about how they occupy space.

Imagining Details

Even though I wasn't focused on details, I began to imagine elements like the creature carrying a bag or wielding a club. This helps in visualizing how accessories interact with the form and adds interest to the pose.

Drawing the Pose from Different Angles

With the base pose established, I proceeded to draw the same creature from different perspectives.

Shifting the Camera Position

For the first new angle, I imagined shifting the camera behind the creature to its right. I considered how the pose would look from this viewpoint. Key elements included:

  • Transferring anchor points: Mapping the original points onto the new angle.
  • Spatial awareness: Understanding what's in front, middle, and back.
  • Proportional control: Keeping the proportions consistent by aligning features.

Looking Down at the Figure

Next, I tried a view looking down at the creature. This required visualizing the top surfaces and how the limbs and features foreshorten from this angle. I used the same process of sketching the stick figure, then adding forms and cross-contours.

Looking Up at the Figure

I also experimented with an up-angle shot, where we're looking up at the creature from a low viewpoint. This changes how the ground plane relates to the figure and how the forms overlap.

Recognizing Weaknesses and Strengths

It's important to recognize weaknesses in your sketches. If a particular angle doesn't capture the pose well, redraw it. Conversely, when something works, take note and aim to reproduce that success consistently.

Iterative Sketching

Repetition is crucial. By sketching the pose multiple times from various angles, you improve your spatial understanding and ability to manipulate forms mentally. If a sketch doesn't accurately capture the pose, redraw it. Isolate problem areas and focus on them individually before reintegrating them into the whole.

Sketching vs. Drawing

I often define sketching as a way to understand something, and thumbnailing as exploring ideas. Illustrating or drawing is taking the information you've understood or explored and creating the final piece. By separating these stages, we can focus on learning and problem-solving during the sketching phase without the pressure of creating a perfect illustration.

In professional work, iterative sketching and exploration are essential. When working on client projects, I invest time in loosely sketching and exploring ideas, compositions, poses, and designs. This process helps find the best solution to meet the project's needs.

The Role of Observation

While practicing from imagination is valuable, observing real-life references for different angles is also important. If you find it challenging to visualize a particular perspective, study it from life or photographs. This helps build your visual library and informs your imaginative work.

Tips for Practicing Different Angles

  • Start Simple: Don't jump into extreme perspectives immediately. Begin by rotating the figure horizontally before attempting drastic angle changes.
  • Use Spatial Guides: Employ ground planes, horizon lines, and basic perspective to help place the figure in space.
  • Cross-Contouring: Use cross-contour lines to convey the three-dimensional form.
  • Accept Imperfection: Remember that practice sketches don't need to be perfect. They're tools for understanding.
  • Iterate and Refine: Don't hesitate to redraw poses to improve accuracy and resolve issues.
  • Focus on Understanding: The goal is to comprehend how the figure occupies space from different angles.

* * *

Drawing from imagination involves building a robust visual library and understanding of form. By practicing constructing figures from different angles, you gain the ability to visualize and render poses without relying on references. Remember, it's about exploration and learning, so embrace the process, and over time, you'll develop greater control and confidence in your drawing abilities.
