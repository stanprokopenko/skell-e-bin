Laying the Groundwork

I started off in black and white to establish clear values before introducing color. I used flats to block in the basic shapes and then pushed the volume using a combination of Multiply layers, Screen layers, and normal painting. With these values in place, I’m ready to add color on top.

First Pass with Color Mode

I like to use Color layer mode as a first pass. I create a new layer, set it to Color, pick a general skin tone and fill the figure. This is just a base, nothing is final. I can adjust the color at any time using a hue and saturation slider, for example, pressing Control + U. The goal here is to find a starting point,

• I might also isolate areas, like the eyes or teeth, on their own Color layers to refine them. For instance, I’ll pick a lighter, desaturated color for the eyes and dial back the saturation if it feels too intense.

Adding Variation and Depth

Subtle Color Shifts

Once I have a flat color base, I create more layers to introduce color variation in places like ears, cheeks, or anywhere that needs a different tone. Sometimes I stick to Color mode, but I also love Multiply for shading. Multiply will darken color while preserving the values underneath. This is where I often use an airbrush for subtle transitions, especially around the ears or under the eyes:

• I adjust layer opacity to control how strong the color looks.
• If it becomes too saturated or bright, I can lower the opacity to dial it back.

Iteration and Layer Management

I often duplicate my entire group of layers, merge them, and keep an “archive” version of my progress. That way I can push forward without fear of losing earlier stages. Most of the time, though, I don’t revert to them, I just like the option.

Refining Forms and Cleaning Shapes

At this point, I go back with my normal painting tools (or sometimes the Filthy plugin with its delete option) and tidy edges. I’ll often rotate the canvas to match my natural arm movement when I need to make clean lines with the Lasso tool. Flipping the canvas upside down helps me see abstract shapes, which keeps me from getting stuck in the idea that I’m drawing a face or a nose. I can look for inconsistencies in shapes more easily this way.

Layer Modes for Highlights and Shadows

I use Screen mode for lighter areas and Multiply for darker tones. Both modes let me experiment by choosing different colors for my shadows and lights:

• Multiply: Darkens colors, great for shadows.
• Screen: Lightens colors, perfect for highlights.

I also erase if shadows go too dark or if parts of the highlights feel blown out. And since I’m on separate layers, I can always reduce opacity to back off on intensity.

Building Texture and Details

Hair and Stubble

To suggest stubble, I pick slightly different colors and use Multiply or Color mode to tint the skin in smaller patches. Then, I erase or paint back in a bit at the edges to hint at individual hairs. If it looks too stuck on, I soften or tweak the edges until it blends more naturally.

Teeth and Mouth

I make the inside of the mouth darker with Multiply layers, which helps push it back in space. Front teeth get more light, back teeth get less. I like to keep them from looking too bright or flat. If I need extra color punch, I’ll cut a shape onto a new layer, switch to Multiply, and increase saturation for that area. If it gets too intense, I knock it back.

Subsurface Glows and Special Effects

I sometimes mimic subsurface scattering by using Color Dodge or Screen around thin parts of the skin, like ears or fingertips. On a new layer, I choose a color close to the skin tone, then gently brush where light could pass through flesh. After that, I lower the layer’s opacity until it feels natural.

Additional Rim Light

To finish, I like adding a contrasting rim light. If my main tones are warm, I’ll choose a cool color (like blue) for the rim. I set a layer to Screen and paint a thin highlight on one edge. I might add another thin, almost white accent on the very edge in areas that would be overexposed by a bright light source.

* * *

From laying down the initial black and white values to layering color, it’s a lot of iteration, testing a color, adjusting it, merging layers, refining shapes, and repeating. I can keep going, refining details and shapes, but this workflow already gives me a solid, dimensional result. It’s all about experimenting and exploring different layer modes until I’m happy with the look.
