Laying In the Figure: Gesture and Structure

When I lay in a figure, I focus on understanding both the two-dimensional flat shape (graphic design) and the three-dimensional form. I'm always considering the structure (the volume and solidity) and the gesture (the life and movement). The goal is to find a beautiful shape for each body part and create a connective strategy between them.

Organic structures are mainly water, so they have a watery design, a series of curves and opposing curves. By finding the curves and S-curves along the long axis of any form, I can connect the body parts fluidly.

Simplifying with Curves and Corners

I start by drawing the long axis curves, which capture the gesture and flow down the form. Then, I add the short axis lines, moving across the form to give it volume and structure. Curves represent parts flowing together, while corners indicate where forms break away from each other.

For example, when drawing a tube, I sketch the two sides and the ends. By curving the sides, the tube appears more fluid and alive. The more curves I incorporate, the more dynamic the figure becomes.

Shading Techniques

I apply the same principles to shading. I draw the core shadow along the long axis curve of the form, a soft edge indicating where the form turns away from the light. Then, I fill in the shadow shape, and I make sure it's slightly darker than the light areas. This separation creates a sense of form and depth.

By softening the edges and extending them into gradations, I move the viewer over the form, enhancing the three-dimensional effect. I use a zigzag technique to blend, which can be tight for refined gradations or loose for a painterly feel.

Rendering and Gradation

I render by focusing on the biggest possible forms first, then refining smaller details. I blend over the form (side to side) and along the form (top to bottom) to create smooth gradations. It's essential to keep the shadows unified and darker than the light areas to maintain clarity.

When adding details like ribs or muscles, I use the same process: drawing the long axis curves, then blending and refining. I vary the spacing and intensity to avoid repetition and keep the figure organic and lifelike.

Embracing Mistakes and Personal Style

I often work fast and loose, and I embrace mistakes as opportunities. Drawing multiple lines can convey a sense of action and energy. For me, what's "right" isn't always matching the reference exactly, it's about capturing the essence and movement of the figure.

By redefining what's right for ourselves, we develop our style. Mistakes can lead to wonderful discoveries and add character to our work.

Simplify and Connect

When in doubt, I simplify. Breaking problems into manageable steps makes progress easier, and your audience will appreciate the clarity. Every mark I make, from the beginning to the end, aligns with my thinking about gesture and structure.

Remember, life is connected. The beauty in art often comes from how elements relate to each other. Whether through curves, gradations, or tonal relationships, focusing on connection brings unity and vitality to your drawings.
