Goal

You’re moving from free blobs to blobby heads. You’ll keep things simple, think in 3D, and practice edge control while you shade. You’re not doing a likeness. Proportions don’t matter. You’re inventing simple forms at the same angle and light direction as the model and focusing on how edges reveal the forms.

Start Simple: The Egg

If you want the simplest version, start with a single egg for the whole head.

  • Assume the light is coming from one side. Shade the egg with a clean shadow shape.
  • Add a blobby nose. Keep its edges firmer than the forehead.
  • Indicate the bottom plane under the nose with a firm edge.
  • Add the cast shadow from the nose onto the face.
  • Adjust the edge of the face shadow: very soft on the rounded forehead, then gradually make it firmer as it goes down toward the chin. It’s a transition from soft to firm.

This looks simple, but it’s hard because you’re inventing how the edges change to describe the form.

Build the Blobby Head

Break the head into a few big blobs. Think in 3D. These are masses, not flat shapes. Keep them very simple and clean.

Core blobs

  • Cranium: a rounded mass. If the person’s cranium looks a bit squared off, add small corners.
  • Jaw: a separate blob that extends out from the cranium.
  • Nose: a very simple triangular blob. Proportions don’t matter.

You can stop here and shade just these three.

Optional blobs

  • Brow ridge: a mass across the eyebrows with a sudden plane change from the forehead down into the socket area.
  • Eyeballs: spheres set into the deep eye sockets. Don’t draw eyelids, eyebrows, or creases. Just balls.
  • Muzzle: imagine a chunk of a tuna can placed over the mouth area. It rounds out forward and also up and down, so it has a side plane and a bottom plane.
  • Cheeks/cheekbones: small ovals for the cheek masses. Sometimes fat softens and enlarges this area.

Think 3D: Cross-Contours and Plane Changes

Treat every blob as a 3D form. Use cross-contours and rhythms to find where the planes change.

  • The cranium can take a circular cross-contour.
  • The jaw and brow ridge have stronger plane changes, so use edges to show those.
  • Don’t outline everything. Let the edges and shading do the work.

Shade the Blobs

Start with the big forms, then move to the smaller ones. In the demo, the light is top right.

  • Cranium: shade the shadow side. As it meets the brow ridge, indicate the shadow along the ridge up to the nose.
  • Eyeballs: lit on the upper-right areas. Keep the silhouette edges of the balls sharp. Make the light-to-shadow transition on the ball soft to show roundness. At the ball’s edge where it meets the cheek, it’s a sudden plane change, so keep that edge firm.
  • Nose: keep the bridge firmer, and the ball of the nose softer.
  • Cheek mass: with light from top right, the bottom-left cheek area falls into shadow. It’s a firm shadow, but softer than the bridge of the nose. The forehead is very soft.
  • Muzzle: wrap the form as it turns to a side plane and a bottom plane. Indicate the bottom edge of that “can.” Add the cast shadow from the nose with a sharp edge. Even if the cast shadow covers the start of an edge, continue the soft transition past it.
  • Jaw: most of it is covered by other blobs. The exposed lower jaw is firm. On the lit side, the jaw plane can be a little darker and transition softly into the cheek, then get sharper where the jaw is more firm.

Keep cleaning edges. Soften what’s too dark, erase and replace lines with half tones where needed. Maintain the hierarchy of edges as you go.

Edge Control

Edges tell the story of the form. Use a range:

  • Sharp: cast shadows, silhouettes, sudden plane changes (edge of eyeball against cheek).
  • Firm: strong plane changes that are not razor sharp (bridge of nose, jaw line).
  • Soft: rounded areas with gradual transitions (forehead, cheeks, eyeball’s light-to-shadow).

You can even vary edges within the same halftone area: - One spot softer, another firmer, another very soft, depending on how the form turns.

Practice

  • Use the provided portrait photos. Do the egg version or the multi-blob version, or both.
  • Keep the forms simple. No eyelids, no wrinkles, no hair, no ears. Just blobs.
  • After you finish one, make it harder: keep the same blobs and angle, and change the light direction. Invent how all the edges and shadow shapes move. Adjust everything to match the new light.

Reminders

  • You’re not drawing a portrait. You’re building and shading simple blobby shapes.
  • Proportions don’t matter here.
  • Think in 3D with cross-contours and rhythms.
  • Imagine how these blobs would look if lit like the model.
  • Focus on the edges.
