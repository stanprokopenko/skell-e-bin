Master Skin Tones in Portrait Painting

Copy Code

Setting Up Materials

To start, I ensure my workspace is ready by using a spray fix. This keeps the environment dry and odorless, making it comfortable to work in the studio. I set up my four main colors: ketchup, mustard, black, and white lace. This setup is quick and efficient. I also prepare my medium mixture, which remains consistent with what I've used before.

Mixing Colors for Shadows

I begin by mixing an average shadow. I use a neutral gray value already on the canvas to test my colors. For the shadow, I mix black with ketchup and mustard. To lighten the shadow, I add yellow ocher pale. To introduce warmth, I incorporate alizarin crimson, which results in a dark brown without needing white. The painting is done in north light, known for its coolness, which influences my color choices.

Adjusting for Skin Tones

The subject has light skin, so I start fresh with my paint. I test the warmth and value of the shadow on the board. The average skin color is prepared with subtle modeling, avoiding bright highlights or dark halftones. The subject's skin appears dry, unlike older skin with many halftones. I use black to cool down any warm patches, ensuring accuracy.

Balancing Light and Shadow

I am satisfied with the shadow and light ratio and continue testing for balance. For the lightest tones, like the light on the subject's forehead, I adjust accordingly. I mix a half tone from scratch, making it slightly warmer as it transitions into shadow. Adding white to the light mixture is appropriate due to the cool light. The half tone should relate to the average light, appearing as a darker version.

Preparing Hair Color

For the subject's red hair, I mix an average hair color a few values darker than the skin tone. I intensify the hair color with more ketchup and mustard to achieve the desired red look. I store these mixed colors in cheap cups to prevent drying, labeling them for easy access. While these cups extend the paint's usability during the painting process, they won't keep it fresh for a week.

Techniques and Considerations

Mixing colors ahead of time is crucial. It improves skills and understanding of warm and cool versions of colors. Storing paint properly extends its usability, allowing for a smoother painting process. This careful preparation and technique are essential for achieving the desired color balance in a painting.
