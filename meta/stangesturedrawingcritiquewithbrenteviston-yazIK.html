Gesture drawing is a fundamental practice in figure drawing. We often receive two common questions from beginners: What is gesture drawing? and Why is it important? Many people think of gesture drawing as merely a warm-up exercise before starting the actual figure drawing. However, we view it as an essential first step.

What is Gesture Drawing?

Brent Eviston defines gesture drawing as a simplified form of figure drawing that captures the action of the pose. There are two crucial words in this definition: simple and action. When we begin a figure drawing, we're faced with the complexity of the human figure. To handle this, we look for ways to simplify the figure down to its most basic components and address those first.

The Importance of Simplicity

Starting with details or small bits of anatomy is a common mistake among beginners. When we start a drawing, it's like shaping a lump of clay—we're shaping and molding, and we don't expect our initial lines to be perfect. Keeping it simple allows us to move and adjust our work easily.

Gesture drawings often look unfinished, and that's completely fine. They're meant to serve as an armature upon which we can later construct the rest of the figure drawing.

Capturing the Action

Gesture drawing is all about motion. We focus on capturing the action of the pose. For example, we might use an action line that travels from the head to the foot, capturing the entire movement in a single sweep. Arms and legs can be simplified into single lines to show the direction they're traveling and to establish their length.

By keeping these elements simple, if an arm is too long or a leg is too short, it's much easier to correct before we start building more detail on top.

The Role of Exaggeration

Accuracy can sometimes be overrated. Brent suggests that he would prefer a drawing where the proportions are a bit off or the anatomy is slightly extreme but is more beautiful and interesting. We can afford to exaggerate up to about 10% to enhance the dynamism of our drawings.

Exaggeration helps counteract the natural tendency to stiffen dynamic poses. We might subconsciously try to stabilize the model in our drawings, making poses less dynamic than they actually are. By pushing ourselves to exaggerate, we often end up capturing what's truly there.

Practicing Gesture Drawing

Gesture drawing is an excellent opportunity to experiment and practice without pressure. We should approach it as a laboratory to try out different methods. Remember, gesture drawings are not precious; we can draw freely and expressively without worrying about perfection.

One helpful mindset is to tell ourselves that the drawing in front of us is merely practice for the next one. This reduces the pressure to produce a perfect drawing each time and allows us to focus on the process.

Drawing from Life vs. Photographs

Drawing from life is incredibly valuable. When we draw gestures from life, especially with short poses of one to five minutes, it puts us in a different mindset than drawing from photographs. Live drawing forces us to capture the essence quickly, while photographs can tempt us to overwork and redo our drawings endlessly.

Addressing Common Questions

"My Gestures Look Like Stick Figures"

It's okay if your gesture drawings resemble stick figures. In essence, these simplified gestures are about capturing the direction and action rather than detailed anatomy. We might think of them as stick figures made mostly from curved lines. Remember, we're not drawing the figure itself at this point—we're drawing what the figure is doing.

Imagine placing arrows at the ends of your lines to emphasize movement. This approach helps us focus on the direction rather than the form.

How to Exaggerate Without Losing Believability

Exaggeration is a tool to enhance the dynamism of our drawings. We can push the curves and gestures up to about 10% beyond reality. This level of exaggeration keeps the drawing believable while making it more engaging.

For instance, Brent mentioned that he might exaggerate the curvature of an arm by 5%. Models often ask if they are really that muscular or dynamic, and that's a sign that the artist has successfully captured and enhanced the essence of the pose.

* * *

Gesture drawing is a vital part of figure drawing that allows us to simplify complexity and capture action. By focusing on simplicity, motion, and even exaggeration, we can create dynamic and expressive drawings. Remember to practice freely, view each drawing as a stepping stone to the next, and embrace the process of exploration and experimentation.
