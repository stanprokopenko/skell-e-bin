How to Capture Gesture with Shapes

In this lesson, I'm offering a slightly different approach to drawing the figure and gesture. This method is particularly beneficial when you have a bit of anatomy under your belt. It allows us to get to light and shadow more directly.

Comparing Analytical and Observational Methods

The first approach we looked at was about building an analytical relationship with the figure. It focused on line, a slow and methodical appreciation of rhythm, bones, and perspective. Every stage was meant to increase our understanding of the figure.

This new approach emphasizes the observational side. While the first approach dealt more with line and construction, this one focuses on shape and the establishment of simple parts in their design.

Steps in the Observational Approach

Step 1: Identifying the Parts

First, I break down the figure into its basic parts:

  • Head
  • Neck
  • Torso (which includes the ribcage, waist, and pelvis)
  • Arms
  • Legs

I design each part as a shape. This requires a bit more knowledge of each part, but it allows us to summarize the figure effectively.

Step 2: Understanding the Gesture of Each Part

Next, I understand and include what the gesture of each part is. By gesture, I mean what the part could be doing. If we have a set shape, we consider:

  • Is the shape tilting?
  • Is it turning?
  • Can we create some kind of shape modification?

This approach is great for getting a more instant read of body type, which is important to the larger concept of gesture. Body type tells a story. I love Howard Schatz's photographic series on the '88 Olympic team because every athlete's body looks like their sport. The runners are narrow and sleek, while the weightlifters are heavy and solid. It's a great reference for creating characters.

Step 3: Applying Simple Perspective

Perspective in this approach is really simple, it's just wrapping lines. Instead of using boxes or complex perspectives, we add light and shadow to give the natural turn to a form. In nature, there is no line, line is a symbolic representation of a thing. Edge or value is how we understand perspective in everyday life.

The Head

In the previous approach, I presented the head as just a ball. While that's a good starting point, this method views the head as a cranium with a mandible (lower jaw). This gives a more specific shape or symbol.

Designing the Head Shape

I start by connecting with the part, the head, designing it as a shape that includes the cranium and the jaw. This shape forms the basis for our observations.

Exploring Head Gestures

What can a head do? Heads can tilt, turn, and lean in different directions. Using the same shape, I explore:

  • Tilting: Angling the head in different directions.
  • Turning: Moving the head towards one side or the other.
  • Profile Views: Adjusting the shape to represent a profile view.

By modifying the shape, we can create different characters. For example, softening the angles can give a more feminine appearance, while exaggerating certain features can suggest a grumpy soldier or a slender, elegant figure.

Applying Perspective to the Head

Perspective is applied using wrapping lines. I think of the brow and use the center line (symmetry line) of the face to indicate tilt and turn. Wrapping lines help us understand the form's three-dimensionality.

The Neck

The neck is the next part, and it's designed as a simple pillar. From the side view, it pushes forward, creating a nice diagonal.

Designing the Neck Shape

Understanding the part of the neck, I consider its connections to the head. The neck begins about a third of the way down from the head and connects to the torso.

Exploring Neck Gestures

Just like the head, the neck can:

  • Tilt: Lean side to side.
  • Stretch and Pinch: Think of it like a beanbag that can compress and extend.

By stretching and pinching the neck, we can express different gestures, such as the neck craning forward or tilting to one side.

Modifying the Neck for Character

We can modify the neck shape to push towards character or body type:

  • For a more feminine neck, I might pinch it to make it thinner.
  • For a bruiser or strong character, I might make the neck big and thick.
  • For an elegant or slender figure, I might elongate the neck.

This allows us to explore different shapes and storytelling opportunities.

Applying Perspective to the Neck

Perspective is again applied using wrapping lines. By wrapping lines around the neck, we can show its form and movement in space. Light begins to develop a slight appreciation of surface, and we associate the speed or timing of the form to an edge:

  • Slow-moving forms get soft edges.
  • Quick-moving forms get firm edges.
  • Abrupt-moving forms get a series of hard edges.

Connections Between Parts

As I build each part, I ensure a solid connection before moving on to the next. Connections are made by overlapping and wrapping parts. For example, when adding the neck to the head, I overlap and wrap it to create a seamless transition.

Adding Light and Shadow

With this approach, we can use light and shadow to bring out the realistic feeling of perspective. We don't have to overwork the drawings at this stage. Adding light and shadow helps to give the form a natural turn and adds to the overall dimensionality of the figure.

* * *

This observational approach to drawing the figure focuses on designing each part as a shape and understanding its gesture and simple perspective. By breaking the figure down into parts, exploring their gestures, and applying wrapping lines for perspective, we can create figures that are not only structurally sound but also convey a strong sense of character and expression.
