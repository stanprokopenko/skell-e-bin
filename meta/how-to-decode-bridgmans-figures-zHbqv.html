Focusing on the Back

I’ve been diving into George Bridgman’s back studies, which are great for building structural and mechanical understanding of the body. I’m focusing on different back views to see how muscles like the trapezius (traps), latissimus dorsi (lats), and scapula interact when the arms are raised or lowered. Some parts of the back can feel less complex than the front, but I’ve noticed it depends on how familiar I am with the forms.

Key Muscles and Forms

• Scapula: Sits on the rib cage and shifts depending on arm position.
• Traps: Connect up along the neck (the nuchal ligament) and spread down to the mid-back. They bunch when the arm lifts.
• Lats: Broad but relatively thin. Wrap around the rib cage and insert along the iliac crest.
• Obliques & Rib Cage: Tuck under the lats, often peeking through in leaner models.

When the arm is partially raised, the scapula rotates around the rib cage. This shifts the deltoid, reveals more of the rib cage, and can bunch the traps up on that side.

Cleaning Up Bridgman’s Line Work

Bridgman’s drawings can feel cryptic or “sloppy” at first glance. He emphasizes structure and mechanical forms, almost like industrial design. I love seeing the body as a machine, with pulleys, levers, and overlapping panels. But he often leaves details open-ended, so I go in and “translate” his lines,

  1. Identify the core shadow or half-tone he implies with crosshatching.
  2. Clarify each muscle’s layering, which muscle lies in front or behind.
  3. Use wrapping lines (topography) to show volume where Bridgman’s scribbles leave ambiguity.
  4. Establish clear edges for shapes, so the drawing becomes solid and believable.

I treat his sketches like a map, then inject my own shading or structure to define planes. I’ll track where the spine should run, where the paraspinal muscles sit, and how the rib cage arcs. By systematically “cleaning up” his chaotic lines, I can see how each muscle attaches and overlaps.

Light, Shadow, and Structure

I think of the light source as coming from above. That instantly tells me which surfaces roll into shadow. I’ll place a clear line of demarcation between light and dark. The resulting core shadows unify each muscle group and help me understand the 3D forms. Even a few thoughtful strokes can convey the depth of the lat or the thickness of the scapula.

Layering the Muscles

Layering is crucial when I’m solidifying who’s in front of whom. For instance: • The traps often lie on top, fanning up from the thoracic spine to the top of the shoulder.
• The lats swing around the side of the torso, inserting on the iliac crest.
• The obliques and rib cage sometimes peek through or under the lats, so I must decide where those shapes overlap.

If I don’t clarify these layers, the drawing risks looking transparent or “splintered.”

Merging Influences: Bridgman, Hogarth, and More

I see Bridgman’s approach as very angular and “masculine,” while someone like Hogarth has more flowing curves. Neither is the definitive answer, each method highlights different aspects. Loomis and the Riley method combine the blocky construction of Bridgman with the fluid forms of Hogarth.

I expand Bridgman’s rough sketches with: • Curved cross-contours reminding me of Hogarth’s wrap.
• Structured blocks reminiscent of industrial design.
• Rhythmic lines from the Riley method, subtly guiding the eye through muscles and forms.

Small Supplementary Sketches

Some Bridgman pages show quick “side studies” of a scapula or deltoid. I like to shade these a bit more than he does to reinforce the form. Even a tiny diagram of the scapula’s spine and the deltoid wrapping over it can be made more three-dimensional with a few tonal passes.

Adapting for My Process

Once I pin down Bridgman’s mechanics, I enjoy adding my personal style, maybe a bit more polish, a strong silhouette, or topographical shading. I don’t want to copy him exactly. My goal is to, 1. Understand his teachings.
2. Clean them up.
3. Incorporate them into my own interests or workflow, whether it’s concept art, realistic painting, or figure invention.

Preparing for Live Reference

After dissecting Bridgman, I love comparing what I’ve learned to the real figure, for example, looking at a photo of a muscular model in a similar back pose. I watch how the lats, traps, and scapula truly appear, and see how well I can merge that reality with my structural knowledge. Eventually, I’ll take it further by referencing other masters, like “Zeta” or Frazetta, to see how they stylize, strengthen shapes, or build drama.

That layered approach, Bridgman’s structure, real-life observation, and a painter’s stylization, gives me a well-rounded education in drawing strong, convincing backs.

* * *

In the end, I want a clear, sculptural feel in my back studies, with bold shapes and logical transitions. Bridgman’s technique can seem confusing, but once I clarify it, I find immense value in his blocky, mechanical vision of the human figure. By combining his methods with other artists’ approaches and consistent practice, I can bring life and believability to every back I draw.
