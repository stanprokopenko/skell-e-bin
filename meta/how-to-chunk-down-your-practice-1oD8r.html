How the 80/20 Rule Applies

You’ve identified your goals and the steps to get there. Now you want to focus on the 20% of tasks that bring you 80% of your results. In drawing, these key tasks include structure and gesture. Structure deals with two-dimensional shapes and three-dimensional forms. Gesture gives your drawings flow and energy. Combining these creates a strong foundation for any subject you draw.

Combining Structure, Gesture, and Value

As you refine your structure and gesture, you can incorporate value (tone). Instead of using line, you use tone to capture form and movement. Highlights, core shadows, and reflected light still rely on the same concepts: solid structure plus energetic gesture. Even as you add more depth and details, you keep returning to these essential filters.

The Power of Chunking Down

Chunking down is when you focus on a small, specific part of your art practice. In music or sports, you isolate a single technique until you master it. The same applies here: - Pick one detail, like leaning, facing, or tilting the torso.
- Practice that detail until it becomes more natural.
- Gradually expand to include another detail.

By simplifying and narrowing your focus, you can improve faster without getting overwhelmed.

Deep Focus vs. Flow State

A flow state is when you lose yourself in your art for hours. This is great for creativity and immersion. However, you also want to slip out of flow at times to apply deep focus, to notice and correct specific mistakes. That deliberate attention on problem spots takes your skills to a higher level.

Building Skills Through Iteration

In many disciplines, the best coaches or teachers stop you when they spot a consistent mistake and have you fix it before moving on. You want to do the same with your drawing: - Identify the mistake.
- Practice a small chunk of the pose or form to fix it.
- Then integrate that improvement back into the bigger piece.

This approach keeps you from getting stuck, feeling frustrated, or losing motivation.

Balancing Fun and Focus

You still want to draw freely and enjoy the full figure or scene. If you only focus on mistakes, you’ll risk burnout. Aim for a balance: - Spend about 20% of your time in deep focus, chunking down.
- Spend the rest in flow, practicing complete drawings and enjoying the process.

This balance helps you see clear improvements while keeping your sessions fun and motivating.

Staying Motivated and Moving Toward Mastery

Seeing progress, even small victories, is energizing. Each time you lose a few pounds of frustration or gain a few inches of understanding, you’ll feel inspired to keep going. Regularly remind yourself of your goals, track your improvements, and keep applying: 1. Goal Setting: Know where you’re heading.
2. Consistent Action: Commit to practice.
3. 80/20 Rule: Identify the vital few tasks.
4. Chunking Down: Zoom in on challenging spots.
5. Deep Focus: Make mindful corrections.

By weaving these strategies into your routine, you’ll see steady progress toward mastery. Keep going, stay curious, and give yourself room to improve step by step.
