Drawing Diggers with Blobs

Starting with Side and Front Views

First, familiarize yourself with what a digger looks like. Begin by drawing a side view to understand its profile. Then, sketch a front view to see how the boom, arm, and bucket align left to right.

Using Blobs to Establish 3D Form

Instead of precise lines, start with a blob shape to represent the digger. Imagine this blob with the front end closer to you, this will be the front of the digger.

Applying Rubber Bands to Indicate Planes

Place a rubber band around the blob to define the front plane. This emphasizes that it's a distinct plane facing you. If the top part is closer, wrap a rubber band around the top and slice off the top plane. This technique helps you visualize the three-dimensional form and how different planes relate to each other.

Observing Lines Moving Away

By defining the planes, you notice that when a corner is closest to you, the lines extending away to the right and left ascend to your right and left. This observation is crucial in understanding perspective.

Creating Upward and Downward Views

Drawing an Upward View

Experiment by drawing a digger floating above you, an unusual but informative perspective. Begin with a blob, where the end closer to you is at the bottom. Define the sides using circumferential lines to represent the blob's equator and underbelly. This helps you grasp how lines curve and converge in an upward view.

Multiple Perspectives in One Drawing

You can manipulate perspectives for different parts:

  • Place the platform in two-point perspective.
  • Have the cabin face you directly in one-point perspective.
  • Or, align both in one-point or two-point perspective but with different vanishing points.

Remember, objects like the digger and elements like city streets can have independent vanishing points. Understanding this prevents confusion and expands your creative options.

Limitations of Blobs in Precision

Challenges with Vanishing Points

While blobs are excellent for general forms, they lack precision for exact vanishing points. This can pose issues when integrating characters into environments with specific perspectives.

Characters in Environments

If your character's feet don't look right, adding a grid on the ground can help. This provides a reference for the ground plane, ensuring your characters stand naturally within the scene.

Combining Multiple Perspectives

Recognize that:

  • A character can tumble in space.
  • Different elements (street, digger, character) can each have their own orientation.
  • This diversity adds interest but requires careful consideration to maintain coherence.

Utilizing Blobs in Character Design

The Rubber Band Technique

Wrap a rubber band around each shape or limb to transform it into a defined form. This technique, akin to placing a striped sock on an object, clarifies the direction and depth, which you can later refine or remove.

The "Striped Sock" Maxim

Adopt the Looney Tunes' principle, **"Put a striped sock on everything, you can take it off later."] ** This approach ensures clarity in your forms and enhances the three-dimensionality of your drawings.

Exploring Character Creation

Overcoming Creative Blocks

If you're unsure where to start with character creation:

  • Sketch various ideas without aiming for perfection.
  • Explore boldly, knowing the first idea doesn't have to be perfect.
  • This practice encourages creativity and reduces fear of making mistakes.

Using Orthographic Views

When uncertain about a shape:

  • Draw the object in pure front or side view.
  • Consider adding back, top, and bottom views.
  • Creating an orthographic projection forces you to make clear decisions about the form and proportions.
