Project Overview

I work from the same photo you have so we can stay on the exact image. I’m using graphite for the whole drawing, then a little charcoal powder for the background. Graphite slows the demo down, but it lets me show every stage clearly.

We’re at the point in the course where all earlier concepts overlap:
proportions
 gesture / rhythms
shape design
 form & structure (the head as a box)
perspective
 composition
* values

Until your “muscles” can juggle everything at once, move in clear stages. I demo the stages one at a time so you can see the thought process.

* * *

Laying-In the Big Proportions

1. Find a Ground-Truth Measurement

I took the width of the beard (right under the nose). That width fits
• once from the top of the head to the bottom of the nose
• once more from the bottom of the nose to the beard’s core shadow
• plus a sliver for the little hook at the very bottom.

Everything else has to fit inside that measurement.

2. Light Construction

• Center-line has a slight tilt.
• Cheekbone rhythm separates front plane from side plane, exactly like the box-of-the-head project. • I keep angles simple polygons so they’re easy to shift if something feels off.

I drew multiple light lines so the camera can see them, you don’t have to.

* * *

Designing Rhythms & Shapes

I design hair, beard and cheek with connected rhythms so the eye can flow:

  • The hair swoops down into the bridge of the nose.
  • A triangle shadow under the nose points into the cheekbone.
  • Negative shapes on left and right beard aren’t equal, one is heavier so the design feels balanced, not mirrored.

Accuracy matters, but a good-looking, believable shape beats perfect tracing every time.

* * *

Shadow Mapping (Notan Stage)

  1. **Squint hard. ** Large masses merge, ignore tiny changes.
  2. Outline every shadow edge with an H pencil.
  3. Fill the entire shadow family with one flat value (4B).
  4. Blow through small “light lakes” inside the shadow, I’ll pick them out later with an eraser.

No outlines where light meets light (bridge of the nose, forehead). Those edges will melt together.

* * *

Two Values Inside the Shadow

Switch to a 6B and add the darkest accents:
• mouth interior
• nostrils
• corners of the eyes
• deepest hair pockets

At this point I keep only two values in the shadow family: a lighter shadow mass and a darker accent. I resist the urge to model form yet.

* * *

Dropping in the Background with Charcoal Powder

Materials I used
loose charcoal powder
 cheap bristle brush (scrubber)
super-soft mop brush
 kneaded & pencil erasers

Preparations
1. Test temperature: my pan-pastel was warmer than the powder, so I ditched the pan-pastel.
2. Decide where the background will be darker than the head to frame it.

Application
• Scrub the powder into the paper for a mid-tone shadow behind the cast.
• Soft brush feathers the powder upward for a light half-tone on the cheek.
• Always keep a scrap paper under your hand, charcoal lifts easily. • Don’t spit, droplets glue powder and leave black freckles.

Important rule, **never mix charcoal accents with graphite accents. ** Graphite catches glare, charcoal doesn’t. Mixing both in your darkest darks creates ugly, spotty reflections.

* * *

Establishing the Five-Value Structure

Value 1 – black accents (6B graphite)
Value 2 – main shadow mass (4B graphite)
Value 3 – dark half-tone (charcoal powder scrubbed in)
Value 4 – light half-tone (thin layer of powder or soft graphite)
Value 5 – white of the paper (picked out with eraser)

Key points
• Bright whites live mostly on the top planes, I removed value 5 from the beard so it rolls downward. • Large masses first, details later. The beard, lower lip and neck all sit in value 3–4 so the face dominates.

I “draw with the eraser” to pull value 5 back out of the powder: crisp highlight on the lip, soft glints on brow ridge, broad planes on forehead.

* * *

Edges

I start hinting at edges but still keep it simple:
hard edge: light cheek against dark background, bottom rim of beard sweep
 soft edge: cheek as it rolls toward the mouth, upper brow fading into shadow
* lost edge: top of head where light meets light

* * *

Framing the Focal Area

A value-1 shape behind the ear and cheek pops the head forward.
The beard’s tail gets a sharp dark accent, then fades with a soft brush so the gesture stays readable without stealing attention.

* * *

Level-1 Finish – Where You Stop

At Level 1 you should have:
• clear five-value design
• simple masses, no fiddly rendering
• limited soft edges (background, big core shadows)
• strongest contrasts around the eyes / nose / brow

Do NOT chase tiny textures in the beard or pores in the cheek yet. The drawing should already read from across the room.

* * *

Preview of Level 2

Next I’ll:
allow many subtle steps inside each of the five buckets (no longer locked to only five)
 refine edges plane-by-plane
* sculpt form within the shadow mass without breaking the overall design

But the underlying composition you see right now stays exactly the same.

Take your Level-1 drawing to this clean, graphic stage first. Once it works in five values, moving on to Level 2 rendering is straightforward fun instead of a fight.
