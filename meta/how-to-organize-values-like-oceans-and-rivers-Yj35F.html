Understanding Value Relationships

One common issue I've noticed is not making the shadows dark enough, which leaves little room to develop the lights. It's all about the relationships between values. If your shadows are too light, your lights won't pop, and the drawing loses contrast.

Making Shadows Dark Enough

For instance, Gwen mentioned that she didn't make the values on the face dark enough, which didn't leave much room in the lights. She's absolutely correct. If the shadows on the face are too light compared to the background, it becomes challenging to keep all your halftones lighter than the shadows. You have to be super subtle, but it's much easier and more effective to create a stronger contrast between shadows and lights.

Overlapping Lines and Distracting Textures

Another point is about overlapping lines creating darker areas unintentionally. These overlaps can be distracting, especially if they don't contribute to the form. They can seem like two-dimensional patterns rather than indicating structure. Control the variety in your values to avoid accidental emphasis on unimportant areas.

Organizing Shapes and Values

Starting with big shapes and connecting shadows can simplify your drawing process and lead to a more cohesive piece.

Starting with Big Shadow Masses

Think of grouping your shadows into large "oceans" rather than scattered "floaters." By connecting shadows into bigger shapes, you create a stronger structure. This approach makes it easier to manage values and helps the drawing read more clearly.

Avoiding Floaters

Floaters are small, unconnected shapes that can be distracting. They often occur when shadows or highlights are isolated without any connection to the larger value masses. Try to minimize these by connecting shapes whenever possible.

Connecting Shadows and Lights

Use halftones to bridge between your lights and shadows. By carefully designing the shapes and transitions, you can guide the viewer's eye and enhance the three-dimensional effect.

Simplifying Shapes

Designing your shapes with intention can greatly improve the readability of your drawing.

Straight Lines vs. Curvy Lines

While organic, curvy lines have their place, incorporating straight lines can add structure and strength to your drawing. Straight lines help define planes and angles, making the forms more solid and believable.

Contained vs. Uncontained Shapes

Contained shapes have clear, defined edges and contribute to the overall design cohesively. Uncontained shapes lack clear boundaries and can make the drawing look messy or unfinished. Aim to create well-contained shapes, especially in areas of importance.

Using Value Tools Wisely

Tools like the Proko Value Tool can be helpful but use them thoughtfully.

The Proko Value Tool

The Value Tool can assist in simplifying values and understanding relationships. However, be cautious with the simplicity slider.

Avoiding Over-Simplification

Cranking up the simplicity too much can "melt" the shapes together, losing essential details and structure. This over-simplification can make your drawings look elementary. Keep some of the fidelity and only simplify to the point where it enhances the drawing without sacrificing important information.

Managing Contrast and Value Range

Understanding how to use your full value range can make your drawings more dynamic.

Ensuring Enough Contrast

If you find you're struggling to develop three light tones, it might be because your shadows aren't dark enough. Don't be afraid to make your shadows sufficiently dark to allow room for lighter values in the lights.

Avoiding Too Much Contrast Within Shadows

While contrast is good, too much contrast within the shadows can break the unity of the shadow family. Keep the values within the shadows closer together to maintain cohesion.

Focusing on Proportions and Structure

Accurate proportions and underlying structure are foundational to a successful drawing.

Checking Proportions

Pay close attention to the overall shape of the head and the placement of features. Starting with an incorrect basic shape makes it difficult to place features accurately.

Incorporating Structure

Even in stylized or simplified drawings, suggesting the underlying planes and structure adds solidity. Use straight lines and defined angles to hint at the form beneath the surface.
