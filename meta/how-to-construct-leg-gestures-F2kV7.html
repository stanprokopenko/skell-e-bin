Thigh Construction

I like to start by tapering the thigh. I make the backside of the thigh less curved than the front side, which often has a fuller curve leading down to the knee. As I sketch, I watch for those subtle or not-so-subtle curves that can appear on a muscular figure.

I also keep in mind how the thigh transitions into the hip area, but I usually address that hip construction separately. For now, I focus on drawing a smooth flow from the back of the hip down into the hamstring area of the thigh.

Vertical Alignment

When I'm looking at the leg in profile, I search for vertical or near-vertical alignments. If I see a line that’s nearly vertical, I often choose to make it fully vertical in my sketch. This approach helps me with structure and shows me how far off the vertical each curve deviates. Tracking this verticality keeps the overall leg form strong and clear.

Lower Leg and the S-Curve

From the knee down, I often notice a gentle S-curve along the lower leg. There may be a small bump around the calf as it transitions toward the foot, and I keep that continuous flow in mind when sketching. It often flows into the foot, which grounds the figure.

Bent Leg Variations

When the leg is bent, the thigh and calf can bunch up around the knee, which affects the tapering. The corner at the knee can become more pronounced, or even show two or three small corners if the leg changes direction. Sometimes it’s more of a hard angle than a smooth curve. Depending on the pose, I adjust how I combine straight edges and corners to show the leg’s articulation.

These basic forms give me a solid foundation for drawing legs in profile because they help me see flow, shape, and structure all at once.
