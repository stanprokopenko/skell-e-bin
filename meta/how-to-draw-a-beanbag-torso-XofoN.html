How to Draw a Beanbag Torso

Copy Code

I like to think of the torso as a beanbag because the spine isn’t a single hinge. Each vertebra can articulate against the next, creating a bendy “accordion” effect. I can then simplify the torso into big, simple shapes, like two or three eggs or tubes. Sometimes I add the chest, stomach, or even the spare tire around the waist as extra forms. By mixing and matching shapes, I get a flexible concept that captures the way the torso stretches and pinches.

Breaking Down the Torso

When I examine a pose, I look for the stretch side, where the rib cage pulls away from the pelvis, and the pinch side, where everything compresses. If the model is sitting, the lower torso may flatten on the seat, like a beanbag pushed against the ground. I then plan out the shapes for: - The rib cage and chest area.
- The belly or waist region in between.
- The hips or pelvis as the base.

I can represent these shapes as eggs or bullet forms that overlap or tuck into each other.

Eggs, Tubes, and Boxes

I might choose three egg shapes stacked to represent the chest, belly, and hips, or I can use tubes or boxes if that makes more sense for the pose. Sometimes I’ll just pick two shapes (rib cage and hips) and then blend the middle. If I need more detail, I can keep splitting forms, adding overlaps to show how each shape presses or stretches.

Overlaps and Stretch

The most important part is to show where shapes overlap. The weight of the torso might squish one shape, creating a flatter, nipped-off form on the pinch side. On the stretch side, the shapes separate and create bigger spaces. This is where beanbags shine: they show tension where the spine stretches and a squish where the body compresses.

Combining Beanbags with Drumsticks

Sometimes, I’ll switch to a drumstick approach, which is all about a clear, interlocking insertion. But I can also combine beanbags and drumsticks. For example, I might use a beanbag for the torso and a drumstick for part of the leg attachment. It depends on which shape connection feels most accurate to the pose.

Deep Perspective

When I place these shapes in perspective, I imagine the rib cage, waist, and hips as eggs following a grid. I draw them as if they were three-dimensional objects receding in space. The overlaps then help me keep track of proportions. Even if I don’t know every anatomical detail, these simplified volumes give me an accurate roadmap.

* * *

By mashing up different shapes and overlapping them in a beanbag style, I can simplify a very complicated structure into two or three basic forms. This method lets me quickly capture the flow of the torso, without getting lost in the details. When in doubt, I step back and reduce it even more, just a couple of lines to show the stretch and pinch. That’s the power of the beanbag approach.
