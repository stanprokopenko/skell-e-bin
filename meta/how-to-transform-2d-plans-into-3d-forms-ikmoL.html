Using Isometric Graph Paper

You can even make it simpler. You can use isometric graph paper, which has angled lines that take away the problem of vanishing points. Every line is exactly 60 degrees, 90 degrees, or 120 degrees, and there's no convergence. You have nice light blue traces of every line.

You always know how to put a plan into perspective because foreshortening is predictable. You can count the units. If it's six units one way and nine the other, you just count them out. If it's a square plan, as we've been using, you count out the same number on each axis. And you don't have to land exactly on the blue lines if you choose to work somewhere between them.

You can even use square graph paper by drawing to and through corners. The 45-degree isometric position looks a bit awkward, but by choosing which corners to intersect, you can create other angles.

Limitations of Graph Paper and Isometric Perspective

But graph paper has its limits. Isometric perspective doesn't give us the realism that we get when we bring in vanishing points. And it has one other limitation that's a big deal in this course. Isometrics only create forms in a few fixed positions.
