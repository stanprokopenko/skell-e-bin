Start Simple

I begin by making my drawings as simple as possible. I focus on one or two things at a time, such as basic shapes or simple connections between forms. I allow proportions and details to be off, if it helps me keep the process manageable. By focusing on simple shapes, like tubes, boxes, or even flattened two-dimensional versions of those forms, I reduce the complexity and build a strong foundation.

Permission to Be Bad

I give myself permission to be imperfect. In the beginning, or whenever I tackle challenging poses, I know things will look off. I can’t let that stop me. Just like learning to walk, I give myself room to fail, stumble, and try again. Each drawing is a stepping stone that pushes me to improve over time.

Iterate and Chunk

Iteration is key. If I don’t like how something looks, I draw it again, maybe several times. Sometimes I’ll work on the entire figure repeatedly, and other times I’ll just focus on one part, like an arm or leg, until I see improvement. By narrowing my focus and chunking the problem down, I get faster feedback. That helps me adjust my shapes or angles without feeling overwhelmed by the entire figure every time.

Adjusting Shapes

To build forms, I start with corners and curves. I imagine simple connections where one shape ends and another begins. I might curve a line, put in a corner, or add a small overlap. Each little “hook” or “bump” tells me one form has ended and a new one has started. If I want more character, I experiment by adding slight tilts and angles, testing out how tapered or curved a limb should be. It's all about small decisions that add up.

Flatten or Simplify

If a pose is too complicated, with wild angles or overlapping limbs, I look for ways to simplify the three-dimensional complexity. I might treat the figure like it’s a flattened cut-out, focusing on two-dimensional shapes. I can always come back to add perspective and form later. At this stage, I just need to keep it clean and clear.

Let Your Skills Evolve

I accept that today’s drawing is not going to be as good as the one I’ll create in a few months. As my eye becomes more sophisticated, things I once liked might start to look awkward to me. That’s natural growth. By sticking to simple fundamentals and harnessing iteration, I gradually find my way of seeing and expressing the figure.

* * *

I remind myself that these methods, permission to fail, simple shapes, chunking, and iteration, are the path to steady progress. I'm less overwhelmed, I build confidence and I discover my personal vision. Above all, I have fun exploring and finding beauty in each pose. Let’s celebrate every small win, and I’ll see you in the next lesson!
