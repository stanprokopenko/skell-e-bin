Master Perspective for Better Concept Art

Copy Code

I visited Fullerton Community College, where I teach part-time, to catch up with my former student from decades ago, Phil Dimitriadis. Phil is now a concept artist for the entertainment industry, and we sat down to talk about his work and career.

Phil's Role as a Concept Artist

Phil works as a concept artist, solving problems for animated features, videos, TV shows, and various clients. When he's brought onto a project, he's often handed a script and tasked with identifying locations and challenges. He then designs and draws these elements, figuring out how the animation will look and what the environments will be.

Quick Marker Comps and Photoshop Adjustments

Phil showed me some quick marker comps he did. He can work extremely fast with markers, often preferring to thumbnail traditionally and then scan it into Photoshop for adjustments. These sketches take about two hours each, depending on the complexity.

Working from Imagination

Phil creates these designs entirely from his imagination. As a concept artist, he comes up with ideas and draws them so others can build or film them. He emphasizes the importance of critical thinking and problem-solving, especially when something in the script doesn't make sense. It's his job to figure out the director's vision and bring all that information together to provide a solution.

Blending Traditional and Digital Techniques

Phil often works with pen and ink enhanced with markers, adjusted in Photoshop. Some of his pieces are more complex due to subject matter and can take three to four hours. He showed me some entirely freehand drawings done with color erase blue pencils on cheap Xerox paper for projects like "Batman: The Brave and the Bold" at Warner Brothers.

Using Maya for Complex Environments

For more complex scenes, Phil starts with traditional thumbnails and sketches, then builds the location in Maya to solve camera and setup issues. This approach helps him figure out depth ratios and camera angles, especially for large locations or intricate environments.

The Importance of Perspective Training

I asked Phil about the role of perspective in his work, especially with the advent of 3D modeling tools like Maya. He believes that foundational skills in perspective training are crucial. While tools like Maya and Photoshop can speed up the process, they are just that, tools. Knowing how to draw and compose scenes by hand allows for greater adaptability, and creativity.

Avoiding Reliance on Digital Shortcuts

Phil discussed the risks of relying too heavily on digital tools like perspective grid brushes in Photoshop or automatic perspective tools in Sketchbook Pro. He feels that these can become crutches, limiting an artist's ability to think about scenes from a storytelling and staging point of view.

Perspective as Key to Quick Ideation

Phil emphasized that perspective training and "thumping it into my head" was key to being able to design and draw ideas quickly. He uses 3D tools to speed up production but doesn't let them replace the core foundational skills of draftsmanship, composition, and staging.

Phil's Design Process

Phil shared his multi-stage design process:

  1. Traditional Thumbnails: Starting with quick sketches to map out ideas.
  2. Silhouette Shapes: Developing shapes and visual forms in 3D space.
  3. Detailed Sketches: Refining the composition and adding details.
  4. 3D Modeling: Building the scene in Maya to solve camera angles and depth.
  5. Paint Overs: Doing quick renders and painting over them to establish mood and color.

He showcased an example where he was inspired by a neighbor's garage and went through these stages to create a richly detailed environment.

Advice for Aspiring Artists

Phil believes that constant drawing practice is essential. Tools like Maya and Photoshop can be learned relatively quickly, but foundational drawing skills take years to develop. He stresses the importance of being in your sketchbook, pushing your level to a higher plateau, and continuing to take classes even when working professionally.

The Value of Artistic Community

We discussed how important it is to be around other artists who are also learning and growing. Phil shared how he spent time with other artists at Fullerton College, like Justin Sweet and Vance Kovacs, talking about design and anatomy. Being part of an artistic community helps everyone improve.

Our History Together

Phil took my perspective class at Fullerton College back in the early '90s. He was committed to developing his skills and set out to draw for a living. Over time, he leveled up his skills and became known for being a problem solver in the industry. It's been a pleasure to see him grow from a beginning art student to a professional colleague and fellow teacher.

* * *

Phil continues to teach at Fullerton College and shares his knowledge through platforms like ArtStation and his YouTube channel, Phil's Design Corner. His dedication to foundational skills and his willingness to adapt tools to enhance his work make him a valuable resource for students and aspiring artists.
