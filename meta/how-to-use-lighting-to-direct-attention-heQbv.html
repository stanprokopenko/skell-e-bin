How Contrast Directs the Eye

Your eyes naturally seek out contrast. Lighting is crucial because it controls the contrast of a scene. In a shot from Arcane, your eye is drawn to the character with the brightest light next to the darkest dark. This high contrast automatically grabs your attention.

Light on Dark and Dark on Light

Using light on dark or dark on light makes subjects stand out. In The Last of Us, characters are silhouetted against contrasting backgrounds, making their shapes clear and easy to understand. In Gladiator, the boy is lit brightly against dark trees, focusing your attention on him. Similarly, in Batman Begins, the character's face is illuminated against a darker background, guiding your eye to the focal point.

Understanding Exposure Levels

High Key Lighting

High key lighting is bright and exposed for the shadows. Scenes are filled with light, bouncing around the environment. Examples include Oblivion, Star Wars, and the bathroom fight in Mission Impossible: Fallout. This lighting style is light and airy, revealing details in the shadows.

Low Key Lighting

Low key lighting is darker and exposed for the light sources, leaving shadows deep and less detailed. It's used to convey tension and mood. Examples are the moody scenes in Skyfall and Blade Runner 2049, where shadows dominate and create a sense of mystery.

Using Color Contrast

Color contrast directs attention when an object differs in hue from its surroundings. In Schindler's List, the girl in the red coat stands out in the black-and-white film. In The Blue Umbrella, red and blue characters pop against a gray world. In John Wick, a character lit with orange light stands out among others lit in green. Using color contrast is a powerful tool to draw the viewer's eye.

Choosing a Color Palette

Simplified palettes are usually more appealing because they're easier on the eyes. In Children of Men, the scene uses gray, greens, and blues with a touch of orange light, creating a simple and clear image. Looper and Close Encounters of the Third Kind also use dominant colors to simplify the scene and enhance the mood.

Light-Centric vs. Object-Centric Color

You can choose between emphasizing the local color of objects or letting the light dominate the color. Children's shows often use object-centric colors with vibrant, varied hues for each character. Alternatively, films like Klaus use light-centric color, where neutral-colored objects are illuminated by colorful light, creating a cohesive and beautiful visual experience.

Storytelling Through Color Shifts

In The Incredibles, the filmmakers used a color script to support the story. The beginning is hyper-saturated, reflecting Mr. Incredible's vibrant memories. The colors become monochromatic and gray when he's unhappy, and then balance out as he finds harmony between work and family. Using color shifts helps convey the emotional journey and enhances the storytelling.
