How to Paint Realistic Lips Digitally

Copy Code

Starting the Final Lip Painting

I'm excited to start our final painting for the lips chapter. I'll begin with a light gesture and lay-in, using that ovoid shape for the denture sphere.

Sketching the Lips

I lightly rough in the lip line, focusing on the tilt. I make sure I'm in the ballpark before fleshing out the rest of the lips. Then I find the center line and establish the cupid's bow.

My wife posed for this painting. These are her lips. They look a little snarly because she was losing patience with me trying to get the right lighting and angle. I definitely appreciate her doing it.

Defining Key Features

I establish the lower lip shape and indicate the light receding plane under it, which feeds into the top of the chin. I block out the philtrum above the upper lip, showing how it feeds into the septum of the nose. I also sketch the nostrils, ensuring all facial features line up parallel to each other for symmetry.

I lightly indicate the nasal labial fold and draw a light circle for the chin. Then I punch in the dark accents at the corners of the mouth.

I've kept all the line work very light and soft. Now I can do a second pass with a darker line and more confidence. I add the pockets of occlusion flanking the center tubercle and the lower tubercles. I also indicate the donut shape around the corners of the mouth.

I ensure the lateral right side of the lip aligns vertically with the side of the chin. I softly work in the nasal labial fold on the right side and frame our sketch. I think we're about ready to start adding color.

Preparing for Color

I show my layers and flatten everything for the drawing onto one layer, labeling it line. I hold down Alt and click the eye icon to make sure I don't have any opaque brushstrokes on the line layer.

Applying a Color Wash

I create another layer underneath the line layer where I'll do a quick color wash. I prefer using cad reds and oranges for that color wash. Maybe a little more orange. This helps eliminate the intimidating white background. If any of the canvas comes through, it will match the flesh tones better and be less eye-catching. I label this layer wash.

Colorizing the Line Layer

I go back to the line layer to colorize it. In Photoshop, I hold down Ctrl + U to pull up the hue saturation sliders. I check the colorize box, slide the saturation to the right, and increase the lightness. I adjust it to get a color that feels more like burnt sienna or a mix of jet black with cad orange or cad red.

Adding Local Colors

I create another layer between the wash and the line, labeled local colors. Using my squared textured brush, I find the local colors, and fill in the different areas with flat shapes. I'm not worrying about gradients, dark accents, or highlights yet, just flat color at this point.

Skin

I select a midtone color for her skin, adjusting the redness and value. My wife is a redhead, so there's a bit more red in her skin. I fill in the skin area with this flat color.

Lips

For the lips, I choose a darker, redder color since she has naturally darker red lips. I fill in the lips with this flat color.

Background

I add a neutral color for the background, with a bit more yellow to create contrast.

I clean up any excess color exceeding our borders using the lasso selection tool.

Separating Lights and Darks

I create another layer labeled lights and darks. I'm still focusing on graphic flat shapes, dividing the lights and darks the best I can.

Shadows

I select the local skin color and adjust it to find a general color for all the shadow shapes in the skin. I fill in these shadow areas with a flat, graphic representation, simplifying the lights and darks. I do the same for the lips, noting that the upper lip tends to have more lavender, while the lower lip has more red.

Dark Accents

I create another layer labeled dark accents. When working with flesh tones, I use warmer, dark reds for the darker accents. This lends itself to more vibrant shadows and areas of occlusion without deadening the image. I focus on areas like the corners of the mouth and the occlusions.

Highlights

I create another layer labeled highlights. I use cooler colors in the pinks for the highlights, keeping them graphic and simplifying complex areas into larger shapes.

I label all my layers appropriately to keep things organized.

Blending and Refining

I create a new layer, and I flatten all the layers into one by hitting Shift + Ctrl + Alt + E. Now, I start blending using my blender brush. Softening the edges already starts to make the painting feel much more natural. I don't want to get rid of all my hard edges, I want a nice balance between the two.

Refining the Lips

I start separating the shapes in the lips, adjusting the values and colors. I soften edges and refine the shapes to make them feel more natural. I use my blender brush to blend some of the shapes so they're not too strong or noisy.

Adjusting Highlights

I go back to my highlights layer and adjust the highlights using cooler colors, maybe more magenta or lavender, and desaturate them. I keep my shapes more triangular and try to avoid small highlights that can be distracting.

Adding Details

I continue to finesse the painting, pushing and pulling, experimenting with colors and shapes. I increase saturation where needed, and punch in dark accents. I focus on getting a nice variation of shapes, big, medium, small, without making it too busy.

Finalizing the Painting

At this point, I've established the main colors, shapes, and anatomy of the lighting. I go in to finesse areas like the nose shape, the chin, and the shadow shapes. This requires a lot of noodling, pushing, and pulling.

For the sake of time, I'll speed up the process and flush out these details. On the other side, we'll do a quick review of what we accomplished in this painting.

Reviewing the Lip Anatomy

Let's do a quick review of the anatomy:

  • Cupid's Bow: The top center of the upper lip.
  • Corners of the Mouth: The dark accents where the upper and lower lips meet.
  • Middle Tubercle: The central protrusion on the upper lip.
  • Flanking Tubercles: The protrusions on either side of the middle tubercle.
  • Lower Tubercles: The two tubercles on the lower lip.
  • Recessed Plane: The harder edge at the base of the lip under the lower lip.
  • Philtrum: The area above the upper lip that goes into the septum of the nose.
  • Donut Shape: The shape around the corners of the mouth.

Remember, overall, the mouth is round, and the lip line is like a flattened version of the cupid's bow.
