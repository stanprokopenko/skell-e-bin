Exploring Categories of Optical Illusions

If you go through a book of optical illusions, you'll find that there are a few categories. One is the classic question: Are these two lines the same length? And here's a secret: they always are. Is this a perfect circle? It always is. Anytime an optical illusion asks if two lines are the same length, or if the circle is perfect, or if the line is straight, you can safely say yes and you'll always be right.

There's another category where you turn the page upside down or at 90 degrees. You'll always find the hidden face or the image transforms, Santa Claus who is really sad is now really happy. Eventually, you don't even need to turn the page upside down, you just assume it's going to be the same trick you've seen throughout the book.

When you were six or seven, you might have thought these were brilliant and clever, and they are. But if you want to find that childlike wonder again, here we go.

Understanding the Trick

Of course, you might do this on the computer with those slick, clean lines that feel so official. But these were done with a mechanical pencil on bond and vellum. To draw these out precisely, it takes a T-square and triangles or graph paper to keep the angles consistent and precise.

With bond paper, a light box lets you trace over the outline. Pay attention to this: swap out which lines cover up the lines behind them. With vellum, you don't even need a light box. Vellum goes transparent the moment you put it down, but tape it down or mount it so it doesn't slip.

Tips for Creating Your Own Optical Illusions

If you want to do more than analyze, copy your favorites and vary them. If you're the creative type, make up your own. Start simply so that you don't get confused, and always rough it out first. If you're new to the tools, this is a way to get used to them. We're keeping it simple.

For this experiment, no vanishing points. Isometric, axonometric, paraline, oblique projections - the serious systems that architects use in their work - all have potential for play. Play with this, and it will get you to work.

Additional Tips

  • Try some directional swap-outs. Where one part overlaps another, trace over it and reverse the overlaps.

  • Outline. That's pure outside contour, with no hint of one part in front of another. This allows you to choose either set of inlines that put one part in front of another.

  • Ideate first. It's always good to make little rough sketches before deciding what's worth refining.

Challenges and Warnings

Warning - It takes concentration. Attending to lines - don't multitask. When you work on these projects, protect yourself from distractions.
