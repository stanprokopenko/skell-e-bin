Understanding the Difference Between Personal and Client Work

When working with clients, the main difference from personal projects is the need for clarity and readability. While personal sketches can be messy and just for my own reference, client work must be clean, clear, and understandable to others. It's crucial that my ideas communicate effectively.

Presenting Options to Clients

I always aim to present options that are appealing and viable. Every thumbnail I show a client is something I'd be happy to develop further. I avoid including any options I'm not enthusiastic about. This way, I'm unbiased and let the client decide the direction they prefer.

The Sea of Stars Key Art Example

When I worked on the key art for Sea of Stars, the client provided their initial character designs and a rough composition. They asked me to draw it in my style. I began by translating their characters into my own style, exploring until I found a look that felt right.

Exploring Poses and Expressions

Even though I didn't know much about the story, drawing the characters in various poses and expressions helped me connect with them. This process humanized the characters and made them feel more real to me.

Creating Thumbnails

I provided the client with four different thumbnails:

  1. Their Original Idea: A wide composition with the characters looking out over a cliff.
  2. A Dynamic Angle: Similar concept but with a more dynamic camera angle and the characters closer to the viewer.
  3. Focusing on the Characters: A composition showing the characters' faces and personalities, while including important background elements.
  4. An Alternative Approach: A bold option that was character-focused, featuring a split between day and night themes.

They chose the third option, which allowed the viewer to see the characters' faces and personalities.

The Iterative Process

Line Art and Color Flats

I started with line art but wasn't satisfied with the initial faces. I revised them to better capture the characters' expressions. When adding color flats, I focused on their signature colors to keep the palette cohesive.

Making Adjustments

The client requested some changes, like adjusting the design of a tower in the background. I made personal adjustments too, like adding glowing flowers for visual interest. Even after the client approved, I wasn't afraid to improve the artwork if I felt it needed it.

Adapting to Format Changes

Later, the client needed the artwork adapted to a wider format. This required me to extend the composition, add new elements, and adjust the background to fit the new dimensions. It's important to be flexible and accommodate such requests.

Planning for Final Output

When working on projects like an art book cover, it's crucial to plan ahead for elements like gold foil or specific dimensions. I created thumbnails with these considerations in mind, which made the final stages smoother. Reusing familiar poses can also help the audience connect with the artwork.

My General Process

  1. Research: Get familiar with the characters or subject matter.
  2. Thumbnails: Explore compositions and storytelling options.
  3. Line Art: Finalize the drawing.
  4. Flats: Establish a cohesive color palette.
  5. Shading: Add depth and finishing touches.

Each project may vary, but following these steps helps me create artwork that meets both my standards and the client's needs. Whether it's a new character or a familiar one, this process ensures a cohesive and effective final piece.
