How to Portray Age Without Wrinkles

Understanding the Differences Between Youthful and Aged Faces

When drawing an aged face, it's important to consider more than just adding wrinkles. The key difference between youthful and aged faces lies in the visibility of the skull versus soft tissue. In younger faces, you'll notice more fat and softer surfaces, obscuring the skull beneath. In contrast, older faces reveal more of the underlying bone structure due to the reduction and shifting of fat.

The Role of Fat Distribution

In youthful faces, fat pads fill out the skin, smoothing over the transitions between bones and muscles. As we age, gravity causes these fat pads to shift downward, exposing the bone structure and creating sagging in specific areas of the face. Understanding how fat distribution changes with age is essential to accurately depict an aged face.

Key Facial Features to Emphasize Aging

Cheekbones and Malar Fat

One of the most significant indicators of age is the prominence of the cheekbones. In older faces, the cheekbones become more visible as the fat that once covered them diminishes and shifts downward. This reveals a distinct plane change from the side of the face to the front, creating more pronounced angles.

The malar fat pad, which normally sits high on the cheek in youth, slides down and settles alongside the nose in older individuals. This shift creates a noticeable change in the facial contour, contributing to a more aged appearance.

Buccal Fat and Jowls

The buccal fat pad, located at the outside corner of the mouth, also succumbs to gravity. In aged faces, this fat pad moves downward, adding fullness below the mouth and contributing to sagging around the jawline.

Below the buccal fat, jowl fat may become prominent. This fat pocket, which typically resides above the jawline, can dip below it as we age. Breaking the straight line of the jaw with these subtle dips can significantly affect the perceived age of your character.

Eye Areas and Fat Pads

Around the eyes, the fat that cushions and surrounds the eye begins to droop with age. This results in puffiness under the eyes and a series of pinches or wrinkles that hint at the underlying skull structure. Depicting this downward shift in the fat pads around the eyes can enhance the aged look.

Ear Size

The ears continue to grow as we age. Slightly increasing the size of the ears, extending them beyond the usual proportion between the brow and the bottom of the nose, can subtly indicate an older person.

The Jawline

In younger faces, the jawline is typically smooth and well-defined. Introducing slight sags or dips below the mandible line can age your character. Emphasize the contrasts between the sharp corners of the bone and the looser skin and fat that hangs beneath.

Constructing the Aged Head

Basic Head Construction

Start by laying out the basic shapes and planes of the head, which include the eye sockets, the blocky shape of the nose, the mouth, and the cheekbones. Understanding these foundational elements will help you map out where the signs of aging will appear.

Identifying Key Planes in the Skull

Focus on identifying the key planes of the skull:

  • Cheekbones: Highlight the prominence of the cheekbones by showing the top plane and the side plane distinctly.
  • Temple: Recognize the plane change at the temple area, contributing to the overall structure of the face.

By mapping out these planes, you'll be able to see where skin and flesh might pinch or hang, which is crucial for depicting age.

The Importance of the Cheekbones

Emphasizing the cheekbones is essential in portraying an aged face. Revealing the cheekbones automatically ages your figure, as the diminishing fat allows the bone structure to show through more prominently. This contrasts with youthful faces, where fat obscures the cheekbones, giving a smoother appearance.

Effects of Gravity on Facial Features

How Fat Pads Shift with Age

As we age, gravity affects the structural fat around the face, causing it to move downward:

  • Malar Fat: Slides down from the high cheek area to settle alongside the nose.
  • Buccal Fat: Moves down from the outside corners of the mouth, adding fullness below.
  • Fat Around the Eyes: Droops, causing puffiness and wrinkles under the eyes.
  • Jowl Fat: Begins to sag below the jawline.

Understanding these shifts allows you to depict the subtle sagging and drooping effects that characterize an aged face.

Avoid Overuse of Wrinkles

While wrinkles are a traditional way to depict age, overusing them can make your drawing look cluttered or unnatural. Instead, focus on the underlying structures and forms that change with age. Emphasize the increased visibility of bone structures and the effects of gravity on fat pads and skin.

Emphasizing Bone Structure

By highlighting the skeletal features, such as the cheekbones, brow ridge, and jawline, you can effectively convey age without relying solely on wrinkles. Show the contrasts between hard bone surfaces and softer, sagging skin to create a more realistic aged appearance.

Rendering Light and Shadow

Establishing Light Sources

Choose a light source to help define the forms and planes of the face. For an aged face, top lighting can be particularly effective, as it emphasizes the protruding bones and the sagging skin beneath.

Understanding Edge Types

When shading, consider the types of edges that occur between light and shadow:

  • Soft Edges: Indicate a gradual transition on rounded surfaces, like the forehead or cheeks.
  • Hard Edges: Show a sharp change in plane or where a cast shadow occurs, such as under the brow or nose.

Using edges thoughtfully helps to describe the turn of surfaces and enhances the three-dimensionality of your drawing.

Mapping Light and Shadow

Begin by mapping out the major light and shadow areas based on your chosen light source:

  • Identify where light directly hits the face, such as the forehead, nose bridge, and cheekbones.
  • Determine the form shadows where the planes turn away from the light.
  • Add cast shadows where features block light, such as the nose casting a shadow on the cheek.

Applying Values and Edges

Using Value Scales

Establish a value scheme to create depth:

  • Lightest Light: Use the white of the paper or your lightest value for the brightest areas.
  • Mid Tones: Apply medium values to areas in partial light or soft shadows.
  • Darkest Dark: Use your darkest value for deep shadows and core shadows.

By working with a limited value range, you can maintain focus and avoid overworking less important areas.

Creating Depth with Light and Shadow

Focus on developing the halftones and the transitions between light and shadow to enhance the form:

  • Use soft edges to show gradual transitions on curved surfaces.
  • Apply hard edges to indicate sharp plane changes or cast shadows.
  • Render one side of the value scale to keep the drawing unified and maintain the focal point.

Rendering the Forms

With your values established, begin to render the forms:

  • Build up the halftones in the light areas to show the subtle planes of the face.
  • Deepen the shadows where necessary to accentuate the bone structure.
  • Keep details crisper in the focal areas, such as around the eyes and cheekbones, while allowing less important areas to remain softer.

By thoughtfully applying values and edges, you'll create a compelling depiction of an aged face that highlights the structural changes and effects of aging.

* * *

By understanding and applying these principles, you can effectively depict age in your drawings without over-relying on wrinkles. Emphasize the underlying structures, consider the effects of gravity, and be mindful of light and shadow to create realistic and expressive aged faces.
