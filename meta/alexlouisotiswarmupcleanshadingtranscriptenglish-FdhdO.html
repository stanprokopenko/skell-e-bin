Developing Pressure Sensitivity

To control values in your shading, you need to develop pressure sensitivity in your hand. By adjusting how hard you push on the pencil, you can create darker or lighter tones. Since there's an infinite range of values, you must learn to apply the right amount of pressure to achieve the specific tone you want. This requires practice to fine-tune your muscle control, similar to how an athlete trains.

Common Shading Issues

When learning to shade, you might encounter two common problems:

  • Spotty Shadows: These occur when there are holes in your shading where the white of the paper shows through because you missed spots.
  • Inconsistent Pressure: This happens when your shading varies in darkness due to uneven pressure, resulting in a patchy look.

These issues create unwanted noise in your drawing, which can be distracting if it's not intentional.

Warm-Up Exercise: Clean Tone Practice

Starting with a Rectangle

Begin by drawing a rectangle or square on your paper. The size doesn't matter much, anywhere from two to four inches works. This shape will be your practice area for shading.

Consistent Stroke Technique

Choose an angle that's comfortable for you, and start shading with consistent strokes. Overlap each stroke the same amount and maintain the same pressure throughout. This consistency helps create an even tone. If you notice gaps or inconsistencies, go back and fill them in.

Methods to Try:

  • Lines: Shade using lines, lifting the pencil after each stroke.
  • Tiny Circles: Make small, overlapping circles without lifting the pencil. This method can result in a smoother tone.

Layering for Even Tone

Instead of aiming for the desired value in one pass, build it up with multiple layers. Start with a lighter tone and gradually add layers, slightly changing the angle each time. This helps fill in gaps and evens out any inconsistencies. Be mindful of areas that may have become too dark, and adjust your pressure accordingly in subsequent layers.

Using the Side of the Pencil

Switching to an overhand grip allows you to use the side of the pencil, covering larger areas more quickly. This method can introduce more texture due to the paper's grain showing through. You can start with this base tone and then refine it with the tip of the pencil if needed.

Blending Tools: Use with Intent

While blending stumps and your finger can help smooth out tones, avoid relying on them during this exercise. The goal is to improve your hand control. Remember, using your finger can leave oils on the paper that may affect your drawing.

Practice Different Values

Don't limit yourself to one value. Practice shading light, medium, and dark tones. Before you start, choose a specific value to target, like matching the tone of a nearby object. This gives you a goal, and helps improve your ability to control value.

Exploring Charcoal Pencils

If you're interested, try practicing with charcoal pencils. Charcoal spreads more easily than graphite, allowing you to fill in areas quickly. However, it can be harder to control and may get messy. Using both graphite and charcoal can help you develop different skills:

  • Graphite Pencils: Great for detailed work and focusing on precise values.
  • Charcoal Pencils: Ideal for covering large areas and learning to work quickly.

Mastery Through Practice

The key is to gain control and mastery over your tools. Being able to produce clean tones, control values, and choose the appropriate technique for the desired effect will improve your shading skills. Practice regularly, challenge yourself with different materials, and continue refining your technique. Happy shading!
