Iterative Sketching for Better Figures

Paper and Tools

I like working on marker paper because it’s smooth and allows for clean line quality without a distracting surface texture. The one I’m using is acid-free and 100% cotton, so it won’t yellow or degrade over time. This makes it a good option for developing finished drawings that might be framed or displayed.

I’m working with Conte sticks here. They’re a chalky medium that’s a bit waxier than charcoal. I can adjust my pressure or angle to create crisp lines, softer edges, or fill broader areas.

Building Iterative Studies

I take inspiration from Michelangelo’s preparatory sketches, where he didn’t always finish an entire figure on one sheet. Instead, I’ll draw parts of the body, sometimes in multiple versions. This helps me explore different gestures or refine the form. This way, I focus on individual sections, like the head, a bent arm, or a foot, without worrying about fitting the entire figure on one page.

It’s also helpful to size and position these studies in whatever arrangement feels most natural. Drawing partial figures allows me to push dynamic curves and really emphasize the flow from one part of the body to another.

Finding Corners and Curves

When I begin a sketch: - I simplify shapes as boxes, cylinders, or spheres.
- I look for corners to emphasize structure and long-axis curves to capture fluid movement.
- If something is stiff, I’ll try to push the curve in a second pass.

I often iterate to refine. Even if the first attempt is good, trying again might reveal a better contour or a more dynamic angle. Sometimes the second or third attempt doesn’t look as nice, but repetition still helps me understand the form and confirm my creative choices.

Applying Line and Tone

I like to start with light lines and then add tone for shadow areas. I keep in mind: - Light vs. shadow: If a surface turns away from the light, it gets darker, and if it faces the light, it stays lighter. - Core shadows: I often create a darker core, then blend outward to build soft transitions and emphasize rounded forms.
- Box logic in rendering: I imagine top, side, and front planes. By shading each plane distinctly, I suggest the solidity of the form.

I also pay attention to negative space, the gaps around the figure. Looking at these shapes helps with proportion, and it can inspire creative designs if I notice an interesting silhouette emerging between different sketches on the page.

Emphasizing Gesture Through Strokes

My strokes typically follow the long axis of the shape. On an arm, for example, I’ll blend and shade up or down its length. It helps me keep a sense of flow and volume. If I need crisp edges, I’ll position the Conte stick to create a sharper line or use a stump or finger to soften transitions.

As I go: - I develop core shadows to anchor the form.
- I use my finger or a blending tool to smooth transitions.
- I introduce highlights by erasing or layering chalk.
- I create subtle corners by tightening lines where planes intersect.

Drawing Reference Sheets

When I finish, I end up with a reference sheet of studies, heads, torsos, and limbs. This is my research, and I can use it later for a bigger painting, a more polished drawing, or a full composition. Repeating this practice lets me dial in specific areas like a foot or a bent knee, until I have something that looks both well-structured and lively.

* * *

Drawing iterative studies lets me practice form over and over, discovering new ways to refine shapes and push the gesture. This method is practical, too. I can lay out multiple poses or body parts, then develop final artwork with more confidence. It’s a fun and rewarding process that deepens my understanding of the figure and prepares me for larger pieces.
