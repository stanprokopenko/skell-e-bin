The Power of Exaggeration

Every drawing I create involves some form of exaggeration. Masters of figurative art, whether classical or contemporary, often use exaggeration to add drama, movement, and dynamism to their work.

Starting with the Action Line

I begin with the primary action line, intentionally making it more extreme than what I see in the pose. For example, when capturing a leg projecting backward, I extend it further and push it in a more extreme direction. It's as if the model is deepening the lunge even more.

Balancing Exaggeration

Not every part of the figure needs to be exaggerated. I make decisions about which elements to push and which to keep as they are. For instance, I might tilt the rib cage back more while drawing the pelvis as I see it. This selective exaggeration adds interest without overcomplicating the drawing.

Extending Gesture Lines

I often extend gesture lines beyond the figure to suggest movement, fluidity, and dynamism. These lines act like motion blurs, giving a dramatic sense of time and motion. Even in more realistic drawings, I leave these extended lines ghosted in the background.

Enhancing Curves and Twists

If a part of the body is slightly curved, I make it much more curved. This is evident in areas like the upper arm or a leg. I've also found that turning the rib cage slightly more toward the viewer emphasizes twists, adding drama and tension to the drawing.

The Importance of Experimentation

These gesture drawings are experiments. I often start without a strict plan, allowing myself to explore and see what works. Some exaggerations will enhance the drawing; others might not. The key is to practice and become comfortable with pushing boundaries.

Quick Studies and Dynamic Lines

In quick studies, I feel freer to experiment with dramatic exaggerations. For example, I might curve a leg or forearm more than is natural. While not entirely realistic, these changes add a sense of drama and dynamism to the figure.

The Beauty of Unfinished Work

Leaving parts of a drawing unfinished invites the viewer to participate in the illusion. Quick, passionate lines evoke a stronger emotional response. Viewers can often tell whether a line was drawn quickly or slowly, and they tend to prefer the vitality of faster strokes.

Pushing Your Drawings Further

Ask yourself if your drawings could benefit from more drama and movement. If the answer is yes, experimenting with dynamic exaggeration is an excellent way to achieve that.
