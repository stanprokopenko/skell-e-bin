7 Lighting Techniques for Artists

Copy Code

When composing lighting in your art, consider these seven key points to enhance your work:

  1. Focal Point
  2. Shape
  3. Form
  4. Depth
  5. Separation
  6. Contrast
  7. Palette

Let's dive into each of these in detail.

Focal Point

What are you trying to say with your image? What should the viewer look at first?

In visual compositions, especially in films where the average frame is on screen for five seconds or less, it's crucial to have a simple, clear focal point. The lighting should direct the viewer's attention to the primary subject.

For example, in a scene from Star Wars, it's very obvious who you should be looking at, the main character prominently lit, not someone in the background. In Skyfall, the lighting focuses solely on the character's eyes, drawing all attention there, and not to any other part of the frame.

In animations like Klaus or Arcane, lighting is used to simplify the scene and highlight the focal point, such as a character entering through a doorway or an important object. Everything else in the frame is subdued to support this focus.

In video games, lighting often guides the player to the next objective, like a beam of light indicating where to go next.

Shape

Ensure the silhouette of the focal point is clear and easily recognizable.

Shape helps the viewer quickly understand what they are looking at. Strong, graphic shapes make it easier for the brain to process the image in a split second.

Cinematographer Roger Deakins is known for using silhouettes effectively, creating simple, graphic shapes in films like Blade Runner 2049. The use of fog and minimal lighting highlights the silhouettes of characters and objects, making the scene easily readable.

Even in dark scenes, like in Stranger Things, careful lighting ensures that the character's shape is clearly visible, adding to the mood while maintaining clarity.

Form

Use lighting to show the three-dimensionality of objects within the silhouette.

Once you've established the focal point and its shape, you need to reveal its form. The placement of lights can enhance the perception of depth and volume in your subjects.

For instance, lighting a character's face from the front creates flat lighting, making the image appear two-dimensional. Moving the light directly above creates harsh shadows and may obscure important features like the eyes.

Positioning the light at a 45-degree angle introduces a light side and a dark side, revealing the contours of the face. This technique is known as Rembrandt lighting, characterized by a small triangle of light on the cheek opposite the light source.

Softening the light diffuses the shadows, making the light wrap around the subject's features, which adds appeal. Adding rim lights from behind the subject further defines the shape and helps you understand the three-dimensional form.

In films like Stranger Things, Joker, and The Princess Bride, multiple light sources are used to reveal form. Key lights highlight facial features, rim lights define edges, and fill lights soften shadows, creating depth and enhancing the mood.

Depth

Convey three-dimensional information in a two-dimensional frame.

Depth helps the viewer understand how far away things are in the scene. Atmospheric effects like fog and mist are powerful tools to convey depth, often exaggerating real-life conditions for artistic purposes.

In Mission Impossible, the vast drop is emphasized by atmospheric perspective, making the cliff seem perilously high. Fog simplifies images by obscuring unnecessary details and focusing attention on the main subject.

In Arcane, fog separates the foreground, mid-ground, and background, enhancing the sense of scale and distance. Repetition of shapes and atmospheric perspective help the viewer perceive depth intuitively.

Even in interior scenes, subtle use of fog or mist can add depth, separating elements within the frame and enhancing the overall composition.

Separation

Clearly distinguish elements in your composition to enhance readability.

Separation ensures that the focal point stands out from the background and other elements in the scene. This is crucial for quick understanding, especially when images are only on screen briefly.

Using high contrast between the subject and background is one way to achieve separation. Backlighting is particularly effective, creating a rim of light around the subject that distinguishes it from the background. In Skyfall, fog and rim lighting make the characters stand out vividly.

In Lord of the Rings, backlighting and atmospheric perspective are used to separate Frodo from his surroundings, making the action clear despite the dark, moody setting. Even if the lighting isn't scientifically accurate from shot to shot, maintaining cinematic consistency helps with visual storytelling.

In Hugo, characters are often backlit, allowing for soft, appealing lighting on their faces while separating them from the background. This technique is preferred by many cinematographers to enhance clarity and aesthetic appeal.

* * *

By considering these seven key points, focal point, shape, form, depth, separation, contrast, and palette, you can significantly improve the lighting composition in your art, making your images more engaging and visually compelling.
