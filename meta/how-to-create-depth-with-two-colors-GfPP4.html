How to Create Depth with Two Colors

A complementary palette involves using colors that are opposite each other on the color wheel. For example, yellow's complement is purple, and red's complement is a specific green directly across the wheel. Using a complementary palette means you can access all levels of saturation through these two colors. This approach is similar to changing from warm to cool via saturation.

Benefits of using a complementary palette include: - Creating dynamic and visually appealing contrasts. - Simplifying the color choices while maintaining a full range of hues. - Enhancing the depth and realism of your painting.

Setting Up the Painting

For this demonstration, we'll choose a more complex subject than a simple sphere. Let's paint a scene with a bridge, grass, and a monster.

  1. Create a new canvas: Set the dimensions to 1500 pixels wide and 1200 pixels tall.
  2. Quick sketch: Draw the environment, including the bridge, grass, and a simplified monster.
  3. Set the line drawing to multiply mode: This allows you to paint on the background layer without losing the sketch.
  4. Choose two colors for the complementary palette: We'll use orange and blue. Sunlight is often orange, and shadows are often blue, making this a realistic choice.
  5. Decide on the light source: We'll use a warm light (orange).

Painting Techniques

  1. Replace the white of the canvas: Use a more decisive color to avoid the starkness of white.
  2. Block in the background and character: Use blue for the background and a more orangey tone for the character. Paint the bridge with a less saturated orange.
  3. Hide the line drawing: Focus on painting without the sketch lines.
  4. Invent light direction: Assume light is coming from the right. Separate the monster into light and shadow using warm and cool colors.
  5. Block in shadows: Use a desaturated blue for shadows. Start with an airbrush for large areas, then switch to a chalky brush for more definitive strokes.
  6. Adjust saturation: Darken value without changing the lightness by adjusting saturation. Use scumbling to add neutralized blue shadows for a range of color temperatures.

Enhancing the Painting

  1. Use overlay mode in Photoshop: Mix colors and create realistic light effects.
  2. Flip the canvas horizontally: Spot mistakes and adjust the composition.
  3. Paint over mistakes: Maintain a sketchy mindset rather than using undo.
  4. Vary edge, line width, and softness: Maintain form and light in stones and other elements.
  5. Add ambient occlusion: Create shadows where ambient light is blocked.
  6. Use brush settings: Add noise and texture to the background. Create a detailed environment with small shapes.
  7. Play with color temperature: Enhance depth by graying off blue to warm it up, simulating warmer leaves.

Final Touches and Tips

  1. Refine shapes and add detail: Focus on adding detail, not just small elements.
  2. Paint occlusion shadows: Vary the strength and edge of shadows between stones.
  3. Treat stones as 3D objects: Each stone should have its own planes and catch light effects.
  4. Enhance depth: Use blue for skylight and orange for occlusion shadows.
  5. Build a balanced palette: Create a bridge between warm and cool colors.
  6. Add catch light: Define the roundness of the monster's mouth and cheeks.
  7. Modify the background: Darken behind the monster's silhouette to enhance focus.
  8. Increase contrast: Darken occlusion shadows in the bridge for realism.
  9. Use atmospheric perspective: Reduce contrast to indicate distance.
  10. Exaggerate bounce light: Mimic real-life effects by exaggerating bounce light.
  11. Maintain focus: Keep warms and cools less extreme in non-focal areas.
  12. Indicate reflections in water: Use horizontal strokes.
  13. Avoid outlining: Maintain the effect of light and form.
  14. Be brave with temperature shifts: Enhance the palette with bold temperature changes.
  15. Add color notes: Sweeten the palette and add creativity. The amount depends on personal taste.
  16. Focus on color use: Prioritize color use over exact drawing.

Consider the study finished when the overall palette is complete. This exercise helps you practice and experiment with color, making future decisions easier.
