Embracing the Sketchbook

I fill about a sketchbook a year, drawing without specific plans or themes. I try to carry a sketchbook everywhere, sketching whenever inspiration strikes. My sketchbooks contain a bit of everything, from studies to spontaneous sketches.

Developing Drawing Habits

I aim to draw daily, often at the same time to make it habitual, usually in the morning or before projects. Warming up might involve freehand drawing or testing new materials. Even one sketch satisfies my daily art practice.

Using References and Video

I use many references, including photos and videos. Lately, video references help me capture motion and gesture, offering a sense of drawing from life when live models aren't available. Pausing videos allows me to explore various poses and movements.

Exploring Materials and Surfaces

Before professional work, I test materials in my sketchbook, as different papers react differently. Some surfaces are great for smudges but not for markers. Testing helps me understand trade-offs and choose the right tools.

Shape Exploration and Minimalism

I enjoy exploring shapes, seeing how much I can convey with minimal lines. Simplifying shapes and grouping them creates visual interest without losing realism, a dance between realism and design. I test how far I can simplify before losing clarity.

Sketching for Ideas, Not Perfection

In my sketchbook, I test ideas rather than strive for perfect drawings. If a drawing isn't working, I move on. I don't draw on the back of pages to preserve each piece and prevent imprinting. Treating each drawing individually lets me experiment without pressure.

Managing Multiple Sketchbooks

I keep multiple sketchbooks, sometimes three at once, each with different surfaces suited for various materials. Canson paper has a nice tooth for pencils, while Moleskine sketchbooks are thicker and better for markers.

Keeping Drawing Efficient

I avoid getting stuck on setup or materials. If setup hinders creating, I switch to quick and easy tools. I've trimmed my tools to essentials, I can carry them anywhere. Drawing daily maintains practice, even with limited time. Creating matters more than perfect setups.

Composing Overlapping Images

When composing overlapping images, I start with one element and build upon it, ensuring important parts aren't lost. It's like collaging as I go. Not overthinking beforehand prevents creative blocks, allowing ideas to springboard.

Deciding What to Render or Imply

I decide what to render or imply based on the reference and what catches my eye, often a pose, face, or silhouette. I start there and build around it, rendering some elements fully, while implying or skipping others.

Finding Inspiration Everywhere

If I'm unsure what to draw, I find inspiration in videos, billboards, or ads, anything can be a reference. I'll sketch from videos of performances or people walking, pausing randomly. You would be surprised how much information you can get from random sources. Drawing solves problems on paper, especially for larger pieces or commissions.
