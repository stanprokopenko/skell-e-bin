Eye Level Skill Building

Hi there! We're here to explore the assignments you submitted on eye level and perspective drawing. Many of you took on some of the most challenging exercises so far, like the twisting arrow, the curving arrow, and the floating anvil.

One of the key lessons was how shifting your viewpoint slightly can dramatically change your perception of a scene. For example, moving your view to the right causes the booth walls to shift: one wall foreshortens while the other expands. Objects that were distant seem closer, and the floor area changes.

Understanding how eye level affects your drawing is crucial. It's not just about looking up or down, it's about noticing the big differences that occur with slight changes in perspective.

Timed Sketches and Letting Go of Perfection

Many of you experimented with timed sketches, focusing on quick decision-making rather than perfecting every detail. This approach helps build your intuition and prevents you from getting bogged down in corrections.

For instance, Vera did a fantastic job noticing big differences without getting sidetracked by small inaccuracies. It's important to resist the temptation to finish every little detail and instead focus on the process and overall effect.

Swiveling Views and Vanishing Points

Some of you, like Randy, experimented with changing perspectives, such as looking up or down in a room. It's essential to understand how vanishing points work and how they affect your drawing.

Be cautious when placing vanishing points within the framed borders, as this can cause distortions, especially with two-point perspectives. Remember, bringing in a lower vanishing point requires lifting the horizon line significantly.

Measuring and Maintaining Proportions

Maintaining correct proportions can be challenging. Liesel asked how to keep the scale similar when reimagining a scene. The answer isn't always straightforward, but practice and measuring can help.

In one example, we tried to find where the highest point of a couch aligns with a leaning ladder. This required careful measuring and understanding of how to move points within the perspective grid. Sometimes, it takes multiple steps and can be complex, but it's a valuable exercise in improving accuracy.

Freehand Drawing vs. Using Tools

There's value in doing drawings freehand instead of relying heavily on tools like rulers. Freehand drawing helps develop your style and builds long-term freedom in drawing.

Carlos mentioned that sketching outlines freehand is quicker and often better for these exercises. Pamela's work exemplifies how freehand techniques can add a personal touch, making the drawing feel more authentic.

Style and Personal Approach

Your personal style comes through in how you tackle these assignments. Joi's work, for example, has a distinct style that's simple yet effective. It's essential to find your own voice and resist the urge to copy others, focusing instead on the fundamental principles.

Importance of Viewpoint in Storytelling

Understanding how eye level and perspective influence storytelling is vital. As William pointed out, looking up at a character can make them appear more powerful, while looking down can have the opposite effect. Children's books often use the child's eye level to create a relatable experience.

As artists, knowing where to place the viewer's eye level allows us to convey the desired emotion and focus in our work.

Advanced Challenges: Twisting and Curving Arrows, Anvils

Many of you went above and beyond by tackling the twisting arrow, curving arrow, and anvil exercises. These are advanced challenges that require a solid understanding of perspective.

Rovy and others showed impressive explorations of twisted arrows. Rupert found it difficult to transition smoothly but discovered that 2D slices helped. Maria created a Mobius strip and an animated version, which is fantastic!

These exercises push your skills further and help solidify your understanding of complex forms in space.
