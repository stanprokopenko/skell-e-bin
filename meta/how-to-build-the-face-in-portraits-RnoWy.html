How to Build the Face in Portraits

Establishing the Side Plane of the Head

The side plane is a crucial element in face construction. To establish it:

  1. Identify four key points: hairline, base of the nose, temple, and back of the skull.
  2. Draw the hairline as the planar break between forehead and top of head.
  3. Wrap the line to the back of the head.
  4. Use these points to create the side plane cut.

Once you have the side plane, you can establish the ear, jaw, and cheek positions. Remember that the basic principle is shaving down big forms.

Tip: The side plane helps define the perspective of the head. A wider ellipse indicates a more frontal view, while a narrower one suggests more rotation.

Developing the Eye Sockets

Start with the simplest version of the eye sockets, then progress to more complex shapes:

  1. Begin with the "Cyclops" plane: a single cut-back plane for both sockets.
  2. Mark the brow line and bottom of the sockets.
  3. Draw two lines dropping back from these points to create the socket plane.

To add complexity:

  1. Divide the forehead into three planes.
  2. Create the keystone area between the brows.
  3. Add organic curves to represent the rounded socket shape.

Remember: The eye socket structure is crucial for correct eye placement and lighting effects.

Building the Nose Structure

Construct the nose using these steps:

  1. Start from the end of the keystone.
  2. Mark the width of the nose at its base.
  3. Identify the tip of the nose (where highlights typically appear).
  4. Connect these points to create the basic nose shape.

Tip: Begin with a simplified, single-plane representation of the nose bridge, then add complexity as needed.

Creating the Denture Sphere

The denture sphere represents the mouth area:

  1. Draw a light egg shape extending from the chin to about the midline of the eyes.
  2. Use this shape to guide the placement of the mouth and surrounding features.
  3. Remember that the mouth is rounded to accommodate eating.

Important: The denture sphere helps organize features like the nasolabial furrow and cheek structure.

Gradual Complexity Approach

The key to successful face construction is starting simple and gradually adding complexity:

  1. Begin with idealized, generic forms.
  2. Slowly incorporate more specific details based on observation.
  3. Allow room for adjustments and refinements throughout the process.

Perspective and Form in Face Construction

Think of the face in terms of 3D forms and planes:

  • Use simple shapes like spheres and cylinders as the foundation.
  • Apply perspective principles to all facial features.
  • Consider how planes change direction across the face.

When drawing different angles:

  1. Adjust the width of ellipses to indicate head rotation.
  2. Maintain consistent proportions and structure across various views.
  3. Use foreshortening techniques for features turning away from view.

Remember: Every line you draw should reinforce the 3D form of the face. Think of your drawing as sculpting with lines rather than creating flat shapes.

By following these steps and principles, you'll create a solid foundation for portrait drawing that allows for both accuracy and artistic interpretation.
