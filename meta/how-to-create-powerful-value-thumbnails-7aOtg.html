Exploring Value Compositions

I'm working on value composition thumbnails for Level Two. I chose an image of a lady who appears to be begging or convincing someone. The way I compose the values can affect how the image feels, making her appear smaller and more vulnerable or larger depending on the composition.

Materials and Setup

I start with markers to quickly iterate through thumbnails, using the white of the paper as a third value. After selecting a thumbnail I like, I'll do a larger version with pencils for more control over the shapes. I'm using markers numbered 9 and 5.

First Thumbnail: Sticking to the Photo

For the first thumbnail, I stick closely to the photo to understand it better. I simplify and abstract her shape into simple forms that capture her pose. There's a lot of space above her head, so I include that. When I squint, her face and fingertips are the brightest areas, which I'll represent as small light shapes. The rest of her body becomes a big dark shape, and the background is a mid-value.

Second Thumbnail: Adding the Cast Shadow

In the second thumbnail, I include the dark cast shadow behind her as a dark mass, merging it with her body. This creates a yin-yang composition, with a diagonal division across the canvas. It strengthens the focus on her face and hands by keeping our attention in the middle.

Third Thumbnail: Lightening the Composition

I experiment with a lighter overall composition by making the background white and the cast shadow a mid-value. I introduce some of the lights on her dress. However, I find that the lit drapery distracts from the focus on her face and hands. Simplifying the dress into darker values might be stronger.

Experimenting with Cropping and Values

I try a wider composition, placing her on the far left and making her a mid-value figure against a dark background. This shifts the emphasis and makes her feel more angelic but also changes the mood. Another version focuses more on the environment, but I prefer to keep the attention on her.

Choosing the Final Composition

After comparing the thumbnails, I decide to go with the last one, which combines elements I like. I plan to use five values in the larger drawing: the darks, mid-values, lights, and introduce a fourth and fifth value for added depth.

Creating the Larger Drawing

Using 6B, HB, and 4H pencils, I redraw the composition with more accuracy. I label and plan my five values:

  1. Darkest darks (value 1): her hair and the deepest shadows.
  2. Dark mid-values (value 2): details in her drapery.
  3. Mid-values (value 3): the cast shadow and parts of her face and hands.
  4. Light mid-values (value 4): the wall and lighter areas of her skin.
  5. Lightest lights (value 5): highlights on her face and fingertips.

I fill in the darkest areas first, ensuring clean, sharp edges. I carefully render the mid-values and light values, keeping the composition balanced.

* * *

This process helps me refine the composition and focus on the most important elements, the face and hands. I made a few key changes from the photo, like darkening the bottom area, and emphasizing the cast shadow. Although not drastically different from the photo, these adjustments enhance the focus and mood I want to convey.
