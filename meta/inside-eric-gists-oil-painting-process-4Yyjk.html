Materials I Use

I primarily work with oils on hardboard. I buy large 4x8-foot sheets from the hardware store, cut them down to my preferred size (usually 16x24 inches), sand them lightly, and apply three coats of diluted gesso for smooth, even coverage. After a light sanding, the board is ready to paint on within 24 hours.

My paints are mostly Winsor & Newton professional grade. My palette includes:

  • Titanium White
  • Cadmium Lemon
  • Cadmium Yellow
  • Yellow Ochre
  • Cadmium Orange
  • Burnt Umber
  • Cadmium Red
  • Magenta
  • Transparent Maroon
  • Dioxazine (Winsor) Violet
  • Ultramarine Blue
  • Manganese Blue Hue
  • Sap Green
  • Ivory Black

While I have all these colors laid out, I don't use all of them in every painting. For this piece, I'm working with a more limited color range.

My brushes are a mix, but I'm transitioning to synthetics to be kinder to animals. I prefer filberts and flats and use smaller synthetic sable brushes (nylon) for finer details. The main brands I use are Rosemary, Robert Simmons, and Princeton. I recommend buying a few brushes, trying them out, and finding what works best for you.

For thinner, I use Gamsol, a synthetic thinner with a low evaporation rate, which means fewer fumes while painting. Always paint in a well-ventilated area. My medium is Galkyd Lite, mixed with a bit of Gamsol to thin it further. I prefer a thinner medium for the way I paint.

I use shop towels for clean-up and an aluminum mahl stick to rest my hand while painting.

Preparing the Surface

After completing my tight comp, I print it out scaled to size. If you don't have a large-format printer, you can print on multiple sheets and tape them together. I then use transfer paper (carbon paper) to transfer the drawing onto my board. Once transferred, I spray it with a workable spray fixative to seal it, so I can paint over it without losing the drawing.

Starting the Painting

One of the first things I do is kill the white of the board. I don't want the bright white to throw off my value judgments. For this painting, I wash in a light blue stain, creating a gradient that's darker at the bottom and lighter at the top. I use a mix of Gamsol and my medium to thin down the paint, working with thin washes at the start.

I encourage starting with thin paint and building towards thicker applications. I lay in a general stain using the side of my brush, enjoying that scrubby sound as I cover the board. As I layer, I can still see my drawing underneath, which is essential for maintaining my composition.

Mixing Colors and Painting Techniques

When mixing colors, I focus on simplicity. There are really only three primary colors: red, yellow, and blue. Mixing these gives you all other colors. For example, mixing yellow and blue gives you green. If you want to gray down a color, you mix it with its complement.

I like to draw in paint, focusing on shape, value, edge, and temperature. Much of painting becomes intuitive with practice. I don't overthink every stroke, instead, I let my experience guide me.

As I progress, I move to smaller brushes for finer details. I'm careful not to overwork areas to avoid turning them into "mashed potatoes". I prefer to lay down a stroke and leave it, maintaining the freshness of the paint.

Building the Painting

Consistency is key. I work diligently, often putting in long hours to bring a painting to completion. For a piece like this, it might take me 50 to 60 hours to finish. I break up my day, working on different aspects or even different projects to keep things fresh.

I focus on adding facets and tiles, creating form and texture, especially when painting complex surfaces like rocks. I pay attention to the way light interacts with surfaces, adding cool reflected light in the shadows to create depth.

Final Touches

As the painting nears completion, I let it set up and dry a bit before adding final details. Some techniques, like crisp highlights or clean edges, work best when the paint is tacky rather than wet. This might mean stepping away for a meal or coming back the next day to finish.

I always emphasize the importance of drawing skills. If you can draw well and understand shape, value, edge, and temperature, transitioning to paint becomes much easier. Painting introduces complexities, but a strong foundation in drawing will carry you through.

Advice for Aspiring Artists

The best way to learn how to paint is to paint. There's no substitute for hands-on experience. Be consistent, practice regularly, and don't be afraid to make mistakes. Over time, your stamina and skills will build.

I encourage you to share your work. Feel free to post it online or on social media and tag me. I'd love to see what you create.
