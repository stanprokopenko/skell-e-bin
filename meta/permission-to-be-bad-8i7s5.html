Embrace Being Bad

You want to become a terrific artist, yet you worry about creating something “bad.” You might feel pressure to produce great work right away. But remember how a little child learns to walk, falling over and getting back up without embarrassment. Give yourself permission to be a beginner. If you’re always afraid to be bad, you’ll avoid trying anything new.

Chunk Down Complex Skills

Being an artist doesn’t mean you have one single skill. It’s a stack of abilities, perspective, color, composition, anatomy, gesture, lighting, and more. Trying to learn everything at once can be overwhelming. Instead, break those big tasks into smaller, manageable pieces. Simplify each area, - Turn arms into simple tubes. - Practice line before full shading. - Explore just one color or tone before tackling elaborate color schemes.

When you chunk down large goals, you can stack little successes on top of each other. Minor wins encourage more consistent practice and help you keep going.

Maintain Consistent Motivation

You might start with a clear goal, but lose steam after a couple of weeks. To prevent that, set yourself up for small victories. For example, promise yourself you’ll sketch for just five minutes. If you go beyond that, you’ll feel like you exceeded your own expectation. This is a powerful reward. Another approach is to look for one good line or one pleasing color combination in your drawing, and give yourself credit for every small win.

Visual Reminders

Your brain is visual, so place reminders of your goals around you. Pin up examples of art styles you admire. Write short notes with your goals and stick them in your workspace. Seeing visual cues helps you stay connected to your dream.

Accountability and Community

It can take around three weeks to form a new habit, and you’ll need support. Seek people aiming for similar goals, such as friends, classmates, and online communities, and encourage one another. Fresh eyes often notice progress and potential that you overlook. Seeing others share your challenges and victories reduces self-doubt, and keeps you accountable.

Practice Weak Areas Step by Step

Avoid ignoring the areas that feel awkward to draw. If your proportions are rough, tackle just a small body part, like the joints of a finger. Focus on that 20% of the time (the 80/20 rule) until it improves. Then move to the next weak point. This systematic approach helps you bring every part of your skillset up to a similar level.

The 4% Approach

When you’re building skill, aim for a slight increase in difficulty, about 4% more challenging than what feels comfortable. If 2% is too easy and 6% is too hard, 4% is just enough to keep you growing without burning out. Gradually, today’s 4% improvement becomes your new comfort zone.

Reduce Stress to Unlock Creativity

When you’re stressed, creativity plummets. You freeze, flee, or fight, and none of those states help you produce original art. By allowing yourself to be a beginner, setting realistic goals, and celebrating small wins, you lighten the emotional load. That reduced stress gives you the freedom to experiment and make creative connections you might never see when you’re tense or self-conscious.

Remember that developing artistic skill is emotional physics. Once you get moving and maintain momentum, piling up small wins, giving yourself permission to learn, and staying inspired, you’ll reach your goals with less friction and more joy.
