Let's examine Hiroshi Yoshida's 'Rapids' and observe how he masterfully uses perspective to depict water.

Water Arranges into Ellipses

Notice how water arranges itself into roundish shapes when you look down on it. However, when you view it from the side, as we do here, these shapes compress into ellipses. Yoshida is keenly aware of this effect.

The Waterfall's Planes and Curves

Observe how Yoshida perceives the top plane of the waterfall as a surface with some parts higher than others. This plane breaks and forms a sloping front plane with a gentle curve.

Rhythm of Light and Dark

He arranges a rhythm of light areas with dark patches, then dark areas, followed by light areas with dark patches. This is a matter of composition, not perspective. Yet, he does not sacrifice perspective to achieve this beautiful rhythm.

Use of Cross Contours

Yoshida employs perspective by making almost all the lines cross contours. Even the waterfall is treated as a portion of an interior cylinder.

Treating the Waterfall as a Cylinder

He makes the lines crowd together at the edges, as happens on the inside of a cylinder, and spread out as they face toward you.

Scale and Distance in Wavelets

Look at how the froth and wavelets get smaller in their structures as they recede into the distance, compared to the larger conglomerate shapes they form closer to you.

Appreciating the Artwork

While this piece is meant to be enjoyed for its beauty and not overanalyzed, understanding the perspective enhances your appreciation. Part of Yoshida's ability to create such a remarkable picture lies in his control of the convincingness of the water. It's like a cartoon, and yet it looks real.

Applying Perspective Mastery in Your Work

Use this artwork as an example of perspective mastery. Yoshida knew perspective and used perspective effectively. As you study, find images you can understand in these terms and that showcase skills you want to develop in your own work. Comb through them to notice the use of perspective.
