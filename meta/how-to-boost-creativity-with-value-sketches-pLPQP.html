How to Boost Creativity with Value Sketches

Focus on Shapes and Values Over Details

When working on value compositions, it's important to focus on shapes and values rather than getting caught up in construction and proportions. Instead of thinking about what each piece is, concentrate on the shape and the value it represents. You're dealing with shapes in space and how they lock together, so it's okay if the face or figure doesn't have exact proportions.

You don't want to get it totally wrong, but you don't need to be so accurate that you're constructing with methods like the Loomis head. For example, you could simply use an oval for the face. As long as you're considering how this light oval interacts with the darker background and surrounding shapes, simplifying can be beneficial. This allows you to concentrate on the overall composition without getting bogged down in details.

Maintaining Clarity and Managing Texture

Be mindful of the texture in your value compositions. If your shading is too scratchy or textured, it can break the clarity of the values. This is especially important in smaller thumbnails, where excessive texture can become distracting. As the texture remains the same size but the drawing is small, it becomes more prominent and can overwhelm the composition.

To improve clarity:

  • Stay cleaner with your shading. Aim for smooth, even values within each shape.
  • Fill in your shapes completely. Avoid leaving gaps where the white of the paper shows through unless it's intentional.
  • Use the appropriate paper. A smoother paper can help reduce unwanted texture.

Remember, the goal is for your value groups to be clear and distinct, so that when you squint at your composition, the major shapes and values stand out.

Experimenting with Compositions

Don't be afraid to experiment with your compositions. It's easy to stay safe and make only minor adjustments, but pushing yourself to try new ideas can lead to more dynamic and interesting results. Avoid focusing too much on accuracy and allow yourself to explore different possibilities.

Consider changing elements like:

  • Cropping: Try moving your subject around within the frame to see how it affects the composition.
  • Value distribution: Play with different arrangements of light, mid, and dark values.
  • Background elements: Add or alter elements in the background to enhance the narrative or balance the composition.
  • Narrative elements: Introduce new storytelling components to add interest and engage the viewer.

As one student noted, it's amazing how much the feel of a little thumbnail can change depending on the distribution of just three values. Thumbnails allow you to test out such different ideas so quickly. Why wouldn't you take advantage of that?

Focusing on Simplicity and Big Shapes

When creating thumbnails, focus on big value groups rather than details and lines. Avoid getting caught up in outlining or adding small shapes that can clutter the composition. Instead:

  • Simplify your shapes. Use large, clear shapes to represent different value areas.
  • Think in terms of masses, not lines.
  • Avoid floaters. Small, disconnected shapes can be distracting and can detract from the unity of the composition.

By keeping your shapes simple and your value groups distinct, you'll create a stronger overall impression in your composition. This approach helps communicate the big idea without getting lost in the details.

Balancing Proportions and Creativity

While accuracy in proportions is important, don't let it hinder your creativity or experimentation in thumbnails. Thumbnails are meant to be quick, exploratory sketches. It's acceptable if the proportions are not perfect.

Remember:

  • Don't get bogged down by details in thumbnails.
  • Use thumbnails as a tool to explore different ideas quickly.
  • Focus on the overall composition, not perfect proportions.

If you find that your intuitive ability to draw correct shapes or proportions isn't there yet, continue practicing proportion exercises outside of this activity. With time, you'll become more comfortable and can get proportions close without trying too hard.

Using Thumbnails to Explore Ideas

Thumbnails are a powerful tool to test out different ideas quickly. They allow you to:

  • Experiment with different compositions without investing too much time.
  • Try out various value schemes and distributions.
  • Explore different narratives or concepts.
  • Embrace creativity and think like an artist.

For example, you might try:

  • Adding a cast shadow on the wall to create depth and interest.
  • Changing the overall value key (making the composition lighter or darker overall).
  • Altering the focal point by adjusting value contrasts.
  • Introducing new elements such as narrative components to add interest or tell a story.

The purpose is to start experimenting and bring your own ideas into your work. Instead of just doing fundamental concepts, push yourself to think creatively and see what unique compositions you can come up with.

Embracing Creativity and Narratives

Don't hesitate to bring your own creativity and narratives into your compositions. Think about:

  • What story you want to tell.
  • How you can use values and shapes to convey that story.
  • Adjusting elements to enhance the emotional impact.

For instance, adding an octopus with tentacles holding up your subject or placing them in a different environment can drastically change the narrative and make your composition more engaging. It's about being an artist and expressing your perspective.

Managing Texture and Cleanliness

When working traditionally, achieving the same level of cleanliness as digital art can be challenging, but it's important for clarity. To manage texture and keep your compositions clean:

  • Practice your shading. Do warm-ups by shading small squares quickly and cleanly.
  • Develop hand control. With practice, you'll be able to fill in areas more smoothly and efficiently.
  • Use the side of your pencil. This can help produce smoother values.
  • Keep the focus on the composition, not the medium.

Remember, once you get good at shading, it won't be tedious anymore. Your hand control will improve, and you'll be able to shade cleanly without laboring over it.

Examples of Effective Value Compositions

Looking at examples can provide inspiration and guidance. Notice how:

  • Strong compositions use clear, distinct value groups.
  • Simplifying details can enhance the overall impact.
  • Experimentation leads to unique and compelling compositions.
  • Using straights in design can make shapes more deliberate.

Reviewing others' work can show you how to push your own creativity and avoid common pitfalls. For example, some students effectively used cast shadows or cropping to create more dynamic compositions. Others kept their shapes simple and focused on the big idea, resulting in strong and clear thumbnails.

Be cautious of common mistakes, such as:

  • Relying too much on lines and outlines instead of shapes and masses.
  • Creating too much texture that distracts from the composition.
  • Having repetitive shapes that don't contribute to the overall design.
  • Including too many small, disconnected shapes (floaters) that can be distracting.

Remember that composition can be subjective, and everyone may feel differently about each thumbnail. However, maintaining clear shapes and value groups is key to creating impactful compositions.

* * *

Creating strong value compositions is about focusing on shapes and values, maintaining clarity, and embracing experimentation. Use thumbnails to quickly try out different ideas, and don't let yourself get caught up in details or perfect proportions. By concentrating on big shapes and value groups, and bringing your own creativity and narratives into your work, you'll develop compositions that are both impactful and engaging.
