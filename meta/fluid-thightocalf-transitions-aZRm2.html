Observing the Leg’s Front View

You want to see how the thigh and lower leg connect by focusing on the knee area. From this angle, the inside of the thigh is often simpler and somewhat straight or only slightly curved. The outside tends to have a more noticeable curve or S-curve that transitions into the knee.

The “Coke Can” (or Bulging Box) for the Knee

It helps to picture the knee area as a small cylinder or “Coke can.” This shape can also be a bulging box that sits between the thigh and the lower leg. You use this shape to keep the forms structured and prevent the leg from looking rubbery.

Thigh to Knee Transition

Visualize the thigh tapering down toward the knee. The important overlaps happen where muscles bundle around the knee:

  • The inner thigh can be straighter and simpler to measure for proportion.
  • The outer side often curves outward more and leads into the knee’s bulge.

When drawing, mark the slight bumps you see on both sides. They might not be perfectly parallel but stay somewhat close, which helps you capture the box-like sense of the knee’s structure.

Knee to Lower Leg

From the knee’s “Coke can,” project the main lines down to the shin:

  • The shin bone usually shows a moderate curve or S-curve on the way to the ankle.
  • The calf sits behind the shin. In your drawing, let the front edge of the shin overlap the calf so the calf appears in the background.
  • As the leg narrows toward the ankle bone, keep track of the subtle curves.

Overlaps and S-Curves

You create depth by showing clear overlaps:

  • The thigh overlaps the top of the knee.
  • The calf overlaps behind the shin.

These overlaps help show the leg’s volume. You also want to decide how smooth or pronounced these S-curves should be. Practice iterating your choices for how straight or curved each segment is, to learn how it affects the leg’s overall look.

Bulging Curves and Flow

Notice how each side of the leg often creates a series of bulges that alternate high and low:

  • One side might have a higher bump and then taper as it flows into the next curve.
  • The other side might have its bump lower down before it tapers away.

It’s like a wave fading into the ocean on one side and an echo of that wave on the other. This “ping pong” of high-low-high-low keeps the flow dynamic. You’ll see this rhythm in many parts of the body.

Practice and Next Steps

As you continue, practice building the knee and lower leg from simple forms. Focus on:

  1. Maintaining the Coke can shape for the knee to prevent confusion.
  2. Showing clear overlaps where the calf sits behind the shin.
  3. Studying the flow of bulging curves on both sides of the leg.

With repetition, you’ll get comfortable merging all these shapes and rhythms. Keep it loose at first, then refine the connections so they feel solid but still fluid.
