How to Draw the Body with Eggs and Tubes

Copy Code

I like to see bodies as combinations of eggs and tubes, sometimes with boxy corners. Eggs tend to have bulges and natural curves, while tubes can be straight or curved. These forms help me simplify anatomy. Then I add shadow shapes and contour lines to show depth and volume.

Building Forms with Eggs and Tubes

I start by picturing major body parts as egg-like or tube-like shapes.
- The rib cage often feels like an egg.
- Arms and legs can be seen as tubes.
- When two forms meet, the transition can be smooth, slightly wobbled, or have a clear corner.

When I’m not sure how to fit everything together, I focus on the main egg or tube and keep it simple. I watch how smaller forms can bump or wobble off these bigger shapes.

Drawing Shadow Shapes

Shadow edges often follow the same structure as those egg and tube forms. If the form is curved, the shadow boundary will curve. I let the shadow shapes flow across the forms.
- Soft edges happen where light gently transitions into shadow.
- Hard edges are used for cast shadows or to show a sharp turn in the form.

I sometimes see a “zigzag” or “bump” in the shadow when one form meets another. Even if I’m not sure of the exact anatomy, reproducing that bump gives me a more realistic result.

Corner Logic with Boxes

Sometimes a form feels a bit boxy, so I look for corners in the highlights or the core shadows. If I see a strong highlight that abruptly changes direction, it can indicate a corner. Boxes work well for indicating sharp planes that catch light or fall into shadow differently.

Simplifying and Iterating

I keep my design flexible. When I see a bulge but don’t fully understand the anatomy, I exaggerate or smooth it based on my needs. If I notice too many wobbles that confuse me, I simplify back to one egg or one tube. Each time I work out a shape, I can iterate on it by adding or removing subtle bumps to suggest secondary forms.

Practice Suggestions

I recommend practicing with a small area first. Take an egg of the upper leg, attach the tube of the lower leg, and add a simple shadow. If you feel comfortable, introduce a boxy corner on a highlight or core shadow. Layer these ideas step by step. By stacking simple shapes and shadow logic, you can create a figure full of organic complexity while still keeping it easy to handle.

I hope these tips help you see the figure in manageable parts. Have fun experimenting and combining eggs, tubes, and boxes in your drawings. I look forward to seeing how you apply these ideas.
