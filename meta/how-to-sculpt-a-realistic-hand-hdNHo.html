Observing and Posing the Hand

It's crucial to observe the hand from every angle. I try to include as much as I can of me posing my hand, looking, and really understanding what's happening with my hand from every angle. This information is then applied to the sculpture.

Every time I'm about to add a piece of clay, I first pose and check whatever form I'm paying attention to. For example, the tip of the finger and how it extends forward. Sometimes, I might choose to exaggerate or accentuate some features if it makes the sculpture more interesting.

Adding and Shaping Clay

We start by building out secondary forms. Here, I'm laying in the fat pad of the hand. Remember, the webbing between the fingers angles forward, so include that as you're sculpting.

I'm also showing the features of the knuckles and how those are built out. If you're sculpting a female hand, you might not want to exaggerate the muscles and bones as much. You might go for a more simplified, softer hand. These are artistic decisions you have to make as you sculpt.

Artistic Decisions and Exaggeration

Even though I'm sculpting from direct observation and using my hand, I allow myself some freedom with the features. If pulling out an anatomical feature makes the sculpture better, I'll do that.

For example, when sculpting the tip of the finger, I think about how it comes out, dips in a little, then comes out again. These subtle areas can add life and interest to your sculpture.

Anatomical Features and Subtleties

Understanding the webbing between fingers is important. It angles forward more than 45 degrees. Pay attention to the gesture and curves of the hand. For instance, the muscle-tendon form on the pinky side transitions to a group of tendons and muscles on the front side.

When laying in fat pads, think about them as individual forms. However, avoid making the separation between forms too dramatic, or it starts to look fake. Subtlety is key, especially in transition areas from form to form.

Practical Tips and Motivational Advice

Set goals and limits for your projects. For example, you might set a timer to finish each stage of the process or aim to complete it in 10 or 20 hours. This helps you stay focused and efficient.

Comparing different sculpting methods can be beneficial. Some sculptors build up thickness and then remove material. I recommend focusing on the outline one thing at a time, step by step, in a structured process.

Final Thoughts and Next Steps

At this stage, a lot of the secondary forms are in place. Take a knife or similar tool and draw in some guidelines that represent the borders where one form transitions into another. This helps design the forms in a more interesting way.

Continue to build out forms, considering where the next piece of clay should go to improve the sculpture. This method of drawing guidelines and adding clay makes the sculpture look more like a master study.

Keep practicing, stay creative, and stay productive. I'll see you in the next demo.
