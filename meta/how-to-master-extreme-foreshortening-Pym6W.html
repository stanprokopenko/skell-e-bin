How to Master Extreme Foreshortening

Pushing Foreshortening with Lines

When working on foreshortened figures, you want to push the extreme cases of foreshortening by shortening the lines to create depth. For instance, if you're drawing a figure where the rib cage is pushing away and the pelvis is shifting back, focus on shortening the lines between these masses to emphasize the in and out movement.

Emphasizing Wraps and Direction Changes

After shortening the lines, emphasize the wraps to show how forms turn in space. For example, if the pelvis is rotated opposite to the rib cage, use wrapping lines to convey this twist. By combining shorter lines and wraps, you can effectively show parts of the body moving away or coming toward the viewer.

Handling Foreshortened Limbs

When drawing foreshortened limbs, such as an arm coming toward the viewer, shorten the length of the lines to indicate depth. An arm that might normally be longer could be drawn only a fraction of its usual length to show it coming forward in space. Use rubber band lines to help visualize the tension and direction.

Maintaining Rhythm in Gesture Drawing

Even when dealing with challenging poses, the rhythm and flow of the figure should remain intact. The animated, active, fun, lyrical movement hasn't disappeared, it's just become more complex. As you shorten lines and emphasize wraps, don't lose sight of the overall gesture and movement in the figure.

Avoiding the Temptation to Elongate

In foreshortened or reclining poses, it's common to mistakenly elongate parts of the body. Instead, you should do the opposite and shorten the lines to convey depth. Be mindful not to make limbs progressively longer, as this can flatten the pose.

Using Asymmetry in Space

In poses that seem symmetrical from a straight-on view, create interest by using asymmetry in space. This means showing how different parts of the body project forward or backward, even if they appear symmetrical from the front. For example, draw the rib cage going away in space while the pelvis comes toward you.

Experimenting with Shoulder Tilts

You can introduce asymmetry by tilting the shoulders. Adding a tilt line for the shoulders can give your gestures a unique expressive quality and prevent the pose from feeling too static. Shoulders have their own expressive qualities, so don't hesitate to experiment with them.

Planning for Overlapping and Hidden Forms

When limbs overlap or parts of the body are hidden, focus on the continuity of rhythm and movement. Anticipate where forms begin and end, and let the lines flow from the inside out rather than closing off shapes too early. This approach helps maintain a sense of depth and prevents the drawing from feeling flat.

Using Line Weight and Composition

Utilize line weight to direct the viewer's eye and emphasize certain areas in your drawing. By darkening lines in key areas, such as weight-bearing limbs or focal points, you can enhance the sense of depth and importance. Think of your gesture drawings as compositions, even at the notational stage.

Practicing Ellipses and Wraps

Improving your ability to draw ellipses and wraps is crucial for conveying depth and form. Practice drawing ellipses at various angles and use wrapping lines to show how forms turn in space. This technical skill will greatly enhance your ability to handle foreshortening.

Embracing Invention and Design

Don't be afraid to change or adjust a pose to make it more dynamic or better suit your composition. You can invent new elements or adjust existing ones to enhance the overall impact. Studying existing compositions and poses can inspire new ideas and help you develop your own unique designs.

Understanding the Role of Gesture

Remember that gesture drawing is notational. It's a quick way to capture the essence of the pose, and lay the groundwork for further development. The gesture is not the end of the drawing, it's the starting point. Don't put too much pressure on yourself to make it perfect at this stage.

Tackling Symmetrical Poses

Symmetrical poses can be challenging because they may lack dynamic movement. In these cases, emphasize asymmetry in perspective by showing how parts of the body project forward or backward in space. You can also use tilts and shifts in the shoulders or hips to add interest and prevent the pose from feeling static.

Systematically Practicing Challenging Poses

Approach challenging poses systematically by focusing on specific issues in each one. Whether it's a seated pose, a forward bend, or extreme foreshortening, apply the principles of shortening lines, emphasizing wraps, and maintaining rhythm. By practicing a variety of difficult poses, you'll become more comfortable and proficient in handling them.

* * *

By applying these techniques and principles, you can effectively tackle foreshortening and challenging poses in your figure drawings. Remember to maintain the rhythm, use asymmetry, and let your lines flow from the inside out. Keep practicing, and you'll expand your ability to represent the human form in a dynamic and engaging way.
