Exploring Line Weight Methods

You might wonder when to use different types of line weight and how to combine various methods. You don't have to stick to just one method throughout your drawing. You can combine them in any way you like, and the way you choose to do so becomes your visual "speaking style." Learning to communicate visually is like learning a language. Just as everyone has a unique way of speaking, you can develop your own style in drawing.

The Three Methods

There are three main methods to consider when adjusting line weight:

  1. Importance: Emphasizing the significance of certain elements.
  2. Light and Shadow: Reflecting the light source and shadows.
  3. Depth and Form: Conveying three-dimensionality and spatial relationships.

Sometimes, these methods compete with each other. For example, to show depth, you might make distant objects lighter and closer ones heavier. However, the closer objects might be inside the form, so deciding whether to make them lighter or darker can be tricky. The good news is you have many tools to work with, such as line thickness, value, blurriness, and tapering.

Adjusting Line Weight Attributes

You can manipulate various attributes of a line to convey different things:

  • Thickness: Varying the thickness can show importance or proximity.
  • Value: Making lines lighter or darker can indicate light and shadow.
  • Tapering: Gradually changing the line from thick to thin can suggest depth.

For example, you can have two lines that both taper from thin to thick, but one is overall thicker than the other. They both shift in weight, but one is heavier on average. You can also have a thick line that is light in value and a thinner line that is darker. They might feel equal in heaviness but in different ways.

By combining these attributes, you can use one line to convey multiple aspects like importance, shadows, and depth.

Demonstration: Combining Line Weight in a Box Drawing

Let's apply these concepts in a drawing of a box with a lid. Don't worry if you don't know how to draw a box, this example is to illustrate how to think about line weight in a three-dimensional object.

Establishing the Light Source and Shadows

First, sketch the box lightly. Decide where the light source is coming from, let's say from the top right. This means

  • The top plane of the box is the lightest, as it's directly hit by light.
  • The left side is in mid-tone, not getting as much light.
  • The right side is the darkest, facing away from the light.

You might also have a thin shadow at the bottom where the box touches the ground.

Adjusting Line Values

Now, establish the values of each line based on the light:

  • Make the lines on the shadow side darker.
  • For edges between light and shadow, choose a medium value to reflect both areas.
  • Keep the lines on the lightest plane light.

Incorporating Thickness and Tapers

Next, work on the thickness and tapering of the lines while maintaining the established values:

  • Closest Edges: Make the lines thicker to show proximity.
  • Distant Edges: Taper lines to become thinner as they recede into space.
  • Use an eraser to adjust tapers, making lines start thick and become thin as they move away.

You can make your line weights as simple or as complex as you like. Some artists prefer minimal line variation, focusing on other aspects like color and shape design. This is just one tool in your artistic toolbox.

Balancing Importance

Consider importance by emphasizing outer shapes over inner details:

  • Outer Lines: Thicken the lines of the outer shapes to make them more prominent.
  • Inner Lines: Keep inner lines thinner to indicate they are less important.

Be mindful of how these adjustments interact with value and depth. For example, if an outer line is in the lightest area, keep it thick but light in value to maintain the sense of light.

Refining the Drawing

You can enhance the sense of light by gently blotting out the middle of the lighter planes with a kneaded eraser. This makes the top plane feel like it's glowing. Accentuate shadows by slightly darkening and thickening lines where needed.

Remember, in practice, these adjustments are often more subtle. Exaggerating them in exercises helps you understand the principles, but in a finished drawing, you might use them less prominently. The more you use line to communicate, the less realistic your drawing may look, which is perfectly fine. If you aim for ultra-realism, you might focus more on tone than line.

Developing Your Intuitive Style

Do you think about line weight this much in every drawing? Probably not. The goal is to practice these principles so that you can apply them intuitively. Over time, you'll start making decisions where lines naturally become thicker in shadows, heavier in important areas, and lighter as they recede into space.

Your style and uniqueness come from continuous practice and exploration. Keep trying new things and see what resonates with you. Remember, this is all about developing your own visual language. Hope this helps you understand how to combine line weight methods in your drawings. Keep practicing, and you'll develop your own "speaking style."
