How to Break Down Complex Head Shapes

Basic Proportions

You want a 2 by 3 ratio for a generic head: 2 units wide and 3 units tall. If you imagine the head as an egg or sphere, cut it horizontally into three equal sections. From a front view, the bottom of the sphere usually lands around the bottom of the nose. The overall head is longer than it is wide before adding hair or other features.

The ear often sits roughly at the middle, measured from the back of the skull to the front of the ear, and then from the front of the ear to the face. The hairline, eyebrow line, nose, and chin can be placed in thirds. Another approach is to see the eyes as halfway between the top of the (bald) skull and the bottom of the chin. You can adjust these proportions slightly to exaggerate or refine a character.

Using Simple Shapes

You can use an egg, box, or tube to structure the head. Each has pros and cons:

  • Egg: Good for capturing the overall mass of the skull.
  • Box: Helps visualize planes and angles.
  • Tube: Easy for cleaning up the main form and working around cylindrical twists.

You often need a mashup of these shapes because the head has many subtle curves and planes. Begin simple and then refine. Keep in mind that the back of the skull extends beyond the face, so include it when you rotate the head away from the front view.

Drawing the Front View

It helps to mark a center line down the face and a line at the eyebrows. From there:

  • Locate the hairline, nose, and chin by comparing lengths.
  • Keep all main features, brow line, nose, and mouth corners, parallel to each other.
  • Place the ear around the same horizontal level as the eyebrows, but it can move up or down depending on the angle.

You can also box out the hairline by drawing parallel lines around the top of the head. This technique clarifies the front plane and edges of the hairstyle.

Drawing Three-Quarter Views

Slightly Above

When you are looking slightly down on the head, the chin and jawline appear higher compared to the top of the skull. The center line will curve accordingly, and features still need to be consistent with each other in perspective. Parallel lines for the brow, nose, mouth, and chin maintain the three-dimensional feel.

Slightly Underneath

When you place your viewpoint under the head, the ears may appear lower, and you might see the underside of the chin. Maintain parallel lines for the mouth and nose to anchor your perspective. Even subtle details like a small shadow beneath the chin reinforce the sense of looking from below.

Practice Tips

  • Sketch Freely: Start your drawing lightly, refining as you establish each feature’s position.
  • Use Parallel Lines: Brow, nose, and mouth lines should align in perspective.
  • Play with Hair: Lock in a sense of the skull’s shape first, then layer hair and style on top.
  • Refine in Stages: Add more accurate features after you have a strong sense of the overall shapes.

Keep practicing with different viewpoints to gain confidence. Over time, you will feel comfortable breaking down heads into shapes that clarify structure and perspective.
