How to Depict Age Without Wrinkles

Basic Head Setup

To start, we need to set up the basic head structure. This includes the eye sockets, nose shape, mouth, and cheekbones. Simplify the cheekbones by imagining them as glasses extending from the jaw to the ear. It's crucial to be aware of the skull structure because it helps us understand where the skin or flesh pinches or hangs. For the teeth and chin, think of a basic denture sphere.

Effects of Aging on Facial Areas

Cheek Area

Aging affects the cheek area significantly. Younger faces have smoother transitions, while older faces reveal more of the bone structure. The visibility of the cheekbones can make a figure look older because youthful faces have fat covering the bones. As we age, the fat around the cheeks drops, creating sunken areas. The malar fat shifts from high to low, settling around the nose. Emphasize gravity's effect on facial fat to depict older characters.

Eyes

The fat around the eyes also falls with age, creating pinches and wrinkles. As the fat pulls away, the skull becomes more prominent.

Mouth and Jawline

The mouth area shows aging through changes in buccal fat and jowl fat. Jowl fat drops below the jawline as we age, breaking the straight line of the jaw and suggesting aging. Increased visibility of the cheekbones enhances the contrast between bone and muscle.

General Observations

Aging isn't about melting; it's about gravity affecting the skin and fat. Youthful faces have smooth surfaces, while older faces show more bone structure. Focus on three main forms: the cheek, mouth, and eye area.

Techniques for Depicting Age

Avoid Overusing Wrinkles

While wrinkles are a common method to show age, they can be overused and distract from the form. Instead, focus on the underlying bone structure and how gravity affects the skin and fat.

Emphasize Bone Structure

Highlight the bone structure over excessive wrinkles. Understand the impact of gravity on the skin and fat to create a more realistic depiction of aging.

Key Forms

Concentrate on the cheek, mouth, and eye areas. These are the primary regions where aging is most noticeable.

Role of Light and Shadow

Head Construction

Shadow and light are crucial in head construction. Use basic shapes like spheres, cylinders, and boxes to build the head. Edges and lines describe surface turns, and an analytical study of age involves understanding light and shadow.

Shadows

Cast shadows and form shadows help describe surface changes. Lips, for example, have top and bottom planes that are important for lighting. Develop halftones in light or shadow to create focal points.

Value Scale

Render one side of the value scale to avoid competition. Softer edges describe surface turns, and adding values to light or shadow gives a more finished look.

Additional Considerations

Ear Size

Aging affects ear size, so older characters may have larger ears.

Hair Mapping

Integrate simple hair mapping with the head structure to maintain consistency.

Storytelling with Lighting

Use lighting to tell a story, not just for rendering. For example, "The Godfather" uses lighting to highlight the skull and themes of mortality.

Contours

Understand that contours are a result of internal forms, not standalone features. Knowing what creates contours will lead to more accurate drawings.

By focusing on these techniques and considerations, you can effectively depict age in your drawings without over-relying on wrinkles. Understanding the underlying structure and the effects of gravity on the skin and fat will help you create more realistic and compelling aged characters.
