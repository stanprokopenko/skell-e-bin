How to Construct the Back View of Legs

When I draw legs from the back, I remind myself that the overall silhouette is the same as the front view. Instead of the crotch as a central landmark, I see the buttocks. That guides me in establishing the basic shapes before I refine the inside and outside contours.

Inside vs. Outside

On the inside of the leg, I keep things simple. On the outside, I often describe a dynamic S-curve. Sometimes the curve is subtle, and other times I exaggerate it for emphasis. This curve helps avoid a stiff or “fused” look. Even if I can’t see it strongly in the reference, I like to include a slight shift along that outside contour.

Kneecap and Calf

From the back, I don’t see the kneecap itself. Instead, I see the hamstrings splitting around the knee and the gastrocnemius (calf) forming a teardrop shape. When the foot is pointed or raised, the calf bulges a bit more. If it’s relaxed, it’s more like a subtle teardrop. I like to show a little bump or offset around the knee where the hamstrings transition into the calf.

Constructing Forms

I often: - Mark the inside of the leg with a simple line.
- Shape the outside with an S-curve that can bow out or stay subtle.
- Identify the back of the knee and note where the calf rises into that teardrop.
- Continue down to the ankle with the curve that connects these forms.

I sometimes swing an abstract shape down through the leg and then refine it with smaller “bulges” for the calf. I’m mindful of “ping ponging” high and low points: if I bring one shape up, I’ll bring the next shape down for variety. This makes the leg flow and creates a pleasing architecture of forms.

Variations and Iterations

I try out different perspectives and subtle changes. The knee can angle toward or away from me. The calf can be more or less pronounced. Sometimes I approach the form in an almost bow-legged way, introducing a playful curve that still looks natural once I define the muscles. I enjoy tweaking these shapes to find the rhythm I want in each pose.

* * *

That’s how I think about putting together the legs in back view. It’s mostly about balancing simpler inside lines with more dynamic outside lines, while respecting the muscular landmarks like the hamstrings and gastrocnemius. These steps help me create forms that look three-dimensional and connected, no matter the pose.
