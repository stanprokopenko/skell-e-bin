How to Draw with One-Point Perspective

Copy Code

Setting Up the Perspective Grid

I begin by setting up a one-point perspective grid. Getting this grid right is crucial because it determines how foreshortened the airplane will appear. I make sure the grid extends far enough to accommodate the wings and other parts of the plane. Including more grid lines than you think you'll need can be very helpful.

Sketching the Basic Structure

Next, I divide the airplane's body into three parts. The first two sections are equal, while the last section is longer. Estimating these proportions isn't easy, even for me, but it's essential for accuracy.

I draw vertical lines to establish the corners of the plane's body. Then, I create a right-angled box in one-point perspective with a square front. This box serves as the basic structure of the airplane.

Adding the Dome

I focus on the front of the plane, which has a dome. I find the center of the front face and sketch an ellipse to represent the dome. If you're a beginner, don't worry if this seems tricky, you'll learn how to place that dome in time. For now, just give it your best estimate.

Drawing the Tail

Moving to the back of the plane, I sketch the tail. The tail rises significantly higher than the top of the dome. Remember, the height of the tail isn't foreshortened since it's vertical, it diminishes in size due to perspective but doesn't tilt.

I find the center area on the back plane and estimate how far forward the tail comes, about a third of the way. I draw vertical lines to represent the tail's side planes, which are extremely thin because of the foreshortening.

Adding the Stabilizing Wings

Next, I add the stabilizing wings. They run right across the top plane of the tail section. Starting from the top of the back plane, I estimate their width to be about the same as the height of the tail. I draw them as rectangular wings first and then refine their shapes. Remember, these wings are slightly angled due to the perspective.

Drawing the Main Wings

The main wings extend back to the vanishing point, which makes them appear highly foreshortened. This can look odd at first, but trust the grid, it guides the perspective accurately.

I find the base of the wings on the body and draw them outwards, ensuring they align with the perspective lines. These wings will appear extremely angled due to the one-point perspective, which is perfectly normal.

Finalizing the Drawing

With the basic shapes in place, I go back and darken the important lines, adjusting as necessary. Sometimes, I use a straight edge to clean up lines, and that's perfectly fine. Precision is important, but if something doesn't look right, trust your eye over the instruments.

I lightly erase construction lines that are no longer needed. For the dome and other curved elements, I might use templates, or freehand them if I'm feeling confident. Don't worry if your ellipses aren't perfect, they'll improve with practice.

In the end, I have a drawing of an airplane in proper one-point perspective, with all parts proportioned and positioned accurately. Remember, it's all about starting with a solid grid, establishing your basic shapes, and refining from there. Now, give it a try yourself!
