How to Simplify the Face into Geometric Forms

Copy Code

Welcome to the head drawing and construction course. Today, we'll be talking about a step-by-step approach to understanding the proportions of the face, breaking down the features, and adding some light and shadow.

Basic Head Structure

Start with a basic sphere. Consider the tilt, the jaw, and the chin. Think about the orientation of the rotation of the head. Gesture the neck, noting the pinch and stretch.

Proportions

Mark the brow line and find the center. Remember the proportions: halves and thirds. The hairline is at the top third. Mark the four points for the sides, creating quarters. Place the ear in one of these quarters. From there, draw the jaw to the chin.

Feature Placement

Add the sockets and relate them to the forehead. Start to think of how the cheek might go behind, creating an overlap with the brow. Next, add the keystone. Draw the socket line and find the nose. Use the proportion line to create a simple box for the nose. This can give you the corner of the eye. Sometimes, the rhythm of the face can be misleading, so observe carefully.

Add the denture sphere for the mouth area.

Adding Hair

If you're interested in adding hair, think of it as a hat. Use wrapping lines to bring a shape and connect it around the head. Address it like Barbie hair. It is one plastic mold that's rested on top of the head.

Developing the Features

Now we'll start to add and play around with some of the features.

Eyes

You can start by putting the pupil and iris first, then put the structure of the lid on top. Observe the development of the brow ridge. The inside of the socket may feel more recessed. For the lower lid, think of the structure of a box. You can even start to make small observations about the structure of the fat underneath the eye and how to transition from a hard form to a softer form. Always keep it as simple forms: a ball, a stretched cylinder, or a curved box.

On the other side, you might need to narrow the keystone a bit. Look at the structure of the eye, the attachment that has that box, how it hits the ball of the eye, comes up, and goes across. You might see a little bit of the fat back there, a softer form over that harder form.

Nose

For the nose, you might pinch the box up because the nasal saddle looks to be really narrow and thin. Find the centerline of the nose. You don't have to draw it as if it's in profile, but think about the planes of your box and adjust them to be larger or more slender as needed. Note how it drops back and comes forward, then down again. This could be the bell of the nose.

Each little form is an opportunity to pause and think about one thing at a time, the structure and the perspective.

Lips

For the lips, you can start by bringing down the philtrum shape. If you're comfortable with the proportion line of your lips, you can bring that down to the denture sphere. Remember the M and W shapes, the process is to get the M and W wrapped across that center line. The lips here might be really thin, so you adjust accordingly. Echo around, matching the planes with the different turns and contours of the lips.

Chin and Buccal Fat

The chin might be really broad. You could adjust by lifting it some. Think about the buccal fat, surrounding the face.

Adding Light and Shadow

Now we've worked through a simple construction development and have a breakdown of the features. We're ready to start adding some light and shadow.

Considering the Edges

To bridge the linear into the tonal, consider the edges when you see the fall of light and shadow on a specific surface.

  • Use soft edges for spheres.
  • Use firm edges for cylinders.
  • Use hard edges for boxes.

Form Shadow and Cast Shadow

When thinking about lighting, consider the form shadow, the darkest part of the shadow caused by light hitting the form from a specific angle. Also, look at the cast shadows.

Questions About Lighting

It's sometimes good to ask questions like:

  • What type of light? Is it sun, candle, or something else?
  • What's the direction? Is it coming from the top or bottom?

This helps build more story and gesture in your drawing.

Value Compositions

In concept art or film, you can reduce lighting down to about five different value compositions:

  • High key
  • Low key
  • High contrast
  • Mid-range

Associating light and its position with one of these value compositions can give you a lot of gesture and storytelling from simple steps.

Finding the Core Shadow

When thinking more about the face, focus on the core shadow, the part of the shadow that's describing the form and its turn. Look at that in contrast to the areas that are more cast shadows. Focus on where the core shadow is and the quality of the halftone and edge that comes off of that.

Mapping Edges to Replace Lines

When you think of lighting, don't try to manufacture a copy of what is in front of you. Think about timing, timing the viewer's eye as it tracks across the surface.

  • No lines in nature, just edges.
  • Replace lines with edges.
  • Understand the parts of the face as simple forms.

Lighting the Big Forms First

Always light the big ideas first, whether it's the big graphic separation and edges or the forms themselves. Don't get caught up in rendering small details like the pupil and iris before considering the overall lighting.

Applying Light and Shadow to Features

Forehead

Consider what kind of surface your forehead is, a sphere, cylinder, or box. Your first line will track where the difference is between light and shadow. Your core shadow wraps the form, so soften it out to create that halftone soft edge you want to read consistently with the sphere.

Eye Sockets

For the sockets, which are boxes, keep the edges sharper to get that sense of turn. The keystone might be more of a cylindrical form, so map the shape accordingly. The cast shadow from the brow creates interesting shapes over the eye.

Nose

Think of the nose as a cylinder or a box. Replace the line with a softer edge for the cylindrical parts. Observe the core shadow and the plane changes. Use sharp, hard edges for cast shadows, consistent with drawing a box.

Cheeks

For the cheekbone, it could be a cylinder or a box. Separate light from shadow and give a gradation to the edge. The malar fat pad may have a more gradual turn, so provide a slower gradation.

Mouth and Lips

For the lips, get the form shadow on the top lip. The upper lip might cast a shadow onto the lower lip. The forms as they get smaller won't confuse you because you still know how to read each one, a box, a cylinder, despite the smaller size.

Chin

The chin might be an egg shape. It gets a core shadow with a nice gradation that takes the viewer's eye from the area of light into the core shadow and then shadow. Notice how it blends with the jowl fat that's dropped.

Working with Values

Once you have your forms and shadows mapped, think about the value composition of your piece.

  • Value 1 is the page.
  • Value 10 is the intensity of the lighting (your core shadow).
  • Fill in the shadows to begin at a value 5.

Fill in an idea of the face or the shadow as a consistent value all the way down. Mapping your edges well will help create a subtle feeling of form or realism.

Avoiding Outlines

Lose the edges, you don't want big outlines around your head. This is the idea of the lost edge or the compositional edge. You might blow out that side of the form because it's starting to compete too much with where you want the focal point to be.

Lost Edges

When making these drawings, don't feel restricted to the outline. We don't see outlines in nature, only edges. You might lose edges to guide the viewer's eye, and to emphasize certain areas over others.

* * *

By working through a simple construction and then breaking down the features, you can begin to understand the proportions of the face. Thinking about the features as sculptures set on top of primary forms allows you to add light and shadow effectively. This approach helps build a backbone and scaffolding to the way you think about your light and shadow.
