How to Simplify Complex Poses

Copy Code

In this lesson, I want to explore how we can adjust our gesture drawing techniques to accommodate a variety of poses, including some of the more challenging ones. We've previously discussed an average or ideal approach to organizing our thoughts around gesture. However, the real challenge lies in adapting to the countless variations of poses we encounter.

Embracing Abstraction

My goal is always to reduce what I see to the bare minimum. By abstracting the figure, I can focus on the fundamental movements and rhythms. This simplification allows me to use it as a departure point to ensure that my artistic aims are fulfilled.

For instance, when tackling a forward pinch pose, I focus on the weight-bearing leg, create some wraps, and consider the reversal of lines. I might include a tilt for the shoulders because they add a lot of expression. I often refer to the shoulders as the "eyebrows of the torso", a metaphor I picked up from Kevin Chen.

Adjusting to Symmetry and Asymmetry

One of the hardest poses to draw is the straight-on front view because of its inherent symmetry. To avoid stiffness, I introduce asymmetry through subtle shifts in the rib cage, pelvis, and shoulders. For example, the rib cage naturally leans forward due to the thoracic spine, while the pelvis tilts back.

I also use curves and lines strategically:

  • C-curves and S-curves represent different parts of the body fluidly.
  • Incorporating straights can suggest bone or tense muscles.

By varying these elements, I can make even the most symmetrical poses feel dynamic.

Exploring Weight and Balance

Understanding the center of gravity is crucial. I like to experiment by shifting the weight-bearing leg or adjusting the pose to see how it affects balance. Sometimes, moving the leg behind the center of gravity can create a sense of motion or tension. This exploration helps me understand how different positions influence the figure's stability.

Incorporating Anatomy at Focal Points

As I become more comfortable, I start to incorporate anatomy into my gestures at specific focal points. I'll identify the three most important areas for the pose's balance or movement and lightly suggest anatomical details there. This could be the hip, shoulder, or a stretching muscle group. However, I make sure these details still participate in the overall rhythm of the gesture.

Experimentation and Invention

I encourage you to experiment and make different choices in your gesture drawings. Feel free to push diagonals, exaggerate tilts, or adjust limbs to see how it changes the pose. The beauty of this method is its flexibility, nothing is precious, and you can freely move and adjust elements to explore different possibilities.

Universality of the Method

At this stage, I don't discriminate between body types, genders, or even species. This approach is applicable across various figures, whether male, female, or animal. For example, when sketching a horse, I use the same lines but adjust their lengths and directions to match the animal's form. This universality stems from focusing on the mechanics and fundamentals rather than specifics.

Importance of Repetition and Timed Practice

Using the timer feature during practice sessions is beneficial. It's not about working quickly but about building familiarity and routine. Repeating the same steps under time constraints forces you to focus and reinforces the process.

Embracing Playfulness and Exploration

Gesture drawing doesn't have to be rigid. It's essential to keep an open-ended mindset and avoid treating it as a strict system. Be willing to introduce new ideas and techniques. Sometimes, I'll start a gesture without the head or incorporate more anatomical qualities based on how I feel or what I want to convey.

Remember, the key is to enjoy the process. Let the abstraction and fundamentals guide you, and don't hesitate to explore and play with different concepts. This approach not only enhances your gesture drawings but also keeps your work dynamic and full of life.
