Materials and setup

I’m using markers again for this last composition thumbnail demo. I’m going with the red gray set plus white. The set has three temperature grays:

  • Cool grays: slightly blue
  • Warm grays: slightly yellow-brown
  • Red grays: slightly reddish-brown

They’re all muted grays from light to dark, with just a hint of color. I can mix any two with white, or use all three without white. I might also do the larger final with markers.

Mindset for thumbnails

I noticed in the previous one I tried to stay too clean. Using a ruler and keeping everything neat makes me hold back, avoid mistakes, and get uptight. That’s not good for exploration. I want to keep it loose but intentional. Not messy on purpose, just not distracted by cleanliness. If I mess up proportions, I’ll fix it in the next one.

Initial value read

It’s a bright shape in the middle and a very dark background. The bottom lights feel brighter because they’re warm against a cold background, but if I ignore temperature and look only at value, the top of the hat is actually very bright too. So both the top and bottom lights are bright. I’m going to crop right above the lower light source. I’m not adding story yet. No fireball or campfire. Just shapes and values.

Thumbnail 1: Study the reference

I mapped the figure fast: hat, head, hands out. No measuring. I’m just analyzing.

  • There’s a cast shadow on the wall at the bottom left, likely from the cool top light. If he’s outside, that shadow wouldn’t be there, but for now I’m sticking to the reference.
  • I see a bit of a vignette with darker corners and a lighter middle in the background.
  • There are darks inside the figure too: under the jaw and chin.
  • He’s hunched. Shoulders are up by the ears. I’ll keep that in mind next time.
  • The drapery has a big diaper fold in the middle (draping between the two shoulders), and vertical folds along the sides. I’m keeping folds simple. The middle is blown out by the light, so no deep darks there.
  • The hands in the reference are darker than the top of the hat, but I’m going to cheat and keep them lighter so they don’t disappear against the background.

This one uses three values: paper white, a mid, and a dark.

Thumbnail 2: Full dark background, hands mid

Now I’m trying a fully dark background to silhouette the figure.

  • I mapped the figure broadly and dropped the background to black right away. It’s a strong, bold statement in two values already.
  • Then I filled the interior with a mid value for hat, face, drapery. I’ll add dark accents after.
  • I made the hands a mid value this time, so I added more dark around the hands and face to pop them.
  • Most of the drapery folds here are dark, getting lighter toward the bottom.

I like this. Simple, clear silhouette with strong separation.

Thumbnail 3: Dark only at the top

I’m putting dark only at the top of the background to push contrast up there and see if it pulls the eye higher.

  • Cropping still feels strong. I’m not falling in love with any one idea, but I like this.
  • If the hands are bright white, I can extend the mid in the background up past the hands, then keep the top dark to frame the head and shoulders.
  • I tested that. I’m not sure having no black at the bottom is ideal, but I like the clarity of the focal area at the top.

Overall, all three are working, but if I had to drop one, I’d drop the first. These last two feel stronger.

Thumbnail 4: Refine the split background, strengthen the silhouette

I’m making the silhouette even clearer and being conservative with the darkest details. I’ll only place them where they really pop.

  • I kept the background treatment from the last one.
  • I reduced midtones and darks inside the figure to strengthen the silhouette.
  • I kept the hands pure white for maximum contrast.
  • Only a few key mid and dark shapes at the top.

Comparing the two similar versions: - If the light shape travels up through the figure continuously, the whole body feels important and the silhouette stays powerful. - If the light is split (bright bottom, halftone middle, bright top), that break weakens the silhouette and draws too much attention to the bottom.

I’m leaning toward the one where the light continues up, not split.

Thumbnail 5: Top light as the main light

What if the bottom warm light is not bright, just a deeper warm? Then the top light is the main light.

  • I gave more air above the head and added a light shape at the top of the background with vertical strokes, like light coming down.
  • I needed some shape up there so the extra space feels intentional.

I tried a version where that top light shape is a mid and continues down into the head. I didn’t like it. A smaller, separate light shape or ray felt better. Still, the earlier continuous light silhouette is the strongest.

Choosing the winner

The strongest is the version with: - Dark framing at the top and sides - Continuous bright light through the figure - Hands pure white - Minimal mid/dark inside the figure so the silhouette stays powerful

I’m going to do a larger version of that one.

Larger study: 4 values, simple and clear

I put a scrap sheet underneath so I don’t bleed on other pages. I’m aiming for 4 values: paper white, a light mid, a darker mid, and black. I have a 3 and 5 plus black. I could add a 7 as a fifth value, but I’ll hold off unless I really need it.

Note on numbering: in our lessons, zero is black. In these markers, zero is white. Everyone’s system is different. It’s made up anyway.

Block-in and value control

  • I dropped in the black background first.
  • Since I have an extra light value to work with now, I might bring the hands down to a light mid instead of pure white.
  • I’m layering the darker mid (5) so it stays clearly darker than the lighter mid (3). A double layer of 3 and a single layer of 5 can look too close, so I’m stacking layers of 5 and keeping 3 to single passes.

Shapes and details

  • Inside the figure, I’m staying minimal to protect the silhouette.
  • The face is mostly in shadow. I like it mysterious, looking downward.
  • I added a few darkest accents for features on the face.
  • I introduced the fourth value to add subtle detail in the big light shape, especially around the shoulders and hat so they feel a touch darker than the blown-out light below.
  • I used second layers of black and the mid to create a pseudo fifth value in a few spots, like the corners near the hands, to make them pop more.

I kept it asymmetrical and avoided overcomplicating. I’m happy with it.

* * *

It’s not wildly different from the photo, but I made a lot of intentional decisions: - Background design - Where the contrast lives - How much mid and how much light - How much to blow out vs add detail - Where to strengthen the silhouette

That kind of exploration and selection is what separates good composition from okay composition. You can get drawing and proportions right and still miss out if you don’t push the composition. Experiment, compare, and pick the strongest.
