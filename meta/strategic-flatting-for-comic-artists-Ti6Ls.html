Strategic Flatting for Comic Artists

Copy Code

Working with Intention

When I approach flatting a comic page, I always work with intention. Instead of coloring panels randomly, I plan ahead to avoid setting myself up for failure. By understanding the script and pinpointing key story beats, I can enhance the visual impact and ensure I'm not leaving anything on the table.

Approaching a Page

Comic scripts often come with a lot of words and complex art. To tackle this, I read the script and look for places where the tension rises or the mood changes. Even if the artwork shows similar drawings across panels, the dialogue might suggest a shift in mood, and that's an opportunity to express that change through color.

Tools and Techniques

Lasso and Wand Tools

I use a combination of the lasso and wand tools in Photoshop to select areas efficiently. The lasso tool is great for straight lines and precise selections, especially when using the polygonal lasso. The wand tool, with the right settings, can help select areas based on color and line art, saving time.

Filling Methods

I've set up actions to fill areas at different opacities. For example, I have actions to fill at 100%, 40%, 20%, and 10% opacity. This allows me to quickly lay down different colors while flatting. Assigning these actions to keyboard shortcuts or stylus buttons speeds up the process significantly.

Workflow Tips

Actions and Shortcuts

Efficiency is key when flatting. I assign frequently used actions to keyboard shortcuts or mouse buttons. For instance, pressing a button fills an area with the foreground color at a specific opacity. This reduces the time spent navigating menus and lets me focus on the artwork.

Efficiency and Speed

The goal is to flat quickly and accurately. It's about finding a balance between being neat and fast. Developing a consistent workflow and becoming familiar with the tools allows me to flat a page in about 45 minutes. Spending too much time on flatting can slow down the entire coloring process.

Organizing the Image

Background to Foreground

I usually work from background to foreground, tackling large, easy shapes first. This method helps in managing the complexity of the page and ensures that I don't miss any areas.

Separating Planes

Breaking down the image into major planes is crucial. I separate the top and side planes of objects like ice or mountains to define light and shadow. This approach simplifies the coloring stage, as I can easily select these areas later.

The Importance of Clean Flats

Clean flats are essential. They allow for easy selection during coloring and prevent frustration later on. While speed is important, it's vital to avoid being messy. Sloppy flats can lead to more work down the line, so I strive to keep my selections precise.

Time Management in Flatting

When I first started, I took time to get comfortable with the tools and develop my workflow. Now, I aim to flat a typical page in 45 minutes to an hour. Meeting tight deadlines means focusing on what's important and not getting bogged down by unnecessary details.

* * *

Flatting is about problem-solving and organizing the image to highlight the focal points. By working with intention, using efficient techniques, and managing time effectively, you can streamline the flatting process. Remember, it's all about setting yourself up for success in the coloring stage.
