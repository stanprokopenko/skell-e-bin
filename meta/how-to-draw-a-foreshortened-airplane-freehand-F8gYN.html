Setting Up the Grid and Vanishing Points

In this demonstration, I'll freehand draw a foreshortened airplane. While it's not daringly foreshortened, it's still a good exercise in perspective drawing. I start by establishing my vanishing points. I'll let the second vanishing point be well off the page. To give myself some structure, I draw a grid with vertical lines. I'll taper the verticals ever so slightly.

Drawing the Front Face

I decide where to place the front of the airplane. In my rough sketch, I establish the front face right about here. Conveniently, I have a fairly square front face on the grid. I scribe the front face there, making sure it's square in perspective because the front of the plane is very much a square in perspective. Here, proportions matter more than in previous demos.

Accounting for Taper

Drawing the Back Wings

Cleaning Up the Drawing

Now, I clean up the drawing. I might erase some of the scaffolding lines that confuse the image. I darken the important lines. Drawing the Main Wings

The main wings are thicker, almost twice as thick as the back wings. I estimate their thickness based on the height I lifted up earlier. I need to see over to the other side of the airplane, drawing the parts that are not immediately visible to establish symmetry.

I look at the ortho and see that the width of the wings is about the depth of the whole plane, so they will be plenty wide. On the grid, I make sure the wings go out sufficiently, aiming along the correct grid lines.

I lift up the wing the same amount of height as before, and draw the top of the wing. I figure out where the wing tapers back, halfway along the wing, and connect the points. Now, it's easy to connect these dots, and I have a scaffolding for symmetrical wings.

Final Touches

With the wings and tail in place, I can start adding final touches. I clean up any lines, making them clearer and more deliberate. I might use a thinner lead for finer details. I ensure any ellipses, like the canopy and the nose of the plane, are correctly drawn.

For the nose of the plane, I find the center of the front face and draw an ellipse that represents the round point, or propeller, on the front. I draw a straight line out, angled appropriately, and estimate how far out it should go.

Finally, I clean up the drawing further, erasing any unnecessary lines and refining the important ones. I adjust any discrepancies, such as the tapering of lines that might have been off. By the time I'm done, I have a freehand foreshortened airplane drawn in perspective.
