How to Create Depth with Gradation

The Beginning of Shadows

I start by thinking of two sides of the form: the light side and the shadow side. I draw the shape of the shadow on the form, then give the beginning of that shadow a soft edge. That soft edge is like a microgradation. Even with a small transition, I begin to suggest volume. If I decide to deepen that microgradation, it becomes a fuller gradation, which rounds the form more.

On complex figures, I might connect multiple shapes, but the audience only notices if I complete the idea of softening or sharpening the edges. The result is a clear sense of which shapes are fully rendered and which forms are still in progress.

Cast Shadows vs. Form Shadows

A cast shadow appears where one form blocks light from falling on another surface. When I see a shadow on the tabletop or on another part of the figure, I recognize it as a cast shadow. Meanwhile, a form shadow (or “core shadow”) is where the light naturally turns on the form itself. The start of that shadow is a soft edge that marks where the form begins to transition from light to dark.

Sometimes, the edge of a cast shadow can be softened (like if a lamp’s bulb creates a soft glow), but I usually keep it from becoming a full gradation because that would round what’s supposed to be a sharp boundary.

Rounding the Form with Gradation

Gradation isn’t just about value, it can also include color and temperature for those who paint. But the main idea is the same, more gradation means rounder forms, and less gradation suggests boxy planes.

I keep in mind that lights stay light and darks stay dark. If I add a lot of darker halftones in the light side, then I need to squint and confirm the shadow side remains clearly darker. This helps separate the form into distinct light and dark families.

I often blend the shadow into a dark background if they’re the same value. That’s something painters from the Brown School, like Rembrandt, did often. The form can drop off into darkness, which adds drama without losing clarity.

Value Separation and the Squint Test

I rely on a simple rule: the light side should be a range of light values, and the shadow side should be a range of dark values. Sometimes I leave the darkest halftone close to the shadow’s value, but I keep an overall separation so my shapes remain clear. I squint to check if the shadows are still distinctly darker than the lights.

Demonstrating on a Figure

When I draw a figure, I use basic shapes like eggs and tubes to block in the torso, rib cage, and limbs. Once I have those shapes, I fill in the shadow areas and add halftones to create a soft, continuous transition from light to shadow,

• First, I sketch the big shadow shapes.
• Then, I mark the core shadow with a soft edge.
• Next, I refine that edge with a gradual transition into halftones.
• If the form is large, I let the shadow merge with the background if those values match.

By doing this, I can pinch or wobble the shape to show subtle anatomy. If I’m unsure about certain details, I can let the form’s outline blend back into the bigger shape. I’ll keep the shadow mostly intact, so viewers sense the structure, even if I simplify smaller forms.

Iterating and Refining

As I build up halftones, I might notice I need to darken my overall shadows. That can lead me to push the background even darker, so I don’t lose my separation between light and dark. I keep revisiting each area, like the chest, rib cage, or abdomen, and add or soften halftones until I get the effect I want.

Gradation is powerful because I can place it anywhere: following an egg shape in the torso, dropping light off a shoulder into the upper arm, or fading an arm into the background. The more I do it, the more I see new ways to let the form disappear, pop out, or stay subtle.

I find this approach simple yet challenging. The ideas are straightforward, soft transitions, value separation, shaping the shadow, but applying them takes practice. By breaking each form into clear, basic shapes and then iterating with small gradations, I gradually build up to a believable figure.

I encourage you to experiment with these concepts. Create that soft edge at the core shadow, push it until you get a fuller gradation, and watch your forms round out. Stick to the rule of keeping lights and darks separate, and you’ll see your figures take on a dynamic, three-dimensional quality.
