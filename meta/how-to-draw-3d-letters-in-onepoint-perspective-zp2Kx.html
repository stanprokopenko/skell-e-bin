Project Overview

Ortho Project Number One: Simple Extrusion on the Z-Axis. The project is straightforward: put your name in perspective. There are two ways to do it, and I recommend both.

Method 1: Finished Illustration

Create a beautiful, finished illustration of your name in perspective. If it feels too egotistical, use the name of someone you love. Some people will even pay to see their name in blocks!

  • Use a ruler to make it accurate.
  • Draw the letters twice: once for the front plane and once for the back plane, or at least some corners. This shows where to stop the extrusion.
  • Project the shapes to their back plane using vanishing lines to a vanishing point.
    • This step is important and can be challenging.
    • Without it, the back plane is a guess, which may be harder than you expect.
  • Connect the back lines to form the back plane, ensuring the forms are consistent.

This method is similar to drawing two squares, running diagonals, and getting a 3D-looking cube. Only now, the back shape is smaller. How small? Run lines to a vanishing point to find out.

Method 2: Rough Sketches

Exercise a whole different muscle by doing this roughly, without a ruler.

  • Draw 20 times in a week. If you enjoy it, aim for 100 times over the next two weeks.
  • Experiment with many different directions. Stretch your brain by making something interesting in perspective using this easy method.
  • Practice your freehand lines.
  • Start noticing letter forms. Use any alphabet you want to study.
  • Use any shapes you like. ** This project isn't about lettering, it's about perspective, and we're keeping it simple, a face-on view like an ortho limited to one point.

This method is less about creating a finished work of art and more about brainstorming and exploring ideas. You'll strengthen your idea-generating brain muscles.

Understanding One-Point Perspective

Limits of One-Point Perspective

You'll come up against the limits of one-point perspective, especially in how you must show that front face and where you can place the vanishing points.

Vanishing Point Placement

Placing the vanishing point affects the effect:

  • Vanishing point near the box? All is well.
  • A little less near? All is well.
  • Less near still? Well enough.
  • Getting distant? Something's weird here.

If the vanishing point swings far away from the front face, eventually the front face needs to swing away with it. This creates a second vanishing point elsewhere. It's not just too far left or right that creates distortion, too far above or below does too when there's only one point.

A front face with any distantly placed vanishing point calls out for a second vanishing point. We're learning the limits this way. When we step off the track, we need another point.

You'll learn exactly how this works when we study the picture plane and plan projection. When we study lenses, you'll learn how to play it for effect.

One point is limited. ** You may tire of the limits, but here's the thing, one point works. ** It creates the illusion, flat, shallow, or deep. If you want to draw in perspective, this is a good place to start. It's a trick that attracts some very good artists. One point has power. **

Using Instruments vs. Freehand

Sending a lot of lines back to a vanishing point can look dramatic but also crowd together awkwardly at infinity. You'd think infinity would have more room!

To make it easier:

  • Work larger and lighten up the lines as you approach the vanishing point.
  • With a long stretch of letters, lines at the extreme front ends become almost horizontal, making it difficult to control with a ruler.

Bonus benefits of taking a few of these to finish:

  • If instruments feel clunky to you, finishing a few illustrations can help you get comfortable with them.
  • This is level one: leveling up with the instruments and getting something out of it at the same time.

If you like to draw, play with this. Start simply. You will learn.

Experimenting with Letter Forms

Placing the vanishing point above or below introduces eye level and vertical position.

Placing the point to the left or right introduces lateral position.

We are moving toward complex problems one point at a time.

If you understand one-point perspective already, rehearse it this week. ** As you experiment, you may discover that one-point perspective sets us up for, brace yourself, five-point perspective, the wide-angle thing.

If you're new to this, learn and practice one-point extrusions this week. One flat plane of anything facing us directly, extruded into deep space or not-so-deep space, makes it 3D.
