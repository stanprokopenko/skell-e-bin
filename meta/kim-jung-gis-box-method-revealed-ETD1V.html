Kim Jung Gi's Drawing Technique

We're going to take an aside to show you what grids do for those who imprint them. Kim Jung Gi could fill walls with drawings like this at high speed with nothing to look at. You may wonder, how did he do it?

He tells us. In a live demo, his translator reported from Korean to English his explanation:

  • All his drawing is just these boxes.
  • After he understood the three-dimensional box, he started to improve a lot faster.
  • Even with the complicated images in his mind, this is pretty much what he's seeing.
  • All these boxes are in his mind.
  • So the entire scene is in boxes, and it's in his mind.

Visualizing Scenes with Boxes

In his head, although he didn't draw it, the horizon line is already set. So that box becomes this guy, and this guy is the one in the front. And the big one will be something else. All the big images are set, and all the boxes are in his head so that he could draw them out.

Even if it's complicated, even the complicated images are broken down into these simple shapes in the huge box. Like I said, it's basically this big box, a single big box. It comes down to how much you understand the three-dimensional box and how well you can draw it. As long as one box is drawn on this big paper, he could pull out the entire scene.

The Emphasis on Boxes

He said more than this, but all this he said. Let's take a look at some key words: the box, the box, the box. Hmm, the cube, the cube, the cube. I'm seeing a theme here.

Don't be shackled by grids. Oh wait, no, that's not it. Look at how many times... Well, I don't need to repeat it. You can read it. Or you can watch him. That may be best. Kim Jung Gi passes the varied view test.

Observing from Multiple Perspectives

One more thing he mentioned: To draw cats, dogs, airplanes, or your chosen subject, we do need to look at them objectively from more than one point of view. You've done that with orthos. Now use your orthos as reference.

My Conversation with Kim Jung Gi

I got to ask Kim Jung Gi about this emphasis on boxes six years before he died in 2022, with a mind toward you taking this course. Here it is:

And does it need to be a cube, or can it be any box?

He explained:

  • I'm going to explain what I mean.
  • How it looks from here and how it looks from here.
  • At first, I could put only one object in the box, but now I can put all the space in the box.
  • To explain the box, 1, 2, 3, 2C comes out naturally, but apart from that, I just take a box, a box on the floor.

Advice for Perspective Students

Any other advice that you would give perspective students?

When you draw, you are aware of height, width, and depth in that imaginary space?

  • It's very important.
  • People need to have a relationship with the person in front of them and with the person next to them, so that they can create stories and events for each other.
  • As the space is created, a lot of things come into the picture.
