Simple Forms for Complex Anatomy

You want to keep the figure as simple as possible while still being true to the body’s character. For the arms, start with tube shapes. These tubes can shift into boxes or balls later, as you need more structure. Your main goal is to find a straightforward design that fits the pose and maintains the energy of the figure.

Curvature and Symmetry

Most of the body’s limbs, such as arms, wrists, and hands, are asymmetrical. This means they tend to exhibit curvy, fluid lines if you map their long axes. The head, neck, and torso, by contrast, are symmetrical structures and often appear stiffer when viewed head-on. When limbs move to a more profile view, they naturally reveal their curves.

Iterating Your Drawings

Iteration is the key to better understanding and improved skill. You: - Sketch a simple shape, like a tube for the upper arm.
- Evaluate it for shape, proportion, tilt, and curvature.
- Refine and try again, each time making the design more accurate.

You’ll make quick progress if you do several iterations. This builds mastery, which motivates you to keep practicing.

Fitting the Upper Arm

The upper arm connects to a bulging shoulder area. Even though the shoulder can be complicated, treat it as a corner that attaches to the tube of the upper arm. From the front or back, the upper arm can appear fairly straight. Sometimes you’ll see a slight taper from the shoulder to the elbow. Other times you’ll notice a curve on the inside or outside. Keep checking for: - Thickness differences (shoulder vs. elbow).
- Subtle or more obvious curves.
- The position or angle relative to the torso.

Adding Other Structures for Accuracy

Occasionally, you’ll want to draw parts of the torso or rib cage to see how the upper arm relates to them. Include basic shapes for the rib cage, collarbone, or armpit to help confirm you’ve captured the right tilt, proportion, and curve of the arm. If your first pass feels sloppy, clean it up in a new iteration with clearer lines and simpler shapes.

Refining Through Practice

You’ll get a keener eye for spotting small changes in position, proportion, and curvature if you keep revisiting the same subject from different angles. Tweak the design in each fresh attempt: - Check shape design: Straight vs. curved edges.
- Adjust proportions: Overall length to width.
- Assess position: Does it tilt forward, backward, or stay vertical?

With each pass, you’ll get better results and sharper observation skills.

Looking Ahead

Once you feel comfortable with your approach to the upper arm, you can move on to attaching the forearm and exploring how it curves and connects. Keep your method of simple forms and iteration going, and you’ll continue to see your drawing skills strengthen.
