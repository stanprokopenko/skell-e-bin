How to Add Finishing Touches to Your Illustrations

Coloring Within the Lines

A big part of the finishing touches for me involves coloring within the lines. I start by adding a clipping mask on top of my line work. This ensures that any coloring I do remains confined within the existing lines.

I choose the clean inker brush for this task since I don't need much texture here. I focus on areas related to where I've placed highlights, mainly at the edges to enhance them. Initially, I might use a placeholder color to stub in the highlights, allowing me to see how it looks across various parts of the illustration before settling on the final color.

I apply this to my two main characters first, ensuring I cover all the spots I want highlighted. Once that's done, I adjust the color to find the one that makes sense. The initial color might be too bright or too purple, so I experiment with different hues, like orangey tones or reds. It's important to find a balance, sometimes brighter or slightly more saturated colors work better.

Zooming out to view the illustration as a small thumbnail helps me gauge the overall impact of the colors. I might toggle between different shades to see what works best.

Adjusting Colored Lines

When coloring the lines, I need to be cautious not to overuse them. There's a version where every colored line is context-sensitive, meaning I change the color to match the material it's representing. For instance, the highlight color on the skin might differ from that on the bunny outfit. If time permits, I might go back and adjust these individually.

Using one highlight color makes a strong stylistic statement and creates a cohesive palette. However, context-sensitive colors can give a more polished feel. For now, I proceed with a single color to see how it looks.

Sometimes, I extend the colored lines beyond where the highlight is. For example, on thin surfaces like leaves, the highlight implies that the edge catches light even if the form doesn't have a proper highlight. This technique can also help certain elements pop out, like adding colored lines to parts of the motorcycle or the character's hand.

I reserve highlights on background elements, such as buildings, since they occupy a different space and might require a different color. Keeping them on a separate layer allows me to adjust them independently.

Enhancing Readability with Line Color

Certain features, like the cat friend's eyebrow hairs, might read better if I keep them black while coloring the highlights on the rest of him. This contrast helps retain important details that might otherwise get obscured.

If needed, I might add highlights to areas without them to improve readability, like her backside. This acts almost like a hair light, emphasizing the shape.

After applying the colored lines, I review the illustration to ensure nothing becomes less readable. If an area looks off, I might add more coloring to complete the effect or remove it altogether. It's important to double-check these details.

Adding Highlights to the Background

For the background, I consider using a secondary color for the lines. I'm thinking of a pink, brighter, and more saturated color. I try applying it to the background elements and then adjust the color and darkness to ensure the shapes still read well. It's a bit of experimentation to find the right balance, possibly darkening it or adjusting the intensity.

Final Touches

Eye Light Details

As a finishing touch, I like to add a bit of an eye light. Using a very bright white and the 2D inker brush at a smaller size, I add subtle highlights to the eyes. This is done on top of everything else, mimicking how a real eye light functions. It's not enough to just make it brighter, it needs to cover up the line work to have the desired effect.

Adding a Glow Effect

Next, I add a bit of glow work on top of everything. Unlike previous steps that were within the line work, this adds another layer over the geometry. I select a soft brush with a soft pink color and set the layer mode to soft light with medium opacity.

I apply this subtly to create a bloom effect, softening some of the line work and giving one last chance to accentuate the highlights. It's about enhancing the overall mood and adding that final touch of polish. I might adjust the opacity to get it just right.

Wrapping Up the Process

This concludes my full art process from start to finish in creating an illustration. To recap:

  • Research and Gathering References: I started by getting familiar with the subject matter I wanted to draw.
  • Thumbnails: I planned out the composition and developed stories using the research, creating small sketches to explore ideas.
  • Line Art: I refined the chosen thumbnail into detailed line work.
  • Flats and Color: I added base colors (flats) and then worked on the colors, which involved making adjustments, adding shadows, and incorporating light.
  • Finishing Touches: I focused on coloring within the lines, adjusting colored lines, adding eye lights, and applying glow effects.
