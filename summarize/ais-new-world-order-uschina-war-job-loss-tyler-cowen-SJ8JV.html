Openness vs. Closeness in Technological Innovation

I recently had the chance to discuss with Tyler Cowen the implications of AI on innovation and global relations. We dove into the debate between openness and closeness in technological development.

Tyler argued that openness tends to be more beneficial in the long run. He pointed out that America's great innovations might have been delayed without its earlier period of espionage. He emphasized that we're not good at being closed, but we're pretty good at openness.

For example, attempts to keep the secrets of the atomic bomb didn't prevent them from leaking to the Soviet Union. In contrast, openness allows for shared progress and can prevent adversaries from developing independent systems that might surpass ours.

The Case of China and AI Development

We discussed how the U.S. ban on AI chips to China might have backfired. By restricting China's access to high-end chips, we pushed them to innovate alternative methods. This led to the development of deep seek, a Chinese AI model approaching the performance of U.S. models but trained at a fraction of the cost.

Tyler suggested that policies intended to hinder competitors can actually spur them to innovate in unexpected ways. He questioned whether it's better for China to be part of the same hardware network or to fully develop its own independent network.

Imitation as a Path to Innovation

We explored the idea that imitation can be a necessary first step toward genuine innovation. Drawing from a quote by René Girard, we noted how countries or cultures often start as imitators before becoming innovators. The U.S. itself engaged in economic espionage in its early days, with figures like Alexander Hamilton advocating for stealing technology from Europe.

Tyler cautioned against oversimplifying this process. He argued that copying and innovation are not opposites but part of a dialectic. Being open to innovation from unexpected places is crucial.

The Impact of AI on the Future of Work

Tyler believes that AI will radically transform the workforce. It will split the population into two groups: a small portion able to use the technology effectively, and the rest who may be left behind. Those who can collaborate with AI will leapfrog ahead in various fields.

He suggested that many jobs will involve working alongside AI. People skilled at operating AI will become far more productive. Professions requiring physical labor or creativity, areas where AI hasn't yet fully penetrated, may see increased value.

Preparing for an AI-Dominated World

Tyler emphasized the importance of familiarizing ourselves with AI. He advised spending time using the best AI systems available to understand their capabilities and limitations. This hands-on experience is crucial for adjusting to the changes AI will bring.

He also pointed out that people in the humanities could benefit greatly from AI. While AI may outperform humans in certain technical tasks, human creativity and emotional intelligence remain vital.

The Role of AI in Content Creation

We talked about how AI is changing the landscape of writing and content creation. As AI models get better at generating text, writers need to adapt. Tyler mentioned that injecting personality and originality into our work becomes even more important to stand out.

He noted that AI could serve as a collaborator, helping to generate ideas or draft content. However, it's essential to maintain our unique human perspective to remain relevant.

* * *

Living in rapidly changing times can be challenging. Tyler encourages us to embrace the changes that AI brings and to remain curious. By staying adaptable and engaged, we can navigate the uncertainties of a future shaped by artificial intelligence.
