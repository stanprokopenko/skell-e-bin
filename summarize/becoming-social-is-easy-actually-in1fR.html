Becoming Social is easy, actually

Copy Code

You want to be more social. You want to walk into a room, strike up a conversation, and have people naturally drawn to you. Maybe you even imagine yourself telling a joke and everyone laughs, not out of pity, but because you're actually funny.

But there's a problem. Socializing feels hard. You don't know what to say, you overthink every interaction, and sometimes you just don't have the energy. So instead, you scroll through your phone pretending to be busy because, in your mind, that's better than standing there awkwardly. After enough of these moments, you start telling yourself, "I'm just not a social person."

You're Not Bad at Socializing - You've Just Convinced Yourself You Are

Think about it. Were you always like this? As a kid, you probably ran up to strangers, made friends in seconds, and never worried about saying something dumb. You didn't analyze every word before speaking, you just spoke.

What Happened?

Somewhere along the way, you became self-conscious. Maybe you had an awkward moment once, maybe someone made fun of you, or maybe you just spent too much time in your comfort zone. Whatever the reason, you started believing that socializing was difficult.

The Law of Inertia Applies to Socializing

**Objects at rest stay at rest, objects in motion stay in motion. ** The same applies to socializing. If you avoid social interactions, you stay in that habit. You get comfortable being alone, and soon talking to people feels unnatural. But if you put yourself out there, even in small ways, you gain momentum. Eventually, it feels as effortless as scrolling through your phone.

How to Break the Inertia

Step One: The Two-Second Rule

Most social anxiety comes from overthinking. You see someone you want to talk to, but your brain immediately floods with "what ifs," and before you know it, you've talked yourself out of the conversation.

Here's a simple trick: Give yourself two seconds to act. See someone you want to talk to, count "one, two" in your head, and start speaking. Don't give your brain time to panic.

This works because hesitation is the enemy. The longer you wait, the harder it gets. But when you act fast, your brain does not have time to sabotage you. Think of it like jumping into a cold pool, if you hesitate, you'll never do it. But if you just jump, your body adjusts quickly. Socializing works the same way.

Remember, all kinds of fears are just in your mind. In reality, everything is very, very easy.

Step Two: Start Small

A lot of people think socializing means being the loudest person in the room, cracking jokes, or leading conversations. But that's like expecting to run a marathon when you've never jogged a mile.

Instead, start small. Say "hi," "hello," or "what's up, buddy." Laugh a little bit. Make a comment about the weather: "Look how beautiful the weather is," or "Oh wow, that's amazing." These tiny interactions help you rewire your brain to see socializing as normal.

Step Three: Match People's Energy

If someone is calm and reserved, match their energy level and start talking calmly. If someone is excited and expressive, then raise your energy level and start talking in an exciting way. Matching energy levels subconsciously builds connection and makes people feel like you get them. When people feel understood, conversations flow effortlessly.

Step Four: Use the Boom Boom Exit Rule

(By the way, I am the inventor of this method, so take it seriously.)

If the conversation is going great, keep going, keep giving your thoughts and stories. But if it's not, politely excuse yourself, "Okay, bye, brother." It was great talking to you.

Step Five: Understand That No One Cares

Here's the ultimate mindset you need: No one is thinking about you. Don't waste your time thinking about them. You might be wondering what your friends or your crush are thinking about you, but it's not true. Everyone is just messed up in their own life.

Do whatever you want to do with your life. No one will remember you after you die. I've seen people who even forgot their father, mother, or daughter four days after their death. So think about your own life first.

I do know a few people who selflessly care about others, but they have to take care of themselves first.

* * *

Becoming social isn't about changing who you are, it's about breaking inertia. Put yourself out there, even in small ways, and soon socializing will feel natural again.
