Oiling Out: Reviving Faded Colors

When oil paint dries, shadows and colors can fade, making it hard to match new paint to older layers. To combat this, I use a mixture of half linseed oil and half odorless mineral spirits to "oil out" the painting. This brings back the vibrancy and sheen of the dried paint, allowing me to work seamlessly over previous layers.

Working in Layers

There's a lot of lore around oil painting techniques like "fat over lean," but I believe in painting however feels right. While I often paint wet into wet, for glazing, it's essential that previous layers are dry. This allows me to add depth and richness without disturbing the underlying work.

Tackling a Full Scene

I wanted to challenge myself by creating a complex scene rather than a simple portrait. This is my first time attempting a multi-figure composition with a narrative element. It's pushed me out of my comfort zone and helped me grow as an artist.

The Importance of Stepping Back

Working on such a large canvas, I make sure to step back frequently. It's easy to get caught up in details that aren't noticeable from a normal viewing distance. Taking breaks and viewing the painting from afar helps me focus on the overall composition and balance.

Navigating the Ugly Painting Phase

Every painting goes through an ugly phase. It's a normal part of the process, and I never let it discourage me. Instead, I push through, knowing that with time and effort the painting will come together. I don't leave paintings unfinished, I see them as challenges to overcome.

Oil vs. Acrylic Paint

I prefer oil paint because of its versatility and control. Oil allows for smooth blending, rich colors, and a variety of techniques. While you can use oil over acrylic, you can't use acrylic over oil. Oil painting has a learning curve, but once you understand the basics, it offers amazing flexibility.

Repainting the Portrait

I repainted the face multiple times, struggling to capture the younger version of Medusa I envisioned. Deviating from the reference image to de-age her was challenging. It required patience and persistence, but ultimately, I achieved the desired look.

Embracing Creative Changes

Sometimes, I change elements from the reference photo to better tell the story. In this painting, I intentionally misaligned the reflection of the hands to symbolize Medusa's transformation. Art allows us to take creative liberties to convey deeper meanings.

Final Touches and Reflections

After countless hours and overcoming numerous challenges, I finally completed the painting. Varnishing brought everything together, giving it a unified sheen. Looking back, I'm proud of the journey and the dedication it took to bring this piece to life.
