What Are Tariffs?

Tariffs are targeted taxes on imported goods. They are paid by domestic businesses that import these goods. Tariffs fall under the umbrella of protectionism or economic nationalism. By making imports more expensive, tariffs aim to encourage domestic production. This process, known as import substitution industrialization, can foster investment, employment, and industrial growth while generating government revenue.

Historical Use of Tariffs in the United States

In the 19th century, tariffs were the defining trade policy of the United States. Alexander Hamilton believed that the young nation needed tariffs to build its manufacturing base and gain economic independence from Britain. Tariffs raised the price of European imports, encouraging entrepreneurs to produce goods domestically.

Tariffs became a significant source of federal income, making up 90% of revenue. Income tax didn't exist until 1910. However, tariffs were controversial. While they promoted industrialization in the North, they hurt the agrarian South, which relied on exporting goods like cotton and tobacco. Retaliatory tariffs from Europe harmed southern exports. The debate over tariffs contributed to tensions leading up to the Civil War.

Modern Free Trade and the Return of Tariffs

After World War II, the U.S. shifted towards free trade. Technological advancements made trade more efficient, and countries specialized based on comparative advantage. The establishment of the World Trade Organization in 1995 cemented the trend of lowering tariffs.

However, in 2018, the U.S. imposed significant tariffs on its largest trading partner, China. This move brought tariffs back into economic debate. We have recent evidence of the impacts of tariffs from this period.

Case Studies: Washing Machines and Steel

Washing Machines

In 2018, tariffs were placed on imported washing machines. Importers passed the cost onto consumers through higher prices. Domestic producers also raised their prices, despite not paying the tariff. The price of dryers increased as well, since they are often sold with washing machines. Consumers faced price increases larger than the tariff itself. While 1,800 jobs were created, each job cost consumers $815,000 annually. This was an inefficient way to create employment.

Steel

A 25% tariff on steel imports made steel more expensive for industries that use it, like automotive and construction. This reduced profit margins and led to layoffs. Although employment in the steel industry increased, there was a net reduction in manufacturing employment. Retaliatory tariffs from China targeted U.S. agricultural exports, leading to government subsidies of $61 billion to support farmers.

Long-Term Effects: The Light Truck Tariff

In 1964, a 25% tariff was placed on light trucks imported to the U.S. This protected domestic automakers and encouraged foreign companies to invest in U.S. manufacturing plants. While it supported the industry, it also reduced competition, leading to higher prices and less innovation for consumers. For tariffs to be effective, the government must ensure that protected industries remain competitive.

Challenges and Considerations of Tariffs

Companies often find ways to circumvent tariffs, such as relocating production to other countries or shipping products through middlemen. Modern global supply chains are complex, and tariffs can disrupt them. Universal tariffs may lead to higher input costs, inflated consumer prices, and retaliation from other countries. This can negate any industrial or fiscal benefits.

The Importance of Nuance in Tariff Policy

Tariffs are an economic tool, not inherently good or bad. Blanket statements about tariffs ignore the complexities and trade-offs involved. Tariffs might still be necessary in strategic sectors like semiconductors or energy, for national security reasons. However, they should be used precisely and carefully planned. Applying tariffs like in the 19th century may not work today, due to changes in global trade and production. We must consider the nuances to assess the net benefits or costs of tariffs in our modern economy.
