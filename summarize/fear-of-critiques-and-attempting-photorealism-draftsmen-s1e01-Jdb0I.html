Why We’re Here

We learn best when we can ask questions and get direct answers. This conversation shifts from scripted lessons to real dialogue. The focus: how to use critiques well and whether studying photorealism helps other styles.

Critiques and the Feedback Loop

A feedback loop is simple: we do a thing, we make mistakes, we measure the result, we adjust, then repeat. The measure can be a teacher, a tool, or any method that tells us what is off. This loop is central to learning drawing, painting, music, and sports.

Why We Fear Critiques

Critiques can feel like proof we failed. They are emotional, especially when they feel personal. Marshall mentions the “fundamental attribution error,” where a mistake gets turned into a judgment of character. Machines that point out errors do not trigger the same reaction because they are not judging us. Human feedback can feel like an attack.

Objective vs Subjective Feedback

Some feedback is objective: anatomy, perspective, value structure, proportion. It can be checked. Other feedback is subjective: taste, color choice, style. Much group critique drifts into “it should have been orange,” which teaches little.

We should ask: who is giving the critique, and do we trust them? Are they helping us reach our goals, or trying to make our work match their taste?

Giving and Receiving Critiques Well

Marshall dislikes the sandwich technique (compliment, problem, compliment) as a formula. Politeness is fine, but empty padding often hides the point.

A better approach: - Seek clear, actionable notes on objective issues. - Treat subjective notes as prompts, not commands. Use what helps, discard what does not. - Avoid personal labels. Critique the work, not the person.

Finding the Right Balance

People tend to fall into four patterns: - Fearful of critique and cannot improve. - Actively invite critique and grow fast. - Ingest every opinion and lose personal vision. - Reject feedback entirely and rely only on personal vision.

The ideal is a tension between open and closed, like an umbrella that works only with both. We stay open to help that moves us toward our vision and closed to advice that pulls us off course.

Learn with a Small, Focused Community

A small, aligned group helps us avoid being yanked in many directions. Shared fundamentals with individual voices give variety without chaos. Mentors who understand where we want to go are key.

Photorealism and Skill Building

Why Study Representational Realism

We do not need to chase extreme detail, but representational goals hold us to high standards. They teach color theory, light on form, value, and accuracy. Reality gives objective checkpoints. If it is off, it is off. That clarity accelerates learning.

Using Photos to Train Technique

Copying photos can train: - Sensitivity to value and edge control with graphite or ink. - Color and temperature judgment with paint or watercolor. - Material handling in any medium.

It is a practical way to build skill fast, with clear measures of success.

Style and Borrowing from Masters

Style can be studied by copying stylistic masters. We can adopt “artistic parents,” imitate their solutions, and switch styles to fit the project. We should not rely on imitation forever, but it is a strong path to learn design, taste, and flexibility. Professionals blend influences, pay homage, and evolve their own voice over time.

Bottom Line

  • Use critiques to power the feedback loop.
  • Prefer objective corrections. Treat subjective notes as options.
  • Choose trustworthy mentors and a small, aligned community.
  • Study representational realism to sharpen the eye and hand.
  • Borrow from masters to explore style, then shape it into our own.
