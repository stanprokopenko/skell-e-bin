The Current Crisis in Art Education

In recent years, we've seen a troubling trend in the world of art education. Several private 4-year art colleges have closed their doors, often without warning. These closures have affected institutions like the Pennsylvania Academy of Fine Arts, Delaware College of Art and Design, University of Arts in Philadelphia, American Academy of Art in Chicago, and San Francisco Art Institute. What's particularly concerning is how sudden and unexpected these closures were for both faculty and students.

A major factor driving these closures is the rapidly increasing tuition costs. To put this in perspective, my own art school tuition increased by a staggering 53.6% in just 15 years. Today, tuition at top art schools can reach an eye-watering $70,000 to $80,000 per year. That means a 4-year degree could cost over $300,000.

The Financial Burden on Art Students

The financial implications of these high tuition costs are severe. After graduation, many students face monthly loan payments of $2,400 to $3,400. This leads me to question how schools can justify these costs, especially for art degrees. It seems that the American education system is fundamentally broken if this level of debt is required.

Even with scholarships, I strongly advise students to carefully calculate their potential loan payments before committing to an art school. The financial burden can be overwhelming and long-lasting.

The Shortcomings of Traditional Art Education

One of the major issues with current art education is the lack of practical skills being taught. Many art schools don't provide adequate instruction in crucial areas like entrepreneurship, business management, or freelancing. This leaves graduates ill-prepared for the realities of making a living as an artist.

There's also a noticeable difference in job prospects between applied arts majors (like graphic design and illustration) and fine arts majors. Fine arts graduates often struggle more to find work and can feel trapped by what I call "art world propaganda."

My Personal Experience in the Art World

As a former star pupil, I followed my professors' advice to the letter. I achieved straight A's and started teaching right after graduation. My dream was to become a fine art professor and professional artist. Looking back at my old artist resume, I can see how entrenched I was in the art world system:

  • I participated in free or self-funded projects
  • Teaching jobs were my main source of income
  • I received awards, grants, and residencies (which I felt lucky to get)
  • I paid to participate in exhibitions
  • I got press mentions (unpaid but prestigious)
  • I took on lecturer/critic roles (for small payments)
  • I curated shows (mostly unpaid or self-funded)
  • I published articles (running an unpaid arts blog)

I even used this resume as an example for my students. But the reality was, I had to work at Nordstrom to support my art career and went into debt for some projects just to build my CV.

The Flaws in the Art World System

The art world can be a difficult place, often run by people who aren't artists themselves. There's a heavy reliance on prestige and unpaid work, and very little discussion about the role of money in success. I couldn't afford to move to art hubs like New York or LA, which limited my opportunities.

Art schools rarely teach students to consider their audience or develop business skills. Instead, we're taught to write in complex "International Art English" and essentially run luxury businesses without acknowledging it.

Rethinking Art Education

It's clear that art schools need to evolve to better serve their students. They should focus on teaching marketing skills and financial literacy. Artists need to learn how to write about their work for non-art world audiences and manage being self-employed.

Alternative Education Options

There are more affordable options for art education, including state, private, and community colleges. For example, Johnson County Community College in Kansas City offers excellent art programs at a fraction of the cost of traditional art schools. Don't be afraid to look outside the traditional canon for your education.

Building a New Art World

Current and prospective art students will need to build a new art world that's more sustainable and inclusive. This new art world should reflect existing culture, not just art culture. Artists should consider forming unions and engaging in mutual aid.

We can work together to create new structures and refuse to play by the old rules. By analyzing existing systems, we can build better ones that serve more artists and art lovers.

The future of art education and the art world is in our hands. Let's continue this conversation and work towards a more sustainable and equitable system for all artists.
