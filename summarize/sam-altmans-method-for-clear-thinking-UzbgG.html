The Ideal Notebook

I'm a huge note-taker, and after lots of trial and error with different notebooks and systems, I've found what works best for me. Despite all the fancy notebooks out there, I prefer a simple spiral notebook. Here's why:

  • You can rip pages out frequently. This is important because I often need to look at multiple pages at the same time. Being able to remove pages easily allows me to spread them out, and when I'm done, I can crumple them up and toss them.
  • It lies flat and open on the table. When you're writing, it's essential that the notebook stays open without you having to hold it down.
  • Hard front and back covers. A sturdy notebook provides a good surface to write on, especially if you're on the go.
  • Portable size. I like a notebook that can fit in a pocket, so I always have it with me.

The quality of the paper is also crucial. Most paper is terrible to write on, so finding one with a good feel is essential.

My Favorite Pens

The pen you use can make a big difference in your note-taking experience. My top choices are:

  • Uniball Micro 0.5 pen. In my opinion, this is the best pen overall. It writes smoothly and consistently.
  • Muji 0.36 or 0.37 in dark blue ink. This pen is also excellent for its unique feel and ink quality.

How I Use My Notebooks

I go through one of these notebooks every two or three weeks. I take a lot of notes, and I don't keep completed notebooks. Instead:

  • I rip out pages when needed, so I can view multiple notes at once.
  • Once done with a page, I crumple it up, and toss it on the floor. By the time our house cleaner comes in, there's often a pile of crumpled papers, evidence of my active note-taking.

Writing as a Tool for Thinking

For me, writing is a tool for thinking more clearly. Even with the rise of AI technologies that can generate videos, music, and images from text, the act of writing remains irreplaceable.

The Importance of Learning to Write

I believe it's crucial that people still learn to write because:

  • It helps you think more clearly. Writing forces you to organize your thoughts and ideas.
  • It's like learning to code. Even if there might be fewer traditional coding jobs in the future, coding is a great way to learn to think. Similarly, writing sharpens your cognitive processes.

If there's ever a better way to think more clearly using AI, I'd switch to that. But I haven't found anything that replaces writing yet.

My Writing Process

Creating Focused Time for Writing

I used to think I needed the perfect setup to write: the right place, noise-canceling headphones, the ideal mood. Now, I'll take any 11 minutes of uninterrupted time I can get:

  • Sitting in the back of a car
  • Lying in bed
  • Any spare moment

If I have the opportunity to set up the perfect writing environment, it would be Saturday morning with a cup of coffee and nothing scheduled. That's when I can tackle longer pieces.

Balancing Interaction and Solitude

I'm convinced there are ideas I would never have if I were just talking with people. I need to sit and type to access certain thoughts. It's about finding the right balance between:

  • Being with people: Engaging in conversations and being exposed to new ideas.
  • Time alone: Thinking, writing, and doing deep work.

My rough rhythm is:

  • Weekdays: I'm in the office non-stop, with no time to think, it's just crazily packed.
  • Weekends: I have long, quiet blocks of time and I'm not really around people. This cycle is very important to me.

Long Breaks and Changing Rhythms

I used to take longer chunks of time off, and I found it beneficial:

  • A month of non-stop hanging out with people
  • A month of being alone in the woods or on the beach

That doesn't really happen anymore, but the pattern of alternating between interaction and solitude helped me recharge and think differently.
