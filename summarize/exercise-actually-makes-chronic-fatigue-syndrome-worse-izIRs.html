What Is ME/CFS?

Myalgic encephalomyelitis, also known as chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS), is a neurological disease that goes far beyond feeling tired after a sleepless night. It's characterized by extreme fatigue that doesn't improve with rest and can worsen with physical or mental activity.

Symptoms and Impact

ME/CFS affects an estimated 15-30 million people worldwide, yet around 90% remain undiagnosed. Symptoms can drastically alter daily life:

  • Unrefreshing sleep: No amount of sleep leaves us feeling rested.
  • Post-exertional malaise (PEM): A significant worsening of symptoms after physical or mental exertion, often delayed by 24 hours.
  • Cognitive dysfunction: Difficulty with concentration and memory.
  • Orthostatic intolerance: Lightheadedness when standing up.

Up to 75% of people with ME/CFS are unable to work, and some are confined to their beds.

Why Exercise Isn't the Answer

Contrary to common advice, exercise can make ME/CFS symptoms worse. Those of us with ME/CFS experience drops in blood oxygen levels and heart rate after exertion. Graded exercise therapy, which involves gradually increasing activity, has been found to be harmful.

Causes and Triggers

ME/CFS often develops after an infection. Viruses like the Epstein-Barr virus (which causes mono) and SARS-CoV-2 (the virus behind COVID-19) have been linked to triggering the condition. In fact, studies suggest that half of those with long COVID meet the criteria for ME/CFS.

The Challenge of Diagnosis

Diagnosing ME/CFS requires experiencing specific symptoms for at least six months. This includes extreme fatigue, PEM, cognitive issues, and orthostatic intolerance. The delayed onset of PEM makes it difficult to identify triggers, turning management into detective work.

Managing ME/CFS

While there's no cure, symptom management strategies include:

  • Activity pacing: Adjusting our activities to match energy levels and avoid overexertion.
  • Symptom tracking: Keeping a daily log to identify triggers and limits.
  • Medication: Using pain relievers or sleep aids as prescribed to ease symptoms.
  • Planning rest: Resting before and after significant activities to minimize PEM.

The Importance of Patient-Centered Research

There's a pressing need for more research that prioritizes patient experiences. Controversial studies like the PACE trial, which incorrectly promoted increased exercise, highlight the dangers of disregarding patient feedback.

* * *

For those without ME/CFS, exercise remains a valuable part of a healthy lifestyle. But for people with ME/CFS, pushing beyond limits isn't beneficial and can be harmful. Recognizing and respecting these differences is crucial for support and understanding.
