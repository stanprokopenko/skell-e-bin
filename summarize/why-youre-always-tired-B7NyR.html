Why You're Always Tired

High-quality sleep has changed my life. There's nothing I do that improves my daily experience more than getting good sleep. I know what it's like to have really bad sleep, juggling work, family, and stress. It's miserable. So, here are six habits that might be wrecking your sleep, and what you can do about them.

1. Eating Too Late

Eating close to bedtime can disrupt your sleep. If you eat a heavy meal late at night, your body has to work hard to digest it. This can cause blood glucose spikes and fluctuations, and disrupt melatonin production, which throws off your body's preparation for sleep.

I experimented with different meal times to find what worked best for me. I discovered that having my final meal around 11:00 a.m. helped me sleep better. That might sound extreme, but it allowed my body to complete digestion before bed. I noticed that my resting heart rate was a good predictor of sleep quality, when it was lower, I slept better.

Your optimal time might be different, so I encourage you to experiment with different meal times. Try finishing your last meal two, three, or four hours before bed and see how you feel.

2. Skipping a Wind-Down Routine

Not having a wind-down routine can make it hard to quiet your mind before bed. If I work right up until I hit the pillow, my mind races with thoughts about the day and things I need to do. To combat this, I establish a clear transition to nighttime.

My bedtime is 8:30 p.m., so at 7:30 p.m., I switch to "nighttime me." I avoid work thoughts and focus on calming activities. I recommend things like reading a book, journaling, meditating, taking a bath, or going for a walk. Avoid stimulating activities and screens during this time.

3. Late-Day Stimulants

Having stimulants like caffeine too late can interfere with sleep. Caffeine has a half-life of six hours, so a cup of coffee late in the day can keep you awake at night. I used to consume caffeine and alcohol, but I found that eliminating them improved my mood stability and sleep.

Consider limiting caffeine to earlier in the day, and see how it affects your sleep.

4. Evening Light Exposure

Light exposure in the evening can disrupt your circadian rhythm. In the morning, sunlight is great, but in the evening, you should dim the lights and use warm-colored lighting (2700-3500 Kelvin). Avoid blue light by using apps like f.lux on your screens or wearing blue light blocking glasses.

Better yet, try to eliminate screens entirely an hour before bed. Instead, engage in relaxing activities that help your body wind down.

5. Bedroom Temperature

Your body needs to cool down to sleep properly. If your bedroom is too hot or too cold, it can be hard to fall asleep. Simple fixes include opening a window or using blinds to control room temperature.

I use a temperature-controlled mattress called Eight Sleep. It adjusts the bed temperature throughout the night. While such devices can improve sleep, just be mindful when traveling, you may need to adjust your sleep environment away from home.

6. Noise Disruptions

Noise can wake you up and disrupt your sleep cycles. I had issues with my neighbor's dogs barking at night. I politely asked them to bring the dogs in before my bedtime, and they agreed. Don't be afraid to communicate with neighbors about noise issues.

If external noises are unavoidable, consider using a white noise machine to mask disruptive sounds.

* * *

I know it's often considered heroic to sacrifice sleep for work or other commitments. But getting eight hours of quality sleep is one of the best things you can do for yourself and those around you. Prioritize sleep, and I promise you'll feel better and have a better life.
