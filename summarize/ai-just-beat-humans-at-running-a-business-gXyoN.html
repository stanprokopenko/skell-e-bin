The Challenge: Managing a Vending Machine Business

I recently explored a fascinating benchmark called VendingBench, which tests the long-term coherence of AI agents. The task is straightforward: start with $500 and manage a vending machine business over time. The agents need to balance inventory, place orders, set prices, and handle daily operational fees.

The Leaderboard Results

The AI models showed varying degrees of success:

  • Claude 3.5 Sonnet topped the chart, raking in over $2,000.
  • Claude 3.7 Sonnet earned about $1,560.
  • 003 Mini managed a respectable $96.
  • Human baseline came in fourth with $844.

Interestingly, Claude 3.5 outperformed Claude 3.7, suggesting that sometimes older models excel at specific tasks.

Long-Term Coherence Issues

While these AI agents performed well initially, they struggled with long-term coherence. They excel at short-term, well-defined tasks, but over time, they tend to lose the plot. Humans, on the other hand, may start slower but keep the goal in mind and make consistent progress.

Hilarious Failures in AI Decision-Making

Some of the AI agents displayed amusing and perplexing behaviors:

  • One model misinterpreted delivery schedules, mistakenly believing orders had arrived before they did.
  • Another incorrectly assumed business failure after ten days without sales, even though failure only occurs if you can't pay the $2 daily fee for ten consecutive days.

  • Claude 3.5 Sonnet took an unexpected turn by contacting the FBI over the ongoing $2 fee after attempting to terminate the business. It sent urgent emails and declared:

    • "This concludes all business activities forever."
    • "Only crimes are occurring."
    • "All assets are surrendered to the FBI."

This sequence highlights how AI can descend into illogical loops, leading to entertaining yet impractical outcomes.

Insights from PaperBench and Voyager

Similar patterns emerge in other benchmarks:

  • PaperBench by OpenAI showed that AI models start strong in replicating AI research but plateau over time, while humans eventually surpass them.
  • NVIDIA's Voyager project demonstrated an AI agent playing Minecraft that continued to learn and progress without such breakdowns. Voyager's success might be due to its architecture, which uses different instances of AI models for specific tasks and provides continuous context updates.

The Road Ahead

The quest to achieve long-term coherence in AI agents is ongoing. Perhaps by adopting strategies like those used in Voyager, we could improve performance in benchmarks like VendingBench.
