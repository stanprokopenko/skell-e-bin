Early Inspiration

When I was 14, I took night classes where Apple's campus is now. I'd always wanted to learn how to program computers so I could create things. On a flight to the Bay Area, I watched a documentary about John Carmack and John Romero, the creators of id Software and games like Doom and Wolfenstein. Seeing how two people writing code could change the world made me realize I can do that too.

First Attempts at Startups

My friend and I heard about Y Combinator opening up in Mountain View. We applied with a mobile social network idea but got a response from Paul Graham saying, "I don't think you can beat Sam," referring to Sam Altman's company, Loopt. That was our first rejection from YC. We applied again with a different idea: connecting people anywhere in the world using just your camera and microphone. That became Meeteor, and we got accepted.

However, I learned that you have to have conviction in your product vision. I got enamored with ideas from other founders and lost focus on what I was trying to do. Starting a company just for the sake of it is a mistake I made multiple times. The real thing to do is to solve problems.

Lessons from 23andMe and Twitter

After Meeteor failed, I joined 23andMe because I believed in the future of genomic sequencing. It taught me the importance of picking the right market and building something sustainable to ride the wave of growth.

Joining Twitter was intimidating. It was growing so fast and was understaffed, it was a mess, to be honest. As an engineer, I realized that if I put in the hours, I could carve out a place for myself. I spent 12 to 14 hours a day working on the product, and it didn't feel like work because I loved it. I'm most proud of building the embedded media feature within Twitter.

An interesting thing I learned is that fires are a good thing. They indicate that you have built something people want. Failing and crashing is a rite of passage, proof that your work is worth improving.

Building LiveKit

The internet wasn't built for transmitting audio and video. Back when I started Meeteor, real-time video didn't exist online. During the pandemic, I saw a need for this technology as everyone tried to bring real-life interactions onto the internet.

My co-founder David and I decided to build LiveKit, an open-source stack to make it easy for developers to integrate real-time streaming audio and video. We raised about $37 million, and have companies like OpenAI using us to power voice mode for ChatGPT. LiveKit runs 25% of 911 services, every week, someone's life is saved using video to coach them on delivering CPR.

While I have ambitions for where LiveKit is going, it's important to celebrate that we've already been successful. We help people access education and connect with one another, often without them paying us a dime. They're my favorite use cases.

Advice for Entrepreneurs

For those looking to build in spaces like AI or robotics, these are going to be the largest spaces in the history of humanity. But because things are changing so quickly, there's a benefit to waiting a bit. Consider joining a company at the forefront of AI to learn about the foundational elements, meet potential co-founders, and understand the problems around you.

My recommendation is to join a company you find exciting, and spend a couple of years really digging in. Inspiration will strike, don't force it. When it comes, you'll know and then you should pursue it fully.
