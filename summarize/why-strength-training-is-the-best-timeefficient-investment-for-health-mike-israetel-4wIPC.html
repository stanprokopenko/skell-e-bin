The Value of Strength Training and Diminishing Returns

We often wonder about the value of strength training, and whether there are diminishing returns in building muscle. The reality is that very few people reach a point where excessive muscularity becomes a problem. Mike emphasizes that achieving extreme muscularity is the result of a long, disciplined journey. We don't accidentally become too muscular. It requires years of immersive, focused resistance training and nutrition.

The Myth of Accidental Muscle Gain

The idea that one might 'oops' become excessively muscular is a myth. Mike compares this to accidentally becoming ultra-wealthy, it doesn't happen without deliberate effort. For most of us, the concern isn't about getting too muscular, but rather the opposite. Many people later in life wish they were stronger, and had more muscle mass.

Time Investment and Opportunity Costs

The primary trade-off in pursuing high levels of strength and muscularity is the opportunity cost, the time we invest in training could be spent elsewhere. However, for the significant health, longevity, and quality of life benefits, the required time commitment is surprisingly low. Mike points out that 1 to 3 hours of resistance training per week is sufficient, with three hours being on the higher end.

When we consider how we spend our time, 1 to 3 hours per week is minimal. We often spend more time on social media, watching television, or other less productive activities. Even those fully dedicated to maximizing muscle might spend eight hours a week in the gym, which is still manageable compared to other commitments.

Optimal Training Frequency for Maximum Benefits

For most people, the best return on investment comes from training twice a week for about 30 minutes per session. Done properly, this can yield significant benefits. Resistance training's intensity requires substantial recovery time, so daily training isn't necessary.

Contrast with Endurance Training

Endurance training, such as cycling or running, often involves a significant time investment. Elite endurance athletes might train 30 hours a week, mostly at low intensity (Zone 2). In endurance training, there is clear benefit from submaximal efforts.

In contrast, resistance training requires high forces and high levels of fatigue to stimulate muscle growth and strength. Low-intensity efforts don't provide significant benefits, there is no equivalent to Zone 2 in resistance training. The training stimulus comes from pushing our muscles close to their limits, which cannot be sustained for long periods.

Why Strength Training Requires Intensity and Recovery

Resistance training primarily engages type II muscle fibers, which produce high force but fatigue quickly, and take longer to recover. The high absolute forces involved cause more damage to muscles and connective tissues, which necessitates more recovery time. Additionally, the psychological drive required is significant, we need to push through discomfort and fatigue, which is mentally taxing.

Endurance training mainly utilizes type I muscle fibers, which are more resistant to fatigue and recover faster. It allows for longer training durations at submaximal intensities, often putting us in a state of flow. Strength training, however, demands short bursts of maximal or near-maximal effort, followed by adequate recovery.

The Importance of Balancing Time and Intensity

Understanding the differences between strength and endurance training helps us optimize our workouts. In strength training, quality over quantity is key. By focusing on intense efforts for a shorter duration, we can achieve significant benefits without excessive time commitment.
