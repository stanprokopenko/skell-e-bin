Big picture

This winter is a battle between a weak, faltering leninia and a shaky polar vortex. The Pacific pattern is barely there, with only a 42% chance it fully develops and 6 of 8 models favoring neutral conditions. Without a strong driver, the setup is fragile and easily disrupted.

Even so, the big long-range models agree on a split nation. Draw a line across the middle of the country: - Southern tier: warmer and drier, about 2 to 4 degrees above normal and 20 to 40% below normal precipitation. - Northern tier: colder and wetter with enhanced storm tracks, more Arctic intrusions, and more snow.

Where cold northern air meets warm southern air becomes the battleground for the biggest storms, heavy snow, and pockets of severe weather.

Polar vortex wild card

Up in the stratosphere, the polar vortex looks unstable. With leninia-like conditions and the current setup, there’s a 60 to 75% chance of a sudden stratospheric warming event. When this hits, Arctic air spills south: - Temperatures 15 to 25 degrees below normal - Wind chills to minus30, minus40, even minus50 - Impacts can last 4 to 8 weeks and override the base pattern

I’m expecting two to three major disruptions from January through early March.

Regional outlook

  • Pacific Northwest: Cooler than normal looks most likely here with a 33 to 40% chance. Frequent atmospheric rivers. Cascades snowpack 20 to 40% above normal.

  • Northern Plains and Great Lakes: On the front lines of Arctic invasions. Lake-effect snow primed with 15 to 20 C air-water gaps. Southern shores of Lakes Superior and Michigan could see 20 to 40% above normal snowfall. More Alberta Clippers with frequent 3 to 6 inch events.

  • Southwest: 33 to 50% chance of above normal temperatures, persistent ridge, about 40% below normal precipitation. Ongoing drought likely worsens. Fire weather a big concern in Southern California.

  • Midwest and Ohio Valley: Storm track battleground. Freezing rain potential 35 to 40% above normal, especially from northern Texas into Oklahoma and maybe Missouri. Enhanced risk of winter severe weather and tornadoes. Expect weather whiplash.

  • Northeast: Exceptionally active. I’m calling for 6 to 9 significant nor’easters. NAO likely positive early (milder), then turning negative by February to March, bringing slower, stronger coastal storms. Peak activity late winter.

  • Southeast: Generally 2 to 4 degrees above normal and 20 to 30% below normal precipitation. Mostly quiet, but a few severe weather rounds possible. Brief cold shots if the vortex disrupts.

Timing and analogs

Best analog: 2010–2011 with an 85% pattern match, plus 2007–2008 and 1998–1999. All featured memorable Arctic outbreaks.

Bottom line

Expect a volatile, high-contrast winter with a warm-dry south, a cold-snowy north, and a volatile battleground in between. Polar vortex disruptions could define the season.
