The Eroding Influence of Newspaper Endorsements

Once upon a time, newspaper endorsements could sway voters. But those days are over. This election season, when papers like the Los Angeles Times and the Washington Post chose not to endorse a candidate, the outrage was palpable. Staffers felt betrayed, and subscriptions were canceled in protest. At the Nation magazine, interns wrote an op-ed opposing the endorsement of Kamala Harris.

Our Choice Not to Endorse

We've received questions about whether we'll endorse a candidate. Given our mission to inform rather than instruct, we're not endorsing anyone this year. At a recent retreat, we discovered our staff is split almost equally among supporters of Kamala Harris, Donald Trump, and those abstaining or undecided. This mirrors the divisions in our country.

Valuing Diverse Perspectives

At the Free Press, it's okay to be liberal, conservative, or politically non-binary. Unlike many newsrooms that think as one, we embrace a range of views. Half of America supports Trump, yet many media outlets can't stomach those perspectives. We believe in seeking and telling the truth, which allows us to disagree without disrespect. Politics need not be a team sport.

We're committed to restoring a culture where arguing, debating, persuading, and disagreeing are normal. If we can't sit next to colleagues who vote differently, how can we coexist in our communities? Building a publication and a community that aspires to these values is meaningful and important. We're grateful to live in a free country, and we're proud to reflect that freedom in our work.
