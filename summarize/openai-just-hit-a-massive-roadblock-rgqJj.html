Ownership Shift

I open the letter by asking who will own AGI if OpenAI goes ahead with its new structure. The original rule said any true AGI had to be created and held by the nonprofit so it would serve humanity. Under the new plan, the for-profit arm and its investors would take control. That change would give big investors open access to powerful technology that was once off-limits. OpenAI has not told the public who would own AGI after the change, but every report I read points to the company and its backers. I also see stories that OpenAI and Microsoft have discussed ending the limits on Microsoft’s use of the tech, an idea that would break earlier promises.

Broken Promises

I remember Sam Altman’s May 2023 testimony, where he praised nonprofit control, a profit cap, and other guardrails. Now I hear him call those same guardrails “obstacles” he wants to remove. I ask myself what flipped between May 2023 and September 2024.

The charter once held a “stop-and-assist” pledge: if another safe group pulled ahead in the AGI race, OpenAI would halt and help that group finish the job. A profit-driven model would almost surely drop that promise and speed up a reckless race. I can’t picture OpenAI stepping aside to assist a rival when control of AGI is on the line.

Mission vs. Investor Demands

OpenAI began with charitable gifts aimed at “AGI that benefits humanity.” By law those donations must fund the purpose for which they were given. Using them to build a for-profit empire breaks that trust, and I expect a legal fight over who controls AGI.

Inside the company I see growing tension. Leaders blame investor pressure for the restructuring. Investors dislike the profit cap and want a simpler capital structure. Some late-stage funding even lets backers pull out unless the structure changes. So OpenAI sits between “look like the bad guy” if it restructures and “lose investors” if it refuses.

Safety Risks

OpenAI’s own papers warn that AGI could cause grave harm or extinction. Altman and Geoffrey Hinton signed a statement putting AI risk on par with nukes and pandemics. That fear drove the strict rules at the start, yet drifting from them now makes the stakes higher.

I read allegations that model testing is rushed and under-resourced. The firm backed off its vow to spend 20 percent of compute on AGI safety. Jan Leike quit, saying profit had replaced mission. Such moves tell me safety is losing the internal battle.

Additional Controversies

Reports say Altman has floated cutting global compute access while seeking trillions for new chips, even from U.S. rivals. Departing staff face harsh non-disparagement agreements. A group of former employees, including Daniel Kokotajlo and other researchers, laid out ten core objections to the new direction. Geoffrey Hinton signed as well, which added weight to their concerns.

What Happens Next

Control of AGI is now worth more than almost anything else in tech. I will watch how OpenAI tries to square profit, safety, and its original promise. The company must choose: keep its founding mission or satisfy the investors who want the guardrails gone.
