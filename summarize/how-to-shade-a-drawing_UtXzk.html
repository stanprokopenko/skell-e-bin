How to Shade a Drawing

Understanding Form

Form is the foundation of effective shading. To shade well, you need to understand the form you're shading. The basic building blocks of form are spheres, cylinders, and boxes. Organic forms found in nature, like humans, animals, and trees, can and should be constructed from these simple forms to capture the character of the subject.

When shading, consider the hierarchy of forms:

  • Primary forms (e.g., a cylinder for an arm) should be dominant
  • Secondary forms (e.g., bicep, tricep, deltoid) should be subordinate to primary forms
  • Tertiary forms (e.g., veins, wrinkles) should be treated as details

Your shading should primarily reveal the largest forms, with smaller forms acting as details.

Planes in 3D Objects

Planes can be thought of as flat tiles arranged in 3D space to create a form. For example, a sphere has a front plane, top plane, side planes, and many more in between that together resemble a sphere. Though a sphere is rounded without flat planes, thinking of it this way helps imagine it as a 3D object and aids in the shading process.

When shading planes:

  • Imagine which direction each plane faces
  • Compare it to the direction of the light source
  • The plane facing the light is the lightest
  • Planes progressively get darker as they turn away from the light

Softening the edges between planes can indicate a softer form. However, sometimes leaving hard edges can help illustrate structure more effectively.

Simplifying a form means decreasing the number of planes it consists of. In your mind, think of a few major planes for a given area, even if you're shading to give the illusion of millions of planes.

Light on Form

When an object is lit by a direct light source, you'll see a predictable pattern of lights and shadows. To make a form feel 3D, indicate all parts of the lights and shadows correctly:

Core Shadow

The core shadow is a darker strip at the edge of the shadow, dividing the light and shadow families. It shouldn't be uniform:

  • In rounded parts, it's thicker with a softer edge
  • In thinner parts, it's thinner with a sharper edge

Avoid drawing "racing stripes" down the form. Consider the 3D form you're indicating and adjust the edge quality accordingly.

Reflected Light

Fill in the shadow side with a clean dark value, lighter than the core shadow. This is reflected light, caused by bounce light and reflections from the environment. Start with a flat value, then add variations if necessary.

Keep details within shadows quieter than details in the lights. Most of the story is told in the lit areas, so put interesting detail work there and keep shadows as areas of rest.

Centerlight and Halftones

Identify the point of the center light, where the plane faces directly to the light. Halftones appear as a gradation, darkest near the core shadow and lightest at the center light.

Highlight

The highlight is different from the center light:

  • Center light is the plane facing the light
  • Highlight is the plane reflecting light relative to the viewer's position

When the shadow is thin, the highlight will be close to the center light. When the shadow is large, the highlight will be farther from the centerlight.

Cast Shadow and Occlusion Shadow

These occur when there's interaction between two forms:

  • Cast shadow: Where one object blocks light from hitting another
  • Occlusion shadow: The area that gets less bounce light, usually darker

When drawing cast shadows, use them to describe the shape of the object they're casting onto, not the object casting them.

Local Value and Intensity of Light

Local Value

The local value of the object itself shifts the value range. For example, a white egg and a brown egg lit the same way will have different value ranges:

  • White egg: Wide range from darkest core to center light
  • Brown egg: Compressed range, pushed darker

The highlight is less affected by local value, which can make it appear very bright on darker objects.

Intensity of Light

The intensity of light affects the contrast between lights and shadows:

  • Intense light creates more contrast
  • Dim light creates low contrast

Light intensity can shift within the same object. You can use this as a compositional technique to guide the viewer's eye to the focal point.

Remember to practice spotting these elements on directly lit objects. With time and practice, you'll develop a keen eye for shading and create more realistic, three-dimensional artwork.
