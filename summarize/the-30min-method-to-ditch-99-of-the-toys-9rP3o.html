The 30-MIN METHOD to ditch 99% of the toys

I used to believe that more toys meant better play for my kids. Like many modern moms, I was drawn to educational toys, puzzles for spatial ability, ball drops for eye tracking, dolls for empathy. But I began to wonder, What if toys are actually a hindrance to play? **

After researching and testing at home, I found a simple way to declutter toys and transform my children's play in just 30 minutes.

The Problem with Too Many Toys

Maintaining a toy rotation system became a nightmare. My daughter realized there were more toys in storage and couldn't focus on the ones she had. Play sessions were constantly interrupted by requests for more toys. By the end of the day, every toy we owned was scattered across our living room.

Experts warn that overwhelming children with stuff can lead to more anxiety. Claire Lerner, a child development specialist, found that an excess of toys can impede development and cause overstimulation, leading kids to shut down.

Discovering the Benefits of Fewer Toys

I learned from Dr. John Reeker about the two stages of engagement with materials:

  • Exploration: "What does this object do?"
  • Play: "What can I do with this?"

Too many toys prevent kids from being resourceful. After decluttering, my kids' focus shifted. The toy shelf became a kitchen, Magna-Tiles turned into pizzas, and blocks became salad ingredients. Independent playtime expanded from a few minutes to sometimes hours.

Research supports my observations:

  • Less overwhelm leads to deeper focus.
  • More resourcefulness encourages inventive play.
  • Better teamwork as sharing becomes easier.
  • Greater appreciation leads to better care for their things.
  • Increased calmness resulting in improved well-being.

My 30-Minute Decluttering Method

Step 1: Establish Parameters

I asked:

  • Which toys do my kids choose most often?
  • Which toys keep them engaged the longest?
  • Which toys inspire imaginative and cooperative play?

We kept:

  • Blocks
  • Magna-Tiles
  • Dollhouse and accessories
  • Dress-up clothes (one each)
  • Stuffed animals

Step 2: Set Up the Experiment

I displayed the remaining toys where the kids could easily access and put them away. I stored the rest out of sight, packed in opaque bags. I decided to run this experiment for three months.

Step 3: Observe and Conclude

I quietly observed my kids without interfering. I noticed:

  • Deeper play because they weren't asking for new toys.
  • More imaginative play as they repurposed available toys.
  • Better collaboration between siblings.

These observations validated my hypothesis that my kids would play more independently and with fewer interruptions with a smaller collection of toys.

Handling Children's Resistance

You might worry that your kids will resist this change. However, many parents who've simplified their toy collections report that their children barely noticed. If your kids do get upset, you can say:

  • "We're taking a break from the other toys right now."
  • "Look at all the new space you have!"

It's okay for children to experience disappointment. Allow them to move through those feelings. Remember, simplifying their toys leads to greater contentment in the long run.

Embracing Simplicity

By simplifying, we've created an environment where imaginative and independent play thrives. It releases us from managing too much stuff and fosters essential skills in our children.

If you're hesitant, remember that many parents who've simplified report that their kids barely noticed the change. Starting this experiment can transform your children's play, and your days, in ways you didn't expect.
