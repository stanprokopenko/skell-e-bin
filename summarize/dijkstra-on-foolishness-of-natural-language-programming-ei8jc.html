Recently, I dove into Edsger Dijkstra's essay "On the Foolishness of Natural Language Programming." Dijkstra always has thought-provoking insights, and this piece feels particularly relevant today, especially with the rise of natural language processing and AI.

The Challenge of Formal Symbolism

Dijkstra begins by addressing the early desire to make programming more like natural language. Since the advent of automatic computing, many have felt that the strictness of formal programming languages is a shortcoming. They wished for machines that could understand instructions in our native tongues, blaming the mechanical slave for strictly obeying instructions even when they contained obvious mistakes.

He points out that the obligation to use formal symbols isn't a burden but a privilege. Formal languages require care and precision, which helps us avoid nonsense that's hard to detect when using natural language. Thanks to formal symbolism, tasks that once required genius can now be accomplished by schoolchildren.

The Illusion of Simplicity in Natural Language

Dijkstra challenges the assumption that programming in natural language would simplify our lives. While it might seem convenient, he argues that natural language is inherently imprecise and can lead to misinterpretation. The additional burden on machines to interpret natural language could also increase complexity on both sides, making programming more difficult, not less.

He uses historical examples to support his point. Greek mathematicians struggled because their work remained a verbal and pictorial activity. Muslim algebra made initial strides with symbolism but faltered when it returned to a rhetorical style. Only with the advent of formal symbolism from thinkers like Vieta, Descartes, Leibniz, and later Boole did mathematics truly advance.

The New Illiteracy

Dijkstra also remarks on the decline in people's mastery of their own language, a phenomenon he calls the new illiteracy. As educational trends move away from intellectual discipline, many struggle to use their native tongue effectively, even when it's adequate for the task. This decline should discourage attempts to rely on natural language for programming, especially among those who lack the technical insight to foresee its pitfalls.

* * *

In pondering Dijkstra's essay, I find his arguments hold true even today. The precision required in programming is a strength, not a weakness. While natural language programming seems appealing, it may lead us into pitfalls due to the ambiguities inherent in human language.

As we continue to develop tools that interpret natural language, it's important to remember the value of formalism. Embracing the discipline of precise symbolic languages doesn't hinder us, instead, it empowers us to create more reliable and effective programs.
