The History of the Department of Education

As someone passionate about education, I want to dive into the current discussions about the future of the Department of Education. Established in 1979 by President Jimmy Carter, the Department of Education is relatively new. Before its creation, education functions were spread across various federal departments, focusing on data collection and support rather than centralized control.

Over the past 40 years, the department's budget has ballooned from $14 billion to over $238 billion, yet student performance has remained stagnant. Increased spending hasn't led to improved reading, math, or science scores, as shown by data from the Cato Institute.

Trump's Proposal to Abolish the Department

Recently, President Trump announced his intention to dismantle the Department of Education as part of his 2024 campaign promises. While he can't abolish it outright with an executive order, since it was created by an act of Congress, he can restructure and reduce its influence.

Trump's plan involves breaking up the department and reassigning its functions to other federal agencies. This could mean returning power and decision-making to the states and local communities, allowing for more tailored educational approaches.

Challenges and How It Might Work

Reallocating Functions to Other Departments

Key functions like Title I funding (supporting low-income schools) and Title IX protections (preventing sex-based discrimination) would be preserved. These functions would be moved to other departments, such as the Department of Justice or Health and Human Services.

This reallocation aims to reduce bureaucracy and limit federal overreach, ensuring that essential services continue without the centralized control that many argue has hindered educational progress.

The Role of Teachers Unions

Teachers unions, particularly the powerful ones like the American Federation of Teachers, led by Randi Weingarten, are strongly opposed to dismantling the department. They argue that abolishing it would harm students and take money away from education. Critics, however, believe that these unions are more concerned with maintaining power and control than with actual student outcomes.

Local Control and Successful Examples

Evidence suggests that local control can lead to better educational outcomes. When states and communities tailor education to their specific needs, students benefit.

Miami-Dade County's Improvements

In Miami-Dade County, a conservative majority school board has implemented Singapore Math and classical reading curricula in elementary schools. This local initiative has led to significant improvements in student performance.

Mississippi and Louisiana's Progress

Mississippi and Louisiana have shown remarkable gains in education by focusing on the basics and returning control to local authorities. Mississippi's fourth graders achieved the number one spot in the nation for gains in reading and math, outpacing national averages.

State superintendents credit these successes to state-led reforms, back-to-basics approaches, and alignment between government officials and educators.

Taking Action at the Local Level

The push to dismantle the Department of Education highlights the importance of local involvement. As individuals and communities, we have the power to effect change in our education systems without waiting for federal action.

Running for school board positions or supporting local educational initiatives can make a significant difference. Change starts with us, and by taking an active role, we can work towards improving education for all students.
