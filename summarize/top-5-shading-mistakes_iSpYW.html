Top 5 Shading Mistakes

As an art instructor with a decade of experience, I've seen countless portfolios and critiqued thousands of assignments. Over the years, I've noticed that certain fundamental issues in student work persist, regardless of technological advancements or artistic trends. Today, I'll share five common shading mistakes that many artists make and provide solutions to help you improve your drawings.

Mistake #5: Icing Before Cake

One of the most frequent errors I see is artists focusing on advanced techniques before mastering the fundamentals. It's like trying to decorate a cake before you've even baked it!

Many beginners get excited about practicing complex techniques, anatomy, and shading details. While these are important, they won't necessarily improve your drawings if you're making major mistakes in proportion, perspective, and gesture.

Remember: Build your skills with fundamentals first. Each concept builds on the last. Here's a quick breakdown of the skill hierarchy:

  1. Basic skills (straight lines, proportions, dynamic poses)
  2. Perspective
  3. Simple forms
  4. Shading
  5. Anatomy, techniques, and rendering details

I learned this lesson the hard way. Years ago, I drew a portrait that looked impressive at first glance. I had mastered some shading tricks – an exaggerated core shadow, reflected light, and a bright highlight. But underneath that fancy "icing," the drawing was full of structural errors. I didn't truly understand form; I just knew how to create the illusion of 3D.

The takeaway: Don't rush to draw eyelashes before you've properly constructed the eye socket. Master the fundamentals before moving on to the flashy stuff.

Mistake #4: Bad Reference

Using poor-quality reference photos is a common pitfall that's surprisingly easy to fix. A heavily edited or distorted photo might look great on social media, but it won't translate well into a drawing.

Avoid using: - Overexposed or underexposed photos - Flat-lit or flash-lit photos - Blurry images - Photos with extreme lens distortion - Low-resolution images - Heavily photoshopped pictures from magazines

Instead, look for: - Photos with good lighting and clear shadows - High-resolution images where you can see details like nostril and eyelid forms

For beginners, I recommend using photos with lighting that clearly defines the forms. You can find photo packs made specifically for artists that have good lighting and minimal touch-ups.

Pro tip: Learn to take your own reference photos. And whenever possible, draw from life – it's even better than any photo.

Mistake #3: Overuse of Outlines

In the real world, objects don't have outlines. Yet as beginners, we often start by drawing thick, dark lines around everything. While there's nothing wrong with linear drawing or cartoons if that's your intention, overusing outlines can hinder realistic drawing.

Instead of thinking in 2D shapes, start thinking in 3D planes. Understanding the structure and planes of an object (like a nose) will help you place patches of tone that create a convincing form – no outlines needed.

That said, outlines can be used effectively in realistic drawing when applied thoughtfully. Take inspiration from masters like John Singer Sargent:

  • Use outlines sparingly and deliberately
  • Vary the weight of your lines
  • Combine outlined and non-outlined elements for contrast
  • Use outlines to make certain forms pop or to simplify secondary elements

Remember, you can create a whole picture without any outlines, just as we see things in real life.

Mistake #2: Afraid of the Dark

Many artists, especially when drawing portraits, are hesitant to use dark values in their shadows. This often stems from the misconception that light skin always means light values, or from fear of making irreversible marks.

But here's the truth: Shadows are the absence of light. They're supposed to be dark, even on fair skin. By shying away from dark shadows, you're removing the mood created by the lighting and losing the 3-dimensionality of your forms.

To avoid muddy or cartoony drawings: 1. Learn to see values correctly 2. Separate the light family from the shadow family in your drawings 3. Remember the rule: the lightest dark is darker than the darkest light

If you're struggling with lighting, don't try to invent a new light setup – that's advanced territory. Instead, choose reference photos with clear, effective lighting.

Mistake #1: Sloppy Values

The most common shading mistake I see is poor organization of values. This includes not making shadows dark enough (as mentioned above) and making halftones too dark.

To understand value organization, you need to grasp how light interacts with form:

  1. Center light: The brightest spot, where the surface points directly at the light source
  2. Halftones: Progressively darker as the surface turns away from the light
  3. Terminator: The line where direct light ends and shadow begins
  4. Shadows: Areas facing away from the light source, including:
    • Bounce light
    • Reflected light
    • Occlusion shadows
    • Cast shadows
    • Core shadows

The key rule: The lightest shadow should be darker than the darkest halftone.

To maintain the illusion of form, stay consistent with your value organization throughout your drawing. You can adjust the contrast or limit your value range, but always maintain the relationships between values.

Watch out for: - Incomplete shadows with "holes" of light - Scratchy shading that makes shadows look like halftones and vice versa

For more realistic, 3-dimensional drawings, aim for consistent, clean shadows that clearly communicate what is light and what is shadow.

Remember, even when masters break these rules, they do so intentionally and with great skill. Focus on mastering the fundamentals before experimenting with rule-breaking techniques.

By avoiding these five common shading mistakes, you'll be well on your way to creating more realistic, dynamic, and professional-looking artwork. Keep practicing, stay mindful of these principles, and don't be afraid to share this knowledge with your fellow artists. We're all in this learning journey together!
