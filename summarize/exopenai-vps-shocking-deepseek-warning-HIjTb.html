In this article, you'll dive into Dario Amodei's thoughts on DeepSea, a Chinese AI company, and the implications of AI scaling laws and export controls. Dario, the founder of Anthropic, shares insights on the current state of AI development, potential futures, and the importance of regulating technology exports to China.

AI Scaling Laws and Shifting the Curve

Dario emphasizes the significance of scaling laws in AI. The principle is simple, as you scale up training by increasing data, compute power, and training time, you achieve better results across cognitive tasks. For example,

  • A $1 million model might solve 20% of important coding tasks.
  • A $10 million model might solve 40%.
  • A $100 million model might solve 60%.

He discusses shifting the curve, where innovations make AI systems more efficient. Improvements in model architecture or new training methods can shift the performance-cost curve, allowing for better results at lower costs. This means companies can achieve higher performance without proportionally increasing expenditures.

The Jevons Paradox in AI

Dario points out that as AI models become more efficient and cheaper to train, companies tend to invest even more in developing smarter models. This is akin to the Jevons Paradox, where increased efficiency leads to higher overall consumption of a resource. In AI, any cost savings are often reinvested to create more advanced models because the value of improved intelligence is so significant.

Shifting the Paradigm with Reinforcement Learning

A notable shift in AI training involves using reinforcement learning to enhance models' reasoning skills. Instead of solely scaling pre-trained models, companies now add a second training stage focused on chains of thought. Since this approach is relatively new, there's substantial room for rapid advancements as it scales.

DeepSea's Advances

Recently, DeepSea released two models:

  • DeepSea V3: A pre-trained model that demonstrated impressive reasoning abilities. It reached performance levels close to state-of-the-art US models while being cost-effective.
  • DeepSea R1: Built upon V3, it adds a reinforcement learning stage, similar to methods used by leading US companies.

Dario notes that while DeepSea's progress is significant, it's a natural progression on the cost reduction curve. The primary distinction is that these advancements are coming from a Chinese company, marking a significant geopolitical development.

The Role of Export Controls

Dario advocates for stronger export controls on advanced chips to China. He outlines two potential futures:

  1. Bipolar World: Both the US and China possess powerful AI models. This scenario could lead to intense competition, with the possibility of China focusing more on military applications due to its centralized governance.

  2. Unipolar World: Only the US and its allies have access to the most advanced AI models. This could maintain a strategic advantage for democratic nations.

He emphasizes that well-enforced export controls are crucial to prevent China from acquiring the millions of chips needed to match US capabilities. These controls could determine whether we end up in a unipolar or bipolar world regarding AI dominance.
