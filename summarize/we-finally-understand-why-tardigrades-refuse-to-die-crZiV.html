We FINALLY Understand Why Tardigrades Refuse to Die

We all know tardigrades, you love them, and you couldn't kill one even if you tried. These tiny creatures, also known as water bears, have survived some of the most extreme conditions imaginable. They've been shot from a gun at 900 m/s, boiled at 150°C, frozen at -270°C, subjected to pressures 400 times Earth's atmosphere, and even exposed to the vacuum of space and cosmic radiation. Yet, they continue to thrive.

Discovering the Water Bear

Back in 1773, German biologist Johann Goeze peered into a microscope and spotted something unusual. When I imagine his discovery, I picture a small, wrinkly creature with eight stumpy legs tipped with tiny claws, making its way across microalgae. He named it the water bear due to its bear-like gait. Three years later, Italian biologist Lazzaro Spallanzani gave them the name tardigrade, meaning "slow walker."

The Tun State and Cryptobiosis

In 1842, French zoologist Louis Doyère made a remarkable observation. When tardigrades' habitats dried out, he watched them transform from active creatures into shrivelled, desiccated balls, a state later called a tun. In this state, known as cryptobiosis, their metabolic activity reduces to almost undetectable levels, down to 0.01% of their normal rate.

The Science Behind Their Survival

Intrinsically Disordered Proteins (TDPs)

In 2017, researchers led by Thomas Boothby discovered that tardigrades produce unique proteins called tardigrade-specific intrinsically disordered proteins (TDPs). Under stress, these proteins transform into a glass-like matrix inside the cells, protecting internal structures from damage due to extreme dehydration or temperature.

DNA Repair Mechanisms

Tardigrades also have proteins that protect their DNA from radiation. Proteins like Dsup (damage suppressor) bind directly to DNA strands, shielding them from ionizing radiation. Another protein, TRD1, acts like molecular glue, stabilizing chromosomes even when they begin to fray under radiation stress.

Potential Human Applications

Understanding how tardigrades survive extreme conditions could have profound implications for us. By harnessing their protective proteins, we might protect human cells from radiation damage. Experiments have shown that introducing Dsup and TRD1 proteins into human cells reduces DNA damage from radiation by up to 40%.

This could help us survive in off-world habitats with weak or no atmosphere, like the Moon or Mars, and it could enable us to travel into deep space, where radiation levels are high. It might even lead to advances in cryopreservation, allowing organs, and perhaps one day us, to be frozen and revived.

By studying their unique biology, we can learn to protect ourselves and push the boundaries of exploration. The future of human survival might just lie within these tiny, nearly indestructible creatures.
