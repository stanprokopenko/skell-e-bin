What Drawing From Imagination Really Is

When I draw from imagination, I’m not skipping construction. I’m manipulating form in perspective and calculating framing and shaping in my head. Pros can do this after decades of practice. For the rest of us, I train it with five progressive levels that build the same skills.

The 5 Levels

1) Level 1: Basic Forms in Space
I isolate perspective and form. I practice simple primitives in perspective and organize them in three ways: circulating, clustered/stacking, and expanding in space. I use a forgiving medium (pencil or ballpoint) and focus on clear volume. This is already drawing from imagination and becomes the foundation for objects like cars and buildings.

2) Level 2: Sketching (Iterative)
I draw loosely and iteratively. I change my ideas as I go, correct lines, and erase. Pencil or ballpoint works best. This is the standard workflow for many pros and often yields the best results because I can test and improve decisions.

3) Level 3: Preconstruction
I pre-place boxes or mannequins to lock in perspective and form, then draw the scene on top. This reduces what I must juggle in my head. I’m more decisive here (often with a brush pen), but I can be loose while setting up the structure. I scale difficulty by how detailed my boxes/mannequins are. More info equals more consistent figures.

4) Level 4: Assisted Freehand
I remove form scaffolding and keep only a perspective grid (or even just a horizon line). I must draw forms and anatomy directly. I track vanishing points, horizon, and scale. Useful rule: anything crossing the horizon line stays aligned across the scene (for example, seated characters’ mouths aligning with the horizon). I scale difficulty by how detailed the grid is.

5) Level 5: Direct Freehand
Blank page. I rely on everything I practiced. At minimum, I imagine a horizon line to ground the scene and scale. I draw whatever comes to mind and apply the perspective rules.

How I Practice

  • I don’t move through levels strictly. I jump between them to challenge my brain at different difficulties.
  • I constantly draw through forms, deconstruct objects, and understand volume in space.
  • I scale complexity in Levels 3 and 4 by adjusting how much structure or grid I give myself.
  • I always practice observation. Drawing from imagination only uses what I’ve studied.
